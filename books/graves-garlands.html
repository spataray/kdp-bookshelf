Graves & Garlands: A Cozy Fantasy Romance of Death, Dahlias, and Second Chances

The steam rising from her mug of chamomile and valerian root did absolutely nothing to cut the damp chill clinging to the floorboards of The Wild Rose.

Amandine Rosier pressed the flat of her palm against her lower back, tilting her pelvis until a sharp, satisfying pop echoed through the quiet shop. Forty-seven. Her body was a map she was still actively relearning to read. For two decades, her ex-husband had mapped her according to his own pathetic, narrow cartography, labeling her softening waistline as neglect and her silver-threaded curls as a tragedy to be managed. But here, in the damp, peat-scented air of her own shop, those details felt like sovereign territory. Hard-won. Her forearms were corded and strong from hauling heavy cedar flats. The skin around her eyes crinkled with the genuine humor of a woman who was entirely done smiling on command.

She dragged a thumb over the velvety, blood-red petal of a late-blooming dahlia. The plant shivered. Under her touch, its stems vibrated with a faint, golden-green hum.

This was her magic. It wasn't theatrical; it was a quiet, stubborn thing that lived in her blood, coaxing roots to drink and tight buds to unfurl. It had gone silent during the final, suffocating years of her marriage, choked out by the sterile, suburban domesticity she had forced herself to inhabit. But in Oakhaven, surrounded by untamed hedgerows and ancient oak forests, her craft had returned like a rising tide.

Then, the air changed.

No wind. Just a sudden, clean theft of heat.

Amandine stiffened. The golden-green resonance in her chest faltered, replaced by a cold, copper taste on the back of her tongue. On the wooden counter, the glass jar of cut marigolds gave a soft, discordant ring. The vibrant yellow petals began to curl at the tips, turning the color of bruised parchment.

Outside, hard, icy pellets of sleet began to click against the windowpanes—a sudden, aggressive departure from the soft autumn mist that had hung over the village all morning.

The brass bell above the door didn't merely chime; it clattered violently against the wood as the door was kicked open.

A man stepped into the shop. He brought the scent of rain-soaked slate, overturned earth, and old, undisturbed stone. He had to duck his head to clear the low oak lintel. He was younger than her—mid-thirties, she guessed—with the massive, quiet frame of someone who spent his days doing heavy, somber labor. His dark hair was plastered to his forehead by the sleet, and his jaw was shadowed by a dense stubble that did nothing to soften the hard, striking line of his mouth.

But it was his eyes that made Amandine grip the edge of her wooden workbench. They were the color of wet river stones, ancient and heavy, carrying the unmistakable, chilling weight of a bone-binder.

A necromancer.

"I was told the shopkeeper here could help with a transition," he said. His voice was a low, resonant baritone that vibrated right through the floorboards, settling in the soles of Amandine’s boots.

Amandine swallowed, her throat dry, but she didn't lower her chin. She had spent too many years shrinking in the presence of dominant men to do it now, even if the man standing before her possessed a magic that made her own skin prickle with primeval alarm.

"The village of Oakhaven has a delicate ecosystem," Amandine said, her voice steady and laced with the quiet authority of her mature years. "The living and the dead are meant to keep their boundaries. Your kind usually stays near the Silent Garden on the hill."

A faint, dry smile touched the corner of his mouth, though his eyes remained intensely serious. He took a slow step forward, his heavy leather boots leaving dark, wet prints on her clean floor. "I am Kerrigan Stone. The new Warden of the parish cemetery. And I assure you, Mrs. Rosier, I have no desire to disturb your boundaries. But the earth next door is old, and it is crowded. The souls there do not always rest easy."

Amandine felt her heart thump against her ribs. He knew her name. More than that, he was looking at her with a heavy, unblinking focus that made her feel suddenly, blindingly visible. It wasn't the casual, dismissive glance she was used to receiving from younger men in the village, nor was it the critical, assessing gaze of her ex-husband. Kerrigan looked at her as if she were a force—a woman of substance, of power, whose maturity was not a decline, but a peak.

"You're the new undertaker," she murmured, her fingers tightening around the wooden handle of her pruning shears. "Clotilde Varga warned me about you. She said the new warden carried a shadow that would curdle the milk in the dairy."

"Clotilde Varga is an elder who enjoys her tea with a side of melodrama," Kerrigan said. He stopped just on the other side of her counter. Up close, she could see the faint lines of exhaustion etched around his eyes—the physical toll of a magic that dealt constantly with ending and decay. Yet, there was an undeniable, raw vitality to him. The heat radiating from his body seemed to fight against the very cold his magic conjured. "I need rosemary. And marigolds. A heavy bundle of both, dried or fresh. The old plague pit at the eastern boundary of the churchyard is stirring. The spirits there are restless, cold, and they are seeking warmth. I need the herbs of remembrance to anchor them, to soothe the rot."

Amandine’s breath hitched. "The plague pit? That shares a stone wall with my greenhouse."

"I know," Kerrigan said, his voice softening, taking on a darker, more intimate quality. "Which is why I am here. Your life-magic is... potent, Amandine. Even from the cemetery, I can feel the warmth of your shop. To the dead, you are a beacon of heat. If we do not quiet them, they will press against your walls, seeking the spark you carry."

The warning sent a shiver down her spine, but it was the sound of her name on his lips—spoken with a low, reverent heat—that made her hands tremble. She turned away, using the movement to hide the sudden, hot flush that crept up her neck. She reached for the wooden drying racks behind her, her fingers finding a thick, fragrant bundle of dried rosemary and a basket of freshly cut, deep orange marigolds.

"We do not mix our crafts, Mr. Stone," she said, her back to him as she gathered the stems. Her voice was tighter now, thick with the internal friction of her pride and the ancient taboos of their community. "The elders are very clear on this. Life-magic and death-magic are fundamentally incompatible. If they touch, they corrupt. Your rot will curdle my green, and my growth will bloat your quiet dead."

"I am well aware of the catechism," Kerrigan said. He was closer now. She hadn't heard him move, but she could feel the cold draft of his presence hovering just behind her shoulder. "But the elders are not the ones living on the edge of a shifting grave. I am. And so are you."

She turned back to face him, the bundle of herbs held between them like a shield. "I can handle my own wards."

"Can you?" He tilted his head, his slate-gray eyes dropping to her mouth before rising back to meet her gaze. "Your marigolds are already dying just from me standing in your doorway, Amandine. Imagine what will happen when the sun goes down and the frost takes the soil."

He reached out, his broad, calloused hand extending to take the herbs.

Amandine did not pull back. Her pride, her fierce, hard-won independence, demanded that she stand her ground. She held the bundle firm, her knuckles white.

When Kerrigan’s fingers brushed hers, the reaction was immediate and terrifying.

A sharp, crackling shock tore through the space between them. It was a physical blow, a sudden, violent clash of polar opposites. The green, vibrant life in Amandine’s veins surged outward, a golden-green spark of pure creation, while the heavy, silent frost of Kerrigan’s necromancy rose to meet it like a wall of dark iron.

The rosemary in his hand instantly blackened, the fragrant green needles curling into ash that drifted down between them. At the same time, a pale, biting frost crept up Amandine’s wrist. The cold was agonizing—a sudden, numbing deadness that threatened to climb her arm and freeze her beating heart.

She gasped, her knees buckling slightly.

Kerrigan didn't let go. Instead of pulling away in horror, his hand clamped down over her freezing wrist. His grip was fiercely warm, his physical strength anchoring her even as his magic sent a wave of icy dread through her senses.

"Look at me," he commanded, his face mere inches from hers. His breath was warm against her cold skin. "Don't fight it. Balance it. Yield a little, Amandine. Let the cold find its level."

"You're... you're killing my light," she whispered, her eyes wide with a mixture of terror and an intoxicating, forbidden thrill. She had never felt a magic so absolute, so heavy. It was the antithesis of everything she was, yet, as his hand held her wrist, she felt a strange, resonant vibration deep in her bones. It was a terrifying, beautiful harmony—the silence that made the music possible.

"I am not killing it," Kerrigan growled, his slate-gray eyes dark with an intensity that burned right through her defenses. "I am containing it. If you let it flare, it will burn you out. Trust me."

She gritted her teeth, her mature resolve overriding her panic. She drew a deep breath, reaching deep into the earth beneath her floorboards, pulling up the deep, slow-burning heat of the autumn soil. She didn't push against his cold; she let her warmth wrap around it, softening the sharp edges of his frost.

Slowly, the blackening of the rosemary stopped. The frost on her wrist began to melt, turning into tiny, glistening beads of water that ran down her arm.

They stood chest-to-chest, panting, their hands locked over the ruined bundle of herbs. The air in the shop was thick, charged with the scent of ozone, burnt sugar, and rich, damp earth.

Amandine looked down at their joined hands. His fingers were large, square-tipped, and covered in light scars from stone and soil. They were the hands of a younger man, full of vigor and drive, yet they held her with a profound, trembling restraint. He wasn't trying to overpower her. He was holding her as if she were the most precious, volatile thing he had ever encountered.

"The elders were wrong," Kerrigan whispered, his voice rough. "It doesn't corrupt. It... it demands everything you have to keep it steady."

Amandine pulled her hand back, her skin tingling where his touch had been. She felt a sudden, profound emptiness in her marrow, a hollow ache that cried out for the cold weight of his hand to return. It was a dangerous, seductive feeling—one she knew she had to resist.

"You must go, Mr. Stone," she said, her voice trembling despite her best efforts to keep it firm. She stepped back, putting the heavy oak counter between them once more. "You have your herbs. What is left of them. Take them and quiet your graves."

Kerrigan looked at his blackened hand, then up at her. His eyes held a promise that made her heart race. "This isn't over, Amandine. The barrier between our gardens is thin. And tonight, the moon is full. Keep your hearth burning. If the cold comes, do not try to fight it alone."

He pulled a silver coin from his pocket, leaving it on the counter with a quiet clink, before turning and stepping back out into the sleet.

Amandine stood frozen behind the counter, her breath coming in shallow, ragged gasps. She looked at her wrist, where a faint, pale ring remained—the ghost of his frost, fading slowly back into her warm, tanned skin. She had rebuilt her life to be quiet, to be safe, to be entirely her own. But the look in Kerrigan's eyes had awakened something she thought had died decades ago: a fierce, demanding desire to be known, to be touched, to be challenged by someone who saw her strength and wanted it anyway.

She shook herself, trying to dispel the lingering warmth in her chest. She had work to do. She had to reinforce the perimeter wards around her shop and the greenhouse before nightfall.

She walked to the back of the shop, where the glass wall of her greenhouse looked out onto the ancient stone boundary wall separating her property from the parish cemetery. The sky had turned a bruised, sickly purple, the sleet turning into a heavy, driving rain that lashed against the glass.

She closed her eyes, placing her hands against the cool glass, and began to sing. It was a low, wordless melody—a song of binding, of roots digging deep, of thorns growing thick and sharp to protect the sanctuary within. She poured her remaining strength into the wards, watching as faint, golden-green vines of light traced their way along the foundation stones, weaving a protective web around The Wild Rose.

She finished the song with a shuddering breath, her forehead leaning against the glass. The ward felt strong. It felt secure.

Then, a sound cut through the steady drumming of the rain.

It was a low, scraping rattle, like iron teeth dragging over stone.

Amandine opened her eyes.

On the other side of the boundary wall, in the shadow of the old weeping willows of the cemetery, the ground was moving. The wet, black earth of the old plague pit was churning, bubbling upward as if boiled from below.

A sudden, violent shockwave hit the greenhouse.

The golden-green vines of her ward flared brilliantly, shrieking in protest, before they shattered with a sound like breaking glass. The protective light dissolved into a shower of cold, lifeless sparks.

From the darkened depths of the cemetery, a low, unnatural howl rose, carrying the scent of rot and old iron directly toward her unprotected door.

The heavy ceramic urn was cold against Amandine Rosier’s thighs. She hoisted it onto the display table with a dull grunt, her body protesting the weight.

At forty-seven, she had learned the hard way that her body kept its own score. It had taken seven years of quiet, post-divorce reconstruction to actually listen to it. Right on cue, her lower back gave a sharp, familiar twinge—the predictable tax of spending six hours hunched over wire frames and wet floral foam. She paused, pressing the flat of her palm against her spine, letting her thumb dig into the tight muscle.

She took a slow, deliberate breath, letting the shop slide into her lungs.

Rosier’s Blooms smelled of crushed eucalyptus, damp peat, and the heavy, honeyed sweetness of late-summer dahlias. It was her sanctuary. She had built it claw by claw, pot by pot, defying the husband who had once sneered that she would wither the moment she left his estate to pursue her "little potting hobby."

Well. She hadn’t withered.

If anything, she had ripened. The magic in her bones had settled, growing richer and less frantic with age. There was a striking silver wing at her left temple now, cutting clean through her thick chestnut waves, and her waist had softened, losing the sharp, starved angles of her anxious twenties. She liked the weight of herself now. She liked the physical space she occupied.

The quiet of her morning shattered with the frantic rattle of the shop’s front bell.

"Amandine! Tell me you've seen what’s landing on our doorstep."

Clotilde Varga, the village baker, swept in with the scent of roasted yeast and melted butter trailing from her woolen shawl. She carried a basket of fresh brioche, her round face flushed with the kind of high-voltage energy only fresh gossip could generate.

"Morning, Clotilde," Amandine said, wiping her soil-stained hands on her canvas apron. "If you’re talking about the moving carts next door, yes. My floorboards have been vibrating since six AM."

"It’s not just carts," Clotilde hissed, leaning over the counter so fast she nearly took out a vase of ranunculus. Her voice dropped to a conspiratorial hum. "It’s him. Kerrigan Stone. He’s leased the old dry goods storefront. Do you have any idea what he’s putting in there?"

Amandine picked up her brass shears, snipping the stems of a few pale peach Juliet roses. Snip. Snip. "I assumed a bookseller. Or maybe an apothecary."

"A soul-guiding parlor," Clotilde whispered, making a hasty sign of the warding cross over her chest. "He’s a necromancer, Amandine. Death-magic. Right next door to your glasshouse. It’s indecent. The council should never have approved the lease. You know what the old texts say about the overlap. Life and death sharing a roof-line? It’s a rot-risk."

Amandine’s hand stilled on the rose stems.

A cold prickle of apprehension traced its way down the back of her neck. The magical community of their small valley was governed by old, unspoken boundaries. Life-magic—her own calling, the quickening of sap, the encouragement of bloom and root—was supposed to be kept strictly separate from the somber, heavy arts of the transitioners. Necromancy wasn't evil, but it was cold. It was the preservation of bone, the guiding of lingering spirits, the quietude of the grave.

"We share a brick wall," Amandine murmured, her eyes drifting to the eastern side of her shop, where the ancient red brick was covered in climbing ivy. Just four inches of clay and mortar.

"Exactly," Clotilde said, nodding. "If I were you, I’d go set your boundaries early. Don't let his draft ruin your stock."

After Clotilde left, leaving behind a warm brioche and a lingering cloud of anxiety, the silence in the shop felt different. Heavier.

Amandine looked down at the Juliet roses in her hand. They were perfect—dense, multi-petaled cups of soft peach, smelling of classic tea and apricot. On a sudden impulse of neighborly defense, she gathered a dozen of them, wrapping the stems in damp brown paper. She wasn't going to hide in her shop waiting for the cold to seep through the brick. She would introduce herself, state her business, and establish the physical and magical boundaries of their co-existence.

She stepped out onto the cobblestones. The autumn air was crisp, but as she approached the door of the next-door storefront—now bearing a discreet, polished brass plaque that read The Last Rest—the temperature dropped.

It wasn't a wind-chill. It was a dense, metallic stillness that made the moisture in her throat catch.

Amandine pushed the heavy oak door open.

The interior of The Last Rest was a stark, beautiful contrast to her own sun-drenched jungle. The walls were painted a deep, matte charcoal, lit only by the pale light filtering through the front window and a few beeswax candles. The air smelled of dry dust, myrrh, old parchment, and cold slate. It was solemn, gorgeous, and utterly devoid of moisture.

On a stepladder in the center of the room, hanging a heavy iron candelabra, was a man.

Amandine stopped, her fingers tightening around the paper-wrapped roses.

He was younger than her. That was her first, distinct thought. Not a boy, by any means, but a man in the full, robust force of his mid-thirties. Perhaps thirty-four. He had broad, powerful shoulders that stretched the dark linen of his shirt, and thighs that gripped the rungs of the ladder with effortless balance. His hair was black as a crow’s wing, save for two striking, symmetrical streaks of silver at his temples that framed a sharp, clean jawline.

As the door clicked shut behind her, he turned.

His eyes were a deep, storm-grey, set beneath heavy brows. They were the eyes of someone who spent his days looking at things most people spent their entire lives trying to ignore. There was no youthfulness in his gaze; it was heavy, ancient, and entirely focused.

He looked at her. Really looked at her.

Amandine felt a sudden, hot flush rise from her collarbone to her cheeks. Not a hot flash—she knew those all too well these days—but a sharp, localized spike of heat. His gaze traveled slowly from the silver in her hair, down the curve of her throat, over the generous swell of her hips beneath her work apron, and finally to the flowers in her hands.

"Can I help you?" he asked. His voice was a low, resonant baritone that seemed to vibrate in the soles of her boots.

"I'm Amandine Rosier," she said, keeping her voice steady despite the sudden flutter in her chest. She took two steps forward, her boots clicking on the dark slate floor. "I own the florist shop next door. I wanted to welcome you to the street."

Kerrigan descended the ladder. His movements were deliberate, fluid, and entirely too confident. Up close, he easily cleared six feet. He smelled of cold rain and pine smoke.

"Kerrigan Stone," he said, offering a hand.

Amandine hesitated, then reached out.

The moment her skin met his, the shock of it went straight to her teeth. His hand was large, the palms calloused, and his skin was shockingly cold—not like ice, but like the deep, shaded earth beneath a winter frost. But beneath that coldness, she felt a sudden, violent thrum of energy. It was like touching a live wire buried in snow.

A low gasp escaped her lips.

Instantly, the magic between them reacted.

The Juliet roses in her hand shuddered. Amandine watched in horror as the vibrant peach petals began to curl at the edges, turning a bruised, papery brown. The sweet apricot scent vanished, replaced by the faint, earthy smell of leaf-mold. The decay spread down the stems, wilting the leaves into limp, dark ribbons in a matter of seconds.

"Ah," Kerrigan murmured, his eyes widening slightly as he looked down at the ruined bouquet. "I apologize. My aura tends to... draw the moisture out of delicate things when I'm concentrated."

But he didn't release her hand. His grip was firm, his thumb resting right over the pulse point of her wrist, where her blood was rushing in a frantic rhythm.

"It's not just you," Amandine whispered.

A sudden, warm draft swept through the cold room, smelling of damp earth and aggressive, wild pollen. It blew from her side of the threshold, a golden, shimmering dust that swirled around Kerrigan's dark boots and rose toward the display cases behind him.

On a mahogany sideboard sat a beautifully articulated, decorative skeleton of a red deer—a piece used to demonstrate the structural preservation of his craft.

As the pollen touched the dry bone, a soft, cracking sound echoed through the quiet parlor. Tiny, pale green shoots erupted from the seams of the deer’s skull. Within heartbeats, delicate, lacy stems of pastel-pink baby's breath burst from the eye sockets and coiled around the antlers, crowning the dead beast in a whimsical, ridiculously cheerful wreath of fresh spring flowers.

Kerrigan slowly let go of her hand. He turned to look at the deer skull, his jaw tightening.

"Well," he said, his tone dry, though there was a distinct spark of amusement—and something far hotter—in his grey eyes. "That is going to ruin my aesthetic."

Amandine took a step back, her breath coming in shallow puffs. Her hand felt scorched where he had touched her, the skin tingling with a warmth that refused to dissipate.

"This is exactly why the elders warn about the overlap," she said, her voice tight. "Our magics are fundamentally incompatible. You are wilting my stock, Mr. Stone. Those roses took three weeks of careful cultivation to bloom."

"And your pollen just brought life-energy into a space meant for quiet departure, Mrs. Rosier," Kerrigan replied, stepping closer. He didn't look angry; he looked intensely, deeply curious. He leaned his hip against the edge of a heavy oak table, crossing his arms. The movement pulled the fabric of his shirt tight across his chest, and Amandine’s eyes involuntarily flicked to the shadow of hair visible at his collar. "The souls who come here need stillness. It’s hard to find peace when the furniture is trying to grow leaves."

"Then we have a problem," she said, her chin lifting. She refused to be intimidated by his size, his youth, or the sheer, magnetic pull of his presence. "I have built my life here. I will not have my livelihood ruined because your draft is seeping through the brick."

Kerrigan looked at her, his gaze dropping to her mouth before rising back to her eyes. "I have no intention of ruining your livelihood, Amandine." He used her first name easily, his voice dropping to a softer, more intimate register that sent a shiver straight down her spine. "I spent five years looking for a location with the right structural grounding. This building sits on a deep granite vein. It’s perfect for my work. I'm not leaving."

"Then you need to ward your walls," she demanded, her fingers curling into her palms. "And I will ward mine. We keep our magic on our own sides of the plaster."

"Agreed," he said, though he didn't move. He remained close enough that she could feel the cold radiating from him, a counterpoint to the sudden, heavy heat gathering in her own lower belly.

It had been years since she had felt this kind of physical awareness of a man. With her ex-husband, sex had been a chore of maintenance, a transactional performance that left her feeling hollowed out and criticized. Since the divorce, she had assumed that part of her life was simply over, a closed chapter she didn't regret leaving behind.

But looking at Kerrigan Stone, she felt a fierce, terrifying tug of hunger. It was a visceral, heavy ache, centered deep in her pelvis, a demand for the very coldness he carried to soothe the hot, restless tide of her own maturity. He was a man in his prime, yet the way he looked at her wasn't the dismissive glance she often received from younger men in the village. He looked at her as if she were a force—a rich, complex vintage he wanted to slowly savor.

"You're shaking," he observed softly, his voice sliding over her skin like velvet.

"I am angry," she lied, her voice trembling slightly.

"Are you?"

He stepped off the table, closing the remaining distance between them. He was so close she could see the tiny flecks of silver in his grey eyes, the faint lines of concentration between his brows. He smelled so good—like the earth after a heavy autumn rain, clean and deep.

He reached out, his hand hovering just inches from her shoulder. He didn't touch her, respecting her boundary, but the warmth of his hand—or was it the cold?—bridged the gap, making the hairs on her arm stand up.

"I think we both feel the pull, Amandine," he said, his voice quiet, stripped of any teasing. "It’s not just the magic. It’s... us."

"We don't know each other," she said, her breath hitching. She hated how breathless she sounded, how much she wanted him to close those last few inches and press his cold, hard mouth against her throat. "And I am not a woman who plays games, Mr. Stone. I am forty-seven years old. I have raised my children, I have survived a marriage that nearly choked the life out of me, and I have built my peace. I do not have time for complications."

Kerrigan’s expression softened, the brooding solemnity of his face melting into something genuine and deeply respectful.

"I'm thirty-four," he said quietly. "I’ve spent my life dealing with the dead, watching people realize too late what they actually wanted. I don't play games either. And I don't care about your age, Amandine. I care about the fact that when you walked into this room, my heart beat faster than it has in ten years. I care that your magic makes my dead things want to live."

He looked over at the deer skull, where the baby's breath was still blooming, vibrant and pink against the white bone.

"We are going to have to find a way to manage this," he murmured, his eyes sliding back to hers, dark with a promise that made her knees feel dangerously weak. "Because I don't think either of us can ignore it."

Amandine swallowed hard, her pulse thudding thick and heavy in her throat. She looked at the wilted Juliet roses in her hand, then at the blooming skull. The physical evidence of their incompatibility was right there, yet the air between them was so thick with tension she could almost taste it—sweet, metallic, and hot.

"Ward your walls, Mr. Stone," she said, her voice dropping to a whisper as she took a step back toward the door. "By tomorrow morning. Or I will have the village council involve themselves."

"Kerrigan," he corrected her, a slow, devastating smile touching his lips. "Call me Kerrigan, Amandine."

She didn't answer. She turned and practically fled the shop, the bell chiming a frantic warning behind her as she stepped back out into the crisp, safe autumn air. But even as she crossed the threshold back into her own shop, the cold tingling on her wrist remained, a stubborn, electric reminder of the man next door.

The damp earth had gone sour. Not the clean, loamy decay of autumn rot that Amandine Rosier actually liked having in her greenhouse, but something slicker. Greasy. It smelled of rancid lard and wet copper, clinging to the back of her throat and refusing to budge, no matter how many bundles of dried eucalyptus she hung from the rafters.

Clotilde Varga wouldn't sit. She stood straight as a fence post by the winter cyclamen, her heavy, hand-woven wool shawl pinned at the throat with a tarnished scrap of silver. Her hands were a roadmap of blue veins and dark liver spots, clamped hard over the head of her ash walking stick.

“The old willow by the creek is weeping gray sap,” Clotilde said. Her voice had the dry, raspy scratch of dried husks rubbing together in a draft. “And the graves in the western plot are shifting. The dead aren't resting, Amandine. They’re cold. This rot is eating the grass roots, and it’s eating the peace of the cemetery right along with them.”

Amandine scraped her dirty palms down her canvas apron, the stiff, unwashed fabric rasping against her thighs. At forty-seven, she knew how to read the weather in this valley. She knew when a storm was coming before her joints did. But today, the air felt wrong. Stale.

“If it’s just root rot, I can mix up a copper-lime drench,” she said. “But if the graves are souring too...”

“It’s both,” Clotilde said, cutting her off. Those milky, cataracts-filmed eyes locked onto hers with a sudden, sharp focus. “And you aren't a child, Amandine. You know the way the soil works. You can’t cure with green growth what death has poisoned, and he can’t quiet the dead when the living earth is screaming. You and Kerrigan Stone are going to have to pool your resources. I’ve already sent a boy to fetch him.”

A tight knot bound itself in Amandine’s chest. Her wool collar suddenly felt hot and restrictive. “Clotilde, the interference alone—our types don't mix—”

“That’s an old wives' tale invented by men who were terrified of women who knew how to use a shovel,” Clotilde snapped. But her eyes softened just a fraction, lingering on the gray streaks at Amandine’s temples. “The real danger is physical. If your current crosses his without a buffer, you’ll sour the ground for three miles around. Keep your hands off each other’s working. Use the copper stills in the back. Just find out what's killing the valley before the first hard freeze locks the rot into the deep clay.”

When Clotilde left, her ash staff tapping a slow, rhythmic retreat against the flagstones, the greenhouse felt suddenly cavernous. And, somehow, entirely too small.

Ten minutes later, the iron bell over the door clattered.

Kerrigan Stone brought the November draft in with him. He was thirty-four, broad-shouldered, and wearing a charcoal wool coat that smelled of woodsmoke, wet soil, and old stone. He didn't look like the local boys who came in to buy lavender bundles for their girls. His skin was weather-beaten, his jawline hard, and a jagged little scar split his left eyebrow. His eyes were the color of cold river slate—the kind of look you get when you spend more time talking to headstones than living people.

“Amandine,” he said. His voice was low, dry, and carried a gravelly edge.

“Kerrigan.” She reached down to smooth her apron, realized her fingers were shaking, and shoved them deep into her pockets instead. She hated herself for that. She was forty-seven years old. She had survived a twenty-year marriage that had slowly chipped away at her soul until she finally had the sense to walk out with nothing but her clothes and her grandmother's shears. She had no business getting flustered by a man thirteen years her junior. None at all. “Clotilde saw you, then?”

“She did.” He pulled off his heavy leather work gloves, jamming them into his coat pocket. His hands were big—blunt fingers, square nails, with the dark gray smudge of grave-chalk ground deep into the creases of his knuckles. “She said we need your back kitchen. Apparently, it’s the only chimney around here that can handle a closed double-boil.”
