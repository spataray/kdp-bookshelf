    Out of the Cold: An Enemies-to-Lovers Wilderness Survival Romance

    The twin-propeller plane slammed down through the dirty grey belly of the clouds. Below, the Alaskan interior rushed up to meet them—a brutal canvas of black spruce forests, blinding white snow, and a slate sky that felt less like weather and more like a heavy, cold hand pressing down on the earth.

    Inside, everything rattled. The metal, the glass, the teeth in her skull. Vasundhara gripped her seat. Her knuckles showed pale against her dark skin. Forty-seven. She’d never felt every single one of those forty-seven years quite like she did now, trapped in this shaking tin can.

    The plane dropped again, and a sharp, familiar throb flared in her right knee. A parting gift from a slick wet floor in the university greenhouse three years back. Just a quiet, constant reminder that her body wasn't the indestructible machine of her twenties anymore. She shifted her weight on the cracked vinyl. Over the last decade, her hips had softened, her thighs thickening into the curves of a settled life. A life that had, without her permission, completely gone to pieces.

    She tucked a wire-coarse strand of hair behind her ear. Silver. Plenty of it. She didn't touch the stuff with dye. When the divorce papers were finally signed six months ago—and her ex-husband started parading his twenty-six-year-old administrative assistant around the faculty lounge—she’d actually gone to a salon with the intention of hiding it. But sitting under those harsh fluorescent lights, looking at her own tired face, she had stopped the stylist. Leave it, she’d said. The silver was hers. She’d paid for every single strand with twenty-four years of a marriage that had vanished like breath on a cold pane, and the raw, humiliating sting of losing her research grant to some younger, slicker colleague.

    She was done shrinking. Done smoothing down her rough edges just to keep the people around her comfortable.

    The skis slammed into the hardpack. The plane bucked, hard, the engine screaming as the pilot threw the props into reverse. Vasundhara let out a long, ragged breath. She’d been holding it since Seattle.

    Kamet Creek was barely a place. Just a handful of weathered, black-log cabins hunkering down against the wind at the very edge of the tree line. When the pilot finally cut the engine, the sudden silence was brutal. It was a heavy, freezing quiet that pressed hard against her eardrums.

    "We're down," a voice grunted from the cockpit.

    Vasundhara unbuckled. Her joints popped, a dry, dusty sound. Standing up, she felt her spine click-click-click as she stretched. She wasn't small. She had a tall, broad-shouldered frame—one she’d spent most of her life trying to apologize for. But out here, with the sub-zero air already seeping through the door seals, she was glad for her own weight.

    She pushed the heavy cabin door open. The cold hit her like a fist to the chest.

    It wasn't just cold; it was sharp. Inhaling felt like breathing in broken glass. Her lungs locked up, forcing her into shallow, guarded breaths. She yanked her wool scarf up over her nose. Her breath instantly turned to frost on the wool.

    "Need a hand with those?"

    She looked down the metal steps. A man was standing in the deep snow, his face mostly buried in the fur-lined hood of a heavy canvas parka.

    "I’ve got them," she said, her voice dry and muffled.

    She reached back into the cabin for her two oversized duffels. This was her entire life for the next three months: glass jars, hand lenses, pressing paper, and a stack of leather field journals. She grabbed the straps. Heavy. Packed tight with books and iron-heavy gear. Her shoulders burned as she dragged the first one toward the threshold.

    "I said, let me get those," the man said. He stepped onto the bottom rung, reaching up.

    "And I said I have them," Vasundhara snapped.

    Too sharp. But she didn't care. She didn't want his help. She didn't want anyone's damn help. For twenty years, she’d let a man run her budget, her schedule, her self-worth, until she’d convinced herself she couldn't even pick out a living room rug without his sign-off. When he walked out, she learned the hard way that depending on someone else was just a slow-motion trap. If she couldn't carry her own gear into the woods, she might as well fly back to Seattle right now.

    She swung the first bag out. He caught it easily, his big hands locking onto the canvas. He dropped it into the snow and reached back up for the second. Vasundhara was already dragging it, but her boot slipped on the icy metal step.

    She pitched forward.

    A hand shot out and clamped around her forearm.

    Even through her heavy parka and his thick leather glove, his grip was massive. Steady. He didn't jerk her down; he just held her dead in the air until her boots found purchase.

    She looked up. Met his eyes.

    Stormy, piercing grey, framed by dark lashes white with frost. He was younger than her—she could tell by the smooth skin at his temples—but there was nothing young about his face. His jaw was wide, shadowed by several days of dark, thick stubble. He looked like something carved out of the mountains and left out to freeze.

    Lachlan. The pilot who was supposed to fly her up to the high ridge where the rare sub-arctic mosses grew.

    He let go of her arm the second she was steady, stepping back into the powder. "You're Vasundhara." It wasn't a question.

    "I am." She climbed down the last two steps, her boots crunching loud in the frozen air. "And you're Lachlan."

    He didn't offer a hand. He just stared, his grey eyes scanning her face, taking in the dark shadows under her eyes, the silver in her hair, the hard set of her jaw. No warmth. Just a cold, calculating assessment that made her feel naked.

    "You're late," he rumbled. "The storm's moving faster than the weather service said. We should’ve been in the air an hour ago."

    "My connection in Anchorage was iced in," she said, keeping her voice flat despite the anger heat rising in her throat. "I got here as fast as the plane could fly."

    "The weather doesn't care about your connections," Lachlan said. He scooped up both heavy duffels, one in each hand, lifting them like they were stuffed with feathers. "Follow me. Pemba's got the stove going in the lodge. We need to look at the maps."

    He turned and walked, his long legs cutting easily through the drifts. Vasundhara watched him go, her eyes tracking the broad span of his shoulders under the dark canvas. Every movement was efficient. No wasted energy.

    She swallowed the tight lump in her throat and hurried after him, her bad knee aching with every single step.

    *

    The main lodge smelled of woodsmoke, kerosene, and old bacon grease. It was warm, but the sudden jump from sub-zero to the stifling heat of the iron woodstove made her skin tingle and itch.

    An older sherpa with deep laugh-lines and a face like cured leather was pouring boiling water into a tin kettle. Pemba. He looked up, his face splitting into a wide, genuine smile.

    "Ah, the professor!" Pemba’s voice was rich and warm. "Come, sit by the stove. You look like an icicle."

    "Thank you, Pemba," Vasundhara said, peeling off her layers. She unzipped her parka, letting the trapped heat escape. She pulled off her hat, letting her thick, wild hair fall around her shoulders. She knew she looked like a wreck—messy, static-frizzled, sweat-dampened from the haul—but she couldn't bring herself to care.

    She sat on a rough-hewn bench near the stove, stretching her hands toward the iron. The heat was so intense it hurt, a sharp prickling as life returned to her fingers.

    Lachlan dropped her bags by the door with a heavy thud. He pulled off his gloves, shoved them into his pockets, and walked over to a big pine table where a topo map was held down by river stones.

    "We have a problem," he said, not looking at her.

    Vasundhara let her shoulders drop. "What is it?"

    He pressed a scarred, blunt finger against the paper. "The ridge where your site is. It's at four thousand feet. Right now, the wind up there is gusting forty knots. Visibility is zero. I can't fly you up today. I can't even get you to the lower cabin."

    "But the moss," she said, her voice rising. "The Hylocomium splendens variant only has a three-week window to sporulate under the ice. If I miss this, the whole study is dead. My funding is gone, Lachlan. I don't have another year."

    Lachlan looked up. His grey eyes were flat, hard, and completely empty of pity. "If I fly you up there today, we die. Not next year. Today. The mountain doesn't care about your research paper."

    "It's not just a paper," she said, her voice dropping, tight with anger and that old, familiar hurt. "It's my life."

    He stared at her. His eyes drifted to the silver in her hair, then back to her dark eyes. "Then you should value it more," he said quietly.

    The words stung like a slap. Vasundhara stood up, ignoring the flare of pain in her knee. She stepped right up to the table, refusing to let him look down on her. "You don't know a damn thing about my life. Or what I value."

    "I know you're a city academic who thinks a pair of expensive boots and a university grant makes you untouchable," Lachlan said. He didn't raise his voice, which only made it worse. "I've flown your type before. You think the wild is a museum. You think you can just look at it, bag your samples, and run back to your heated apartment. It doesn't work that way."

    "I am not a tourist," she hissed, her face burning. "I’ve spent twenty years in the field. I've worked in the high Andes, in the monsoon forests of India. Places that make this little outpost look like a playground."

    "Those places weren't twenty below with a blizzard coming in," Lachlan said. He leaned forward, flat-palming the wooden table. His face was inches from hers now. She could smell him—cold air, woodsmoke, and the sharp, clean scent of leather and sweat. He was warm, radiating a quiet, dangerous energy that made her heart thud against her ribs.

    "The wind is going to drop the temperature to minus forty tonight," he whispered. "If we get stuck on that ridge, your blood will freeze before you can dig a snow cave. I'm not risking my plane, and I am sure as hell not risking my neck, for a bunch of weeds."

    "They are not weeds," she said, her breath catching. Up close, she noticed a tiny, jagged scar just under his left eye, and the small flecks of gold in his grey eyes. There was a heavy, dark grief clinging to him. She knew that look. It was the look of someone who had lost something irreplaceable and decided the world was his enemy.

    Pemba stepped between them, holding two steaming mugs of black tea. He set one in front of her, one near Lachlan.

    "The mountain is always there," Pemba said softly, his voice a quiet shield. "But today, Lachlan is right, professor. The sky is closing. We stay here tonight. Tomorrow, we look at the sky again."

    Vasundhara looked from Pemba to Lachlan. The pilot had already stepped back, his face a shuttered mask again. He picked up his tea, took a long swallow, and walked over to the window, staring out into the dark.

    A wave of exhaustion hit her, grey and heavy. She sat back down, wrapping her cold hands around the warm clay mug. The heat seeped into her palms, but inside, she felt a cold, empty ache.

    This trip was supposed to be her comeback. It was supposed to prove to the department, to her ex-husband, and mostly to herself, that she wasn't done. That she wasn't just some discarded, middle-aged woman whose best years were behind her.

    Now, she was trapped in a drafty cabin with a hostile pilot who saw her as nothing but a liability.

    *

    Dinner was quiet—canned stew and thick, heavy bread Pemba had baked on the woodstove. Vasundhara ate in silence, watching the two men.

    Lachlan barely spoke, but when he did, it was with a quiet respect for Pemba that he clearly didn't have for her. They talked about fuel levels, a broken generator at the lower camp, and a name: Thomas.

    When the name left Pemba's mouth, a heavy, suffocating silence dropped over the table. Lachlan’s hand tightened around his fork until his knuckles went white. He didn't finish his food. He just pushed his plate away, stood up, and walked out into the freezing night without a word.

    Vasundhara watched the door swing shut, a blast of arctic air sweeping across the floorboards.

    "He lost his co-pilot last winter," Pemba said quietly. "Thomas. They were flying supplies to a remote village. Hit an unexpected downdraft over the pass. Thomas didn't make it. Lachlan... he blames himself."

    Vasundhara felt a sudden, sharp ache in her chest. "Was it his fault?"

    "No," Pemba said, shaking his head. "The mountain decided. But Lachlan carries the sky on his shoulders now. He thinks if he is hard enough, cold enough, he can keep everyone safe."

    "By keeping them away," Vasundhara murmured.

    "Yes," Pemba said, his dark eyes wise and steady. "But sometimes, the things we try to keep out are the very things we need to let in."

    She didn't know what to say to that. She knew all about keeping things out. After the divorce, she’d built a wall around herself so thick that even her closest friends couldn't climb it. She’d convinced herself that isolation was the same thing as strength.

    But looking out the window at the black, wind-whipped night, she wondered if she’d just built herself a different kind of cage.

    *

    Later, the wind began to scream. It rattled the thick log walls, whistling through the moss-chinked gaps and making the oil lamps flicker.

    Vasundhara’s room was a tiny space at the back of the lodge. A narrow cot, a small pine chest, and a mountain of wool blankets. But even under the wool, the cold crept up through the floorboards, settling deep into her bones.

    She lay on her back, staring at the dark ceiling. Her body ached from the travel, but sleep was miles away. Every time she closed her eyes, she saw Lachlan’s cold, grey eyes looking at her with that intense, searching stare.

    She wasn't used to being looked at like that. For the last ten years of her marriage, her husband had looked through her, like she was just a piece of furniture he’d grown used to. She’d become invisible.

    But Lachlan had seen her. Really seen her. He’d seen her anger, her pride, her fear. He had looked at her silver hair and her older body not with the polite pity she’d come to expect, but with a sharp, heavy focus that made her skin run hot.

    She rolled onto her side, pulling the blankets up to her chin.

    Then, she heard it. A low, heavy boot-step on the floorboards outside her door.

    She waited, her heart thumping against her ribs.

    Her door didn't have a lock, just a simple wooden latch. The latch clicked. The door creaked open.

    Vasundhara sat up, clutching the blankets to her chest.

    Lachlan stood in the doorway. He’d taken off his parka, wearing only a thick, dark green wool shirt that clung to his broad chest and shoulders. He looked massive in the tiny room, his head nearly touching the low timber rafters.

    "The generator is struggling," he said, his voice a low, gravelly rasp. "The heat in these back rooms is going to dump. I brought you another blanket."

    He held out a thick, heavy buffalo hide.

    "Thank you," she whispered, her voice suddenly thin.

    He walked over to the bed, his movements slow, deliberate. He didn't just drop the hide on her; he leaned down, spreading it carefully over her legs, his hands smoothing the heavy fur.

    As he leaned, his face was close. In the dim light from the hallway, she could see his breath rising in pale, slow plumes. His scent hit her again—cold skin, woodsmoke, and a raw, heavy heat that made her stomach drop in a way she hadn't felt in twenty years.

    He didn't pull back. He stayed bent over her, his hands resting on the edges of the cot, trapping her beneath his weight.

    His grey eyes locked onto hers. The anger from earlier was gone. In its place was a dark, heavy hunger that made her breath catch in her throat.

    "You're shivering," he said softly.

    "It's... cold," she whispered.

    "Is that the only reason?"

    She swallowed hard, her fingers bunching the wool of the blanket. He was so close. She could see the pulse jumping in his throat, just above the collar of his shirt. He was thirty-four, in the absolute prime of his life, and she was forty-seven, with a soft body and a scarred heart.

    But looking at him, she didn't feel old. She didn't feel invisible.

    She felt wanted.

    "Lachlan," she murmured, his name a soft plea.

    He reached out, his big, rough hand tracing the line of her jaw. His fingers were warm, his touch surprisingly gentle. He brushed a strand of her silver hair away from her cheek, his thumb lingering on her bottom lip.

    "You're a proud woman, Vasundhara," he whispered, his voice thick. "But you can't survive this cold alone."

    "I've survived worse," she said, though her voice shook.

    "Maybe," he said, his eyes dropping to her mouth. "But you don't have to tonight."

    He leaned down, and his lips met hers.

    The wind hit Vasundhara’s cheek like a slap of wet ice the second she stepped out of the rental car. It smelled of cold kerosene and pine—the raw, sharp scent of a place that didn’t care if you lived or died. The sun was already gone, leaving behind a sky the color of a dirty bruise. She yanked her wool scarf up over her nose and ran for the hangar.

    Everything about this trip felt like a mistake. A desperate, last-minute run to escape the wreckage of her life back east.

    Inside, the hangar was a massive sheet-metal box groaning under the wind. It was warmer than the runway, but not by much. It smelled of spilled oil, old woodsmoke, and wet dog. In the middle of the concrete floor sat a tiny, battered plane on fat skis. Its red-and-white paint was flaking off in scabs.

    A man was hunched over the engine cowl, a heavy steel wrench clutched in a greasy gloved hand. He had his back to her. He was broad—his canvas jacket was worn thin at the shoulder blades—and his silver hair was pulled back into a messy knot at his collar.

    This had to be Lachlan.

    The pilot Pemba had warned her about. The guy’s got a death wish, Pemba had said. And he hates city people.

    He turned around slowly. His eyes were the color of frozen lake water. He didn't look like a rugged hero from a travel magazine; he looked like thirty years of sub-zero wind and cheap coffee. He was fifty-one, and every single year was written in the deep lines around his mouth.

    "You must be Dr. Sharma," he rumbled. His voice sounded like gravel sliding down a chute.

    He didn't offer his hand. He just looked her up and down—taking in her shiny new parka, her pristine boots, the leather satchel across her chest. She felt the silent judgment like a needle prick.

    "Vasundhara," she corrected, trying to keep her voice from shaking. "And you’re Lachlan."

    He grunted, wiping a smudge of grease across his forehead with the back of his sleeve. "Where's your gear?"

    She pointed to the two yellow Pelican cases by the door. "Packed and weighed. Exactly forty-two pounds each, just like Pemba said. I’m very meticulous."

    Lachlan dropped the wrench into a metal toolbox. Clang. The noise made her jump.

    "Meticulous," he muttered, shaking his head. "Out here, lady, forty-two pounds of books won't keep your toes from turning black if the cabin heater fails. You know how to build a fire in a blizzard?"

    Her jaw tightened. "My research is on extreme isolation and human survival. I’ve spent three years preparing for this."

    "Prepping in a heated library." He walked past her, picked up both forty-pound cases at once without even leaning into it, and carried them toward the plane.

    "My grants paid for this flight, Lachlan," she said, her voice turning sharp. "And those grants pay your salary."

    He stopped, turning his pale eyes back on her. "Those grants don’t mean jack when you’re freezing to death in a snowdrift. The weather is turning faster than the radio said. We leave now, or we don't leave at all."

    Vasundhara looked through the narrow hangar window. The sky had turned almost black, heavy with coming snow. Her stomach did a slow flip.

    "Is it safe?"

    Lachlan let out a short, dry laugh. "We’re flying into the Wrangells in January. Safe is a word for people who stay in hotels. But I’ll get you there."

    She followed him to the plane. The cabin was tiny. When she climbed into the co-pilot’s seat, she realized how small the space really was. Her shoulder was pressed right against his. He smelled of tobacco and cedar.

    "Ever been in a bush plane?" he asked, his hands flying across the instrument panel.

    "A few puddle-jumpers in India," she said, bracing her feet against the floorboards.

    He smirked, showing a flash of white teeth. "This isn't a puddle-jumper. This is Pemba."

    Vasundhara blinked. "You named your plane after the guy who booked my flight?"

    "Other way around," Lachlan said. The smirk vanished, and his voice dropped. "The kid was named after the plane. He was my co-pilot. Died in a whiteout four years ago."

    Before she could ask, he pulled a knob, and the engine roared to life. The whole metal frame vibrated, shaking her teeth.

    The plane taxied out onto the icy runway, skis rattling against the hard pack. Then, with a sickening lurch, they were off the ground, climbing straight into the gray soup.

    Below them, the lights of the tiny town vanished instantly.

    "Turbulence already?" she yelled over the engine.

    "That's just the wind saying hello," he called back, his hands light on the yoke. "Wait till we hit the pass."

    The plane dipped, hard. Her stomach stayed behind.

    "You find this fun?" she asked, clutching the dashboard.

    "I find it real!" he yelled. "Can't study this in a book, Doctor!"

    "I’m not trying to study the wind, I'm studying the human mind!" she shot back.

    Lachlan chuckled. It was a warm, surprisingly deep sound. "Sometimes, Vasundhara, you have to stop thinking and just feel the ride."

    He looked at her then—just a quick, five-second glance—but his eyes were incredibly bright. For a second, the fear in her chest shifted into something else. A sudden, hot flush that had nothing to do with the plane's heater. It had been years since a man had looked at her like she was actually there.

    Then the engine coughed.

    It wasn't a loud bang. It was a wet, choking rattle. The cabin went instantly cold.

    "What was that?" she whispered.

    Lachlan didn't answer. His face had gone totally still. His hands were moving fast now, flicking switches, pulling the throttle back and pushing it forward. The engine spluttered again, then died completely. The silence was deafening.

    "Carb ice," Lachlan said, his voice flat and calm. "We’re losing altitude."

    The plane fell. Not a dive, but a heavy, helpless slide through the swirling white.

    "Can you fix it?" Her knuckles were white on the armrest.

    "No time. Brace yourself, Vasundhara. Put your head down."

    She bent double, grabbing her knees. Her heart was hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird. Through the window, she saw a blur of dark green pine tops rising up to meet them.

    "Hold on!"

    The skis hit the snow with a sound like tearing metal. The plane spun, jarring violently sideways. The left wing clipped a spruce tree with a loud crack, throwing her head against the side window.

    Stars exploded behind her eyes. Then, absolute quiet.

    Vasundhara opened her eyes slowly. Her head throbbed, and she could taste copper in her mouth. Snow was plastered against the windshield, turning the cabin gray.

    Beside her, Lachlan was leaning his forehead against the control wheel, breathing hard. He slowly sat up, his silver hair messy across his face. He looked at her, his eyes wide with adrenaline.

    "You alive?" he asked, his voice hoarse.

    She touched her temple. Her fingers came away wet and red. "I think so. We... we stopped."

    He let out a short, breathless laugh and looked out at the endless, empty white desert surrounding the plane.

    "Yeah," he said, turning off the battery switch. "Welcome to the middle of nowhere."

    The cabin door slammed shut, rattling the rusty hinges. It blocked the howling white fury of the Alaskan blizzard, but did nothing to stop the killing cold. Inside, the air tasted of dry rot, dust, and the sharp, chemical bite of old kerosene.

    "Get him down," Lachlan grunted. His voice was raw, scraped clean by the gale. Ice caked his cheeks, turning his dark beard into stiff, frozen needles.

    Vasundhara dragged her boots across the gritty floorboards. Every single muscle screamed. She was forty-seven years old. Until this morning, her biggest physical challenge had been standing for eight hours in a humid university greenhouse, cataloging rare ferns. Dragging an unconscious pilot through a mile of waist-deep snow had broken something inside her. Her knee joints throbbed with a sickening, hot pressure. Her lungs burned with every ragged breath.

    They dropped Pemba onto a stack of dry spruce boughs and heavy canvas tarps in the corner. Lachlan moved fast. He stripped away the unconscious pilot's wet outer gear, his hands moving with a desperate, practiced efficiency. He wrapped the man in the plane’s emergency foil blankets, then threw the heavy canvas over the top.

    "Is he... breathing?" Vasundhara's teeth clicked together so hard the words came out fractured.

    Lachlan pressed two thick fingers against Pemba’s throat. Blood oozed from the split skin on his knuckles. "He’s breathing. Concussion is bad, and he's freezing, but he's dry. The boughs will keep him off the floor. He’ll hold."

    Lachlan stood. His broad shoulders nearly brushed the low, hand-hewn rafters of the cabin. He scanned the dark room. A rusted cast-iron stove sat in the center. Next to it was a small stack of graying split wood. Against the far wall sat a single wooden cot with a bare canvas mattress. That was it.

    He knelt by the stove. His clumsy, frozen fingers stuffed dry moss and birch bark into the rusty grate, then struck a match from a waterproof case. The tiny flame sputtered. It died once, then caught on the second try, licking at the bark. But the iron of the stove was thick and dead-cold. It would take hours for any real heat to reach them.

    Vasundhara stood in the center of the room, arms locked around her ribs. Then the shaking started. Not a mild shiver, but a violent, teeth-rattling spasm that started deep in her belly and vibrated all the way to her fingertips. She couldn't feel her toes anymore. A heavy, dangerous gray fog began to creep into the edges of her mind.

    Lachlan turned. His hazel eyes were dark under a heavy brow, staring at her with an intensity that made her throat lock up. He wasn’t looking at her like an academic passenger anymore. He wasn't looking at her age, or her damp, expensive gear. He looked like a man calculating the exact distance between survival and a body bag.

    "Strip," he said.

    Vasundhara blinked. Her thoughts felt like cold molasses. "What?"

    "Your clothes are damp. If you keep them on, your body heat will waste itself trying to dry the fabric, and you’ll freeze to death. Strip down to your underwear." He stepped toward the narrow cot, grabbing two heavy, coarse wool blankets from a high shelf. "We have to share the bed. It’s the only way to save our core temperature."

    "I... I can sleep on the floor. Next to Pemba," she stammered. Her pride flared up, even as her knees buckled.

    "You'll die on the floor," Lachlan said. It wasn't an argument; it was a fact. He crossed the room, his large hands reaching out to grab the zipper of her heavy parka. "Look at your hands. They're blue. You're freezing to death, Vasundhara. I'm not playing games."

    His hands were surprisingly gentle for how massive he was. He slid the zipper down, peeling the heavy, wet nylon off her shoulders. The draft hit her skin. She gasped—a sharp, ragged sound.

    "I've got you," he muttered, his voice dropping low.

    He helped her slide her arms out of the stiff sleeves. Underneath, she wore a tight merino wool top and matching gray leggings. She felt naked. For five years—ever since her husband had packed his bags to sleep with a twenty-six-year-old grad student in his department—Vasundhara had treated her own body like an unwelcome guest. She had buried her curves, her softness, and her age under baggy cardigans and sensible trousers.

    Now, she stood in a freezing shack in the middle of nowhere, stripped down in front of a thirty-four-year-old pilot who looked like he’d been hacked out of the mountain itself.

    Lachlan didn't hesitate. He yanked off his heavy outer coat, then pulled his thick wool sweater over his head in one smooth motion. Next came his thermal shirt.

    Vasundhara’s breath caught. He was big and raw and completely unpolished. His chest was broad, covered in dark hair that tapered down into his trousers. Muscles bunched in his shoulders, but there was a weariness to his frame—a shadow of the grief he'd carried since his co-pilot died in a crash a year ago.

    "Get in," he said softly.

    She climbed onto the narrow cot. Her joints were stiff, aching with a dull, throbbing pain. The canvas mattress was hard as a board and smelled of old dust. She curled on her side, pulling her knees to her chest to save whatever heat she had left.

    Lachlan climbed in behind her. The old wood creaked, groaning under their combined weight. The cot was so narrow there was no way to avoid touching him. He settled his large frame, pulling the heavy wool blankets and the crinkling foil sheets over them both, tucking them under the mattress to seal out the wind.

    Then he pulled her back against his chest.

    Vasundhara gasped at the sudden, shocking heat of his body. He felt like a furnace. He wrapped a thick, heavy arm around her waist, pulling her flush against him. His chest pressed solid against her back. His thighs slotted perfectly behind hers.

    "Shh," he whispered. His warm breath stirred the damp hair at the back of her neck. "Just breathe. Let yourself get warm."

    She was shaking so hard her teeth clicked, her entire body vibrating against his. Lachlan didn't pull away. He only held her tighter, burying his face in the crook of her neck.

    "You're freezing," he muttered. He found her icy hands and rubbed them between his broad, calloused palms. His skin was rough, like sandpaper, but the friction sent a stinging, prickling heat right up her arms.

    "I'm sorry," she whispered. Her eyes burned with sudden, humiliating tears. "I'm not... I'm not made for this."
