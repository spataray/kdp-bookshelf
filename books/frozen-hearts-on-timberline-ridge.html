    Frozen Hearts on Timberline Ridge

    The door of the rented Jeep groaned, resisting her shoulder before finally giving way against a frozen bank of plowed snow. Alaska didn't just feel cold. It felt heavy. A thick, static weight that bit the back of her throat the second she inhaled. At forty-six, Gisela MacLeod had survived her share of Oregon winters, but Timberline Ridge wasn't Oregon. This was a brutal, monochrome expanse of black spruce and jagged stone, drowned in slate grey and blinding white.

    She lingered on the edge of the vinyl driver's seat, her boots hovering inches above the packed crust, and let out a long, ragged breath. The steam of it evaporated before she could even watch it rise.

    There was no backup plan. None.

    She looked down at her hands. Her knuckles were still stained with the faint, stubborn yellow of old walnut husks and marigold dye from her Portland studio. For twenty-three years, these hands had lived on someone else’s clock. They’d packed Gretchen’s school lunches, scrubbed grease off kitchen counters, and held a dying marriage together until the seams simply rotted away. Now? Gretchen was a freshman at Oregon State, her empty bedroom a quiet museum of old concert posters, and the divorce papers were locked in a metal filing cabinet back home.

    All she had left was her studio. It was her identity, sure, but more practically, it was her rent. And she was three months behind. This conservation grant—a grueling, isolated project to document and illustrate sub-arctic lichens on the ridge—was her last stand. A desperate, final line of defense against a foreclosure notice. If she failed out here, she wasn't just losing her lease. She was losing the only version of herself she actually liked.

    Gisela swung her legs out. Her left knee gave a sharp, wet pop—that familiar, dull ache blooming right behind the patella. She gritted her teeth, rubbing the joint through her thick denim. The damp sub-arctic cold had an uncanny way of finding every old fracture, every place where forty-six years of gravity and hard work had worn down her bones. It was a humbling, irritating reality. Her body was no longer the self-healing, bulletproof machine of her twenties.

    The silence here was massive. Deafening, even. No sirens. No hum of the Portland traffic. Just three things:

    1. The dry, paper-like scrape of wind through the spruce needles. 2. The ominous, low rumble of shifting snowpack somewhere on the peaks. 3. The loud, rhythmic crunch of her own boots.

    She dragged her canvas rucksack to the lip of the tailgate. Her shoulders burned as she hoisted it. Inside were specimen jars, magnifying lenses, botanical sketchbooks, and just enough dry rations to keep her alive in the field cabin for her first week. Next came the snowshoes—heavy, modern aluminum frames with aggressive, toothy steel crampons.

    She bent over to thread the bindings.

    Then, the heat hit.

    It started right in the center of her ribcage. A sudden, suffocating surge of fire that had absolutely nothing to do with the Alaskan weather. Within seconds, the heat rushed upward, clawing up her throat, painting her neck crimson, and blooming across her cheeks. It was her own biology misfiring with spectacular, mocking force. Sweat broke out along her hairline, turning instantly cold and greasy in the sub-zero air.

    "Not now," she whispered.

    The heat was thick. Suffocating. She felt trapped inside her own skin, strangled by the high-tech layers she'd bought to survive. Her fingers shook as she ripped off her insulated gloves and threw them onto the tailgate. She yanked the zipper of her Gore-Tex parka down to her waist, then tore at the buttons of her heavy wool overshirt, stripping both off until she stood in nothing but her thin, grey merino wool base layer.

    The freezing air hit her bare neck like a bucket of iced water. Actual steam rose from her collar, curling up into the grey sky. She closed her eyes, tilting her chin up, letting the biting wind freeze the sweat on her chest. It was a ridiculous, intoxicating contrast: the private, hormonal fire of midlife colliding with the ancient, indifferent ice of the wilderness. She stood there, chest heaving, feeling exposed, raw, and incredibly alive.

    "Are you trying to kill yourself, or are you just stupid?"

    The voice was gravelly, dry, and entirely too close.

    Gisela's eyes snapped open. She whipped her head around, her heart jumping.

    A man stood at the edge of the clearing, blending so perfectly with the deep shadows of the spruce that he might have grown there. He was tall—over six feet—with the kind of broad, heavy shoulders that filled out a weathered, wax-canvas duster. His face was shadowed by a low-profile winter cap, but she could see a hard, square jaw covered in dark, frost-rimed stubble. He looked thirty-five, maybe thirty-six. He had the quiet, unhurried posture of a man who worked with his hands in a place where a single mistake was permanent. He smelled of diesel fuel, woodsmoke, and cold pine pitch.

    He was staring at her bare collarbone, watching the steam rise off her skin with a mix of deep annoyance and something sharp.

    "Excuse me?" Gisela’s voice hardened. She didn't put her shirt back on yet. She refused to let him see her shiver.

    The man took three deliberate, heavy steps toward her, his boots packing the snow with a slow, steady rhythm. "You’re standing in sub-zero weather in a single layer, sweating," he said. His eyes were a stormy, metallic grey, framed by fine lines carved by wind. "That’s classic stage-two hypothermia behavior. Paradoxical undressing. Your brain convinces you you’re burning alive right before your heart gives out. Put your coat on."

    "I am not hypothermic," Gisela said, squaring her shoulders. She didn't back down. "And I don't recall asking for a lecture."

    "I'm Lachlan Hale," he said, his tone flat and unbothered. "I manage trail safety and the cabin leases out here. Which means if you freeze to death within five miles of this trailhead, I’m the one who has to haul your stiff body out of a drift. Put the coat on, lady."

    An entirely different kind of heat flared in her chest. This one was pure, unadulterated irritation. She looked at him—this young, rigid specimen of a man. Thirty-five, maximum. The kind of guy who still believed sheer willpower could control every variable in a day.

    "Mr. Hale," she said, keeping her voice level, cool, and deliberately mature. She looked him straight in the eyes. "I am forty-six years old. What you are looking at is not a medical crisis. It is a hot flash. My body is currently generating enough raw thermal energy to heat a small cabin, and if I put that heavy parka back on right now, I will soak through my base layer and then I will get hypothermia when the sweat freezes. Now, if you'll excuse me, I have bindings to adjust."

    Hale blinked.

    For a split second, his professional authority cracked, revealing a flash of absolute bewilderment. He clearly didn't get many city women talking to him about their hormonal thermostats with such clinical indifference. His gaze dropped from her flushed face to the hollow of her throat, where her pulse was still jumping against her damp skin. His eyes lingered a second too long.

    "You're flushed," he said, his voice dropping, losing its aggressive edge but none of its weight. He stepped closer. The heat off him was real—a solid, physical presence of wool and muscle. "And you're breathing too fast. Let me see your hands."

    "My hands are fine—"

    Before she could pull back, his hand closed around her right wrist. His leather glove was warm, his grip firm but careful. He slid his thumb up to the inside of her wrist, pressing directly against her radial artery.

    The contact sent a strange, electric jolt right up her arm. Her breath hitched. It had been a very long time since a man had touched her with that kind of uninvited, protective authority.

    "Pulse is racing," Hale murmured, his grey eyes fixed on her wrist before slowly lifting to meet hers. He was close enough that she could see the tiny gold flecks in his irises, close enough to catch the scent of cold wind on his neck. "Clammy, too. That’s a heavy tax on your heart at this elevation."

    "I told you, it's a hot flash, Mr. Hale," she said, keeping her voice steady despite her racing pulse. "I can manage my own heart."

    He didn't let go. Instead, he pulled his right glove off with his teeth, dropped it onto the bumper, and pressed his bare palm against hers. His hand was huge, his skin rough and heavily calloused, but his touch was surprisingly gentle. He ran his fingers down her palm to check her fingertips. The sensation of his bare skin against hers felt like a low-voltage wire.

    "Your fingers are freezing," Hale said, his thumb brushing slowly over her knuckles, feeling the dry, dye-stained skin. He frowned. "You work with your hands. Hard work."

    "I'm a textile artist," she said, her throat suddenly dry. "I dye and weave. I'm here on a conservation grant to study sub-arctic lichens. I need the natural pigments."

    Hale's jaw tightened. The mention of her work seemed to bring back his cold, defensive wall. He slowly let go of her wrist, stepping back.

    "A textile artist," he said, his voice laced with that dry, familiar cynicism she’d heard from bankers and landlords alike. "An outsider. A tourist with a sketchbook and a hobby."

    "It isn't a hobby," Gisela snapped, the vulnerability of the moment evaporating into anger. "It's my business. It's my mortgage. And I have a signed, legal permit for the ridge cabin."

    "The ridge cabin is three miles of steep, unmaintained trail," Hale said, crossing his heavy arms. "The snow is four feet deep in the drifts, and the wind is shifting. You’ve got a bad knee—I saw you rubbing it the second you stepped out. You’re physically unprepared for this, MacLeod."

    Gisela’s eyes narrowed. "You know my name."

    "I read the log. I knew a woman from Portland was coming up here to paint weeds," he said simply. "I didn't expect someone who..." He trailed off, his eyes scanning her face, taking in the fine lines at the corners of her eyes, the silver threads running through her dark hair, the mature curve of her waist. He swallowed. "...someone who doesn't have the sense to dress for the weather."

    "I told you—"

    "I don't care about your biology," Hale interrupted, though his eyes said otherwise. "I care about the mountain. Timberline Ridge doesn't give a damn about your grant, your lease, or whatever transition you're going through. It kills people who don't respect it. I’ve seen it happen to people younger and stronger than you."

    There was a shadow of old, heavy grief in his eyes—a brief, dark flicker that disappeared as soon as it arrived. But Gisela had spent twenty-three years decoding a husband who had mentally checked out; she knew the signs of a man carrying a quiet, heavy trauma. Lachlan Hale wasn't just being a tyrant. He was terrified. He didn't see an annoyance; he saw a body he’d have to carry down the mountain.

    "I have plenty of respect for the mountain," she said softly, her voice losing its edge. "But I also have some for myself. I didn't drive three days, leave my life behind, and spend the last of my savings to turn around because some thirty-something trail boss thinks I'm too fragile to hike."

    Hale winced slightly at the age reference. His jaw set. "I didn't say you were fragile."

    "You implied it."

    "I implied you're stubborn."

    "On that, we agree," Gisela said. She picked up her wool shirt, shook the dry snow from it, and slipped her arms into the sleeves. The burning heat of the hot flash had finally ebbed, leaving her skin cool and refreshed. She buttoned it up, pulled on her Gore-Tex parka, and zipped it straight to her chin. "But stubbornness is the only reason I'm standing here. It's what kept me alive when my marriage dissolved, and it's what's going to save my studio."

    She stepped onto her snowshoes, aligning her boots with the bindings. With quick, practiced tugs, she cinched the straps tight. She’d practiced this in her Portland living room, but out here in the freezing mud, her fingers were stiff and her knee throbbed with a dull, insistent warning. She didn't let a single wince show on her face.

    Hale watched her, his bare hand still holding his glove. He looked like he wanted to physically grab her, throw her back into the Jeep, and lock the doors. But there was also a reluctant, quiet respect in his grey eyes.

    "You're going to freeze," he said, his voice quiet. No anger left. Just grim resignation.

    "Then you'll get the satisfaction of saying 'I told you so' when you find me," Gisela said, looking up at him through her lashes.

    She stood up, testing her weight. The crampons bit into the crust with a sharp, solid crunch. She grabbed her trekking poles, checking the baskets, and slipped her wrists through the straps.

    Hale didn't move. He stood there, a massive, dark silhouette against the stark white of the snow. "The first mile is the steepest," he said, his voice low, almost like a warning to himself. "If you aren't at the halfway shelter by three, I'm coming up. And I won't be polite about bringing you back down."

    "I'll make sure to move fast, then," Gisela said, a tiny, genuine smile touching her lips. It was the first time she had smiled in months, and it felt surprisingly light. Like a reclamation of her own strength.

    She took her first step past him, her snowshoe gliding smoothly over the hard-packed trail. As she brushed past his shoulder, the heat from his body hit her again—thick, heavy, and intoxicating. She caught the scent of him—woodsmoke, grease, and raw, masculine energy—and felt a sudden, fierce tug of desire so intense it made her lungs tighten. It was a feeling she thought had died years ago, buried under decades of domestic neglect and the quiet silence of an empty nest.

    To feel it now, in this brutal, freezing place, sparked by a man ten years her junior who looked at her like she was the only thing on the mountain... it was terrifying. And absolutely thrilling.

    She didn't look back. She planted her poles, drove her hips forward, and began the climb up Timberline Ridge.

    Behind her, she heard the slow, heavy breath of Lachlan Hale standing in the wind, watching her go. She could feel his eyes on her back—a physical, burning weight that kept her warm long after the cold had settled into her bones.

    The wind off Timberline Ridge didn’t blow; it threw. It flung a dry, blinding powder that stung Gisela MacLeod’s eyes and took whatever was left of the trail with it.

    She stopped. Her breath came in short, rattling gasps that instantly turned to frost on the collar of her canvas jacket. She was forty-six years old. She knew her body—knew its creaks, its limits, and the exact moment when stubbornness turned into something fatal. Right now, her right knee—the one she’d busted five years ago hauling sixty-pound bolts of Belgian linen up the stairs of her Portland studio—was screaming. Her fingers, stuffed into cheap, supposedly water-resistant gloves, had gone entirely numb three ridges back.

    Just a walk, she’d told herself. A quick loop to clear the gray static from her head, to escape the unpaid invoices piled like drift on her desk. The textile mill she’d spent twenty years building was bleeding money, suffocated by supply chains that had gone dry and a market that no longer cared about hand-dyed wefts. Her daughter, Gretchen, had practically forced her out the door. “Mom, you’re trying to thread a loom with a broken hand. Go touch some trees. Please.”

    So she had come to the ridge. But the mountain didn’t care about her small business, or her mortgage, or her quiet, grinding panic. The mountain only understood gravity.

    A sudden, malicious gust caught her side-on. Her boot caught a hidden root beneath the powder, and her balance went. She slid—clumsy and heavy—down a steep embankment, a brief, terrifying blur of white and gray ending in a bone-jarring crack against the trunk of an old-growth fir.

    The air left her lungs in a dull woof. She lay there for a second, tasting copper and pine needles, her throat tightening with the first real spikes of panic. When she tried to stand, her leg sank waist-deep into a soft, treacherous drift.

    "Damn it," she muttered. The wind ate the words before she could even hear them. She reached for a low branch, her shoulder muscles burning. This was the stupidest way to die. To end up a frozen statistic in a local paper because she’d wanted to feel something other than the slow, middle-aged dread of obsolescence.

    Then, the white space ahead of her shifted.

    It wasn't a gradual approach. A shape simply materialized from the gray—thick-shouldered, dark-clad, moving through the waist-deep snow with a heavy, rhythmic efficiency that made her feel incredibly small.

    Before she could even call out, a pair of massive, canvas-gloved hands hooked under her armpits. With a grunt of pure, levered weight, the stranger hauled her out of the sinkhole.

    "What the hell are you doing up here?"

    The voice was rough, a low-register growl that sounded like gravel being kicked down a well.

    Gisela blinked the crust of ice from her eyelashes. The man was huge. His face was mostly hidden beneath the deep hood of a grease-stained waxed parka, but she could see a silver-shot beard and a mouth set in a hard, angry line. His eyes were a pale, stormy gray, boring into her with a look that was fifty percent calculation and fifty percent raw fury.

    "I... the trail vanished," she got out, her jaw stiff from the cold.

    "The storm was on the radio three days ago," he said, his grip on her arm tight enough to bruise, though he didn't let go. "Only a tourist walks up Timberline with a low-pressure system sitting right on the peak."

    A sharp, defensive heat flared in her chest, warming her more than her damp layers ever could. "I’m not a tourist. I got caught."

    "Same difference to the search and rescue guys," he snapped. "Can you walk?"

    "Yes," she said. But when she tried to put weight on her right leg, the knee gave way completely.

    He didn't argue. He didn't ask permission. He simply bent, scooped her up—one arm under her knees, the other behind her back—and stood.

    "Put me down!" she protested, her hands automatically grabbing his shoulders for balance. Underneath the heavy canvas, his frame felt like old cedar. He was fifty, maybe older, his face lined with the kind of deep creasing that only comes from decades of squinting into wind and sun. The heat coming off him was intense, almost suffocating in the freezing air.

    "Keep your mouth shut," he growled, turning his shoulder into the squall and stepping forward. "You’re breathing in ice."

    Left with no choice, Gisela buried her face against his neck. He smelled of dry woodsmoke, old grease, and the heavy, mineral scent of wet wool. Despite herself, the sheer physical safety of his hold sent a strange, long-buried ache through her chest. It wasn't romance; it was the simple, terrifying relief of being held by someone who wasn't asking her to make a decision.

    The cabin looked less like a home and more like a bunker. It was low-slung, built of heavy, dark logs with a steep tin roof that let the snow slide off in great, frozen slabs.

    The man kicked the heavy door open, stepped into the dark interior, and slammed it shut with his boot heel.

    The silence was sudden, almost violent. The wind became a dull, rhythmic thud against the logs. The air inside was thick and smelled of cedar shavings, kerosene, and the deep, radiating heat of a massive soapstone stove in the corner.

    He dropped her onto a heavy pine bench by the hearth. Gisela’s legs felt like wet cardboard, and she leaned back against the log wall, her lungs burning as they took in the warm, dry air.

    "Coat off," he ordered, already throwing back his hood and ripping open his own parka.

    With his hood down, she could see him clearly. He had a strong, blunt nose that had been broken at least once, a heavy brow, and dark hair cut short and messy. His face was the color of old leather, but his eyes were bright, sharp, and entirely too intense.

    "I can do it," Gisela said, her fingers fumbling uselessly with her wet zipper. Her hands were shaking so hard they looked like they belonged to someone else.

    "You can't even hold a zipper," he said, stepping into her space. He knelt before her, his large, calloused hands brushing hers aside without a hint of gentleness.

    His proximity was a physical weight. As his rough fingers worked the metal teeth, his knuckles brushed her chin. His skin was incredibly hot, the texture of fine sandpaper.

    "Boots first," he muttered, his voice dropping into a lower register as he tackled the frozen laces. He pulled the leather boots off and set them near the hearth, where they immediately began to steam. When he reached for the hem of her damp wool sweater, she went rigid.

    "I have dry clothes in my pack," she said, her voice tight. "I can manage the rest."

    He stopped, his gray eyes lifting to hers. Up close, she could see the tiny amber flecks in his irises. "Hypothermia doesn't care about your modesty, lady. That synthetic shirt is holding water right against your ribs. If you don't get dry, your core temperature is going to tank."

    "My name is Gisela," she said, trying to claw back some shred of her agency. "Not 'lady.' And I am perfectly capable of changing myself."

    He let out a short, dry sound that might have been a laugh. "There's no bathroom here. Just an outhouse out back in a sixty-mile-an-hour gale. You can change by the stove, or you can sit there and get the shakes. I don't care which. But I'm not hauling a corpse down the trail tomorrow."

    The sheer, unvarnished bluntness of him made her flinch. "You are remarkably rude."

    "I'm alive," he said, rising to his full height and looking down at her. "Which is more than I can say for most people who think this mountain is a park." He walked to a pine chest, pulled out an oversized flannel shirt—thick as a blanket—and a pair of heavy gray socks, and tossed them next to her. "Put these on. I'll turn around. You have two minutes."

    He faced the dry sink in the corner, his broad back a wall of absolute dismissal.

    Gisela swallowed, her heart knocking against her ribs. The shivering was getting worse now, a deep, rhythmic shuddering that made her teeth click. She peeled off her wet coat, then pulled the heavy wool sweater over her head. The cool air of the room hit her skin, making her gasp. Underneath, she wore a simple silk camisole—a small, private luxury she always kept close to her skin. It was damp, clinging to her breasts, showing the soft, full curves of a woman who had lived, who had carried a child, whose body had settled into its mature, heavy strength.

    She reached for the flannel shirt, but her hands were too stiff. It slipped through her fingers and fell to the floorboards.

    "Damn it," she whispered.

    She heard him turn. She expected another sharp comment, but instead, the room went entirely quiet.

    Roderick Hale stood frozen.

    The air in the cabin shifted, suddenly heavy, thick with a tension that had nothing to do with the storm outside. He didn't look away. His eyes moved slowly over her—from the silver strands in her dark hair, down the long curve of her neck, to the damp silk of her camisole.

    It wasn't a polite look. It was the raw, heavy gaze of a man who hadn't touched another human being in a very long time, and more than that, a man who saw her—not as an inconvenience, but as a woman, soft and real and desirable.

    Gisela’s breath caught in her throat. A sudden, deep flush rose from her collarbone, a heat that had nothing to do with the soapstone stove. For years, she had felt invisible. In the wake of her divorce and the long, quiet nights in her empty house, she had accepted the gray, sexless space society leaves for middle-aged women. But under his unblinking gaze, she felt a sharp, heavy ache of desire pool in her stomach.

    "You're freezing," he said. His voice was lower now, rougher, the anger gone, replaced by something far more dangerous.

    "I dropped the shirt," she whispered.

    Roderick took two slow steps toward her. The wide pine floorboards groaned under his boots. He picked up the heavy flannel, but he didn't hand it to her. He held it open.

    "Arms," he murmured.

    She slid her arms into the massive sleeves, her eyes never leaving his face. The wool was warm, smelling of cedar and him. Roderick didn't step back. He stood between her knees as she sat on the bench, his thighs almost touching hers.

    He reached down to button the shirt. His hands were huge, but his fingers were surprisingly deft as they worked the horn buttons. Each time his knuckles brushed the bare skin of her chest, a tremor went straight down her spine.

    "Your skin is like ice," he muttered, his eyes dropping to her mouth before rising back to her eyes.

    "You're very warm," she said, the words slipping out before she could stop them.

    Roderick’s thumbs lingered on her collarbone, his rough, calloused skin scratching gently against hers. Gisela’s eyes closed, a soft, low sound escaping her throat. It was the sound of someone who had been cold for a very long time.

    Roderick’s jaw clenched. He let go of the shirt and stepped back, his face turning hard again as the walls went back up. He walked to the woodstove, grabbed a heavy iron poker, and began violently shifting the logs inside. "You need to get warm. I don't need you catching pneumonia. I don't need the paperwork."

    Gisela pulled the oversized shirt tight around her, the cold returning the moment he moved away. "Is that all I am to you? An administrative hassle?"

    He didn't look at her. "Out here, people are either a nuisance or a liability."

    "I am not a nuisance," she said, her voice rising as her temper returned. "I run a business. I have people who rely on me. I made a mistake in the weather. It happens."

    "A mistake in the weather on this ridge gets you dead in thirty minutes," Roderick said, turning to face her. The poker was still in his hand, his eyes dark with an old, buried fury. "You think because you’re tough in the city, it translates out here? The mountain doesn't care about your independence, Gisela. When the wind shears, you're just meat."

    Gisela stood up, ignoring the sharp protest of her knee. The flannel shirt fell to her knees, but she stood straight, her eyes flashing. "And what about you? Who made you the judge of who gets to live? You sit up here in your little wooden castle, hiding from everything, just so you can look down on people who actually have the courage to live a real life!"

    Roderick’s eyes went dark. He dropped the poker; it clattered against the stone hearth. He crossed the small space between them in two long strides, looming over her, his chest inches from hers.

    "I don't hide," he growled, his voice vibrating through her. "I survive. And I know what happens when people like you come up here to 'find themselves' and get careless. They die. And then some poor bastard has to carry their frozen, heavy body down the mountain."

    "I am not careless!" she yelled, the tears finally hot in her eyes. All of it—the threat of losing her studio, the quiet, empty rooms of her house, the sheer terror of the whiteout—came rushing up at once. "I have spent my entire life being careful! For my daughter, for my employees, for everyone! I came out here for one afternoon where I didn't have to carry everything myself! And I get dragged into a cabin by a bitter, arrogant hermit who treats me like a chore!"

    She was shaking, her chest heaving, her face wet with a mixture of melted snow and angry tears.

    Roderick stared at her. The anger in his face slowly cracked, revealing something raw, worn, and deeply damaged.

    "I'm not a hermit," he said, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "I'm a man who watched a twenty-two-year-old kid freeze to death in a crevasse five years ago because my rope was ten feet too short. I watched him go quiet while he was screaming for his mother. I swore then I’d never let another civilian die on this ridge. Not on my watch."

    The words hung between them, heavy and suffocating.

    Gisela’s anger died, leaving only a hollow ache. She looked at his face, seeing the deep, structural grief in the lines of his mouth. He wasn't angry at her. He was terrified of his own ghosts.

    "Roderick," she murmured, her hand rising instinctively to touch his forearm.

    He didn't pull away. He stood there, his breath ragged, his chest nearly touching hers. The tension between them, coiled tight since he'd pulled her from the drift, finally snapped.

    Slowly, his gaze dropped to her hand on his sleeve. He looked back up, his eyes searching hers, finding the same weariness, the same hunger, and the undeniable, heat-filled pull that neither of them could fight anymore.

    "You're a dangerous woman, Gisela," he whispered.

    "Why?"

    "Because you make me want to care about something other than this rock," he said.

    He reached out, his massive, warm hand cupping the back of her neck, his thumb tracing her cheekbone with a gentleness that made her breath shudder.

    Then he bent his head and kissed her.

    It wasn't a gentle kiss, but it wasn't hurried, either. It was the heavy, desperate collision of two people who had spent too long pretending they didn't need anyone. His lips were warm and tasted of salt and the cold outside. He pulled her against him, his arms locking around her waist, lifting her slightly until she was pressed flush against his solid, heavy frame.

    Gisela let out a low sound into his mouth, her arms wrapping around his neck, her fingers digging into the thick, silver-shot hair at his temples. The sheer size of him, the absolute certainty of his weight, swept away the bills, the studio, the long years of loneliness. For the first time in a decade, she felt entirely, fiercely present.

    He deepened the kiss, his tongue brushing hers with a slow, heavy rhythm that made her knees go soft. She clung to him, the rough wool of his shirt rubbing against her bare thighs as he held her off the floor.

    When he finally pulled back, just an inch, his forehead rested against hers. His breathing was hard, his eyes dark with a quiet, wild heat.

    "I can't let you die," he whispered against her lips, his voice raw. "But I don't think I can let you leave, either."

    Gisela looked into his eyes, seeing the high, defensive wall he’d built around himself—a wall of guilt and cold stone. She knew then that the blizzard outside was nothing compared to the storm inside this cabin, and that surviving Roderick Hale was going to be the hardest thing she’d ever done.

    Twelve by sixteen feet. That was all that was left of the world.

    Outside, Timberline Ridge was burying itself under four feet of wet, blue-white cement, sealing the cabin's single window behind a slab of gray ice. Inside, the air was thick with the scent of green pitch, damp wool socks drying over the hearth, and that sharp, salt-grease musk of two people trapped in a space meant for one.

    Fiona MacLeod gripped the edge of the dry-sink, her fingernails digging into the rough pine. She was forty-six. She knew her body's quirks by heart now—the slackness of her lower belly that no amount of work could ever tighten back up, the silver strands looping thick through her dark hair, the dull, nagging ache in her sacrum from twenty years spent threw-throwing a heavy shuttle across a loom. She wasn't some fragile girl waiting for a savior. She’d buried a marriage and raised two kids; she didn't need rescuing. Yet, there it was—a slow, heavy heat pooling low in her gut.
