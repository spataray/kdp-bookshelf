    Reclaiming the Grain

    The basement smelled of vinegar and rot.

    Specifically, the vinegar of decaying acetate film stored three aisles over, mixed with the damp chill of concrete foundation and the dry, vanilla scent of decomposing rag paper. Eulalie Fontaine leaned over the custom light box. Her lower back gave a familiar, dull throb. She held her breath, sliding a thin micro-spatula under a strip of yellowed tape.

    Some well-meaning volunteer in 1957 had tried to mend a tear in the 1784 ledger with cellophane tape. Now, seventy years later, the adhesive had turned into a dark brown, crusty stain that ate through the rag paper like slow acid. It was a quiet death for a piece of history. Eulalie was the only one standing between this book and the trash heap.

    "Patience," she muttered.

    The concrete walls of the St. Jude Historical Society archives swallowed the sound instantly.

    She worked in a slow, steady loop. Lift. Scrape. Dab a microscopic dot of warm wheat starch paste with a fine-tipped sable brush. Lay down a strip of mulberry paper so thin it was practically a ghost. Press it with the bone folder.

    Her fingers were steady, but they weren't young. The knuckle of her right thumb was thick and swollen, aching whenever the autumn rain set in. Her nails were short, unpolished, and stained a stubborn yellow from chemical solvents. They were useful hands. Hard-working hands. The hands of a forty-eight-year-old woman who spent her days preserving other people’s histories because her own present had fallen apart.

    For twenty-four years, those hands had done different work. They had rubbed the tension out of Richard’s shoulders. They had packed school lunches, ironed small cotton dresses, and planted climbing roses that eventually died of black spot in the backyard of a house she no longer owned. Finally, they had held a pen to sign a divorce decree that stripped her of almost everything she had spent her life building.

    She had kept the name, though. Fontaine. It was a small, petty victory. Mostly, she just couldn't stomach the bureaucratic nightmare of changing her driver’s license, her bank accounts, and her professional credentials at forty-seven. But now there was another Mrs. Fontaine in town. A younger, firmer version. Idalia.

    The archives were kept at a strict sixty degrees to protect the paper. But suddenly, Eulalie felt a strange, tight prickle at the back of her neck.

    The heat hit her like an open furnace door.

    It started behind her breastbone—a sudden, violent blush that flooded up her chest, over her collarbones, and pooled hot and heavy in her throat. Her cheeks burned. Her heart began to rattle against her ribs like a trapped bird.

    "Not now," she whispered.

    She dropped the bone folder before her hand could shake and ruin the mulberry paper. Her palms were slick. She tried to push her reading glasses onto her head, but they slid right back down, greasy with the sweat suddenly pooling on her forehead.

    She stood up too fast. Her chair shrieked against the concrete floor. Her head spun, and she had to grip the edge of the heavy oak workbench to keep from falling.

    She yanked at the buttons of her heavy wool cardigan. Her fingers felt like thick, clumsy sausages. She ripped the sweater off and threw it on the stool, standing there in her thin cotton camisole, her skin flushed an angry, bright red.

    The sweat was everywhere now. It ran in cold, miserable trickles down her spine, pooled under her breasts, and damply glued her hair to the back of her neck. She grabbed a piece of heavy, acid-free cardstock and began to fan herself, her chest heaving.

    It felt like a betrayal. Her own body was mutating without her permission, a constant, sweaty reminder of middle age.

    She caught her reflection in the dark screen of the archival scanner across the room.

    Her outline was soft. The waist she used to have was gone, replaced by something thicker and sturdier. Her breasts sat lower. In the dim overhead light, the silver in her dark hair caught the glare like spiderwebs.

    She looked tired. Not just sleepy, but the kind of bone-weary that sleep couldn't touch.

    "Forty-eight," she said to the dark screen.

    The number felt heavy. In her twenties, forty-eight had seemed ancient—the age of grandmothers and orthopedic shoes. But she didn't feel old. She just felt unfinished. Like a book left out in the rain, its pages swollen and warped, but the story still there, waiting.

    But who was actually looking?

    To the rest of the world, she had become invisible. It had happened slowly, then all at once. Men’s eyes slid right past her on the street. Clerks at the grocery store looked through her to the person behind her in line. Even here, she was tucked away in the basement, a ghost among ghosts, preserving the lives of dead people while her own life evaporated.

    The heat finally began to break, leaving behind a cold, clammy shiver. She pulled her cardigan back on, but the fabric felt damp and miserable against her skin.

    Today was the anniversary. Exactly one year since the judge had banged his gavel and ended her marriage.

    One year of living in the drafty apartment on Elm Street. One year of sleeping on the far left side of a double bed because the right side felt too empty. One year of learning how to buy groceries for one, of watching the silence stretch across her living room.

    She still remembered the exact tone of Richard’s voice when he told her. They had been sitting in the kitchen of their restored colonial.

    "I’m not happy, Eulalie," he had said. He hadn't even looked at her. He was staring at his phone, his thumb idling over some feed. "We've just grown apart. We’re in different places."

    The "different place" was a glass condo downtown with Idalia.

    Idalia was twenty-six. She taught yoga, laughed like wind chimes, and smelled of coconut oil. Her joints didn't ache. She didn't have to worry about grey hair or hot flashes.

    And now, Idalia was pregnant.

    Richard, who had spent ten years complaining about the cost of their daughter’s college tuition, who had claimed he was "too old and too tired" to ever think about starting over, was going to be a father again at fifty-two.

    Eulalie had found out through a mutual friend three weeks ago. The news had felt like a physical blow, a fist to the ribs that left her gasping on her kitchen floor.

    The sheer unfairness of it burned hotter than any hot flash. Richard got to press a reset button. He got a shiny new life, a young wife, and a do-over baby. He got to treat their twenty-four years together as a dry run.

    And Eulalie got the scraps. The old memories, the empty nest, and a body that was shutting down operations.

    She reached out and touched the edge of the 1784 ledger. The leather was dry and peeling. If she didn't treat it with lanolin, it would eventually turn to dust.

    "I am not going to crumble," she said, her voice louder this time.

    She looked at her hands. They were strong. They had saved thousands of books. Surely they could save her.

    She had spent the last year mourning a man who didn't deserve it, fading into the background. She was done being the sad, discarded first wife.

    By five o'clock, a cold rain was tapping against the narrow basement windows.

    Eulalie wrapped the restored ledger in acid-free tissue and placed it in its archival box. She cleaned her brushes, capped the paste, and wiped down the light box. Everything in its place.

    In the staff bathroom, she stared at herself under the harsh fluorescent lights. She didn't look away.

    She leaned close to the mirror. There were fine lines around her eyes—laughter lines, though many had been paid for in tears. Her skin was pale, but her cheeks still held a faint, rosy color from the flush. Her neck was long and elegant.

    She pulled the pins from her hair. It fell down her shoulders in thick, dark waves streaked with silver. It wasn't twenty-six-year-old hair, but it was thick and soft.

    "You're still here," she told herself.

    She gathered her things and walked up the concrete stairs.

    The main floor of the historical society was empty and quiet. She nodded to the evening guard, a retired cop named Artie who was working a crossword.

    "Night, Eulalie," he grunted.

    "Goodnight, Artie."

    The cool, damp evening air hit her face, smelling of wet asphalt and fallen leaves. She closed her eyes for a second, letting the mist cool her skin. It felt incredible.

    She walked the six blocks to her apartment. She had sold her SUV during the divorce to pay her lawyer, and now she walked everywhere. At first, she had hated it—it felt like a public badge of her reduced status. But now, she liked the physical work of it. It made her feel connected to the ground.

    Her knees ached by the time she climbed the three flights of stairs to her place, but she didn't mind. It meant she was moving.

    The apartment was small, but it was entirely hers.

    She had decorated it with everything Richard hated. The walls were a deep, rich plum instead of his sterile beige. Mismatched Turkish rugs covered the floors, stacks of art books piled up on every table, and a velvet armchair she had salvaged and restored herself sat in the corner.

    She dropped her keys in the bowl by the door. The quiet wrapped around her.

    In the kitchen, she poured a glass of cold pinot grigio. It was crisp and sharp on her tongue. She took a long, slow sip, letting the warmth spread down her throat.

    She didn't want dinner.

    Instead, she went into the bathroom and turned the hot water on high. She dumped a handful of lavender salts into the tub, watching them dissolve in the steam.

    She undressed slowly, letting her clothes pile on the tile.

    She stood in front of the full-length mirror on the door. This was always the hardest part—the part where she couldn't hide behind layers of wool or structured jackets.

    Her body looked forty-eight.

    Her belly was soft, marked with the faint, silver stretch marks of the pregnancy that had brought her daughter into the world twenty-one years ago. Her thighs were thick, her skin less tight than it used to be.

    But standing there in the warm, yellow light, she didn't feel disgusted.

    She felt a strange, quiet curiosity.

    This was her body. It had survived. It had carried life, endured a broken heart, and kept going. It was a good body. It deserved to be touched.

    She stepped into the tub, the hot water rising up her legs, her hips, her waist. She sank down until the water hit her chin, her hair floating around her head.

    She closed her eyes.

    For twenty-four years, she had been a supporting character in Richard’s life. She had been the wife who hosted the dinners, the mother who drove the carpools, the quiet face in the background.

    She was done with the background.

    She wanted to feel something real again. Not just the quiet safety of surviving, but a spark. She wanted to be held by someone who actually saw her—not as a mother, not as a reliable fixture, but as a woman.

    Under the water, she let her hand slide over her stomach, then lower, to the soft hair between her thighs. The touch was light, tentative.

    Her breath caught.

    The feeling was sharp, a sudden jolt that made her stomach tighten. It had been so long since she had touched herself with anything other than hurried utility.

    She closed her eyes tighter, her fingers finding the small, sensitive center of her. She began to stroke herself, slowly at first, then with a growing, quiet hunger.

    She didn't picture Richard. She would never picture Richard again.

    She pictured a shadow. A man with strong, patient hands—hands that didn't mind the texture of her skin, hands that would curve over her hip and pull her flush against him. A man who would look at her silver hair and see something beautiful.

    The pleasure built fast, a tight, hot coil that had nothing to do with hormones and everything to do with her own desire. Her hips moved slightly against the bottom of the tub, her breath coming in short, ragged gasps.

    The release was sudden and clean, a burst of warmth that spread through her belly and left her limbs heavy and loose.

    She let out a soft cry, the sound echoing off the wet tiles.

    She lay there in the cooling water, her heart thumping against her ribs. Sweat beaded on her forehead, but this time, it wasn't a hot flash. It was her own fire.

    She opened her eyes, looking up at the ceiling.

    She was Eulalie Fontaine. She was forty-eight, she was divorced, her knees ached, and she was entering the autumn of her life.

    But she wasn't dead.

    She was raw, she was alive, and she was ready.

    As she finally dried off and wrapped herself in a plush towel, a quiet determination settled into her bones. She wasn't going to hide anymore.

    Tomorrow, she would go back to work. She would finish the 1784 ledger. And then, she would start writing her own story.

    The next morning, the rain was gone, replaced by a crisp, bright autumn day. The air was sharp with the scent of pine and wet earth.

    Eulalie walked to work with a different stride. She didn't look down at her shoes; she kept her chin up, looking at the faces of the people passing her.

    When she got to her desk at the historical society, a thick courier envelope was waiting for her.

    She frowned, setting her bag down. She never got mail here.

    She slid her finger under the flap and pulled out a heavy, cream-colored card.

    An invitation.

    You are cordially invited to celebrate the grand opening of the Fletcher Gallery at the St. Jude Historical Society.

    Eulalie’s stomach dropped.

    The Fletchers were one of the oldest, wealthiest families in the city.

    They were also Idalia’s family.

    And at the bottom of the card, in elegant gold ink, was a handwritten note:

    Eulalie, I hope you can make it. Richard and I would love to see you there. We really want to put the past behind us and be one big, happy family. — Idalia Fontaine

    Eulalie felt the heat drain from her face.

    One big, happy family.

    The sheer, unadulterated gall of it. Inviting her to a public gala where her ex-husband and his pregnant, twenty-six-year-old wife could parade their new life in front of the entire town.

    She crushed the card in her hand, her knuckles white.

    Her first instinct was to throw it in the trash, run down to her basement, and lock the door.

    But then she remembered her promise in the bath.

    I am not going to crumble.

    She slowly smoothed the crumpled card out on her desk, her fingers trembling. She looked at the name Fletcher.

    She knew about the Fletchers. She knew they had funded the new wing. She also knew Idalia had a brother.

    Keir Fletcher.

    She had seen him once, years ago, at a museum gala. He had been striking—tall, broad-shouldered, with dark eyes that seemed to look right through the crowd. He was thirty-four now, she thought. A man of wealth and serious influence, completely out of her orbit.

    If she went to this gala, she would be walking straight into a trap. She would have to face Richard, Idalia, and the entire Fletcher family.

    But if she didn't go, she would be proving them right. She would be the sad, bitter first wife hiding in the dark.

    She stood up, her jaw set.

    She would go.

    And she wasn't going as the quiet, invisible archivist.

    She was going as Eulalie Fontaine.

    She picked up her phone and dialed the number of a high-end boutique downtown—the kind of place she had avoided for a year because the clothes were too expensive, too bold, too noticed.

    "Hello," she said, her voice clear and level. "I'd like to book a personal shopping appointment. For an event."

    She hung up, her heart hammering in her chest.

    The heat was back. And this time, she was going to let it burn.

    Chapter 2: Dry Rot and Deadwood

    Plink. Plink. Plink.

    Water hit plastic. It was the only sound down in the basement of the St. Jude Historical Society, regular as a dying heartbeat. Eulalie Fontaine knelt on the bare concrete, her knees cracking like dry twigs. She reached for another heavy, acid-free box. Her lower back didn't just ache anymore; it throbbed with a dull, steady resentment.

    Forty-eight wasn't old, not really, but her joints hadn't received the memo. The autumn air out on Main Street was crisp and smelled of woodsmoke. Down here? Nothing but damp limestone and the wet chill of 1890. It clung to her wool sweater and crawled right into her bones.

    She pushed a stray lock of hair behind her ear. Silver threaded her temples now. She’d given up on the six-dollar box dye back in April. Why bother? She wore faded, high-rise jeans that didn't quite hide her soft stomach, and a bulky green sweater with frayed cuffs. This was her "after" body—the one left behind by a twenty-year marriage and a daughter who had just packed three suitcases and headed off to college. Now the house on the hill was too quiet. The refrigerator hummed too loud. So, Eulalie spent her evenings down here, buried under a hundred years of other people's damp, forgotten lives. It was warmer than her own kitchen.

    "Terrific."

    She lifted a stack of town records from 1895. The bottom of the box gave way, turning to wet mush in her hands. She barely caught the ledger before it hit the floor.

    Above her head, the main support beam of the old building looked swollen and black. The upstairs kitchen had been leaking for a week, water pooling behind the plaster and bleeding down into her quiet space. The smell was everywhere. Wet dirt, rotting pine, and old mold. It made her throat itch.

    Then came the boots.

    Heavy, slow footsteps on the wooden stairs. These weren't the quiet, shuffling steps of the retired schoolteachers who volunteered on Thursdays. The old pine steps groaned under real weight.

    Eulalie stood up, dusting off her palms, and turned around.

    A man ducked under the low copper water pipes and stepped into the dim basement. He was big. Not just tall, but broad across the shoulders, filling out a faded red flannel shirt. His hair was thick, silver-grey, and chopped short, matching the rough stubble on his jaw. He carried a worn leather tool bag that looked heavy enough to kill a man.

    "Are you the keeper of the lost secrets?"

    His voice was a deep, gravelly rumble. It caught her right in the center of her chest.

    "Eulalie Fontaine," she said, finding her voice. She offered her hand, suddenly wishing she hadn't wiped her dusty palms on her jeans.

    He dropped the heavy bag with a thud that echoed off the stone. When he took her hand, his skin was surprisingly hot. And dry. His palm was huge, covered in thick, rough calluses that scraped against her fingers with a friction that made her stomach flip.

    "Keir Fletcher."

    He didn't let go. Not right away. His eyes were a startling, clear grey under thick, dark brows. He looked straight at her. Not past her, not through her—which was what most men her age did these days. He saw the silver in her hair. He saw the smudge of dirt on her forehead. And he didn't look away.

    "The board called me," he said, his voice dropping an octave. "Said you had a wet beam."

    "Right. Yes." She pulled her hand back, but her skin still tingled where he’d touched it. "The main support. Back here."

    She led him to the dripping corner. Keir clicked a button, and a circle of bright white LED light cut through the shadows, hitting the dark, wet wood.

    He stepped in close behind her. Too close. She could smell him now—cold autumn rain, cedar shavings, and some kind of clean, basic soap. It was a thick, comforting smell. It hit her with a sudden, sharp ache of realization: she hadn't been this close to a man in years.

    "Old-growth white pine," Keir murmured. He reached up. His flannel sleeve slid back, showing a thick forearm with dark hair and a pale, jagged scar near the wrist. He pressed his thumb hard into the wet wood. "Look at those rings. Tight. You can't buy wood like this anymore. They cleared these forests before our grandparents were born."

    "The 1880 renovation," Eulalie said. Her voice came out softer than she wanted. She liked the way he looked at the wood. Most guys just wanted to tear everything out and slap up cheap drywall and fast-growth spruce. "They wanted it to last."

    "They knew what they were doing," Keir said. He tracked the flashlight beam along the joist. "But nothing survives standing water. If it sits, the fungus takes over. It eats the wood from the inside out. Once that happens, the strength is gone."

    "Dry rot."

    He looked down at her, his lips twitching into a small, pleased smile. "Exactly. Dry rot. It’s a stupid name, really. It needs water to start, but once it gets going, it turns the timber into dry, brown powder. Looks like charcoal. Crumbles when you touch it."

    "Can we save it?" she asked. She found herself staring at his mouth. His lips were full, framed by deep creases that came from working out in the wind and sun.

    "Maybe. If the heart of it is still dry." Keir reached up again, his fingers sliding along the dark edge of the wet patch. "Come here. Feel this right here."

    She took a half-step closer. Her shoulder brushed his chest—he felt as solid as the foundation of the building. She reached her hand up toward the dark wood.

    "A bit higher," Keir murmured.

    He didn't wait. He put his hand right over hers, guiding her fingers up the wet timber. His palm was massive, hot, and heavy. Eulalie’s heart did a sudden, violent flip against her ribs. This wasn't one of her middle-aged hot flashes. She knew those. This was a direct, sharp spark that hit her right in the gut—a feeling she had assumed was dead and buried years ago.

    His rough fingers pressed hers into the wood. The timber felt slick, spongy, and dead. But she could barely register the wood. All she could feel was the weight of his hand, his thumb resting right over her pulse on the inside of her wrist. He had to feel how fast it was racing.

    "See how it gives?" Keir’s voice was a low whisper. His breath stirred the loose hair at her temple. "The sapwood is gone. Totally rotted through. But press harder..." He pushed down on her knuckles, forcing her hand deeper into the wood. "...right there. Feel that?"

    "Yes," she whispered.

    "That's the heartwood," he said. "It’s still holding."

    She could barely get the word out. Her throat was bone-dry. She was staring at the wood, but every nerve in her body was screaming about the man standing behind her, his chest warm against her shoulder blades.

    He didn't pull away. They stood like that in the damp dark, hands pressed together against the wet beam, while the water dripped behind them like a clock ticking down. Eulalie felt raw. Exposed. It had been three years since her ex-husband, Richard, had walked out the door with his suitcases. Three years since anyone had touched her like she was a woman, not just an object in the room. Richard had treated her like an old, out-of-print book—nice to have on a shelf, maybe, but dusty, boring, and easily swapped for a glossy new paperback.

    But Keir’s hand was heavy and steady. He wasn't rushing.

    "You’ve got a bit of dirt on your cheek," Keir said.

    He let his hand fall from the beam, but he didn't step back. Instead, he gently turned her to face him. His fingers were warm as they touched her cheekbone, brushing away the smudge of wood dust. His thumb lingered, the rough skin of his callus scraping light and slow against her cheek.

    Eulalie looked up at him, her breath caught in her throat. She saw the deep lines around his eyes, the silver cutting through his beard. He looked like a man who had actually lived. A man who worked with his hands and knew that some things took time to build.

    "I’m a mess," she whispered.

    "You look beautiful."

    It wasn't a line. He just said it, flat and honest. His eyes drifted down to her mouth. For a second, Eulalie was sure he was going to lean in. She wanted him to. Her body leaned forward without her permission, her heart hammering against her ribs. She wanted those big, rough hands on her hips. She wanted to feel that heat against her in this cold basement.

    Keir let out a slow, heavy breath, his eyes turning dark. Slowly, he let his hand drop. He stayed close, though, close enough that she could feel his warmth. "I shouldn't have done that. I'm sorry. I don't usually try to hit on my clients in dark basements."

    A laugh escaped her—high and slightly giddy, like a teenager's. "Well. It’s a very atmospheric basement."

    "That it is," he said, and the corners of his mouth twitched. The heavy tension broke, but the air between them still felt electric. He took a step back, giving her room to breathe, and ran a hand through his thick hair. "Here's the plan. I can save the beam. I’ll have to cut out the rotten section and bolt a new piece of treated timber right alongside it. 'Sistering' it, we call it. It’ll take two of us to lift the new wood, but I can do the prep work alone."

    "How long?" she asked, trying to find her professional, librarian voice.

    "Two, three days," Keir said, looking back up at the ceiling. "I’ll clear some time next week. I was supposed to help my brother-in-law build a deck, but this is worse. The town hall shouldn't be resting on a wet sponge."

    "Thank you," Eulalie said. A small, warm spark flared in her chest. She had something to look forward to next week. She would get to see him again. They’d talk about old buildings, about wood, about things that lasted.

    "Sure thing," Keir said, grabbing his flashlight. He clicked it off, and the basement slid back into its familiar, quiet shadows. "Besides, I don't mind putting Richard off. He's got enough going on anyway."

    Eulalie stopped.

    The warm feeling in her chest didn't just fade—it froze, solid and cold as river ice.

    "Richard?" she said. The name came out dry, empty of all the soft warmth from a moment ago.

    Keir didn't notice. He was already shoving his flashlight back into his leather bag. "Yeah. My brother-in-law, Richard Fontaine. Just bought that big, ugly Victorian over on Elm Street. He's trying to get the whole place gutted before the baby arrives."

    The words hit her like a fist to the stomach. The damp air suddenly felt too thick to breathe.

    "Richard Fontaine," she repeated. Her hands balled into tight fists, her nails digging into her palms.

    "Yeah. You know him?" Keir asked. He finally looked up. He stopped, his grey eyes narrowing as he saw her face. The color had drained completely out of her skin. She was standing stiff, like she'd been turned to stone. "He’s in real estate."

    "I know him," Eulalie said. It sounded like she was chewing on sand. That old, familiar shame flared up in her throat, hot and choking, burning away every trace of the sweet, stupid heat she’d just felt. "He was my husband for twenty-two years."

    Keir froze. The strap of his tool bag slipped through his fingers. It hit the concrete floor with a heavy, dull clatter.

    "Your... husband?" Keir’s face went white.

    "Ex-husband," she corrected. Her voice was sharp enough to cut glass. She could feel the walls slamming down inside her, thick and heavy, locking everything away. "Until three years ago. That’s when he decided he needed a younger model. He left me for a girl half his age. A girl named Idalia."

    Keir stared at her. His eyes were wide, filled with a sudden, terrible dawning horror.

    "Idalia is my sister," he said.

    The silence in the basement was absolute.

    Plink. Plink. Plink.

    The dripping water above seemed to grow louder, echoing off the limestone walls like a timer counting down.

    Eulalie looked at him. Really looked. The warmth was gone, replaced by a cold, sharp clarity. In his strong jaw, in the shape of his eyes, she could see it now—the faint, cruel family resemblance to the girl who had shattered her life. Idalia Fletcher. Now Idalia Fontaine. The twenty-six-year-old who was living in the house Eulalie had spent twenty years building. The girl sleeping in the bed Eulalie had picked out, carrying the baby Richard had always told Eulalie they were too old to have.

    And this man—the guy whose hands had just made her feel alive for the first time in years—was her ex-husband's new brother-in-law. A direct line to the betrayal that had broken her.

    "Eulalie," Keir started. He took a step forward, his hands half-raised. "I didn't know. I swear to God, I didn't know."

    "It doesn't matter," she said, stepping back until she hit the cold metal shelf of the 1920 tax records. Her heart was hammering again, but this time it was a sick, greasy panic. "It’s a small town. Everyone’s connected."

    "My sister... she didn't say much about his past," Keir said, his voice dropping. He looked genuinely sick. "Just that he was divorced. I didn't ask for names. I don't get mixed up in her business."

    "Well, now you're in it," Eulalie said. A hot tear prickled the corner of her eye. She swiped it away fast, pretending she was just wiping off more dust. She wasn't going to cry. Not in front of him. Not over Richard. "The support beam needs fixing. The board hired you, so please, do the work. But from now on, let’s keep this strictly professional."

    Keir stared at her. His grey eyes were dark, a messy mix of frustration, pity, and that lingering spark he couldn't quite put out. He looked like he had a hundred things to say, but her stiff shoulders and crossed arms locked him out.

    He slowly reached down and grabbed his tool bag. "Strictly professional," he said, his voice flat. "I'll send the estimate to the board by tonight. I'll be back next Tuesday."

    "Thank you, Mr. Fletcher," she said, her voice dry and formal.

    He lingered for one more second, searching her face for some crack in the armor. Finding none, he turned. His heavy boots thudded up the stairs, a slow, retreating beat that left the basement feeling three times colder and emptier than before he walked in.

    Eulalie stood in the dim, yellow light. Her hands shook as she reached for another wet cardboard box. The smell of dry rot filled her nose, and for the first time since she started working here, the silence of the archives felt less like a sanctuary and more like a cell.

    Chapter 3: Beneath the Rafters

    The crack didn't sound like wood. It sounded like a pistol shot. Sharp, dry, snapping right through the steady drum of the autumn rain.

    Eulalie Fontaine froze in the center of the archive room. She held a heavy, leather-bound ledger tight against her ribs. Above her, the hand-hewn oak rafters of the carriage house groaned. Then came the dust. A fine white curtain of plaster drifted down, settling in her dark, silver-threaded hair and coating the shoulders of her oversized wool cardigan.

    Then came the drip.

    A slow, rhythmic patter. Directly onto the top shelf of the seventeenth-century municipal records.

    "No, please," Eulalie muttered. She shoved the ledger onto a dry table and lunged toward the tall shelves. The ceiling was giving. The heavy October storm had finally found the rotten spot in the roof, and the old timbers were bowing under the weight of the water.

    She reached for the top box. Her fingers strained. At forty-eight, her shoulders didn't have that easy, effortless stretch anymore. A sharp pinch flared in her lower back—a dull, familiar ache from years of hunching over desks and hauling heavy crates. She gritted her teeth, blinking through the falling plaster.

    "Need a hand?"

    Keir Fletcher stood in the doorway. He was dripping wet. His dark hair clung to his forehead, his heavy canvas work jacket black with rain. He carried a blue plastic tarp and a tool bucket that rattled with wrenches. He smelled of cold air, sawdust, and the sharp, chemical tang of WD-40.
