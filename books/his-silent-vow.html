    His Silent Vow: A Grumpy Sunshine Bodyguard Romance

    The rusted teeth of the crowbar bit deep into rotting pine. Bryony Abernathy threw her weight against the iron bar, her boots slipping on the slick, damp brick of the greenhouse floor. A sharp, metallic shriek cut through the heavy air. Then came the wet, splintering groan of wood giving way.

    She paused, wiping sweat from her forehead with her sleeve, leaving a streak of dark grit across her brow. The air inside the underfunded university dome was always like this—thick, warm, smelling of rich earth, moldy stone, and the sickly sweet rot of peat moss. At forty-three, her joints felt every drop of that moisture. A dull, stubborn ache throbbed in her lower back. It was her daily tax for spending hours hunched over cold stone potting benches, coaxing half-dead tropical ferns to survive on a heating budget that wouldn't keep a closet warm.

    Still, she preferred the ache. The quiet of the glass house was the only place her mind ever stopped spinning.

    She dropped the crowbar onto her metal supply cart with a loud clang and reached into the dark cavity of the crate. It had been sitting in a forgotten basement locker over in the biology hall for decades, part of some dusty, uncatalogued backlog from a 1950s expedition to Yunnan province.

    Her fingers brushed dry, flaky moss. Underneath, her hand closed around cool, heavy glass.

    Bryony pulled the jar out, holding it up to the weak afternoon light filtering through the grimy panes above.

    Her breath hitched.

    Inside, tucked into a bed of cloudy, gel-like agar, was a knot of thick roots. It wasn't dead. Even through the dirty glass, she could see tiny, pale green shoots clawing upward. A yellowed paper tag was tied to the cork with a bit of frayed twine. The elegant, faded handwriting read: Cypripedium aurum. 1952. Specimen 04.

    "No," she breathed. The word died against the damp leaves of the surrounding ferns. "It's not possible."

    The Golden Slipper.

    Every botanist knew the story. It was an orchid thought to have been wiped clean off the map by logging and mudslides in the late fifties. Rumors about its chemistry—compounds that could supposedly jump-start cellular healing—made it a ghost story. A myth. The kind of holy grail that kept pharma scouts and illegal collectors up at night.

    Her hands started to shake. She set the jar down on the scarred wood of her workbench.

    For three years, since the divorce stripped her of her home and her self-respect, she had felt like a shadow moving through someone else's life. Her daughter, Fiona, was off at college in Boston. Bryony was left in a drafty, rented cottage, working a low-paying university gig just to keep her hands dirty enough to forget.

    But this orchid? This was her way back.

    If she could get this specimen to grow, she could secure her tenure. She could stop the dean from tearing down her greenhouse to build a shiny new locker room for the football team. She could finally stand on her own feet without hearing her ex-husband’s voice in her head, calling her research a "glorified gardening hobby."

    She worked for three hours in a haze of quiet intensity. She sterilized her tools, sliced a tiny, precise tissue sample from the dormant root, and set up a fresh growth medium. The main jar went into her personal steel locker in the back office. It wasn't much of a safe—just a cheap, rusted cabinet—but it was the only thing with a key.

    By the time she locked up the botany building, the sun was gone. A cold, gray November fog hung low over the campus. She drove home, ate a bowl of lukewarm soup, and slept without dreaming for the first time in months.

    The next morning destroyed everything.

    Bryony reached the greenhouse at six, her hands wrapped around a hot mug of coffee. The morning air bit at her face. As she rounded the gravel path, she stopped dead.

    The double wooden doors were wide open. One of the heavy glass panes lay shattered across the stone steps, glittering like ice in the gray dawn.

    A cold, heavy knot dropped into her stomach.

    "Hello?" her voice was thin, swallowed by the wind. "Is someone in there?"

    Only the whistle of the draft answered.

    She stepped over the threshold, her boots crunching on glass. The sight made her fingers loosen. Her mug hit the flagstones, shattering and splashing hot coffee across her boots, but she didn't feel it.

    The conservatory was ruined.

    Heavy terracotta pots had been smashed to dust. Rare, decades-old ferns and orchids were ripped from their soil and ground into the dirt under heavy boot heels. Her workbenches were flipped. The glass display cases holding her field notebooks were broken, the pages torn out and scattered like dead leaves in the mud.

    "No," she whispered. "Oh, god, no..."

    She ran for the back office.

    The door frame was splintered, the lock kicked completely through. Inside, her metal desk lay on its side. The steel locker was warped, the door bent back like paper.

    The jar was gone.

    A dry, choking sob caught in her throat. She gripped her chest, trying to force air into her lungs as she stared at the empty, bent shelf. Everything she had rebuilt, every scrap of peace she had fought for, wiped out in a single night.

    Then, she saw the paper.

    A glossy photograph was sitting on her overturned desk chair.

    Bryony forced her legs to move. She reached out, her fingers trembling so hard she could barely pinch the edge of the paper.

    It was Fiona.

    The shot had been taken from above, looking down at her nineteen-year-old daughter walking across her college campus in Boston. Fiona was wearing her oversized green sweater, her heavy backpack slumped over her shoulder, completely oblivious to the camera.

    A strip of white tape was stuck to the bottom of the photo. Black, blocky letters were printed across it:

    WE HAVE THE ORCHID. YOU HAVE THE JOURNAL AND THE SAMPLES. ABANDONED FOUNDRY ON COAL ROAD. FRIDAY NIGHT. ONE COP, OR IF YOU LEAVE THE COUNTY, FIONA DOESN'T MAKE HER SATURDAY EXAM. WE ARE WATCHING.

    The air left Bryony’s lungs in a sickening gasp. She collapsed onto her knees in the wet dirt, clutching the photograph to her chest.

    Fiona. Her baby. Three states away, with someone watching her through a lens.

    Her hand scrambled into her pocket, pulling out her phone. Her thumb hovered over the emergency dial screen. Her heart beat against her ribs like a trapped bird.

    But she couldn't press it.

    The campus security guards were just two retired guys who spent their nights watching television in the guard shack. If she called the local police, they’d spend hours filling out forms. They'd have to call Boston. By the time anyone did anything, the people watching Fiona would know. They had resources. They knew her name, her schedule, and exactly what she had found in that crate.

    If she made a mistake, Fiona would pay for it.

    She stood up, using the edge of the tipped desk to steady her shaking legs. The panic was still there, but beneath it, a cold, hard anger began to settle in. They were not going to touch her daughter.

    She remembered the card.

    Two months ago, after some kids stole copper pipes from the basement, the campus facilities manager had slipped her a thick, plain card. "If things ever get messy," he’d said, "and you need someone who knows how to handle real trouble without the paperwork, call him. He isn't cheap, but he gets it done."

    She dug into her coat pocket, her fingers finding the stiff cardstock.

    Hayes Security & Investigations. Declan Hayes. No address. Just a phone number and a silver key logo.

    She didn't call. A phone call could be tracked, or intercepted. Instead, she grabbed her keys, stuffed her leather cultivation journal—which she had luckily taken home the night before—into her bag, and walked out of the broken greenhouse.

    The Hayes Security office was a low brick building on the industrial edge of town, tucked between auto shops and warehouses facing the gray, choppy river. The rain was coming down harder now, drumming against her car roof.

    She sat in her station wagon, her knuckles white on the steering wheel. She took a ragged breath, trying to smooth back her wet hair. She felt small, old, and completely out of her depth.

    She pushed open the heavy steel door of the warehouse and stepped into a cold, concrete lobby. No front desk. No secretary. Just a couple of worn leather chairs and a metal desk.

    "Can I help you?"

    The voice was deep, dry, and rough as gravel.

    Bryony turned. A man stood in the doorway of the back office.

    Declan Hayes didn't look like any investigator she'd seen on the news. He was in his mid-thirties, built thick and solid, like someone who spent his life carrying heavy loads. He wore a dark thermal shirt with the sleeves pushed up, showing forearms crossed with a pale, jagged scar near the left wrist. His hair was cut short, his jaw covered in dark, rough stubble.

    But his eyes were what stopped her. They were a cold, sharp hazel-gray, tracking her every movement like a hawk watching a field. He didn't look friendly. He looked dangerous.

    "Are you Declan Hayes?" she asked, her voice cracking.

    "I am," he said, stepping into the room. He didn't offer a hand. "We're closed for walk-ins today. If you need an appointment—"

    "They have my daughter," Bryony said.

    Declan stopped. His face didn't change, but his posture shifted. His eyes went from her face, down to her muddy boots, to the desperate grip she had on her bag. He'd seen that look before.

    "In here," he said, nodding toward the back office.

    She followed him into a room that smelled of old coffee and wet wool. A massive oak desk sat in the center, crowded with three computer monitors showing blinking security feeds.

    "Sit down," he said, pointing to a chair.

    "I don't want to sit," Bryony said, her fingers twisting the strap of her bag. "I don't have time. They gave me until Friday night, and it’s already Wednesday afternoon."

    Declan didn't push it. He leaned back against the edge of his desk, crossing his arms. "Start at the beginning. Who has her, and what do they want?"

    Bryony pulled the photo from her bag and laid it on the desk between them. Her finger lingered on Fiona's face before she let go.

    "I work at the university greenhouse," she said, the words spilling out fast. "Yesterday, I found an orchid specimen—a Cypripedium aurum. It was thought to be extinct. It's worth millions. This morning, my lab was torn apart. The orchid is gone. They left this photo of my daughter. She's in Boston, at school."

    Declan picked up the picture. He didn't look at the girl; he studied the angle of the shot, the background buildings, the lighting.

    "They told me they'll kill her if I call the police," she whispered, her throat tightening. "They want my research notes and the tissue samples I took. I have them in my bag. I can't risk the police, Mr. Hayes. I need someone who can get her out without them knowing."

    Declan put the photo down. His face was hard. "You're asking me to pull a girl off a crowded college campus while a hostile group is actively watching her."

    "I'm asking you to save her," she said, her voice dropping into something raw and fierce. "I'll pay whatever you want. I don't have much left after my divorce, but I have my savings, and I'll sign over the rights to the orchid research. Just help me."

    Declan watched her. He took in the wild strands of copper hair escaping her clip, the dirt under her fingernails, the desperate, stubborn line of her jaw. She was terrified, but she wasn't breaking.

    "If I take this," Declan said, his voice quiet and heavy, "we do it my way. No exceptions. You do what I say, when I say it. You don't call anyone, and you don't go back to your house. Until your daughter is safe, your life belongs to me. Understood?"

    The sheer weight of his voice made her chest tighten. For three years, she’d fought to never let a man tell her what to do again. Every independent bone in her body wanted to push back, but she looked at Fiona’s face on the desk and swallowed her pride.

    "Understood," she said.

    "Good." Declan stood up, his broad shoulders blocking out the light from the window. He walked to a heavy steel safe in the corner and punched in a code. "First step is securing the girl. I have a guy in Boston—ex-military. He owes me. I'll have him put eyes on her dorm within the hour. We don't move her yet. If we pull her out too fast, the watchers here will notice and panic."

    "But they're watching her now," Bryony said, her voice rising. "What if they—"

    "My guy is a professional," Declan cut her off, his gray eyes locking onto hers. "He won't be seen. If we panic, we lose. Right now, they think they have all the cards because they have the plant and they think you're frozen."

    "I am frozen," she whispered.

    "They don't need to know that." He pulled a black gear bag from the safe, throwing in a few heavy metal cases with quick, practiced movements. "We have forty-eight hours before they expect you at the foundry. That’s our window to figure out who they are, trace their location, and pull the teeth out of their operation."

    He zipped the bag with a sharp, heavy rip. He turned to her, his face completely blank of emotion, entirely focused on the work.

    "Let's move. My truck is in the back. We're going to a safe house where we can look at those notes and get organized."

    Bryony looked at him, the cold reality of it finally sinking in. The quiet world of her greenhouse was gone. She was stepping into the dark, led by a man who looked like he’d spent his entire life in it.

    "Okay," she said, grabbing her bag. "Let's go."

    The late afternoon heat inside the humanities building was a physical weight. Thick. Wet. The air conditioning had died on Tuesday, leaving three days of old paper, floor wax, and damp brick to rot in the humid August air.

    Bryony Abernathy dragged a damp hand across the back of her neck. Her fingers caught on the loose, silver-threaded curls that had escaped her clip. She’d been at her desk for four hours. Her shoulders were locked into a hard, angry knot. Usually, the campus went quiet in the summer, but this year the silence felt heavy. Suffocating.

    Fiona was gone. Three weeks ago, her daughter had packed two suitcases and a box of instant ramen, moving into a dorm across state lines. For twenty-one years, Bryony’s life had been a noisy, beautiful mess of burnt toast, missed school buses, and the comforting hum of another heartbeat in the house. Now? Silence. The kind of quiet where you can hear the refrigerator coils rattle from three rooms away. She’d spent years praying for peace after the divorce. Well, she had it now. So why did it feel like a trap?

    She picked up her tea. Lukewarm. Stale. She set it back down with a quiet grunt. Richard would have had something to say about that. You’re too sensitive to the temperature, Bryony. You let the weather ruin your mood. He always said it softly. Smiling. That polite, venomous little smile that made her believe her own discomfort was some kind of character flaw. It had taken five years and a signed decree to realize she was allowed to be hot, tired, or pissed off without needing a man’s permission.

    Three sharp knocks rattled the frosted glass of her office door. Not a polite tap. A demand.

    Bryony cleared her throat. "Come in."

    The door swung back, and the tiny office shrank.

    The man in the doorway was huge. Six-foot-four at least, with shoulders that ate up the hallway’s yellow light. He wore a charcoal tactical shirt that stretched tight across a broad chest, heavy canvas pants, and scuffed black boots. His skin was the color of old copper, marked by pale, thin scars. His hair was cropped close, dark and damp at the temples.

    But his eyes—they were cold, slate grey. They did a quick, professional sweep of the room before locking onto her. He was thirty-four. She remembered the safety bulletin from this morning. New night-security specialist. A response to the break-ins at the science labs. But reading a sterile email didn't prepare her for the raw, heavy gravity of him. He was a man in his prime, carrying a dangerous, quiet confidence that had absolutely nothing to do with syllabus reviews and everything to do with muscle memory.

    "Professor Abernathy?" His voice was low, a deep, gravelly drag that vibrated right through her floorboards.

    "Yes." She stood up, smoothing her linen trousers, trying to look like a department head and not a woman who had just been caught staring. "You’re Declan Hayes."

    "Yes, ma'am." He didn't move to shake her hand. He just stood there, thumbs hooked into his utility belt. "Doing a physical sweep of the older buildings before dark. Ground floor offices are high-risk."

    Ma'am. God, why did that word make her stomach do a slow, dizzying flip? She wasn't a girl. She was forty-three. She had a daughter in college. But the way he looked at her—not past her, but at her—made her acutely aware of her own skin. The curve of her hips, the weight of her breasts under her linen shirt. For years, she’d felt like a ghost walking these halls. Invisible. But under his gaze, she felt solid. Heavy. Real.

    "High-risk?" She leaned back against her mahogany desk, crossing her arms. "There's a rose garden right outside that window. The thorns are usually enough of a deterrent."

    He didn't smile. His jaw, dark with a couple days of stubble, stayed set. "Thorns don't stop someone who wants in. Especially not when the lock is ancient."

    He stepped into the room. His boots made the old wood creak. Before he even reached her, she smelled him—rain, cedar, clean sweat, and the sharp, chemical tang of gun oil. It was a fiercely masculine scent. It made her mouth run dry.

    He stopped a foot away. The contrast was almost comical. She was five-foot-five, her body soft and rounded by time and motherhood. He was a mountain of muscle, radiating a dry, furnace-like heat.

    "Mind if I check the window?"

    "Go ahead," she said, her voice dropping an octave.

    Declan moved past her. To get to the window, he had to squeeze through the narrow gap between her chair and her desk. His thigh brushed hers. Just a brief, rough slide of heavy canvas against thin linen. A sharp, electric shock went straight down her spine. Her fingers gripped the edge of the desk until her knuckles turned white.

    If he felt it, he didn't show it. He leaned over her desk, reaching for the latch. The dark fabric of his shirt stretched tight across his shoulder blades, mapping the thick muscles beneath. His sleeves were rolled up, showing heavy forearms covered in dark hair and a jagged, pale scar that ran from his wrist to his elbow.

    He was so close she could feel his body heat. It was intoxicating. Her eyes drifted to the back of his neck, spotting a small, dark tattoo of a compass rose peeking out from his collar.

    "The latch is stripped." Declan gave the metal handle a firm tug. It rattled loosely. "A kid could jimmy this open with a butter knife."

    "I’ve been putting in work orders for two years," Bryony said, her heart hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird. "They keep telling me there's no money."

    "There's money now," Declan said. He turned around.

    He was right there. Inches away. She had to tilt her head back just to look him in the eye. His slate-grey gaze settled on hers, dark and unreadable. The silence stretched between them, thick and hot, broken only by a low rumble of thunder from the western sky.

    Her eyes dropped to his mouth. Full lips, slightly parted. A wild, sudden ache flared up inside her. She wanted to reach out. She wanted to slide her hands over that rough stubble, to feel the hard, heavy muscle of his chest. It had been decades since she’d felt this kind of raw, animal hunger. With Richard, intimacy had been a performance. A duty she checked off to avoid the sighs and the quiet disappointment. But Declan? She wanted to be handled. She wanted those massive, scarred hands to hold her down until she forgot her own name.

    Declan’s eyes darkened. His chest rose and fell, his breathing turning heavy. His gaze slid down to the hollow of her collarbone, following a single bead of sweat as it rolled slowly down into the open V of her blouse.

    A muscle in his jaw jumped. He cleared his throat—a harsh, sudden sound—and took a step back, breaking the spell. The sudden absence of his heat felt cold.

    "I’ll get a temporary lock on this by tomorrow morning," he said, his voice clipped, professional, and entirely detached. "Until then, don't stay here after dark."

    The sudden frost stung. Bryony’s chin went up, her old defenses slamming back into place. "I have three sections of Victorian literature to prep, Mr. Hayes. I work when I have the hours. Often, that’s late."

    "Not in this wing," Declan said flatly. "Security over here is garbage. You want to work late, do it at your kitchen table."

    "I don't bring my work home," she snapped, her voice sharpening. "And I don't need a lecture on how to manage my time. I’ve been in this office for twelve years, Mr. Hayes. I’ve never had a single incident."

    "You haven't had one yet," Declan countered, his voice dropping into a quiet, dangerous register. "My job is to keep it that way. That means you follow the rules I set. When the sun goes down, this building locks. If you're still here, I'll remove you."

    Anger, hot and bright, flared up to burn away the embarrassment. After years of Richard controlling every aspect of her existence—what she wore, who she invited over, how she spent her weekends—she had sworn she’d never let another man tell her what to do. She had fought for this quiet life. For her independence. She wasn't about to take orders from a thirty-four-year-old security guard with a chip on his shoulder.

    "I am a tenured professor," she said, stepping back into his space, her eyes flashing. "I don't need a babysitter. And I don't have a curfew."

    Declan didn't budge. He stood there like a stone wall. "I don't care about your tenure, Professor. I care about your safety. If you're in this building after eight, I will find you, and I will escort you to your car. Whether you like it or not."

    "Is that a threat?"

    "It's a promise."

    They were chest-to-chest now. The air between them felt charged, like the sky before lightning strikes. Bryony was shaking, but it wasn't fear. It was the rush of the fight, the wild thrill of standing up to him, mixed with the heavy, pulsing attraction that refused to die. He was arrogant. He was bossy. But God, he was beautiful when he was angry.

    Declan’s eyes scanned her face, tracking the flush on her neck, the rapid rise and fall of her chest. His hand clenched into a tight fist at his side. For a split second, the professional mask cracked, revealing a dark, raw hunger that matched her own. He wanted her. It was right there in the tension of his shoulders, the sudden heat in his eyes, the way his gaze lingered on her mouth.

    Then, just as quickly, the shutter slammed closed. He took a slow step back, his face turning back to stone.

    "Have a good evening, Professor Abernathy."

    He turned and walked out, his heavy boots ringing down the empty hallway.

    Bryony stood frozen. The silence of the office rushed back to fill the void. Her knees felt weak, and she slid back into her chair, her heart thumping against her ribs.

    She touched her collarbone, her skin still tingling where his eyes had rested.

    She was forty-three. A mother. A scholar. A woman who had built a quiet, safe life. She didn't need the mess of a younger man, let alone one who thought he could dictate her schedule. He was a temp. A contractor who would be gone by winter.

    But as she looked at the window, where the first heavy drops of rain were starting to lash against the glass, she knew she was in deep. The wall of Declan Hayes was right in front of her, and she wanted nothing more than to break it down.

    The storm broke twenty minutes later, turning the campus into a gray, blurred mess of wind and water.

    Bryony stared at her desk, reading the same paragraph for the fourth time. The words were just ink on paper. Meaningless. Every creak in the hallway made her jump, her ears straining for the heavy, rhythmic thud of those tactical boots.

    Get a grip, she muttered, snapping the folder shut. You're acting like a teenager.

    She packed her leather bag with sharp, jerky movements and grabbed her umbrella. She wasn't staying. Not because he told her to leave, but because the air in the room was too thick. It smelled like him—cedar and rain. It was suffocating.

    She locked the door, her hand trembling as she turned the key. The hallway was dark, the overhead lights flickering as the storm rattled the building’s old bones. Her heels clicked sharply against the wood, echoing in the empty hall.

    She reached the heavy wooden double doors at the entrance. And stopped.

    Declan was standing in the glass vestibule, his arms crossed over his massive chest, watching the rain hammer the concrete steps outside. He’d swapped his grey shirt for a black rain jacket, the wet fabric tight across his shoulders. He didn't turn around, but she saw his posture go rigid the moment she stepped near.

    "Thought I told you to leave before dark," he said, his gravelly voice cutting through the roar of the rain.

    "And I believe I told you I don't take orders from you," Bryony said, stopping two feet behind him.

    Outside, the rain was a wall of water. The wind was howling, ripping leaves off the old oaks in the courtyard. Walking to the faculty lot in this would be misery, even with her flimsy umbrella.

    Declan turned slowly. The shadows of the vestibule caught the hard lines of his face, making him look even more rugged. Dangerous. "You don't have a coat."

    "I have an umbrella."

    "The wind will shred that thing before you clear the steps," he said, his voice flat. "Wait here. I’ll bring my truck around."

    "No," Bryony said, lifting her chin. "I can walk, Mr. Hayes. I’ve survived worse storms than this."

    "Stubborn," he muttered, stepping closer.

    "Independent," she shot back, raising her voice over the thunder. "There is a difference. I don't need you to rescue me."

    "I'm not trying to rescue you," Declan said, his voice dropping into that deep, dangerous register that made her chest tighten. He was right there now. She could see the silver flecks in his grey eyes, the tight pull of his mouth. "I'm doing my job. If you slip and break your neck on my watch, that's my problem."

    "Is that all I am? A liability?"

    Declan’s gaze fell to her mouth. His breath was warm on her face, smelling of rain and mint. The silence between them grew heavy, thick with a tension that had absolutely nothing to do with the weather.

    "You're a distraction," he whispered.

    The word hung in the air, raw and honest. Bryony’s heart did a slow, heavy roll. Her defenses crumbled. He was thirty-four, beautiful, and completely wrong for her. But in this dark vestibule, with the wind howling outside, nothing else mattered.

    "Declan," she breathed.

    His jaw clenched. His hand twitched, hovering just inches from her arm, as if he wanted to grab her, pull her against his chest, and never let go. She saw the war playing out in his eyes—the professional guard fighting the hungry man who had finally found the one thing he wanted.

    Slowly, his hand dropped back to his side. The mask slid back into place. Cold. Unyielding.

    "Stay here, Professor," he said, his voice hard as iron. "I’ll get the truck."

    He pushed the heavy glass door open and stepped out into the pouring rain, leaving her alone in the dark, her heart hammering, her body aching with a quiet, desperate hunger.

    The storm came from the north, swallowing the Cascades in a flat, blinding white. Outside the truck, the pines had vanished. Inside, the heater rattled, spitting out dry, dusty air that did nothing to thaw the cold ache in Bryony’s knees.

    Beside her, Declan Hayes kept his eyes locked on the road. Or where the road used to be. His hands were clamped on the wheel, his jaw tight. He was thirty-four, nine years younger than her, and built like a brick wall. For two weeks, he’d been in her house, in her space, taking up too much room. Now, in the cab of the Ford, his shoulders blocked out half her view.

    "We’re not going to make the driveway," Declan said. His voice was a low, dry rasp. "The drifts are too high. We'll have to hike the last hundred yards."

    Bryony pulled her collar tighter. "Then we walk."

    She wasn't about to complain. She was forty-three. She had survived a brutal divorce, built a design firm from nothing, and raised a daughter who was currently safe at college. She wasn't a porcelain doll. But the last three weeks had stripped her bare. First came the letters. Then the brick through her kitchen window. Then the shadow by her back porch. When the police suggested she leave town, Declan had appeared—hired by her business partner to act as a shield. But his quiet, heavy presence felt less like safety and more like a cage.

    Now they were hiding in a forestry cabin owned by a friend. A place with no cell service.

    Declan killed the engine under a thick cluster of hemlocks. The silence that followed was heavy, broken only by the wind whistling through the door seals.

    "Stay put," he said. He didn't look at her. He just shoved his door open, letting in a blast of dry, freezing powder, and stepped into the waist-high drift.

    Bryony watched him through the glass. He moved with a heavy, deliberate pace, hauling their duffels from the truck bed and throwing them over his shoulders. A strange, hot spike of annoyance flared in her chest. She hated being protected. She hated the way her heart did a stupid, erratic skip whenever he got too close.

    She opened her passenger door and dropped into the snow. The cold hit her ribs like a physical blow, stealing her breath. She took three steps and stumbled.

    A large, gloved hand caught her elbow. Declan was right there.

    "I have my footing," she snapped, her pride stinging.

    "The hell you do," he said. He didn't let go. His grip was firm, guiding her through the drifts as the wind whipped snow into their eyes. "Just walk, Bryony."

    She shut her mouth and let him pull her toward the dark shape of the cabin.

    The place was small—just a square of rough-hewn logs with a tin roof. When Declan unlocked the door and shoved it open, the air inside was even colder than the storm.

    He flipped the wall switch. Nothing.

    "Generator’s dead," he muttered. "Must be the lines. I’ll check it tomorrow. Tonight, we use the stove."

    He clicked on a small pocket flashlight, cutting a yellow circle through the dark. Bryony stood by the door, shivering so hard her teeth clicked. The light swept over a small laminate counter, two pine chairs, and a single, narrow bed tucked into the corner, piled with faded green wool blankets.

    One bed.

    "I'll take the floor," Declan said immediately. He knelt by the iron stove, clearing out the old ash with his bare hands and laying down dry cedar kindling. "You take the mattress."

    "The floor is bare pine, Declan. You’ll freeze."

    "I’ve slept in dirt trenches." He struck a match. The small flame illuminated the hard lines of his face—the flat bridge of his nose, the heavy dark stubble, the exhaustion etched around his eyes. He blew gently on the kindling until a small, orange tongue of fire caught the wood.

    Bryony watched him. He was young, his body still hard and unbroken by the years. She felt her own age tonight. Her lower back throbbed from the six-hour drive.
