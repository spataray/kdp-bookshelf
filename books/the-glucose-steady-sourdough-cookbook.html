Chapter 1: The Glucose-Steady Sourdough Pantry & Starters

That 3:00 PM fog is a beast. You know the feeling: thirty minutes after polishing off a turkey sandwich on soft, store-bought wheat, your eyelids turn to lead. Your brain shuts down. By 3:30, you're scavenging the pantry for anything sweet, desperate for a quick hit of energy.

For decades, if you were diagnosed with pre-diabetes, type 2 diabetes, or chronic inflammation, doctors handed you a devastating, one-sentence life sentence: Give up bread forever.

To anyone who loves the smell of a warm kitchen, that feels like a prison cell. You’re forced to choose between keeping your blood sugar out of the red zone and enjoying a crackling crust with a soft, sour crumb. So you try the alternatives. You buy those expensive, low-carb loaves from the supermarket. Let's be honest: they taste like toasted cardboard and feel like wet sponges. They're packed with synthetic binders, isolated starches, and preservatives that leave your gut ballooned with gas. It's a bad deal.

But you don't have to live in bread exile. The answer isn't in some laboratory-designed chemical binder. It’s sitting right there in the ancient dance between wild yeast and the human gut.

Here is the secret nobody tells you about commercial bread: it is built for speed, not health. Modern industrial bakeries use fast-acting yeast and chemical dough conditioners to force flour from a dry powder into a packaged loaf in under two hours. This process bypasses fermentation entirely. It leaves the starch molecules fully exposed and rapidly digestible. When you eat it, your body converts these simple starches into glucose almost instantly, forcing your pancreas to pump out massive amounts of insulin.

Traditional sourdough fermentation changes everything. Over hours and days, wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria work in tandem to pre-digest the starches and sugars in the flour.

The Metabolic Reality Check
* Commercial Bread: Fast yeast $\rightarrow$ Zero digestion of starches $\rightarrow$ Instant glucose spike.
* Wild Sourdough: Slow fermentation $\rightarrow$ Bacteria eat the starches $\rightarrow$ Slower, steadier absorption.

Lactic acid lowers the bread’s pH, which slows down how fast your body processes the starch during digestion. For you, this means your stomach empties slower, and carbohydrates convert into glucose at a gentle, manageable crawl rather than a sudden spike.

Then there is the flour itself. I get this question all the time: “But doesn’t ancient grain flour still have carbs?”

Yes, it does. But we aren't using standard white flour. Our house low-glycemic blend balances ancient grains with sweet lupin flour and organic coconut flour. Lupin is a legume-derived flour with practically zero active carbs, while coconut flour brings loads of insoluble fiber to the table. When you combine these ingredients with the organic acids from a wild sourdough fermentation, the glycemic impact of the finished loaf drops to a fraction of standard wheat bread.

Cultivating your starter is the first step toward reclaiming real bread. The recipes below will guide you through establishing your wild ferment, preparing your pantry, and mastering the low-glycemic building blocks that will support your baking.

The Everyday Whole-Wheat Culture

This is your workhorse. It uses stone-ground whole grains to culture wild yeasts, delivering a deep, classic sour flavor and reliable rising power. If you are new to sourdough, start here.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 1 starter culture

Ingredients

  • 500 grams Stone-ground organic whole-wheat flour
  • 500 milliliters Filtered water (chlorine-free, lukewarm)

Instructions

1. Day 1: Get yourself a clean, wide-mouth 1-quart glass jar. Combine 50 grams of whole-wheat flour and 55 milliliters of lukewarm water. Stir thoroughly with a wooden spoon or spatula until all the dry flour is gone. Cover the jar loosely with a lid or a clean cloth secured with a rubber band. Let it sit in a warm spot (around 70°F to 75°F) for 24 hours. 2. Day 2: Don't worry if nothing seems to be happening yet. Discard all but 50 grams of your starter. Add 50 grams of whole-wheat flour and 50 milliliters of lukewarm water. Stir well, cover loosely, and let it rest for another 24 hours. 3. Days 3 to 7: Repeat this daily feeding process at roughly the same time each day. By day 4 or 5, you should see tiny bubbles forming and catch a sweet, yeasty aroma. By day 7, the starter should double in size within 4 to 6 hours after a feed, smelling pleasantly fruity and sour. 4. Maintenance: Once your starter is active and bubbling reliably, store it in the fridge. Feed it once a week using that same 1:1:1 ratio of starter, flour, and water to keep it happy.

Chef's Tip

Always use filtered, chlorine-free water. Municipal tap water contains chemicals designed to kill off the exact wild bacteria we want to grow.

Working with Einkorn: An Ancient Wild Ferment

Einkorn is the oldest wheat known to humanity. Its gluten structure is weak, which makes it much friendlier on sensitive stomachs. It absorbs water a bit differently than modern wheat, so we keep the hydration slightly lower here.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 1 starter culture

Ingredients

  • 500 grams Organic whole-grain einkorn flour
  • 400 milliliters Filtered water (chlorine-free, lukewarm)

Instructions

1. Day 1: Mix 60 grams of einkorn flour and 45 milliliters of lukewarm water in a clean glass jar. Stir until it forms a thick paste. Cover it loosely and set it in a warm corner for 24 hours. 2. Day 2: Stir the mixture. Discard all but 50 grams of the paste. Add 50 grams of einkorn flour and 40 milliliters of lukewarm water. Mix well, cover loosely, and let it rest. 3. Days 3 to 7: Feed the starter at the same time every day. Use 50 grams of your active starter, 50 grams of einkorn flour, and 40 milliliters of water. 4. Look for active bubbling and a slightly sticky, marshmallow-like texture. By day 7, you should have a gentle, nutty-smelling culture ready for action.

Chef's Tip

Because einkorn lacks structural strength, this starter won't dome or rise as high as traditional wheat. Don't panic—as long as you see bubbles and a spongy texture, it’s working.

Earthy Dark Rye (The Blood-Sugar Cheat Code)

Rye is a metabolic powerhouse. It is loaded with natural compounds called pentosans that slow down your digestion, keeping your blood sugar stable. It’s incredibly active and difficult to kill.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 1 starter culture

Ingredients

  • 500 grams Organic dark rye flour
  • 500 milliliters Filtered water (chlorine-free, lukewarm)

Instructions

1. Day 1: Whisk 50 grams of dark rye flour and 50 milliliters of lukewarm water in a glass jar until smooth. Rye ferments incredibly fast because of its high enzyme content. Cover loosely and let it rest for 24 hours. 2. Day 2: You will likely see bubbles already. Discard all but 50 grams of the mix. Stir in 50 grams of dark rye flour and 50 milliliters of lukewarm water. Cover and set aside. 3. Days 3 to 7: Keep up the daily feeding cycle: 1 part starter, 1 part rye flour, and 1 part water. The mixture will become highly bubbly, looking almost like a chocolate mousse, and will develop a deep, vinegary tang. 4. By day 6 or 7, your rye starter is fully mature and ready to lift your loaves.

Chef's Tip

Rye starters work fast. If your kitchen is warm, you might need to feed this starter twice a day to keep it from getting too sour.

A Grain-Free Alternative Using Wild Rice

For those who want a completely wheat-free option, this starter uses a blend of wild and brown rice flour to trap local yeasts. It has a beautiful, floral aroma.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 1 starter culture

Ingredients

  • 250 grams Organic wild rice flour
  • 250 grams Whole brown rice flour
  • 500 milliliters Filtered water (chlorine-free, lukewarm)

Instructions

1. Whisk the wild rice flour and brown rice flour together in a bowl to create your flour base. 2. Day 1: Mix 50 grams of your new rice flour blend with 55 milliliters of lukewarm water in a clean jar. Gluten-free flours need a little extra water to stay hydrated. Cover loosely. 3. Day 2: Discard all but 50 grams of the mixture. Add 50 grams of the rice flour blend and 55 milliliters of water. Stir vigorously to get some air into the mix. 4. Days 3 to 8: Feed daily. Since there is no gluten to trap the gas, this starter won't double in height. Instead, look for a spongy texture, tiny surface bubbles, and a clean, sour smell to know it’s healthy.

Chef's Tip

Stir this starter with real enthusiasm during feeds. Incorporating extra oxygen helps the wild yeast grow in a gluten-free environment.

Seed-Based Binding Gel (Flax & Chia)

This is our secret weapon for grain-free and low-carb baking. It acts like gluten, holding your dough together without adding heavy starches. Plus, the extra fiber helps blunt any potential blood sugar rise.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 8 servings

Ingredients

  • 40 grams Organic golden flaxseeds (whole)
  • 20 grams Organic white chia seeds (whole)
  • 300 milliliters Warm filtered water

Instructions

1. Toss the flaxseeds and chia seeds into a high-speed blender or a clean spice grinder. Grind for about 30 seconds until you have a fine, uniform meal. 2. Pour the ground seeds into a small glass bowl. 3. Whisk in the warm water quickly so the seeds don't clump together. 4. Let it sit on the counter for 15 minutes. The mixture will absorb the water, turning into a thick, gelatinous gel. Give it one final stir before adding it to your dough.

Chef's Tip

Always grind your seeds fresh. Pre-ground flax and chia seeds sit on shelves and oxidize, which ruins their flavor and strips away their healthy fats.

Our Go-To Low-GI Flour Mix

This is the backbone of your low-glycemic baking. By cutting traditional flours with high-protein lupin and high-fiber coconut flour, we slash the carb count while keeping that classic, rustic texture.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 10 servings

Ingredients

  • 300 grams Organic einkorn flour
  • 300 grams Organic spelt flour
  • 250 grams Sweet lupin flour
  • 150 grams Organic coconut flour (fine ground)

Instructions

1. Pull out your digital kitchen scale—precision matters here. 2. Place a large mixing bowl on the scale, tare it to zero, and weigh out your einkorn, spelt, lupin, and coconut flours. 3. Use a large wire whisk to blend the flours together for a solid two minutes. You want to make sure the highly absorbent coconut flour is completely distributed throughout the mix. 4. Sift the mixture through a fine-mesh sieve into an airtight container to get rid of any lumps. Store it in a cool, dark cupboard for up to three months.

Chef's Tip

Make sure you buy "sweet" lupin flour. Standard lupin flour can sometimes have a bitter, chalky taste, but the sweet variety is mild and buttery.

Rich Bone Broth (For Extra Dough Strength)

Instead of plain water, use this gelatin-rich broth as your hydration liquid. It infuses your loaves with collagen, amino acids, and a rich savory flavor while giving your dough better elasticity.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 12 hours | Servings: 6 servings

Ingredients

  • 1.5 kilograms Grass-fed beef marrow bones
  • 2 tablespoons Apple cider vinegar
  • 3 liters Filtered water
  • 2 stalks Organic celery (rough chopped)

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 400°F (200°C). Roast the marrow bones on a baking sheet for 30 minutes until they are deeply browned and fragrant. 2. Transfer the roasted bones to a large stockpot or slow cooker. Pour in the filtered water and apple cider vinegar. Let it sit cold for 20 minutes; the acid in the vinegar helps pull the beneficial collagen and minerals from the bones. 3. Toss in the chopped celery and bring the liquid to a bare simmer over medium-high heat. 4. Drop the heat to low, cover, and let it simmer gently for 12 hours. Strain the liquid through a fine sieve or cheesecloth, discard the solids, and let the broth cool completely in the fridge. Once cold, scrape away the hard layer of fat from the top before using the rich, gelatinous broth in your bread recipes.

Chef's Tip

Don't skip the resting step with the vinegar before heating. It's the secret to getting a highly gelatinous broth that gives your bread dough incredible stretch.

Slow-Roasted Garlic Oil

Soft, sweet cloves of garlic preserved in extra virgin olive oil. This mixture adds incredible depth to your loaves. You can fold the garlic directly into the dough or brush the oil over the crust before baking.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12 servings

Ingredients

  • 3 whole Heads of garlic
  • 250 milliliters Extra virgin olive oil
  • 2 sprigs Fresh rosemary

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 325°F (160°C). 2. Peel away the loose, papery outer layers of the garlic heads. Slice the top quarter off each head to expose the cloves inside. 3. Place the garlic heads cut-side up in a small baking dish. Pour the olive oil over them until the cloves are completely submerged, then tuck the rosemary sprigs into the oil. 4. Cover the dish tightly with foil and bake for 45 minutes, or until the garlic is buttery soft and golden. Let it cool completely before pouring the oil and garlic into a clean glass jar.

Chef's Tip

Keep this infusion in the refrigerator and use it within two weeks to keep it perfectly fresh and safe to eat.

To keep your starters thriving and ready for the baking ahead, follow these maintenance steps:

Heritage Einkorn Rustic Boule

Einkorn is the original. The genetic grandfather of wheat. It has a beautiful yellow hue and a rich, deeply nutty flavor that pairs beautifully with grass-fed butter or savory spreads. Because einkorn's gluten is weak, it hates excess water. If you treat it like modern bread flour, you’ll end up with a puddle of sticky mud. This recipe keeps the hydration low so your loaf actually stands tall.

Prep time: 40 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active einkorn sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 400g whole grain einkorn flour
  • 240g filtered water (warmed to 80°F/27°C)
  • 9g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Whisk the active starter into the warm water in a large mixing bowl until it looks like a cloudy, milky slurry. 2. Toss in the einkorn flour and salt. Mix with a stiff spatula until you have a sticky, cohesive dough. Do not try to knead this on the counter yet. 3. Cover the bowl with a damp towel. Let it rest for 30 minutes to autolyse. This gives the flour a head start on absorbing the water. 4. Slip a wet hand under the dough, pull it up, and fold it over itself. Rotate the bowl and repeat. Do this gentle stretch-and-fold routine three times, spaced 45 minutes apart. Treat it gently; you don't want to tear the weak gluten strands. 5. Cover and let the dough rise at room temperature (around 70°F/21°C) for 8 to 10 hours. You're looking for about a 50% increase in size, not a double rise. 6. Tip the dough onto a lightly floured surface. Gently shape it into a round ball. Place it seam-side up into a proofing basket lined with rice flour. 7. Slide the basket into the fridge for 12 hours. This cold rest firms up the dough and makes it easy to score. 8. Drop a Dutch oven into your oven and preheat to 450°F (230°C) for 45 minutes. It needs to be screaming hot. 9. Flip the cold dough onto parchment paper. Use a sharp blade to make one clean slash across the top. Carefully drop it into the hot pot, put the lid on, and bake for 25 minutes. 10. Take the lid off. Bake for another 20 minutes until the crust is deep gold, then let it cool on a wire rack. Don't slice it hot, or the inside will turn gummy.

Key Takeaway

Einkorn gluten dissolves in water easily. Never over-knead this dough. If you overwork it, you'll end up with an unmanageable paste that cannot hold its shape.

High-Hydration Spelt Batard

Spelt brings a sweet, slightly nutty flavor that has been cherished in Europe since the Bronze Age. This recipe uses high hydration to get those coveted, airy pockets in the crumb, but we add a tiny splash of standard bread flour just to give it some backbone.

Prep time: 50 mins | Cook time: 40 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter (fed with spelt flour)
  • 450g whole spelt flour
  • 50g unbleached bread flour (for structural support)
  • 380g filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Mix the spelt and bread flours together in a large bowl. Pour in 360g of the water and squish it together with your hands until no dry spots remain. Let it rest for 45 minutes. 2. Add the active starter, salt, and the remaining 20g of water. Fold and pinch the dough with your fingers until the slippery ingredients are fully integrated. 3. Perform four rounds of stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes over the first two hours of the rise. You'll feel the dough tighten up with each round. 4. Let the dough rest undisturbed at room temperature for another 4 to 5 hours until it looks bubbly and alive. 5. Tip the dough onto a floured surface. Shape it into an oval loaf (a batard) and tuck it into a floured oval basket. 6. Seal the basket in a plastic grocery bag and put it in the fridge for 14 hours for a slow, cold rise. 7. Preheat your oven and Dutch oven to 475°F (245°C). 8. Flip the cold dough onto parchment, slice a long line down the center, and bake inside the closed Dutch oven at 450°F (230°C) for 20 minutes. 9. Remove the lid. Bake for another 20 minutes to get a blistered, dark mahogany crust. 10. Let it cool on a wire rack for at least two hours before cutting. Seriously, wait.

Key Takeaway

Spelt absorbs water quickly but gets tired and slacks out fast. Always use a proofing basket to support its shape during the final cold proof in the fridge.

North Sea Rye and Caraway

If you want real bread—the kind that got northern Europe through bitter winters—you make rye. Rye is packed with soluble fiber, which forms a thick gel in your gut, slowing down glucose absorption like nothing else. But warning: rye has almost zero gluten-forming proteins. If you don't use sourdough, you get a brick. The acidity of your starter is what stands between you and a gummy, unbaked mess.

Prep time: 35 mins | Cook time: 50 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 120g active rye sourdough starter
  • 300g dark rye flour
  • 200g whole wheat flour
  • 370g warm water (85°F/29°C)
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • 1 tbsp whole caraway seeds

Instructions

1. Whisk the rye starter into the warm water in a large bowl, then stir in the caraway seeds. 2. Combine the rye flour, whole wheat flour, and salt in a separate bowl, then mix them into the wet starter mixture. It will look and feel like thick, sticky clay. 3. Wet your hands so the dough doesn't stick to you. Press the mixture into a cohesive ball. Cover and let it rest for 1 hour. 4. Do not try to stretch this dough; it will just snap. Instead, wet your hands and perform "fold and press" movements, folding the edges into the center. Do this twice over the next 2 hours. 5. Let the dough rise for 6 to 8 hours at room temperature. You’ll know it’s ready when you see small cracks on the surface and it smells sour and yeasty. 6. Gently shape the dough into a round ball. Dust it generously with rye flour and place it in a bowl lined with a well-floured kitchen towel. 7. Let it rise at room temperature for 1.5 to 2 hours until you see cracks starting to open up on top. 8. Preheat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 9. Carefully flip the dough directly into the hot Dutch oven, cover with the lid, and bake for 30 minutes. 10. Remove the lid, turn the oven down to 425°F (220°C), and bake for another 20 minutes until dark and fragrant.

Key Takeaway

Rye won't give you a dramatic puff in the oven. Focus on getting a dry, moisture-rich crumb by letting the baked loaf sit on the counter for a full 24 hours before you cut into it.

Egyptian Emmer and Walnut

Emmer—often called farro—was the grain of the pharaohs. It has a heavy, dark, herbal flavor profile. By throwing in some toasted walnuts, we're not just adding a great crunch; the healthy fats and fiber in the nuts throw another speed bump in front of your glucose response, keeping your insulin steady.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter
  • 350g whole emmer flour
  • 150g hard white wheat flour
  • 350g warm filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • 80g toasted walnuts, coarsely chopped

Instructions

1. Stir the emmer flour, white wheat flour, and warm water together in a bowl. Let it sit for 45 minutes to hydrate. 2. Squeeze the active sourdough starter and salt into the dough using your hands until everything is completely mixed. 3. Cover and let rest for 30 minutes, then do your first set of stretch-and-folds. 4. During your second set of stretch-and-folds, sprinkle the cooled, toasted walnuts over the dough, folding them gently into the center. 5. Do two more sets of stretch-and-folds over the next 90 minutes. 6. Let the dough ferment at room temperature for 5 to 6 hours until it feels light and full of air. 7. Shape the dough into a round ball, place it into a well-floured basket, and let it rise in the fridge for 12 to 16 hours. 8. Preheat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 9. Transfer the cold dough to parchment, score a cross on top, and bake inside the covered pot for 25 minutes. 10. Uncover and bake for 20 minutes more until the crust is dark and the walnuts smell rich and toasted.

Key Takeaway

Always let your toasted walnuts cool completely before mixing them in. Hot nuts will warm up the dough and cause it to over-ferment, leaving you with a flat, sour loaf.

The Trio: Spelt, Einkorn, and Rye

Why choose? This loaf brings together three ancient titans. You get the sweetness of spelt, the rich gold of einkorn, and the earthy staying power of rye. It’s a dense, moist bread that stays fresh on the counter for days without a single preservative.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter
  • 170g whole spelt flour
  • 170g whole einkorn flour
  • 160g whole rye flour
  • 360g filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Whisk the active starter into the water, then dump in all three flours and the salt. 2. Mix thoroughly with a spatula or your hands until you have a uniform, sticky dough with no dry pockets. 3. Cover the bowl and let the dough rest for 45 minutes. 4. Perform three sets of coil folds (wet your hands, lift the dough from the center, and let it fold under itself) at 45-minute intervals. 5. Cover the bowl tightly and let it rise at room temperature for 6 to 7 hours. 6. Gently move the dough to a floured surface, shape it into a tight ball, and put it into a basket dusted with rice flour. 7. Let it sit in the fridge for 12 to 14 hours. 8. Preheat your oven and a heavy cast-iron combo cooker or Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 9. Score the chilled dough with a decorative pattern and bake covered for 25 minutes. 10. Remove the lid and bake for another 20 minutes until the crust is a dark, caramelized amber.

Key Takeaway

Keep your hands wet during the shaping process. This dough is naturally tacky, and water is your shield against a sticky mess.

Obsidian Barley and Sesame Batard

Black barley looks like dark cocoa when milled. It’s an heirloom grain with an incredibly low glycemic index. We’re rolling this loaf in sesame seeds before it bakes to create a crackly, nutty armor that smells incredible when it hits the hot cast iron.

Prep time: 50 mins | Cook time: 40 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter
  • 200g black barley flour
  • 300g bread flour
  • 375g warm filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • 50g unhulled sesame seeds

Instructions

1. Mix the black barley flour, bread flour, and water in a large bowl. Let it sit for 1 hour. 2. Add the active starter and salt, mixing until the dough comes together and starts to pull away from the sides of the bowl. 3. Do four rounds of stretch-and-folds spaced 30 minutes apart to develop the gluten from the bread flour. 4. Let the dough rise at room temperature for 4 to 5 hours until puffy. 5. Shape the dough into a tight, oval batard. 6. Spray the top of the dough lightly with water, then roll it in a shallow tray filled with the sesame seeds until it’s evenly coated. 7. Place the coated loaf seed-side down into a lined basket and let it rise in the fridge for 12 hours. 8. Preheat your oven and baking stone or Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 9. Turn the loaf out onto parchment, score down the center, and bake with steam (or in a closed pot) for 20 minutes. 10. Remove the steam source or lid and bake for another 20 minutes until the sesame seeds are toasted and fragrant.

Key Takeaway

A light misting of water right before rolling the dough in sesame seeds is the secret to keeping the seeds attached to the crust rather than falling off on your cutting board.

Buttery Kamut (Without the Honey)

Kamut is giant Khorasan wheat. It has a natural, buttery sweetness that tricks your brain into thinking there’s honey or sugar in the dough. We don't need any sweeteners here. Just pure, slow fermentation to unlock that natural sweetness.

Prep time: 40 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter
  • 400g Kamut flour
  • 100g whole wheat flour
  • 340g warm filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Whisk the starter into 320g of the warm water, add both flours, and mix until you have a shaggy dough. Let it rest for 30 minutes. 2. Sprinkle the salt and the remaining 20g of water over the dough, working it in with your fingers until the dough feels smooth. 3. Do three rounds of stretch-and-folds at 45-minute intervals during the first phase of the rise. 4. Let the dough ferment at room temperature for 6 hours, or until it has expanded by more than half and shows small bubbles. 5. Gently shape the dough into a round ball, taking care not to pop all the nice air bubbles inside. 6. Place the dough in a floured basket and refrigerate for 12 to 15 hours. 7. Preheat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 8. Flip the dough onto parchment and score a clean, curved line across the top. 9. Bake inside the covered pot for 25 minutes, then uncover and bake for 20 minutes until the crust is a glowing golden-orange.

Key Takeaway

Kamut is incredibly stretchy but can lose its shape quickly. Give it one extra set of folds during the rise to build the structural strength it needs to rise upward instead of outward.

Black Pepper and Spelt Boule

This is a savory showstopper. The warmth of freshly cracked black pepper pairs with the earthiness of spelt in a way that makes you want to eat the entire loaf in one sitting. Brush a slice with cold-pressed olive oil, toast it, and you have the perfect low-glycemic snack.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter
  • 450g whole spelt flour
  • 50g dark rye flour
  • 365g filtered water
  • 10g fine sea salt
  • 1.5 tbsp black peppercorns, coarsely cracked

Instructions

1. Mix the spelt flour, dark rye flour, and water in a bowl until combined. Let it rest for 1 hour. 2. Work in the active starter and salt, stretching and folding the dough until it’s smooth and holds together. 3. During your first stretch-and-fold, sprinkle the cracked black pepper evenly over the dough, folding it inward to distribute it. 4. Complete three more rounds of stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes. 5. Let the dough rise undisturbed at room temperature for 5 hours. 6. Shape the dough into a tight ball and place it in a basket dusted with rye flour. 7. Cold proof the loaf in the fridge for 14 hours. 8. Preheat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (230°C). 9. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment, score with an "X", and bake covered for 25 minutes. 10. Remove the lid and bake uncovered for 20 minutes until the crust is crisp and the cracked pepper smells incredible.

Key Takeaway

Chapter 4: Flatbreads, Naan, & Tortillas

Have you ever eaten a store-bought tortilla and felt starving just an hour later? There is a simple reason for that.

For thousands of years, every culture on earth had its own version of a flatbread. They were quick, simple, and perfectly designed to hold local ingredients. But today’s commercial versions are a nutritional disaster. Rushed through factory lines with fast-acting yeast and chemical softeners, they have been stripped of everything that made them good. They hit your stomach and instantly turn to sugar.

Sourdough changes the game. When you let natural wild yeast ferment ancient grains like spelt, emmer, or rye, the whole chemistry of the bread changes. The lactic acid in the starter slows down your digestive enzymes. Instead of a massive blood sugar spike and the inevitable mid-afternoon crash, you get a slow, steady burn of energy. It is the difference between throwing dry pine needles on a campfire and throwing on a solid oak log.

"Ancient grains aren't just about rustic flavor; they have a physical structure that modern wheat has lost. A slow sourdough fermentation protects that structure, keeping your blood sugar stable."

Here is the secret to getting that perfect, pillowy texture without messing up your health: fat and water. Adding a splash of extra virgin olive oil, a spoonful of grass-fed ghee, or a bit of avocado oil does more than make the dough taste rich. Fat slows down your digestion. This means you can eat a warm, fresh piece of naan without worrying about a sugar crash later.

Before you heat up your pan, keep these three basic rules in mind:

  • Blistering, Dry Heat: Flatbreads need to cook fast. If your pan is only warm, the bread will turn into cardboard. Use a heavy cast-iron skillet and get it hot.
  • Let It Rest: Ancient grains do not have the tough, elastic gluten of modern wheat. If you overwork the dough, it will fight you. Let the dough rest so the flour can fully absorb the water.
  • A Lively Starter: You want those classic charred bubbles. A weak, sleepy starter will give you flat, heavy disks. Use your starter when it is bubbly and active.

THE STARCH SHOWDOWN: AMYLOSE VS. AMYLOPECTIN

To keep your energy steady, you need to understand what is actually happening inside the flour. Modern dwarf wheat is packed with quick-digesting starches. Ancient grains like einkorn and spelt, however, have a completely different molecular makeup.

Here is how the two main starches stack up in your body:

  • Amylose (The Slow Burn): Think of this as a straight, tightly wound chain. Because it is physically compact, your digestive enzymes take their time breaking it down. The result? A slow, steady release of energy. Einkorn and spelt are loaded with it.
  • Amylopectin (The Spike): This is a highly branched, open starch. Your enzymes shred through it in minutes, dumping glucose straight into your bloodstream. This is what dominates modern white flour.
Why This Matters: When you pair an high-amylose ancient grain with the organic acids from a slow sourdough fermentation, you create a physical barrier for digestion. It slows down the rate at which your body absorbs carbs, protecting your liver and keeping your insulin levels flat.

Ingredients

Savory Bacon and Chive Sourdough Biscuits

These towering, laminated biscuits are loaded with crispy pasture-raised bacon and fresh chives. By using cold butter and sourdough discard, they bake up into flaky, golden layers with a distinct, tangy bite.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 18 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 200g einkorn flour
  • 2 tsp baking powder
  • 1/2 tsp baking soda
  • 1/2 tsp sea salt
  • 75g cold unsalted grass-fed butter, cubed
  • 100g cold sourdough discard (100% hydration)
  • 60ml cold heavy cream
  • 4 slices pasture-raised bacon, cooked crispy and crumbled
  • 3 tbsp fresh chives, finely chopped

Instructions

1. Whisk the einkorn flour, baking powder, baking soda, and salt together in a large bowl. 2. Toss the cold, cubed butter into the flour mixture. Using your fingertips or a pastry blender, press the butter into the flour until the mixture looks like coarse meal with flat, pea-sized pieces of butter remaining. 3. Stir in the crumbled bacon and chopped chives. 4. In a small bowl, whisk the cold sourdough discard and heavy cream together. To get the highest rise possible, make sure the biscuits touch each other on the baking sheet. As they bake, they will lean on each other and push upward, climbing higher than they would on their own.

Chapter 7: Savory Discard Snacks & Crackers

Every time I see someone rinse a cup of sourdough starter down the kitchen sink, a little part of my baker's soul dies. It is a massive missed opportunity. We have been conditioned to treat "discard" as waste—a useless byproduct of keeping a starter alive. But if you are trying to manage your blood sugar and keep your energy stable, that acidic, fermented flour mixture is pure gold.

While your starter sits in the fridge, wild yeasts and lactic acid bacteria are quietly working for you. They eat the simple starches, converting them into organic acids and proteins. By the time you pour that bubbly discard into a mixing bowl, the heavy lifting is already done. The glycemic impact of the flour has plummeted.

Here is the secret most people miss: the lactic acid in your starter acts as a natural speed bump for your digestion. It slows down the rate at which your body breaks down carbohydrates into glucose. Instead of the sharp blood sugar spikes and foggy crashes that come with commercial chips, you get slow, sustained energy. Pair this fermented base with fiber-rich seeds and healthy fats, and you have a snack that satisfies your craving for a crunch without wrecking your metabolic health.

Even better? Discard is incredibly easy to work with. You do not need to wait for a rise. The fermentation process has already softened the gluten, making the dough incredibly pliable and easy to roll paper-thin. Whether you want the snap of an Italian flatbread or a salty pretzel thin, these recipes prove you do not have to give up your favorite crunchy snacks to stay on track.

---

How to Build a Glucose-Friendly Snack

To keep your blood sugar completely steady, use these three simple kitchen rules whenever you bake with discard:

  • Load Up on Seed Fats: Throw in sesame, flax, or chia seeds. Their fiber and healthy fats slow down stomach emptying, which delays glucose absorption.
  • Bring in Aged Cheeses: Grate in some Parmigiano-Reggiano or sharp cheddar. The protein and fat naturally lower the glycemic impact of the flour.
  • Drizzle Real Olive Oil: Always brush your dough with extra virgin olive oil or avocado oil. These fats coat the starch molecules, making them much harder for your digestive enzymes to break down quickly.
A Baker's Note on Hydration
All the recipes in this chapter are designed for a standard "100% hydration" starter. That just means your starter is fed with equal parts flour and water by weight. If your discard has been sitting in the fridge for a week and has a layer of gray liquid on top, don't panic. Just stir that liquid—called hooch—right back in before measuring. It adds a fantastic, sharp tang.

Why Dry Seeds Will Ruin Your Dough

Baking with seeds means you have to throw out your old water calculations. If you toss dry chia or flax seeds straight into your mixing bowl, they will steal every drop of moisture from your flour. The result? A crumbly, dense brick that refuses to rise. I've seen home bakers ruin perfectly good starters this way more times than I can count.

Here is the cheat sheet on how to prep your seeds so they don't bankrupt your dough's water supply:

| Seed Type | Water Absorption Ratio (Seed to Water) | Optimal Prep Method | | :--- | :--- | :--- | | Chia Seeds | 1:3 | Hot soak for 20 minutes to form a gel | | Flax / Linseed | 1:2 | Cold soak overnight or hot soak for 30 minutes | | Hemp Hearts | 1:0 (No soak required) | Add directly to dough during autolyse | | Pumpkin / Sunflower | 1:0.5 | Lightly toast, then cool and soak for 15 minutes |

Soaking your seeds first locks in the moisture they need. This leaves your flour free to absorb its own water and build a strong gluten network.

Early in my career, I tried to make a "super-healthy" flaxseed sourdough for a weekend rush. I was lazy and threw two cups of raw, dry flax seeds straight into the mixer. Nutty Hemp and Chia Sourdough

This is a dense, deeply satisfying loaf packed with healthy fats. The chia seeds create a thick gel that keeps the crumb incredibly moist for days, while the hemp hearts add a rich, buttery taste that reminds me of toasted pine nuts.

Ingredients

  • 350g high-protein bread flour
  • 50g whole wheat flour

  • 15g chia seeds
  • 45ml warm water (for soaking chia)

Instructions

Nigella seeds are potent. Stick to the measurements in the recipe; if you get heavy-handed, they will completely drown out the delicate, sour notes of your bread.

Ancient Spelt and Golden Linseed Batard

Spelt is an ancient grain with a sweet, nutty personality. Pair it with golden linseeds (which are just yellow flaxseeds) and you get a beautiful contrast: a tender, soft interior with a light, seedy crunch.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 10

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 250g high-protein bread flour
  • 150g whole spelt flour
  • 40g golden linseeds
  • 80ml boiling water (for soaking linseeds)
  • 260ml water (for dough)
  • 9g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Toss your golden linseeds into a small bowl and pour the boiling water over them. Stir, cover, and let them sit for 30 minutes. They will turn into a thick, gloopy gel. Let it cool down to room temperature. 2. In a large bowl, mix the starter and 260ml of water. Stir in the bread flour and spelt flour until no dry patches remain. Cover and let it rest for 45 minutes. 3. Add the salt and the cooled linseed mixture (pour in all the gel too) to your dough. Squish and fold it with your hands until it’s fully combined. 4. Spelt gluten is fragile and can get lazy. To keep the loaf from spreading flat, do stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes for the first 2.5 hours to build structure. 5. Let the dough rise undisturbed at room temperature for another 2 hours. 6. Shape the dough into a tight, oval batard. Place it in an oblong basket, cover, and slide it into the fridge for 12 to 16 hours. 7. Heat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (232°C). 8. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment. Hold your razor at a flat 30-degree angle and slash down the length of the loaf to get a beautiful "ear" as it bakes. 9. Bake covered for 25 minutes, then uncover and bake for 20 more minutes until the crust is a deep caramel color. Let it cool on a rack for at least 2 hours.

Baker's Secret

Because spelt gluten is so delicate, be gentle during the final shaping. If you handle it too rough, you will tear the outer skin and lose your oven spring.

Crunchy Poppy and Quinoa Loaf

This recipe uses cooked, cooled quinoa folded right into the dough. It makes the inside of the loaf incredibly soft and moist, while a heavy coat of blue poppy seeds on the crust gives you a satisfying crunch with every bite.

Prep time: 50 mins | Cook time: 50 mins | Servings: 10

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 350g bread flour
  • 50g whole wheat flour
  • 80g cooked quinoa (must be completely cold)
  • 30g blue poppy seeds
  • 280ml water
  • 9g fine sea salt
  • 20g extra poppy seeds (for coating the loaf)

Instructions

1. In a large bowl, mix the starter and water. Stir in the bread flour and whole wheat flour until you have a messy, shaggy dough. Cover and let it rest for 45 minutes. 2. Sprinkle the salt, cold cooked quinoa, and 30g of poppy seeds over the dough. Fold and pinch until the quinoa is distributed evenly throughout. 3. Do four sets of stretch-and-folds during the first 2 hours of bulk fermentation, spacing them 30 minutes apart. Then, let the dough rise quietly for another 3 hours. 4. Shape the dough into a neat, oval batard. 5. Pour the extra poppy seeds onto a flat plate. Spray the top of your shaped loaf with water, then roll it in the seeds until it is evenly coated. 6. Put the loaf seam-side up in a proofing basket, cover, and chill in the fridge for 12 to 14 hours. 7. Heat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (232°C). 8. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment and score a long line down one side. 9. Bake covered in the Dutch oven for 25 minutes. Remove the lid and bake for another 25 minutes. The quinoa adds a lot of moisture, so that extra baking time is necessary to ensure the middle cooks through completely. Cool on a wire rack.

Baker's Secret

Make sure your quinoa is bone-dry and cold before you put it in the dough. Warm or soggy quinoa will break down your gluten and turn your dough into a sticky, unmanageable mess.

Warm Pecan and Flaxseed Sourdough

A deeply savory loaf that pairs the rich, buttery oils of toasted pecans with ground flaxseeds. A pinch of cinnamon and cardamom gives it a warm, cozy smell without adding a single gram of sugar.

Prep time: 40 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 10

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 300g bread flour
  • 100g whole wheat flour
  • 60g raw pecans, chopped and lightly toasted
  • 25g ground flaxseed meal
  • 1/2 tsp ground cinnamon
  • 1/4 tsp ground cardamom
  • 290ml water
  • 9g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Toast the chopped pecans in a dry pan over medium heat for 3 to 4 minutes until they smell rich. Let them cool down completely. 2. In a large bowl, mix the starter, water, bread flour, whole wheat flour, and flaxseed meal into a rough dough. Cover and let it autolyse for 40 minutes. 3. Add the salt, cooled toasted pecans, cinnamon, and cardamom. Fold and squeeze until the nuts and spices are mixed in. 4. Do stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours. The ground flaxseed will soak up water quickly, making the dough feel strong and easy to handle early on. 5. Let the dough rise quietly at room temperature for another 3 hours. 6. Shape the dough into a tight round ball and put it seam-side up in a floured basket. Cover and refrigerate for 14 hours. 7. Heat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (232°C). 8. Flip the cold dough onto parchment. Score a classic cross or crescent on top with a razor. 9. Bake covered for 25 minutes. Uncover and bake for another 20 minutes until the crust is dark gold and your kitchen smells like toasted nuts. Let it cool completely before slicing.

Baker's Secret

Always use ground flaxseed meal here rather than whole flaxseeds. The ground meal absorbs water evenly, which gives you a softer crumb and keeps the bread fresh for days longer.

Savory Walnut and Fenugreek Loaf

This one is for the adventurous baker. We're matching the bitter crunch of toasted walnuts with ground fenugreek, which has a distinct, maple-syrup-like aroma. We also slide a bit of chickpea flour into the mix to bump up the protein.

Prep time: 40 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 10

Ingredients

  • 100g active sourdough starter (100% hydration)
  • 320g bread flour
  • 80g chickpea flour (besan)
  • 60g walnuts, chopped and lightly toasted
  • 1/2 tsp ground fenugreek seeds
  • 300ml water
  • 9g fine sea salt

Instructions

1. Toast your chopped walnuts in a dry pan for 5 minutes until warm and fragrant. Let them cool completely. 2. Whisk the starter, water, ground fenugreek, bread flour, and chickpea flour together in a large bowl. Mix until you have a shaggy dough. Cover and let it autolyse for 45 minutes. 3. Add the salt and toasted walnuts. Knead gently right in the bowl for about 2 minutes to distribute the walnuts. 4. Do stretch-and-folds every 30 minutes for the first 2 hours. Make sure to scoop deep down to the bottom of the bowl to keep those heavy walnuts from settling at the base of your dough. 5. Let the dough rise quietly at room temperature for another 3 hours. 6. Gently shape the dough into a tight oval batard and put it seam-side up in a floured basket. Cover and chill in the fridge for 12 to 16 hours. 7. Heat your oven and Dutch oven to 450°F (232°C) for at least 45 minutes. 8. Turn the cold dough out onto parchment, make one long cut down the middle, and slide it into the hot Dutch oven. 9. Bake covered for 25 minutes. Take the lid off and bake for another 20 minutes until the loaf is deeply browned and sounds hollow when you tap the bottom. Let it cool on a wire rack before you cut a slice.

Baker's Secret

To keep your bread from turning a muddy purple color, give your chopped walnuts a quick rinse under cold water, then dry and toast them before they go into the dough.

Avocado and Cilantro Lime Smash

Ingredients

Cultured Herb and Grass-Fed Garlic Butter

Ingredients

  • 2 tbsp fresh flat-leaf parsley, minced

Sugar-Free Chia Seed and Blackberry Jam

Store-bought jam is basically liquid sugar. Even the organic brands spike your blood sugar before you finish your first cup of coffee. This version uses chia seeds to gel naturally, while blackberries offer a tart flavor and plenty of antioxidants.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 16 (1 tbsp per serving)

Ingredients

  • 300g (2.5 cups) fresh or frozen blackberries
  • 2 tbsp black chia seeds
  • 3 tbsp powdered allulose or erythritol
  • 1 tbsp fresh lemon juice
  • 2 tbsp water

Instructions

1. Combine the blackberries, sweetener, and water in a medium saucepan over medium heat. 2. Cook for 8 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally. Once the berries soften and release their juices, use a wooden spoon to mash them up. 3. Stir in the lemon juice and chia seeds. Turn the heat to low and simmer for 5 minutes, stirring constantly so it does not burn. 4. Remove from the heat and let it sit for 10 minutes. The chia seeds will plump up and naturally thicken the jam as it cools. 5. Spoon into a clean glass jar, cover tightly, and refrigerate for 2 hours before using.

The Takeaway

If you hate seeds in your teeth, run the warm berry mixture through a blender before you stir in the chia seeds for a smoother finish.

Smoky Roasted Red Pepper and Walnut Dip (Muhammara)

This rich, nutty dip offers a savory, smoky flavor that cuts through the sour notes of your bread. It is packed with healthy fats and contains zero added sugars.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 large red bell peppers (or 300g jarred roasted red peppers, drained)
  • 120g (1 cup) raw walnut halves
  • 2 garlic cloves, peeled
  • 1.5 tbsp lemon juice
  • 1 tsp ground cumin
  • 0.5 tsp smoked paprika
  • 3 tbsp extra virgin olive oil
  • 0.5 tsp sea salt

Instructions

1. If using fresh peppers, roast them under a broiler until charred. Steam them in a bowl covered with plastic wrap for 10 minutes, then peel off the skin and discard the seeds. 2. Toast the walnuts in a dry skillet over medium heat for 3 to 5 minutes until they smell rich and look golden. Keep moving them so they do not burn. 3. Toss the toasted walnuts and garlic into a food processor and pulse until finely ground. 4. Add the roasted peppers, lemon juice, cumin, smoked paprika, and salt. 5. Process the mix while slowly pouring in the olive oil until you have a thick, textured paste.

The Takeaway

Classic versions of this dip use pomegranate molasses, which is loaded with sugar. We swap that out for fresh lemon juice and cumin to keep your glucose flat while keeping that signature tang.

Tangy Lemon Dill Labneh

If you have never tried labneh, think of it as Greek yogurt’s thicker, richer cousin. It is a strained cheese that gives you a high-protein, probiotic-packed base for your morning toast.

Prep time: 10 mins (plus 24 hours straining) | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 8

Ingredients

  • 500g (2 cups) plain, full-fat Greek yogurt
  • 1 tsp lemon zest, finely grated
  • 2 tbsp fresh dill, finely chopped
  • 0.5 tsp sea salt
  • 2 tbsp extra virgin olive oil (for serving)

Instructions

1. Stir the Greek yogurt, lemon zest, and salt together in a medium bowl. 2. Line a strainer with a double layer of cheesecloth and set it over a deep bowl. 3. Spoon the yogurt into the cheesecloth, gather up the corners, and tie them tightly with kitchen twine. 4. Let it drain in the fridge for 18 to 24 hours. The longer you wait, the thicker and more spreadable it gets. 5. Unwrap the labneh, toss the liquid whey, and put the cheese in a bowl. Stir in the fresh dill and top with a generous pool of olive oil.

The Takeaway

Don't throw away that acidic whey liquid from the bottom of the bowl. Use it instead of water in your next sourdough bake to get an incredibly tender crumb.

Creamy Almond and Macadamia Butter

Skip the peanut butter. This blend of almonds and macadamias is rich, velvety, and loaded with healthy fats. It creates a massive buffer against glucose spikes, turning your morning slice into slow-release fuel.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 12 (2 tbsp per serving)

Ingredients

  • 150g (1 cup) raw almonds
  • 150g (1 cup) raw macadamia nuts
  • 0.5 tsp flaky sea salt
  • 0.25 tsp vanilla bean powder

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Spread the almonds and macadamias on a baking sheet. 2. Roast for 8 to 10 minutes, stirring halfway through, until they smell fragrant and toast slightly. The Takeaway

Do not add oil or water to speed things up. Warm roasted nuts release their own oils as they blend. Patience is your only ingredient here.
