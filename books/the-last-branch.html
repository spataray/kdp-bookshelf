    The Last Branch: A Forensic Genealogy Thriller

    Chapter 1: The Shared Chromosome

    The air in the basement lab of the State Forensic Science Unit always tasted like stale ozone and dry ice. It was a concrete tomb, really, insulated just enough to drown out the relentless hammering of the Oregon rain. It had been pouring for three straight weeks. The kind of wet that gets inside your bones and stays there until July.

    Under the twin halogen spotlights of her stereo microscope, the plastic microcentrifuge tube looked like a drop of amber resin. Inside it lay everything left of Cynthia Rook. Just a tiny, gray-white pellet of sediment, scraped from beneath the torn edge of the young woman’s left ring finger.

    The evidence envelope had arrived with Hector MacInnis’s signature gouged into the cardboard. Hector never just signed his name; he used his pen like a weapon, tearing into the paper. Rosalind could still smell the faint, sad trace of his wintergreen mints and stale rolling tobacco on the packaging. It was the scent of a midnight panic call.

    “Just give me a ghost, Rosalind,” his voice had rumbled through her voicemail six hours ago, thick with the gravel of a man who’d been living on highway coffee and pure desperation since they dragged Cynthia’s body out of the drainage ditch near the old Carrow timber bounds. “The brass is already talking about calling in the Bureau. Give me a name before they lock us out.”

    Rosalind adjusted the focus. Her gloved finger clicked against the pipette. The silence in the basement was absolute, save for the low, rhythmic hum of the MiSeq sequencer in the corner—seventy thousand dollars of high-grade plastic and lasers designed to translate human tragedy into strings of digital code.

    She’d spent forty-eight straight hours trying to coax something—anything—out of Cynthia Rook’s final moments. It was brutal work. The DNA was shredded, chewed up by the acidic forest soil and the constant wash of the rain. Traditional CODIS profiling? Useless. The short tandem repeats were too degraded, leaving nothing but a jagged, incomplete strip of markers. No semen. No saliva. No blood. Just those tiny flecks of skin under Cynthia’s fingernails, scraped loose when she fought for her life in the wet ferns.

    But Rosalind didn’t play in the state’s shallow genetic pool. She was a forensic genealogist. While the state police looked at twenty specific markers, she hunted for Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms. SNPs. Nearly eight hundred thousand individual variations scattered across the vast, messy map of the human genome.

    She turned to her monitor. The run was done. The raw data had been scrubbed, filtered, and converted into a clean text file.

    Her hand hovered over the mouse. On her second screen, the digital whiteboard displayed the sprawling, chaotic family tree of the Carrow clan. For three weeks, she’d been building this monster branch by branch, convinced the Highway 9 killer belonged somewhere in its shade. The geography was too neat. The five victims had all been dumped within a ten-mile radius of the crumbling estate built by old Alistair Carrow.

    Alistair had been one of those old-school timber barons who ruled the valley like a feudal lord, leaving behind nothing but clear-cut hillsides, poisoned soil, and a pack of bitter descendants. The living ones—Phineas and his sister Tamsin—still huddled in the decaying family manor on the ridge, watching the hemlocks reclaim the lawn. Phineas was a recluse who hadn’t been seen in town since the nineties. Tamsin was a bitter, sharp-tongued matriarch spending her twilight years selling off parcels of timberland just to pay the property taxes.

    Rosalind had spent her nights digging through digital graves—census records, digitized obituaries, marriage certificates from the turn of the last century—trying to find a genetic bridge. Some distant cousin or half-brother who shared enough blood with the killer to give him a name.

    She caught her reflection in the dark window of her office. A gray smudge on the double-paned glass marked where she’d rested her forehead during the worst of the 3:00 AM slump. She looked away, her eyes dropping to the browser tab for VeritasGene, the public database.

    Millions of profiles. People looking for their real fathers, or trying to trace their lineage back to some long-lost European village. And, for people like Rosalind with the right judicial warrants, it was a hunting ground.

    She clicked Upload.

    The progress bar crept across the screen. 12%... 34%...

    Rosalind leaned back, her office chair groaning. Her mind drifted, as it always did when the waiting started, back to the drafty house in eastern Oregon where she’d grown up. The quiet there had been different. Not sterile, like this lab, but heavy. Smothering. Her adoptive parents were decent, silent people who smelled of starch and old church hymnals, but they treated her origin like a secret disease. They gave her a home, a library card, and a debt she could never repay. When they died during her second year of grad school, they left her a single steel lockbox. Inside was her adoption decree.

    Single female infant, the paper said. Parentage unknown.

    It was a cruel irony, wasn't it? She spent her life rebuilding the broken families of strangers. She found sisters for crying middle-aged men and named the bones of girls left in the desert. Yet her own tree was a single, dead stump. She’d never uploaded her own DNA to a public database. The fear was too specific, too cold: the fear of finding absolutely nothing. Or worse, finding exactly what she came from.

    The sequencer beeped—a sharp, high-pitched chirp that signaled the end of the cooling cycle.

    Rosalind snapped back. The progress bar was gone. The VeritasGene portal had refreshed, displaying the matches for the unknown profile from Cynthia Rook’s nails.

    Normally, this was a slow, frustrating game of inches. You’d get a list of fourth cousins in Ohio sharing 15 centimorgans of DNA. Maybe a third cousin in Alberta sharing 32. From those tiny crumbs of genetic bread, you had to build massive trees, working backward through centuries of census data to find where the lines crossed.

    She looked at the top of the list.

    There was no fourth cousin.

    At the very top of the screen, highlighted in a pale, warning amber reserved for immediate family, was a single entry.

    Relationship: Full Sibling Shared DNA: 2,650.4 cM Shared Segments: 48 Longest Block: 112.3 cM

    The air left her lungs in a quiet, ragged gasp. The lab felt suddenly pressurized, like her ears were about to pop.

    "No," she said. It didn't sound like her voice. Just a dry rasp.

    She leaned in, her eyes burning as she stared at the numbers. In forensic genealogy, the centimorgan is the ultimate truth. Identical twins share about 3,400. Parents and children share the same, but with a different biological signature.

    A share of 2,650 centimorgans, spread across forty-eight distinct segments, with massive, unbroken blocks of identical DNA on every single autosome, meant only one thing.

    A biological brother.

    She looked at the username associated with the match: CTree88. The email was an encrypted ProtonMail address.

    Her hand shook so violently she could barely guide the cursor to the Chromosome Browser tool. This was the visual map of the match, showing the shared segments across the twenty-two human chromosomes.

    The screen filled with twenty-two horizontal bars. They were painted in thick, overlapping bands of yellow and blue. These weren't the tiny, fragmented splinters of shared ancestry from some great-great-grandfather who died in the Civil War. These were massive, solid ribbons of shared life. The killer and CTree88 had drawn from the exact same genetic well. Same mother. Same father.

    Rosalind let go of the mouse. Her hand fell to her lap like a dead weight.

    The database didn't have the killer's profile until she uploaded it five minutes ago. The match wasn't comparing the killer to some stranger. It was comparing the killer's DNA to a profile that had already been sitting in the system for years.

    She looked at the account details for CTree88.

    Registered three years ago. IP address in Washington.

    And then she saw the kit number.

    A cold weight dropped into her stomach. She knew that number. She didn't need to look it up, but her brain, desperate for any escape hatch, forced her to prove it.

    She stood up, her chair rolling backward and slamming into the metal leg of the workbench. She stumbled to her filing cabinet, her heart hammering a wild, erratic beat against her ribs.

    She yanked open the drawer labeled Personal. She bypassed her degrees, her mortgage paperwork, and grabbed the small, sealed envelope containing her own raw genetic data. She’d run it three years ago at a private lab, keeping the file on an encrypted flash drive in her desk, never quite brave enough to upload it to the public servers.

    But she had used it once.

    Three years ago, during a pilot study for the state's cold case unit, she’d needed to test the database’s privacy firewalls. She’d uploaded a control profile under a pseudonym to see if the system's security protocols held up. She had used her own raw data.

    She had named the kit CTree88.

    The room seemed to tilt. The fluorescent lights overhead hummed with a sudden, deafening frequency.

    The DNA from Cynthia Rook’s fingernails—the skin of the man who had choked the life out of her—shared 2,650 centimorgans with CTree88.

    With her.

    The Highway 9 Killer was her brother.

    She backed up until her spine hit the hard edge of the workbench. A glass beaker rattled in its metal rack, a tiny, fragile sound in the silence.

    Single female infant. No other issue.

    The state of Oregon had lied. Or her mother had kept one child and thrown the other away. Or maybe they had abandoned both of them, casting them out into different corners of this damp, dark country, where one grew up to read the code of life, and the other grew up to destroy it.

    Her phone began to vibrate on the desk. The sharp, mechanical buzz sounded like a chainsaw in the quiet room.

    She didn't move. She just watched the phone slide a fraction of an inch across the polished wood. Hector MacInnis flashed on the screen.

    If she picked up, what was she going to tell him? Hector, I found your ghost. He's in my veins.

    She looked back at the screen. The yellow and blue bands of the chromosome browser looked like a mockery. A map of her own identity, split in half and handed to a monster.

    Then her eyes drifted to the Carrow family tree on the whiteboard.

    Alistair. Phineas. Tamsin.

    Why had she been so obsessed with them? Was it really just a professional hunch? Or was it something deeper? A phantom memory? The scent of cedar and damp wool from a bedroom she couldn't remember?

    She dragged herself back to the desk, her knees feeling like wet cardboard. Ignoring the vibrating phone, she ran a triangulation of the shared matches between herself, the killer, and the rest of the database.

    The screen spun for three agonizing seconds.

    When the page refreshed, three other matches appeared, sharing smaller, distinct segments of DNA with both her and the killer.

    Match: PCarrowEst Shared DNA: 412.1 cM Predicted Relationship: First Cousin / Half-Uncle

    Rosalind forgot how to breathe.

    PCarrowEst. Phineas Carrow.

    The connection was real. The Carrow family wasn’t a lead she’d built out of geographic convenience. They were her family.

    She was a Carrow. And so was the man who had dragged Cynthia Rook into the mud.

    She looked down at her hands. The exact same hands she’d used to extract the DNA from the evidence tube. Hands that shared their shape, their nails, and their genetic blueprint with the person who had wrapped them around Cynthia's neck.

    The phone stopped vibrating. The lab fell back into its cold, heavy silence.

    On her desk, the crime scene photo of Cynthia Rook’s hand stared up at her. The nails were short, torn, caked with the dark, peaty mud of the highway shoulder.

    And beneath those nails, her own blood.

    Rosalind reached out, her hand shaking, and closed the browser. The screen went black, reflecting her own pale face in the glass. Behind her, the door to the lab remained locked, but she knew she wasn’t alone anymore. The shadow of her brother had just stepped into the room.

    Chapter 2: Lies in the Blood

    The sequencer didn’t hum. It vibrated—a low, rhythmic rattle that got right into the marrow of Rosalind Mercer’s teeth and stayed there. In the sterile, over-cooled vacuum of the state forensic genealogy lab, that deep vibration was the only real proof that time was actually moving.

    Outside, a relentless November rain smeared the high windows, blurring the Boston skyline into a bruised, shifting watercolor of charcoal and violet. Inside, the air was different. It smelled of sharp ozone, isopropyl alcohol, and that faint, sweet undercurrent of bleach that never quite leaves a place like this, no matter how hard you scrub.

    Rosalind stared at the monitor. The blue glare reflected off her safety glasses, casting two pale, glowing boxes over her eyes. On screen, the raw sequence data for the Cynthia Rook homicide was still rendering. It looked like a jagged mountain range of green, red, and blue peaks—the final, desperate testament of a murdered woman, reduced to binary code.

    Then came the knock. Heavy, sudden, and loud enough to rattle the reinforced glass door of the cleanroom.

    Rosalind went rigid. Her fingers hovered over the keyboard, ready to slam the shortcut to minimize the screen.

    Through the double-paned glass, she saw Detective Hector MacInnis.

    He looked like a man who had spent the last forty-eight hours breathing nothing but cruiser exhaust and terrible diner coffee. His dark wool overcoat was damp, the shoulders soaked nearly black by the rain, and his tie was pulled loose. The salt-and-pepper hair at his temples—a premature dusting that had gotten noticeably heavier since they had called off their engagement eighteen months ago—was plastered to his forehead. He didn’t bother waiting for her to buzz him in. He used his swipe card. The sharp, plastic clack of the lock felt like a physical intrusion in her clean space.

    "Rosalind," he said. His voice had the gravelly, raw edge of pure exhaustion.

    "Hector." She stepped back from her desk, crossing her arms to hide the slight, betraying tremor in her fingers. "You know you aren’t supposed to be back here without an escort. The chain of custody protocols—"

    "I don't give a damn about the protocols tonight, and neither does the commissioner," Hector cut her off, his boots squeaking on the pristine vinyl floor as he stepped deeper into the lab. He smelled of cold rain, wet leather, and the bitter dregs of stale tobacco. "The Rook family just held a press conference. The mayor was right there on stage with them. They’re calling Cynthia’s death a failure of state security. I need a name, Rose. I need a face I can put on a warrant. Today."

    Rosalind’s stomach twisted.

    This was the moment she always dreaded. The "Ghost" of her own past—the quiet, terrifying knowledge that she was building a career on a foundation of carefully guarded secrets—reared its head. She had made a name for herself by being the absolute best, the only forensic genealogist who could coax a profile out of a degraded, contaminated sample when the state crime lab gave up. But looking at Hector now, she felt the thin ice beneath her feet beginning to crack.

    "DNA isn't a vending machine, Hector," she said, keeping her voice flat, cool, and dry. "You don't just slide in a cartridge and get a mugshot. The extraction from the victim's fingernail scrapings was highly degraded. It’s a mess."

    Hector stepped closer, his heavy shadow falling over her desk. He leaned against the edge of the workstation, his hand leaving a faint, smeary print on the glass partition. "The DA is threatening to pull our entire investigative funding if we don't have a solid lead by the weekend. You told me this new high-throughput platform could build a family tree from a single skin cell if it had to. Tell me you have something."

    A cold spike of panic flared in her chest. She couldn't tell him the truth. Not yet.

    The initial run of the suspect's DNA had matched something she hadn't expected—an anomalous, jagged familial link that touched way too close to her own private, unauthorized research. If she handed over that pedigree chart now, Hector would do his job. He would follow the branches of the tree, and those branches would lead straight to a past she had spent her entire adult life trying to bury. Her career, her reputation, her identity—gone.

    She had to buy herself some time.

    "I don't have a pedigree chart for you," she lied. Her voice remained steady, even though her heart was hammering against her ribs. "The crime scene sample yielded an incredibly low call-rate. It’s sitting at barely seventy percent."

    Hector frowned, his brow furrowing as he tried to translate the lab-speak. "Meaning what, exactly?"

    "It means the machine couldn't read thirty percent of the single nucleotide polymorphisms," she said, leaning back and gesturing to the screen, where she had quickly pulled up a generic, mock-up run of an unrelated case from three months ago. "The data is too noisy. If I try to upload this to the public databases now, we’ll get thousands of false-positive matches. It’ll point us toward every third cousin in the tri-state area."

    "So, what's the fix?" Hector’s eyes narrowed, studying her face.

    He knew her tells. Or, at least, he used to. He was looking for the subtle twitch in her jaw, the slight shift in her posture, the way she avoided direct eye contact when she was holding back.

    Rosalind forced herself to look right into his gray, bloodshot eyes. "I have to perform a manual phase-alignment. I need to separate the maternal and paternal alleles manually to clean up the noise before I run the triangulation algorithms. It’s painstaking work, Hector. It takes time."

    "How much time?"

    "Forty-eight hours. At least."

    Hector swore under his breath, turning away to pace the narrow aisle between the massive sequencers. His heavy footsteps echoed off the metal cabinets. He stopped, staring at his own reflection in the darkened glass before turning back to her.

    "You're stalling, Rose," he said softly. The old nickname slipped out, heavy with history. "Why do I feel like you're keeping something from me?"

    "I’m keeping you from making a fool of yourself by arresting the wrong third cousin of a high-profile victim," she shot back. The subtext was sharp as a scalpel: Leave me alone. Let me do this. Don't dig. "Go home, Hector. Get some sleep. Let me do my job."

    He stared at her for a long, agonizing moment. The silence between them was thick with everything they had never managed to say—the broken promises, the shared apartment they had emptied out box by box, the quiet that had grown between them until it became a wall.

    Finally, he let out a long, defeated sigh. "Twenty-four hours, Rosalind. That’s all I can give you before the captain takes this out of my hands and hands it over to the FBI’s regional unit. If they get it, we both lose control of the narrative."

    "I'll have something for you," she said.

    He gave her a single, curt nod and turned on his heel. The heavy glass door clicked shut behind him, the squeak of his boots fading down the long, sterile corridor until the low rumble of the sequencers reclaimed the room.

    Rosalind sank into her chair, her knees shaking. She waited five full minutes, counting the seconds by the rhythmic flashing of the sequencer’s status light, before she walked over and locked the door from the inside.

    Now, she was completely alone.

    She sat back down at her terminal, her fingers flying across the keys. She closed the decoy window and pulled up the raw FASTQ file for the Rook suspect.

    The real data flooded her screen.

    The call-rate wasn't seventy percent. It was a pristine ninety-nine point eight percent. The extraction had been flawless, the DNA beautifully preserved in the deep freeze of the morgue. She had just lied to the lead detective on a high-profile homicide investigation—a federal offense if anyone ever found out.

    But she didn't have a choice.

    Rosalind opened her proprietary triangulation software. It was her own creation, a program she had coded specifically to run deep-level familial searches across multiple public-access genetic databases. She uploaded the suspect’s genomic profile and watched as the system began to cluster the matches.

    Almost immediately, a red warning box popped up on the screen: High rate of mixed calls on X-chromosome. Potential contamination or structural variation.

    Rosalind frowned, leaning closer. A "mixed call" meant the sequencing software was reading more than one nucleotide at heterozygous sites on the X-chromosome—almost as if there were two distinct maternal profiles overlapping in the exact same sample.

    "Degradation," she muttered to herself, her voice a dry whisper in the quiet room.

    It was the only logical explanation. The sample must have been contaminated at the crime scene, or perhaps the enzyme buffer had broken down during amplification. It was a common artifact in older, poorly preserved forensic samples, though highly unusual for a fresh extraction. She brushed the warning aside, forcing the software to bypass the X-chromosome anomaly and focus on the autosomes—the twenty-two pairs of non-sex chromosomes that would yield the true pedigree.

    The software began to map the matching segments, measuring them in centimorgans.

    As the digital branches began to form on her screen, Rosalind felt the blood drain from her face.

    The suspect's profile didn't just match a random family. It clustered heavily with a specific genetic signature she had seen once before—in her own private database. The one where she kept her own genetic profile, the one she had uploaded under a pseudonym to search for her biological family.

    The suspect shared a massive, three-hundred-centimorgan segment of DNA with her.

    In genetic terms, three hundred centimorgans wasn't a distant relative. It was a first cousin, a great-uncle, or a half-sibling. The killer of Cynthia Rook was a direct branch on Rosalind's own family tree.

    Her hands began to sweat inside her nitrile gloves. The sterile walls of the lab felt like they were closing in, the humming sequencers sounding less like machines and more like a chorus of accusing voices.

    She had to trace her own lineage. She had to know who this was before Hector did.

    She opened her private project file, the one labeled M-01-RECON. For years, she had been quietly assembling her own maternal tree, trying to piece together the fragments of an identity she had never been allowed to own. She had been adopted at birth, her birth certificate a sanitized document of state-approved lies.

    She initiated a maternal lineage search, tracing the mitochondrial DNA and the autosomal matches she had painstakingly verified over the last decade. She watched the digital lines crawl backward through time, bypassing the false leads and the dead ends she had spent years clearing away.

    The software narrowed the search down to a single, localized cluster in Western Massachusetts.

    A name materialized at the root of the branch.

    Zelda Carrow.

    Rosalind’s breath caught. She stared at the name, her mind racing.

    She opened a secure browser, bypassing the state network through a virtual private network she had installed for her private searches. She typed the name into the historical archives: Zelda Carrow, Massachusetts.

    A series of digitized newspaper clippings and public records began to populate the screen.

    Zelda Carrow had not lived a quiet life. According to a clipping from the Berkshire Eagle dated August 14, 1995, Zelda had died at the age of forty-two.

    But she hadn't died in a hospital or in her own bed.

    She had died inside the Whispering Pines Psychiatric Center, a notorious state-run facility nestled in the Berkshire hills. The facility had been shuttered in the late nineties following a series of state investigations into patient abuse, medical neglect, and the mysterious disappearance of several residents.

    Rosalind clicked on the death record index. Zelda’s cause of death was listed as cardiopulmonary arrest, a generic clinical euphemism that often hid more sinister realities in institutions of that era.

    But it was the next document that made her heart stop.

    It was a probate court index entry from the county courthouse, dated three months before Zelda’s death in 1995.

    In Re: Adoption of Female Infant Carrow.

    The record was heavily redacted. The name of the infant, the adoptive parents, and the biological father were all blacked out, sealed by a judge's order under a defunct state privacy statute.

    Rosalind stared at the court docket number.

    #95-AD-0442.

    She knew that number. She didn't need to look at her locked drawer at home to verify it. It was the same docket number stamped on the corner of the faded, water-damaged page her adoptive mother had handed her on her twenty-first birthday—the only piece of her biological history she had ever possessed.

    Zelda Carrow was her biological mother.

    And the suspect in the Cynthia Rook homicide—the killer whose DNA was currently sitting in the sequencer behind her—was directly related to Zelda.

    Alistair Carrow? Phineas Carrow? Tamsin Carrow?

    Rosalind stared at the screen, her mind spinning as she tried to make sense of the names that were now populating the Carrow family tree on her monitor. The names were old, rooted in the stony soil of New England, but they were no longer just historical data points. One of them was a killer. And one of them was her family.

    She leaned forward, her forehead resting against the cool, plastic edge of the monitor casing. The sterile, cold air of the lab seemed to leak into her chest, freezing her from the inside out.

    She had twenty-four hours before Hector took the case away. Twenty-four hours to untangle a web of blood, madness, and sealed records.

    If she told him the truth, she would lose everything.

    If she kept the secret, a killer would walk free, and the blood of Cynthia Rook would remain on her hands.

    Rosalind sat up, her eyes hardening as she looked at the blinking cursor on her screen. She clicked on the anomaly warning again, staring at the mixed calls on the X-chromosome.

    There was a story in that noise. A story written in the blood. And she was the only one who could read it.

    Chapter 3: The Taxidermist's Cabin

    The foothills of the Cascades didn’t just hold water. They digested things.

    Rosalind Mercer kept her hands locked at ten and two, her knuckles white against the cheap leather of her Subaru’s steering wheel as the gravel road dissolved into a red clay soup. It was the kind of road that felt less like a route and more like a trap. Thirty miles east of the interstate, the canopy closed over her like a damp, green fist. The rain had finally stopped its steady hammering, but it hadn't really gone anywhere; it had just settled into a thick, greasy mist that clung to her windshield like sweat. Through the floor vents, the car breathed in the stench of wet hemlock, cold granite, and the sharp, vinegar tang of wood rot.

    She killed the engine forty yards from the gate.

    The silence that followed was immediate and suffocating. The only sound was the heavy, rhythmic plip-plop of water dropping from the fir boughs onto her hood.

    Rosalind didn't get out right away. She sat there, her thumb tracing the laminated edge of the press pass in her pocket. It was a forgery, of course. A beautiful one, run off on a high-end card printer by a former colleague from her days in the district attorney’s database unit.

    Tressa.

    The name hit her like a cold draft under an old door. Three years ago, Tressa’s DNA profile had sat on Rosalind’s desk in a sterile manila folder. Just a string of digital alleles. A code waiting for a match. Rosalind had hesitated, playing by the rules, waiting for some county administrator to sign off on the three-hundred-dollar budget line for a deep-dive genealogical search. By the time the paperwork cleared, they were pulling Tressa out of a sandbar near the Columbia River, her throat packed with river silt.

    Rosalind let go of the card. She wasn't waiting for permission this time. The Highway 9 killer had a twenty-year head start, and the branches of the Carrow family tree were rotting off one by one.

    She pushed the door open. The mud clawed at the soles of her hiking boots with a wet, greedy suction.

    The cabin sat in a small clearing where the daylight seemed to curdle into a dull grey. It was a low-slung, miserable thing built of hand-hewn cedar logs, its roof shingles green with a carpet of moss thick enough to grow ferns. On the front porch, a row of bleached deer skulls hung from the eaves by rusty wire, their empty snout cavities pointed down toward the porch boards like pale, silent bells.

    She climbed the three wooden steps. The second tread didn't just creak; it shrieked—a high, dry scream of ungreased timber.

    Before her knuckles could touch the weathered hemlock of the door, it swung inward.

    Phineas Carrow didn’t look like a man who had survived eighty winters in these mountains. He looked like something the mountains had chewed up and decided not to swallow. He was tall, stooped, and wrapped in a flannel shirt so faded the plaid had worn down to a grimy, uniform grey. His face was a map of dry gullies, but behind his wire-rimmed spectacles—thick enough to make his pupils look like black flies floating in water—his eyes were sharp, damp, and hostile.

    "We don't get lost up here," Phineas said. His voice had the dry, scraping rattle of dead leaves being dragged across asphalt. "Not unless we’re looking for something we shouldn't be."

    Rosalind forced her shoulders to drop. She gave him the smile she’d practiced in the rearview mirror—the one that looked academic, harmless, and slightly dull.

    "Mr. Carrow? I'm Rosalind Mercer. I'm working with the Oregon Historical Society. We're doing a survey of the early homesteads in the Clackamas basin, and your family—specifically Alistair and Tamsin Carrow—built the first mill race down near the creek."

    Phineas didn't blink. His right hand was buried deep in his trousers pocket, but his left hung loose at his side. His thumb was twitching. Tap-tap-tap. A frantic, rhythmic ticking against his index finger, like a telegraph key sending a message in a language everyone else had forgotten.

    "Alistair’s been in the ground since sixty-four," Phineas said. "Tamsin went five years after. There’s nothing here but their trash."

    "Sometimes the trash is where the real history is," Rosalind said softly. She kept her eyes locked on his, resisting the urge to peer past his shoulder into the darkness of the cabin. "I'm tracing the collateral branches. I want to make sure the family's contribution to the valley isn't lost."

    The old man stared. A sudden gust of wind rattled the canopy, dumping a cold shower of rain off the roof right onto the back of Rosalind’s neck. She didn't flinch.

    Slowly, Phineas stepped back. He didn't invite her in, but he left the door open. It was a dare.

    She stepped inside. The cabin smelled of things that had died decades ago but had been chemically forced to pretend they were still here. It was a suffocating mix of borax, turpentine, and white arsenic, cut by the greasy, heavy scent of mutton tallow.

    Against the far wall, a half-finished bobcat sat on a workbench. Its raw skin was pinned to a papier-mâché form, the stitches along its spine thick, black, and crude. One of its amber glass eyes was missing, leaving a dark, empty socket that seemed to watch her with far more intensity than the glass one.

    "Don't touch the skins," Phineas barked, shuffling toward a small, paper-cluttered desk near the woodstove. "They're salted. Get that salt in a cut and you'll be screaming for a week."

    "I won't," Rosalind said.

    She stayed on her feet, her eyes moving across the room with a quick, forensic greed. A row of heavy skinning knives with custom antler handles hung from a magnetic strip over the sink. The blades were spotless, but the horn handle of the smallest knife was stained near the brass guard with a dark, iron-rich varnish that had sunk deep into the porous bone.

    A red herring? Maybe. A man who had skinned deer for fifty years was bound to have blood on his tools. It didn't mean he’d used them on a twenty-year-old girl found by the highway.

    Then her gaze hit the desk.

    Sitting between a rusted box of carpet tacks and a half-empty bottle of mineral spirits was a small mason jar. Inside, mixed in with brass wood screws and old fishhooks, was a single, tarnished silver cufflink.

    Rosalind’s chest tightened, but she didn't let her breathing change.

    She knew that cufflink.

    In the state police archives, there was an image—magnified three hundred times under an electron microscope—of a tiny, metallic thread found in the woolen collar of the third victim on Highway 9. The trace team had identified it as a rare, silver-plated copper wire used in high-end European jewelry from the 1950s, braided in a unique, triple-loop pattern.

    The cufflink in the jar was an ornate silver square, its face stamped with a crest she couldn't quite read from five feet away, but the border—the border was a triple-loop braid of silver wire.

    "You look like you've seen a ghost, girl," Phineas said. He had dropped into a creaking wicker chair, his twitching hand now resting on his knee. He kept up the rhythm. Tap-tap-tap.

    "I’m just... admiring the work," Rosalind lied, gesturing vaguely toward the bobcat. "It takes an incredible amount of patience to make something look alive after it’s been taken apart."

    "It's not about patience," Phineas said, his dry lips curling back over yellowed teeth. "It's about knowing where the skin stretches. You pull too hard, it rips. You don't pull hard enough, it sags and rots from the inside out. You have to know the tension of the thing."

    Rosalind took a half-step closer to the desk, her boots silent on the bare fir floorboards. "I noticed you have some old family papers here. Did Alistair keep records of the property lines?"

    "Alistair kept records of everything he owned. And some things he didn't." Phineas reached out and tapped the glass jar. The silver cufflink clinked against the side. The sound was high and clean. "Is that what your history society wants? To find out who owned the dirt under the highway?"

    "We're interested in the people," Rosalind said. She leaned slightly against the edge of the desk, her coat brushing a dusty stack of old newspapers. "Your sister, Zelda. The records from the county courthouse show she lived here until seventy-two. But after that, her paper trail just... ends."

    The tapping stopped. The silence in the cabin grew thick, heavy with the smell of old formaldehyde.

    "Zelda’s none of your business," he said. The rattle in his voice had turned to gravel.

    "I’m just trying to complete the tree, Mr. Carrow. It's for the historical record. If she moved out of state, or if she—"

    "She didn't move out of state," Phineas spat. He stood up, his long, awkward frame casting a jagged shadow across the woodstove. "She stayed right here in this valley. Until she died. But she didn't leave anything behind that you’d want to put in your little book."

    Rosalind took a slow, deep breath, anchoring her heels to the floor. "Sometimes there are things that don't make it into the official county registries. A child, perhaps? Someone who might still have a claim to the old homestead?"

    Phineas walked toward her. His boots thudded against the floor with a slow, heavy weight. He stopped a foot away. He smelled of old tobacco, woodsmoke, and dry rot.

    "You think you’re clever, don't you?" he whispered. His watery eyes were wide, the pupils dilated until they looked almost entirely black. "Coming up here with your historical society talk. You’re not the first person to ask about Zelda. There was a man. Years ago. A man with a notebook and a face like a shovel. He wanted to know about her boys."

    Rosalind’s heart slammed against her ribs. She kept her voice dead level. "Her boys?"

    "She only had one," Phineas said, his voice dropping to a harsh, wet murmur. "A mistake. That’s what Alistair called him. A sin born in the brush. She had him down in Portland, in one of those homes for fallen girls. Signed him away to the state before the cord was even dry. Alistair made sure of it. Said we didn't need that kind of blood in the well."

    "What was his name?" Rosalind asked, her hand tightening inside her pocket.

    "Didn't have one," Phineas said. He leaned in closer, his dry breath hot against her face. "Just a number on a state ledger. If he’s still alive, he’s fifty years old now. A ghost with no name, wandering around looking for a mother who died forty years ago. Is that what you want for your book?"

    "Do you know what happened to him?" Rosalind asked, her professional veneer slipping just a fraction. "Where he went?"

    "I don't know, and I don't care," Phineas growled. "He was none of ours. Now, get out of my house."

    He didn't wait for her to agree. He grabbed her elbow. His grip was surprisingly powerful—his fingers felt like dry oak branches digging through the wool of her sleeve. He shoved her toward the door.

    "Mr. Carrow—"

    "Get out!" he screamed, his voice cracking into a high, wild screech that sounded like a wounded bird. "Tell your historical society that the Carrows are dead! There’s nothing left here but the hide!"

    He slammed the door behind her. The heavy bolt slid into place with a dry, metallic thunk.

    Rosalind stood on the porch, her chest heaving as the cold mountain air hit her face. The rain had started again, a light, freezing drizzle that stung her cheeks. Her heart was a wild, trapped thing in her chest.

    She had to think.

    Alistair Carrow had forced Zelda to give up an illegitimate son. A boy born in Portland, fifty years ago. A boy with no name, whose family had treated him like a stain to be scrubbed out. A boy who would have grown up in the state-run orphanages of the 1970s—places where the lonely and the angry were left to ferment.

    And then there was the cufflink.

    She looked down at the porch railing.

    Sitting on the wide, mossy timber near the steps was a heavy ceramic mug. It was stained with dark blue glaze, its rim chipped near the handle. Inside, a pool of black coffee had frozen into a cold, greasy skin.

    It was Phineas’s mug. He must have left it out here before she arrived.

    Rosalind looked toward the window. The yellow light inside the cabin was blocked by a thick, grime-streaked curtain, but she could see his shadow moving back and forth—a restless, twitching shape.

    She reached into her shoulder bag. Her fingers found a clean, sterile plastic evidence bag and a pair of latex gloves. She pulled them out, her hands shaking as she struggled to get the blue latex over her cold fingers.

    She stepped toward the railing, her boots making no sound on the wet wood.

    She picked up the mug. The ceramic was cold, damp from the mist. Her fingers avoided the handle and the rim where his mouth would have touched, gripping it instead by the very bottom. She slipped it into the plastic bag, pulling the seal shut with a soft, plastic zip.

    She tucked the bag deep into her coat pocket, her heart hammering a frantic rhythm against her ribs.

    If Phineas Carrow was the killer, or if the DNA on that mug matched the biological profile they had built from the Highway 9 crime scenes, she would have the anchor she needed. But if the DNA belonged to someone else—if it matched the partial profiles from the victims but not the killer—she might have just found the key to Zelda’s missing boy.

    She turned and walked down the steps, her boots sinking into the red clay.

    Behind her, the forest seemed to close its ranks, the dark hemlock branches reaching down through the mist to swallow the cabin whole. Rosalind did not look back. She got into her car, placed the bagged mug on the passenger seat, and turned the key.

    As the engine roared to life, she looked at the plastic bag in the grey light of the dashboard.

    The DNA inside that mug was a map. And for the first time in three years, Rosalind felt like she was finally holding the compass.

    Chapter 4: The Frame-Up

    The phone didn't ring. It shuddered. A low, heavy vibration that rattled the brass paperweights on Rosalind Mercer’s mahogany desk until the metal clinked against glass. In the dead quiet of her third-floor study, it sounded like a wet cough. Or a latch clicking into place.

    She didn't grab it. Instead, she stared at the screen. The Carrow family tree branched across her monitor in clean, digital lines—gray nodes spreading out like winter ivy choking a brick wall. The study smelled of stale chamomile tea, wet wool from her coat hanging by the door, and the dry, warm ozone of a hard drive running hot. Outside, the rain was a wall of water. It lashed the glass, turning the Portland streetlights into messy, bleeding streaks of yellow and brake-light red.

    She swiped the screen. "Hector?"

    "We got him, Rosalind."

    Hector MacInnis’s voice was too loud. It had that thin, vibrating edge that comes when you’ve been living on bad diner coffee and no sleep for two days straight. Behind him, the line hummed with the growl of a diesel engine—the command van—and the occasional high-pitched squeal of wet brakes. He was moving.

    "What do you mean, you got him?" Rosalind pressed her hand flat against the desk. The polished wood felt cold, solid, real. "The scrapings from Rook’s fingernails are still at the state lab. We aren't due for results until tomorrow morning at the earliest."

    "We didn't need the lab," Hector said. His breath hitched, raspy and dry. "AFIS hit. A latent partial off the chrome trim on Cynthia Rook's driver's-side door. Right under the handle. Right where you’d grab if you were shoving someone back into the seat. It’s a clean match."

    A cold knot pulled tight in Rosalind’s chest. Her hand hovered over her cold teacup, her finger tracing the chipped rim before she pulled back. "To who, Hector?"

    "Phineas Carrow."

    The name fell between them like a stone. Heavy. Impossible.

    "That's not possible," Rosalind said. Her voice sounded thin, almost lost to the steady drum of rain against the cedar shingles.

    "It’s a lock," Hector snapped. "The print matched a juvenile card from 1979. Some stupid break-and-enter that was supposed to be expunged, but the county basement never throws anything away. We scanned the physical card. Nine points of comparison on the right index. It’s him, Rosalind. It’s your uncle."

    "Phineas was sixteen in seventy-nine," she said, her voice dropping an octave. She stood up so fast her chair screeched against the pine floorboards. She paced the length of the narrow room, her knuckles turning white around the phone. "He took a dare to break into an abandoned boathouse. That's not a record, Hector. It's a schoolboy prank."

    "The law doesn’t care about the story behind the ink, Rosalind. It cares about where we found it," Hector said, his voice flat, professional, and entirely unyielding. "We have his print on the door of a car belonging to a girl who was found three miles away in a ditch with a crushed throat. I just signed the warrant. SWAT is already gearing up at the precinct. We’re breaching his cabin in thirty minutes."

    "No," she said, her chest tight. "No, Hector, don't. Listen to me. If you send a tactical unit through his door, he will panic. He's fragile. His heart—"

    "He’s a prime suspect in a homicide," Hector cut in. "Cynthia Rook was twenty-four. She died in the freezing mud because someone decided they had the right to choke the life out of her. I'm not taking chances with an old hunter who has a rack of .30-30s on his wall."

    "Hector, wait—"

    The line went dead. Just the dry, static click of a dropped signal.

    She slowly lowered the phone. It felt like a block of lead in her palm. On the monitor, Phineas’s name sat in its neat little gray box, linked by a thin line to her mother, Tamsin, and up to her grandfather, Alistair.

    Her mind went cold. The panic evaporated, replaced by the muscle memory of a decade of hard forensic work. But underneath it, the old ghost woke up.

    The Hargrove case. Three years ago, she’d been too eager, too sure of the math. She’d misread a tiny segment of shared DNA—a false positive that looked like a father-son link. Because of her report, a local mechanic named Ephraim spent six months rotting in a state pen while the actual killer was out there shooting up heroin in cheap motels, eventually overdosing before they could ever put cuffs on him. Ephraim was cleared, but his shop was ruined, his wife had left him, and Rosalind still couldn't look at a genetic profile without seeing his hollow, haunted face in the courtroom.

    She had promised herself: Never again. Certainly not with Phineas.

    She pulled her chair back in and clicked open her local server. She had the raw, unedited sequence from the skin cells found under Cynthia Rook's nails—a file a sympathetic lab tech had slipped her on a flash drive before it was sent to the state repository.

    She ran her custom comparison script. On the left: the unknown killer's profile. On the right: her own.

    The processor whined, the fan kicking into high gear as it sorted through millions of base pairs.

    A second later, the terminal spat out the comparison log:

    ``` [SHARED DNA ANALYSIS: USER "MERCERR" VS. UNKNOWNMALE_09] --------------------------------------------------------- • Total Shared Centimorgans: 2,650 cM • Longest Shared Segment: 114.2 cM • Shared Segments: 41 • Relationship Probability: 99.9% (Parent/Child, Full Sibling) ```

    Rosalind stared at the screen. The glowing digits felt like a physical slap.

    2,650 centimorgans.

    Her fingers went numb as she pulled up the reference profile she had on file for Phineas.

    ``` [SHARED DNA ANALYSIS: USER "MERCERR" VS. CARROWP] --------------------------------------------------------- • Total Shared Centimorgans: 1,720 cM • Relationship Probability: Uncle/Niece ```

    "It’s impossible," she muttered, her breath fogging the glass of her monitor. She wiped the smudge away with her sleeve, her heart hammering against her ribs.

    Here's the thing about genetic genealogy: centimorgans are the absolute currency of truth. They don't lie, they don't have bad days, and they can't be paid off by a dirty cop or botched by a sleepy tech. A maternal uncle shares roughly a quarter of his DNA with a niece—usually somewhere between 1,300 and 2,100 cM. Phineas’s 1,720 cM was as textbook as it got.

    But the killer? 2,650 cM.

    That number is a different beast entirely. You don't hit 2,650 cM unless you are looking at a first-degree relative. A parent. A full sibling. Or a child.

    She had no children. She had no brothers or sisters. She’d spent her childhood alone in a draughty, silent house on the coast, listening to the creaking floors, her mother Tamsin’s quiet weeping behind closed doors, and the stern, heavy footsteps of her father, Alistair. Her mother had been in the ground for ten years now.

    Which left only one possible donor for that DNA.

    The thought was a cold needle in her brain. She forced it out, refusing to touch the monstrous implications of that math. Not yet.

    Instead, she focused on what she could prove right now: Phineas was innocent. The laws of biology are stubborn things. Meiosis doesn't make exceptions for warrants, and recombination doesn't care about a county print database. An uncle cannot share 2,650 cM with his niece. The DNA under Cynthia Rook's fingernails belonged to someone else.

    So how did his print end up on Cynthia's car?

    She closed her eyes, trying to picture him. She’d driven out to his cabin near Estacada just three weeks ago. She could still smell it: the heavy scent of woodsmoke, wet wool, and the bitter tang of the liniment he rubbed into his joints. She remembered the high, thin whistle of the kettle on the stove, and the way Phineas gasped just trying to reach for a mug.

    Most of all, she remembered his hands.

    They weren't hands anymore. They were gnarled, twisted clubs, the knuckles swollen to the size of walnuts. Rheumatoid arthritis had spent fifteen years systematically destroying his joints, turning his fingers into stiff, claw-like things that drifted uselessly toward his outer wrists. He could barely hold a spoon. When he’d tried to pour her tea, his hand had shaken so badly he’d splashed boiling water across the checkered oilcloth.

    She'd sat there and watched him spend five agonizing minutes trying to guide a plastic button through the loop of his flannel shirt, his jaw clenched, refusing to ask for help. The pride of a dying breed.

    She clicked over to the PDF of the preliminary autopsy report—another quiet favor from a deputy medical examiner. She scrolled past the clinical photographs of the bruises on Cynthia Rook's neck down to the internal exam:

    Hyoid bone fractured bilaterally. Deep, localized contusions consistent with high-pressure, single-handed manual strangulation. The assailant possessed exceptional grip strength, sufficient to crush the laryngeal cartilage almost instantly.

    Rosalind let out a breath she hadn’t realized she was holding.

    Phineas couldn't open a jar of pickles. He didn't have the strength to crush an empty beer can, let alone the throat of a healthy, fighting twenty-four-year-old woman. If he’d even tried to squeeze someone that hard, his own finger joints would have dislocated before he could have left a mark.

    He was being set up. It was clean, deliberate, and terrifyingly precise.

    She paced back to the window, her mind racing. But how do you fake a print on a car door? Phineas hadn't left that cabin in six months. He didn't drive. He had his groceries dropped off by a kid from the local general store. He’d never even seen Cynthia Rook's car.

    The juvenile record from seventy-nine. That had to be it.

    Back then, they used black ink and thick paper cards. When they decommissioned the old county courthouse five years ago, thousands of those physical cards were left rotting in cardboard boxes in the basement. Some were digitized, but the hard copies? They were either shredded or left to mold in unsecured basement rooms.

    Could someone have stolen the physical card? Or did they lift it off an old glass bottle from the family home? A brass heirloom? It’s not hard to transfer a latent print if you have a little bit of gelatin and a lot of patience.

    She grabbed her dark wool trench coat off the hook, the fabric smelling of lavender and old books, and shoved her keys and phone into the pockets. She couldn't sit here and wait for Hector to realize his mistake. By the time the state lab got around to confirming the DNA match, Phineas would be sitting in a county holding cell—or worse, shot dead in his own living room because he didn't hear the tactical team shouting commands over the wind.

    She flew down the wooden stairs, her boots hammering the steps. The entryway was freezing, the autumn storm whistling through the gaps in the door frame.

    As she reached for the keys on the hall table, her sleeve caught the edge of a silver photo frame. It tipped over. She caught it before it hit the hardwood.

    In the faded photograph, a young Tamsin Carrow stood on the dock of the family lake house, her arm slung around Phineas’s shoulders. Phineas was grinning, his face bright, his hands whole and strong as he held up a flapping lake trout. But it was the figure behind them that made Rosalind freeze. Sitting in the deep shade of the porch was Alistair Carrow. His face was half-hidden by the brim of a canvas fishing hat, his eyes nothing but dark, hollow sockets in the shadow.

    She stared at her father's face.

    Two thousand, six hundred and fifty centimorgans.

    The number wasn't just data anymore. It was a verdict.

    She set the frame down with a quiet click, stepped out into the blinding rain, and let the heavy front door slam shut behind her.

    Chapter 5: The Golden Locket

    The rain off Highway 9 didn't drop. It hung. A heavy, greasy mist that tasted of diesel fumes and rotting hemlock needles.

    Rosalind Mercer yanked her collar up. The stiff, paraffin-scented canvas of her coat didn't help; the dampness had already settled into her shirt hours ago, cold as a wet dog against her spine. Down the slope, fifty yards into the brush, three county cruisers strobed red and blue, turning the wet hemlock trunks into a cheap, rhythmic disco.

    The deputies had tried to shovel out a path in the red clay, but twenty pairs of boots had already chewed it into a slick, brown slide. Rosalind took each step sideways, heels dug in, her fingers white-knuckled around the handle of her steel kit. A hidden root caught her toe. Her calf muscle seized, hard and hot. She didn't drop the box. You don't drop the box.

    "Watch the drop," a voice grunted from the bottom.

    Hector MacInnis was wedged under a dripping branch, collar turned up to his ears, the brim of his felt hat sagging under a pool of brown water. He looked fifty. He was forty-two. Yesterday's clean shave had dissolved into a gray, sandpapery stubble that made his jaw look hollow. He didn't offer to carry her gear. He knew better.

    "How fresh?" she asked, sliding under the yellow perimeter tape. The wet plastic whipped across her face, leaving a cold smear of road grit on her cheek.

    "Loggers found her at dawn," MacInnis said. He pointed a fat, gloved finger toward the root wad of a fallen cedar. "Or their dogs did. Easy digging in this soft rot."

    She bypassed him and went straight to the tarp. Under the green plastic, the air smelled different—not just wet wood, but that sharp, chemical transition of a body turning cold.

    The girl was young. Maybe nineteen. She was curled on her side, knees tucked toward her chin like she was trying to keep warm in a drafty bedroom. A smear of yellow clay crossed her upper lip, parting her mouth just enough to show the edge of two slightly crooked front teeth.

    "She's recent," Rosalind said, cracking open her kit. She didn't bother with the kneeling pad; she went straight to her knees in the mud, feeling the cold soak through her canvas trousers in three seconds flat. She snapped on the blue nitrile gloves. They were stiff from the cold, smelling of cheap latex. "Twenty-four hours, max. The stiffness in her jaw is already giving way."

    "It's impossible," Hector spat. He was pacing the narrow dry patch under the tarp, his boots making a wet, sucking sound that grated on her nerves. "We locked Phineas Carrow in a concrete box at three yesterday afternoon. I watched the turnkey bolt the latch. Cynthia Rook was at her desk three hours ago, typing up the grand jury indictment. She's probably leaked it to the Herald by now."

    "Then Phineas has a very busy ghost," Rosalind said. She leaned down, her camera's small flash firing with a pathetic pop-whine in the dimness. She focused on the purple-black band around the throat. "Or he didn't work alone."

    "He did it," Hector whispered, his voice dropping into that low, dangerous register he used when his career was on the line. "The cedar shavings in the hair of the first three. That weird, triple-loop clove hitch on the wrists. It's his work, Rosalind. But if Rook finds out we've got a warm body with the same signature while Phineas is eating county stew, she'll have to pull the plug. The Carrows have three lawyers on retainer who would love to strip this department to the drywall."

    "You want me to lie about the lividity, Hector?"

    "I'm asking what the margin is," he said, his eyes darting toward the flashlight beams bobbing up on the highway. "Could she have died Tuesday? Before we picked him up? Give me two days."

    "No." She didn't look up. "Blood doesn't lie to make your warrant look good. The lividity is purple, but it still blanches when I press it. If she’d been lying here since Tuesday in forty-degree rain, she’d be rigid. She died last night. While Phineas was listening to the pipes hum in cell four."

    He swore under his breath, a short, wet word, and turned his back to her.

    Rosalind ignored him. She took an orange plastic trowel from her case. The soil here was mostly leaf mold and rotted wood—stuff that didn't scrape, just turned to mush under pressure. She worked the trowel around the victim’s collarbone, clearing the black muck to find whatever the killer had dropped.

    The work was boring until it wasn't.

    The plastic edge hit something hard. A tiny, distinct click.

    "What?" Hector was instantly behind her, his sour coffee breath hot on the back of her wet neck.

    "Nothing. Root," she lied instantly.

    She didn't use the brush. She used her gloved thumb, wiping away a smear of gray silt. Metal. A tiny, dull yellow oval.

    Her heart didn't jump; it seemed to stop entirely, leaving a cold, hollow space behind her ribs.

    "Rosalind?"

    "Just a button," she said. Her voice sounded flat, even to her.

    She pinched the oval and pulled. The wet clay let go with a sticky kiss.

    It was gold-plated brass. The plating was mostly gone, worn down to the greening metal beneath by years of rubbing against denim or skin. On the front, the faint, scratched outline of a wild rose.

    But it was the back.

    There was a deep, jagged scar across the reverse. Three lines, like a broken arrow.

    A blue gravel driveway in Coos Bay. She was twelve again, running after a sister who wouldn't stop, her heel catching, the locket flying off her neck to skid across the sharp river rock. She had spent three hours crying on the porch, trying to buff out that scratch with a sleeve.

    Her thumb found the tiny, corroded notch on the side. It resisted, then popped open with a dry crunch of rust.

    Under the yellowed plastic inside was a pinch of dark brown hair, tied with a thread. Next to it, a scrap of thick sketchbook paper. One word, written in sheener ink that had faded to a dull charcoal:

    Rose.

    The woods went dead. The rain didn't sound like rain anymore; it sounded like sand falling on tin.

    This wasn't her sister's locket. It was hers. The one she'd kept in the cedar box on her dresser until three years ago, when some junkie had kicked her apartment door in and taken fifty dollars in quarters and a cheap silver ring she didn't care about. She’d assumed the locket had been melted down or tossed into a pawn shop bin in Portland.

    "What is it?" Hector's voice was too loud, too close. "Mercer. Is there an inscription?"

    "No," she said. She snapped the metal shut, the edge cutting through the thin nitrile glove to pinch her skin. "It's a zipper pull. From a jacket."

    "Let me see." He reached out a hand.

    "I need to bag it," she snapped, her voice cracking. She pulled her hand back into her lap, out of his reach. She saw his eyes go small, the sharp, cynical cop brain clicking behind his tired forehead. "It's got organic trace on it. If your grease gets on the seal, we lose the profile."

    He watched her for five long seconds. He didn't believe her, but before he could argue, his radio squealed.

    "MacInnis," Rook's voice was a tinny scrape of static. "Tell me you're wrapping up. The Herald has a stringer at the gate. We need the booking photo released."

    Hector pressed the black rubber button on his shoulder. "We're still processing, Cynthia. There's... details."

    "We have our man, Hector. The grand jury is Monday. Make the scene fit. I don't want any creative writing in the report."

    Hector looked at the mud, then at Rosalind. "On our way," he muttered. He hooked the mic back onto his lapel. "We aren't done with this, Mercer. Pack it up."

    She waited until the sloshing of his boots died out up the ridge.

    When the woods were only rain again, she opened her fist.

    The locket was there, cold and heavy as a tooth.

    This wasn't a mistake. It wasn't some trophy the killer had dropped because his hands were cold. It was a card left on her desk. The hair wasn't her sister’s; the shade was too dark, the texture too coarse. It was her own. Someone had stood over her bed while she slept, holding the scissors, smelling the lavender she used to wash her sheets, and she hadn't even stirred.

    She looked at the girl in the dirt.

    Dark hair, cut bob-short. Narrow collarbones. A small, pale face that looked almost gray under the hemlocks.

    A prototype. A test run.

    Rosalind didn't put the locket in the blue plastic evidence tub. She slipped the small ziplock bag into the internal pocket of her coat, letting the cold brass rest right against her ribs, right where her heart was pounding.

    She stood up, her knees popping like dry twigs. She looked back down the valley one last time. The yellow tape was twisting in the wind, a bright, ugly line across the gray woods. Somewhere beyond that line, in the thick brush where the loggers hadn't cleared, she knew someone was standing, watching her carry her heavy box back to the car.

    Chapter 6: The Surgical Sibling

    The rain over Saint Jude’s County Hospital didn’t just fall. It drifted. A greasy, grey mist smelling of wet asphalt and industrial coal scrubbers. Through the smear of the corner diner's window, the concrete towers of the medical center rose like rotting teeth against a leaden sky.

    Rosalind Mercer traced the rim of her heavy ceramic mug. Her thumb caught on a chipped edge in the glaze. She didn't mind the pain; it kept her awake. The coffee was black, bitter, and tasted like a burnt pot left on the burner since yesterday morning. On the sticky Formica table sat a printout. She didn't need to look at it again. She had the numbers memorized. The closed adoption files she’d pulled after thirty-six hours of sleepless digging. The original birth certificate naming Zelda Carrow as the mother. The amended record that turned Tamsin Carrow into Tamsin Avery—adopted by a wealthy Boston vascular surgeon and his blue-blooded wife.

    The lineage was a straight, clean line. The blood, however, was filthy.

    Rosalind’s throat went dry as she stared across the street. She had promised herself this job would be different. Cold. Objective. Professional distance. But the dead don't care about professional boundaries. Every time she mapped a pedigree chart now, she saw Giles Pendelton. Five years ago, a simple clerical error on her part had cleared him. Just a single misplaced name on a family tree. That oversight gave him three extra weeks of freedom. Three weeks. Long enough to claim his final victim. The agency called it an administrative anomaly. Rosalind called it a blood-debt. She couldn’t afford to be wrong twice.

    The brass bell above the diner door jingled. Sharp. Obnoxious.

    A woman stepped inside, shaking a wet, black umbrella. She didn't just walk; she marched. Tamsin Avery.

    She was rail-thin, looking as though her bones were held together solely by the starch in her green surgical scrubs and the heavy wool of her water-spotted black coat. Her dark hair was pinned back with metallic surgical clips. It exposed a high, pale forehead and eyes that moved with the rapid, twitching focus of a kestrel. She didn’t look tired. She looked wired, vibrating at a frequency that threatened to shatter the surrounding glassware.

    Chapter 9: The Trap in the Trees

    Chapter 10: The Last Branch

He was scratching at them absentmindedly against the rough canvas of his trousers—a dry, rhythmic scritch-scritch that filled the silence between his words.

    The graft-versus-host disease.

    She remembered the medical files now. During her late-night deep dives into the Carrow family history, she’d found the records of Alistair’s childhood leukemia. The bone marrow transplant from an anonymous donor had saved his life, but it had left him with a chronic, low-grade nightmare. His immune system was a permanent war zone, his body constantly trying to reject the foreign cells. It manifested as severe, intractable contact dermatitis—skin so hyper-sensitive that the wrong laundry detergent, the touch of certain fabrics, or even raw metal could send him into a state of burning agony.

    "You killed them," Rosalind said, keeping her tone conversational, trying to anchor him to the facts. "Cynthia Rook's brother. Phineas and Tamsin's circle. You wanted to wipe out everyone who ever touched this family."

    "I wanted to burn the tree to the ground," Alistair said, his voice dropping to a low, rhythmic hum. "Every rotten branch. They abandoned me. They threw me in the dark and expected me to rot quietly. But I didn't. I grew up. I got a job in the state forensics lab. Pretty poetic, isn't it? The unwanted kid becomes the keeper of the dead."

    He took a step forward, dragging the gun barrel across Hector’s scalp. Hector didn't flinch, though his eyes squeezed shut.

    "I was running the DNA from the cold cases," Alistair continued, a thin, dry smile stretching his lips. "The old Carrow homicides. The blood on the glass from the ninety-two robbery. I ran it through the system, expecting the usual dead ends. And then... there you were. A partial match. Not a suspect, but a sister. A genealogist who was suddenly digging up the old graves. You were hunting me, Rosalind. My own flesh and blood was building the gallows."

    "I was trying to find the truth," Rosalind said. "I didn't know who you were."

    "The truth is a luxury for people who can afford the rent," Alistair sneered. "I had to act. I couldn't let you hand me over. So I swapped the samples. I took the DNA from the crime scene and replaced it with a synthetic sequence that would point the finger at the cousins. And then I planted the locket. The old silver one with our mother's initials. I knew you’d find it. I wanted to draw you in, Rosalind. I wanted you to see what you left behind."

    The puzzle pieces clicked together with the cold, heavy clunk of a deadbolt. The weird discrepancies in the lab reports, the sudden, convenient discovery of the locket at the secondary search site—it wasn't incompetent police work. It was Alistair, pulling the strings from inside the forensics division, orchestrating a grand performance to frame the rest of the family before 'discovering' the definitive evidence that would ruin them.

    And now, she and Hector were the last loose ends to be burned.

    "You’re going to frame Phineas and Tamsin for our deaths, too," Rosalind whispered.

    "A tragic murder-suicide," Alistair said, his eyes scanning the dark corners of the ceiling, already visualizing the report. "The retired detective gets too close. The paranoid Carrow siblings panic and pull the trigger. It’s clean. The police will find Phineas's revolver. They’ll find Tamsin's fingerprints on the casings. And the Carrow name will finally be dragged through the mud where it belongs."

    He raised the gun, aiming it directly at her chest.

    Her heart hammered against her ribs like a bird in a chimney. Her hand went to her coat pocket, her fingers brushing against the small, plastic spray bottle of industrial-strength isopropyl alcohol she’d taken from her lab kit that afternoon. She had meant to use it for sterilizing slides, but now, looking at the raw, weeping lesions on Alistair’s wrists, she saw something else.

    "Alistair," she said, her voice dropping its professional distance, softening into something almost intimate. "Look at your hands."

    He blinked, momentarily thrown off by the shift in her tone. "What?"

    "You're bleeding," she said, taking a slow half-step forward. Her boots made no sound on the grit-strewn floor. "The wool. It's killing you, isn't it? The lanolin in the fibers. It's triggering the dermatitis. Your skin is on fire."

    Alistair’s eyes darted down to his wrists. The suggestion alone seemed to trigger the nerve endings. His fingers twitched, scratching frantically at the raw flesh. The red patches were angry, weeping clear fluid where he had torn the skin.

    "I... it's fine," he muttered, but his grip on the gun loosened just a fraction. The constant, low-grade agony of his condition was a weak point—a physical crack in his armor that he had lived with for decades.

    "It's not fine," Rosalind said, her voice dripping with a false, soothing sympathy. She took another step. She was within six feet of him now. "Your body is rejecting your own skin, Alistair. You've been fighting yourself since you were a boy. Let me help you. I have the high-potency steroid cream in my bag. The prescription stuff. It stops the burning. Immediately."

    "You're lying," he whispered, but his eyes drifted to her leather shoulder bag anyway. The itch was a physical torment, a madness that crawled under his skin, made worse by the sweat and the sheer tension of the room. He reached up with his left hand to scratch at the side of his neck, where the rash had spread in a violent red collar.

    "I'm not lying," Rosalind said. She reached into her bag, her fingers wrapping around the cool, hard plastic of the spray bottle. "I'm your sister, Alistair. I know what you suffer from. I know the pain."

    She pulled her hand out.

    Instead of a tube of cream, she brought the small bottle up and squeezed the trigger. A fine, pressurized mist of ninety-nine percent isopropyl alcohol sprayed directly into Alistair's face and onto the raw, open sores of his neck and wrists.

    Alistair screamed.

    It was a high, thin sound of absolute, white-hot agony. The alcohol hit the weeping, exposed nerve endings of his dermatitis like liquid fire. His eyes snapped shut as the chemical burned into his skin, and his hands flew to his face in an involuntary reflex of self-preservation.

    The revolver slipped from his fingers, hitting the floorboards with a heavy thud.

    In that fraction of a second, Hector moved. Despite his injuries, the old detective threw his weight forward, the heavy wooden chair tipping with a loud crash. He slammed into Alistair’s knees, sending both of them crashing to the pine planks.

    The gun skittered across the dusty floor.

    Rosalind didn't think. She lunged forward, her knees hitting the grit, her fingers closing around the cold steel of the revolver. She scrambled back, bringing the weapon up, her arms shaking as she aimed it at the writhing figure on the floor.

    "Don't move!" she screamed, her voice cracking under the strain.

    Alistair was on his knees, his hands clutching his neck, his face twisted in a mask of pure pain and fury. The skin where the alcohol had hit was turning a deep, angry crimson, the chemical reaction causing his nerve endings to scream. He looked up at her through tear-filled eyes, his breath coming in ragged, whistling gasps.

    "You... you bitch," he hissed.

    "It's over, Alistair," Rosalind said, her finger steadying on the heavy trigger.

    The sound of heavy boots crunched on the gravel outside, followed immediately by the red and blue glare of police lights cutting through the grimy windows of the cabin. The sirens, muted by the deep woods, finally grew loud enough to rattle the glass in its frames.

    Cynthia Rook was the first through the door, her service weapon drawn, her face grim. Behind her, three state troopers flooded the small room, their boots thudding against the floorboards as they secured the area.

    "Drop the weapon, Alistair," Cynthia commanded.

    But Alistair didn't move. He remained on the floor, his hands still clutching his raw neck, weeping quietly as the officers pulled his arms behind his back and clicked the metal handcuffs into place. The cold steel of the cuffs against his irritated wrists made him wince—a final, physical reminder of the body he could never escape.

    As they dragged him out into the cold rain, he didn't look back at her.

    The rain had stopped by midnight, leaving the air clear and biting.

    Rosalind sat on the bumper of an ambulance, a rough wool blanket wrapped around her shoulders. She had refused the paramedics' offer to take her to the county hospital, choosing instead to watch the flashing lights paint the hemlocks in rhythmic pulses of red and blue.

    Hector was being loaded into the back of a separate rig, a thick white bandage wrapped around his head. He gave her a slow, tired nod as they closed the doors. He would survive. The physical wounds would heal, though the old detective would likely never look at a cold case file the same way again.

    Cynthia Rook walked over, two paper cups of steaming, terrible utility-grade coffee in her hands. She offered one to Rosalind, who took it, letting the heat seep into her frozen fingers.

    "The lab confirmed the sample swap," Cynthia said, looking out over the dark treeline. "Alistair had been manipulating the database for months from his terminal. We found the original profiles in his secure digital drive. Phineas and Tamsin are cleared. The locket was indeed a plant."

    "The Carrow family tree is dead," Rosalind said quietly.

    "And you?" Cynthia asked, her eyes searching Rosalind’s face in the blue light. "Are you going to look for the other branches?"

    Rosalind looked down at the coffee cup, watching the dark liquid ripple in the faint light. She thought of the neat, beautiful charts she had spent her life constructing. She thought of the names she had searched for, the ancestors she had tried to pull back from the silence of the past.

    She had wanted so badly to find where she belonged. She had wanted a bloodline, a heritage, a deep root in the earth.

    But looking at the wreckage of the Carrow family—the madness, the secrets, the poison that had passed from one generation to the next—she realized that blood was not a sanctuary. It was merely a blueprint. And a blueprint could be discarded if the house it built was rotten.

    "No," Rosalind said. She stood up, letting the heavy blanket slip from her shoulders. The air was freezing, but she didn't shiver. "I have a family. The ones who took me in. The ones who held my hand when I was sick, who watched me grow, who loved me without needing a DNA match to prove it."

    She walked away from the flashing lights, toward her station wagon. The mud was already drying on her boots, the forest quiet once more. For the first time in her life, she didn't feel the need to look back at the tree behind her. She was her own branch, and she was finally ready to grow.
