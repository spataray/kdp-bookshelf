The Smart Home Caregiver: Simple Tech Solutions to Help Aging Parents Stay Safe, Independent, and Connected

Chapter 1: The Midnight Phone Call: Balancing Elder Independence with Absolute Peace of Mind

The silence of 2:14 AM has a weight. It is cold. Heavy. Absolute. When a smartphone buzzing on a wooden nightstand cuts through that quiet, your heart does not just beat faster. It stops.

For Maren, this was the soundtrack to her chronic insomnia. Her eighty-two-year-old dad, Dion, was still holding down the fort in the drafty, two-story craftsman home he built with his own hands. Dion was a retired master carpenter. He had a razor-sharp mind, but his knees were shot. And he lived three states away. Every time Maren's phone lit up after midnight, her brain immediately flashed to worst-case scenarios. Did he tumble down the basement stairs? Did he leave the front burner going after making tea? Is he lying on the linoleum right now, staring at the ceiling, unable to reach the wall phone?

Most of those late-night calls were false alarms—a wrong number, a telemarketer from a different time zone, or Dion's palm accidentally bumping his speed-dial. But that did not stop the adrenaline from flooding her system, leaving her trembling in the dark. This constant state of red alert was eating her alive. She was exhausted, spacing out in meetings, and drowning in guilt. She wanted her father to be safe, but every time she suggested moving him to an assisted living facility, Dion's chin would jut out with defensive pride.

"I built every square inch of this porch," he would snap. "I’m not leaving it to rot in some sterile, carpeted box where they feed me lukewarm soup and wait for me to kick the bucket."

Here is the brutal reality of the caregiver’s trap. You are pinned between two immovable forces: the terrifying urge to keep your parent safe, and their stubborn, beautiful need to hold onto their dignity, privacy, and control.

Caregiver Burnout Self-Assessment

Before we go any further, take a moment to do a quick, honest reality check:

  • Do you look at your phone every ten minutes, feeling a spike of panic if your parent doesn't pick up on the first ring?
  • Have you skipped vacations, passed on career moves, or ignored your own spouse and kids because you feel like you always have to be "on call"?
  • Has your relationship with your parent degenerated into a series of tense arguments about what they can and cannot do anymore?

If you checked any of those boxes, you are not failing. You are simply carrying the crushing weight of long-distance caregiving. Fortunately, there is a way to ease this tension without forcing a painful move or starting a family feud.

The Dignity Dilemma: Why "Safety" Can Feel Like a Trap

When panic takes the wheel, our default setting is overprotection. We want to wrap our parents in bubble wrap. We insist on putting cameras in every room, or we demand they wear those bulky, bright-red emergency buttons around their necks. To us, these seem like small compromises for safety. To them, they feel like a total surrender of adulthood.

Think about it. You spend eighty years on this earth. You raise kids, build businesses, fix engines, and manage mortgages. Then, almost overnight, your child starts treating you like a toddler who cannot be trusted around sharp corners. That hurts. When we strip away their privacy to buy ourselves peace of mind, we are not just protecting them; we are chipping away at who they are.

Dion would not even look at a traditional panic button. "I’m not wearing a dog collar that tells the world I’m half-dead," he told Maren. When she brought up a living room camera, he threatened to throw a dish towel over it—or worse, poke it with his broomstick.

That is not just old-man stubbornness. It is a basic survival mechanism. The second someone feels like they are losing the steering wheel of their own life, their health takes a nosedive. Depression moves in. Physical decline speeds up.

The real challenge is finding a middle ground. How do you keep an eye on your parent without staring at them? How do you know they are safe without making them feel watched?

Enter the Invisible Caregiver

This is where we turn to ambient technology, or what I like to call the "invisible caregiver." This is not some creepy, sci-fi surveillance state. It is a quiet network of small, cheap, smart devices that work in the background of a home. They do not watch, they do not record video, and they do not require your parent to wear a single thing or change their daily habits.

Instead of filming your parent, ambient technology monitors the environment and the natural rhythms of daily life. It uses simple motion sensors, contact sensors on doors, and smart plugs on everyday appliances to paint a digital picture of safety.

Think about how your dad starts his day. He rolls out of bed, pads down the hall to the kitchen, opens the fridge, and turns on the kettle.

By placing a few inexpensive, discreet sensors around the house, you can track this routine without ever seeing a single image of your parent.

  • A tiny, matchbox-sized motion sensor tucked under the nightstand registers that his feet hit the floor.
  • A magnetic sensor on the fridge door logs that he’s getting the milk out.
  • A smart plug on the wall notices the coffee maker is drawing power.

If that chain reaction happens, you get a simple, silent notification on your phone: Dad is up. No intrusive phone calls at 7:00 AM. No interrogation. Just a quiet sigh of relief while you get your own day started.

The Rule of Thumb: Track Action, Not Looks

Unlike video cameras, which strip away privacy, ambient sensors only measure actions. They tell you that your parent is safe, not what they look like while doing it. This preserves their dignity while giving you real-time reassurance.

Flipping the Script: How to Build a Baseline

To make this technology work, you have to change your mindset. You have to move away from reactive panic and focus on proactive observation.

Here is the mistake almost everyone makes: they wait for the disaster. They wait for the hip fracture or the kitchen fire, and then they rush in like a SWAT team, plastering the house with tech. By then, everyone is traumatized, defensive, and ready to fight. The trick is to build a baseline of normal, quiet days before the crisis hits.

When Maren first stuck a few motion sensors around Dion's house, she did not set up any alarms. She just watched the data for a week. She learned his rhythm: he woke up between 6:30 AM and 7:15 AM, hit the bathroom immediately, and opened his medicine cabinet by 8:00 AM.

Once she knew his normal, she set her app to only buzz her if something broke that pattern.

If it was 8:30 AM and the bathroom motion sensor had not activated, or if the medicine cabinet had not been opened, Maren’s phone would give a quiet nudge.

This is the magic of ambient support. You do not spend your day worrying about what might be happening; you are only alerted when something is out of the ordinary. It transforms caregiving from a source of constant, low-grade dread into a manageable, organized partnership.

Dion loved this setup once he realized how it worked. He did not have to remember to push a button. He did not have to answer daily check-in calls that felt like depositions. He simply lived his life, and the house quietly kept Maren informed.

Non-Intrusive Smart Tools at a Glance

  • Smart Contact Sensors: Two tiny magnets, about the size of a stick of gum. You stick them on the fridge, the medicine cabinet, or the front door. They tell you when things open and close.
  • Ambient Motion Sensors: Little plastic squares you tuck in the corner of a room or under a bed. They detect movement, not faces.
  • Smart Plugs: Simple pass-through plugs that sit between the wall and the coffee maker or kettle. They let you know when the morning brew is running.
  • Water Leak Detectors: Tiny plastic discs you drop under the kitchen sink or behind the toilet. If a pipe starts dripping, you know before the ceiling caves in.

Chapter 2: The Bright Path: Eliminating Nighttime Hazards with Automated Lighting and Voice Controls

A 3:00 AM phone call never brings good news. Your heart hits your throat before you even reach the nightstand. For Marita, that dread became a cold reality on a rainy Tuesday when she saw her father Oswaldo's name flashing on the screen.

He had woken up needing to use the bathroom. Like most stubborn seventy-eight-year-olds, he didn’t want to bother his daughter. He was sure he knew the hallway by heart. Why fumble with the lamp? He took three steps into the pitch black, misjudged the doorway, and caught his foot on the edge of a heavy wool runner. He fell hard. He didn't break a hip—thank God—but he was bruised, terrified, and stuck on the cold hardwood floor for forty-five minutes before he could crawl back to his phone.

This isn't a rare accident. It happens thousands of times every single night. The CDC reports that more than one in four older adults falls each year, and the vast majority of these tumbles happen in the dark.

Why? Because aging eyes are fundamentally different. The pupils shrink. They stop reacting quickly to light changes. A seventy-year-old actually needs about three times more light to see than a twenty-year-old. Think about that. What looks like a soft, romantic evening glow to you is a pitch-black cave to your aging parent. Combine that visual gap with the groggy haze of half-sleep, and you have a perfect storm for a trip to the emergency room.

The fix isn't to force your parent to sleep with bright, annoying lights shining in their face all night. Instead, you want to build a smart, quiet path that guides them from the mattress to the bathroom and back again without them having to think about it.

### ⚠️ Key Takeaway: The Vision Gap
What looks like a clear hallway to you is a cavern of shadows to your parent. Designing a home for safety means adjusting your standards of brightness to match their biological reality.

The Dark Corridor: Why Nighttime Falls Are So Dangerous

The physical damage of a fall is bad enough, but the psychological toll is worse. It steals their confidence. After Oswaldo fell, he changed. He stopped drinking water after 6:00 PM because he was terrified of having to get up at night. That led to severe dehydration and a painful urinary tract infection. It’s a vicious, silent cycle I've seen play out dozens of times in my career.

Human balance is a three-legged stool. It relies on your inner ear, your muscles, and your eyes. Turn off the lights, and you instantly kick out one of those legs. A parent who walks perfectly fine during the day will stumble in the dark because their brain has no visual anchor.

The good news? You don't need to hire an expensive contractor to rip out your drywall and run new copper wire. You can build a safe, automatic light path using cheap, off-the-shelf tech.

---

The Motion-Activated Path: Affordable, No-Rewiring Solutions

Let’s start with the easiest, cheapest fix: motion-activated path lights. These are small, simple lights that plug directly into wall outlets or stick to the baseboards with double-sided tape. They stay off during the day, then glow softly the moment they detect a step.

When Marita set out to fix her dad's house, she started with a few plug-in LED nightlights in the hallway and bathroom. She didn't place them at eye level. She plugged them in low, about a foot off the floor.

Now, when Oswaldo swings his legs out of bed, a soft amber glow blossoms under his nightstand. As he takes his first step into the hall, the next sensor catches him, lighting up a runway of safety.

Here's a detail most people miss: skip the cool blue-white lights. Blue light mimics morning sunlight. It blocks melatonin and tells the brain it's time to wake up, making it impossible for them to get back to sleep. Go for warm yellow or amber tones instead. It’s enough light to guide their feet without waking up their brain.

### 💡 Pro-Tip: Avoid the Pet Trap
If your parent has a cat or a dog, motion sensors placed too close to the floor will trigger every time the pet wanders by at night. To prevent this, mount stick-on motion lights at knee height (about two feet off the floor) and angle them slightly downward. This ensures they detect human movement while ignoring low-lying pets.

---

Smart Switches and Voice-Activated Lighting

Plug-in lights are great for floors, but they don't light up whole rooms. For a big master bedroom or bathroom, you need overhead light. This is where smart home tech becomes a literal lifesaver.

A voice assistant lets your parent command the light. If Oswaldo wakes up dizzy, he doesn't have to grope around in the dark for a switch. He can just say, "Alexa, turn on the bedroom light."

If you want to set this up, you have to choose between smart bulbs and smart switches:

  • Smart Bulbs: These screw directly into existing lamps. They are cheap and incredibly easy to install, but if someone flips the physical wall switch to "off," the bulb loses power, and the smart features die.
  • Smart Switches: These replace the actual switch on the wall. They require a tiny bit of wiring work—though many modern options take just fifteen minutes with a screwdriver—but they ensure the light is always controllable, whether your parent uses their voice, an app, or the physical wall switch.

If your parent has a lifetime of habit built around flipping wall switches, go with smart switches or specialized smart button covers. This prevents them from accidentally cutting power to their smart bulbs.

Creating "Scenes" for Late-Night Trips

Here is where the magic happens: routines. Instead of turning on five different lights individually, a single trigger can activate a sequence of lights tailored specifically for nighttime safety.

Marita set up a "Midnight Walk" routine for her dad. She linked a motion sensor next to his bed to the smart bulbs in the hallway and bathroom.

Here’s how it works in real life:

1. Between 11:00 PM and 6:00 AM, if the bedside sensor detects movement, the routine triggers automatically. 2. The hallway light comes on at a dim, soft 15% brightness. 3. The bathroom light turns on at 20%. 4. After ten minutes of zero movement, the lights fade back to black.

It’s entirely hands-off. Oswaldo doesn't have to talk to a speaker or fumble for a button while half-asleep. The house just takes care of him, wrapping him in a soft, guiding light that escorts him to his destination and tucks him back into the dark when he is done.

### ⚠️ Key Takeaway: Low and Slow
When programming automated nighttime routines, always set the brightness level to 20% or lower. Sudden, bright light can cause temporary blindness in older eyes, actually increasing the risk of a fall rather than preventing it.

Chapter 3: The Safe Hearth: Defeating Kitchen Fires, Water Leaks, and Temperature Extremes

A sharp, charred smell. That was the first clue.

Maritza felt her stomach drop the second she unlocked the front door. She’d expected the usual comforting scent of her father Osvaldo’s house—shaved cedar from his workshop and old paperbacks. Instead, she was met with a thin, greasy gray haze hanging in the hallway.

In the kitchen, a pot of vegetable soup had boiled down to a blackened, smoking crust on the electric burner.

And Osvaldo? He was sitting right in his favorite armchair in the living room, completely lost in a mystery novel. He didn't smell a thing. Decades of working with wood dust had dulled his nose years ago. To make matters worse, the hallway smoke detector was so old and clogged with dust that it didn't even chirp.

This wasn't some hypothetical training scenario. It was a random Tuesday afternoon, and it came within five minutes of burning a house down with a grandfather inside it.

Here is the cold, hard truth: cooking is the single biggest cause of house fires and injuries. For an aging parent dealing with fading senses or memory slips, the kitchen is easily the most dangerous room in the house. You can’t stand guard by the stove twenty-four hours a day. You have your own life, your own job, your own kids. But a few simple, cheap pieces of hardware can act as your eyes and ears when you’re not there.

Pro-Tip: The "Helpful Assistant" Shift
No one likes to feel like they're being managed. If your parent insists their nose is just fine, don't argue the point. Instead, frame new safety gadgets as "home assistants" or "energy savers." It's not about their failing senses; it's about making the house run smoother.

Stoves That Turn Themselves Off

For forty years, Osvaldo’s kitchen was his absolute domain. The man took serious pride in his slow-simmered marinara and his morning espresso. Taking away his stove privileges would have felt like stripping away his manhood. But after the dried-up soup incident, Maritza knew she couldn't leave him alone with an open flame or an active burner anymore.

How do you protect them without making them feel like a child locked out of their own kitchen? You install an automatic stove shut-off.

The best option for electric stoves is a motion-sensor controller. It plugs directly into the heavy-duty outlet behind the stove. Here is how it works: when the stove is turned on, a timer starts counting down. As long as your parent is actually in the kitchen chopping vegetables or washing dishes, the motion sensor sees them and resets the clock.

But what if they walk away? What if they lie down for a quick nap, or get distracted by a phone call in the other room? The sensor notices the empty kitchen. Once the countdown hits zero—usually after ten or fifteen minutes—it simply cuts the power.

If your parent has a gas stove, you have a different challenge. You can't just cut the electricity. Instead, you need an automatic gas valve timer. This is a mechanical dial you install on the gas line. To cook, your parent has to turn the dial to thirty or sixty minutes. When the time runs out, the valve physically snaps shut, cutting off the gas even if the burner knob is still turned to high.

For smaller, everyday hazards like electric kettles or toaster ovens, go with smart plugs. You can program these through an app to automatically turn off at 9:00 PM every single night. That way, a forgotten kettle will never sit piping hot on the counter until dawn.

What to Look For:

  • Smart Knobs: These replace the standard dials on the front of the stove and mechanically turn themselves back to "off" after a set time.
  • Power Shut-offs: Heavy-duty plugs that cut the circuit if the kitchen remains empty for too long.
  • Portable Induction Burners: If the stove is just too risky, unplug it entirely and place a single induction burner on the counter. It stays cool to the touch and shuts off the second you lift the pan.

Catching the Drip Before the Flood

Fire is the obvious threat, but water is the sneaky one. Water damage doesn't always start with a spectacular burst pipe. Usually, it’s a quiet, slow drip behind a cabinet or under a floorboard, rotting the wood and growing mold long before anyone notices.

Maritza discovered this firsthand when she helped Osvaldo clean his bathroom. She noticed the drywall behind the guest toilet felt slightly soft and spongy. A tiny, slow leak from a five-cent rubber washer had been dripping for weeks. Osvaldo couldn’t bend down far enough to see it, and his nose wasn't sharp enough to catch the musty smell. If that pipe had actually burst over a long weekend, the entire first floor would have been ruined.

The fix for this is incredibly cheap and takes about two minutes to set up. You need smart water leak sensors.

These are tiny plastic discs, about the size of a hockey puck, that sit right on the floor. On the bottom are small metal prongs. The moment water touches those prongs, it completes an electrical circuit, and the sensor does two things: it lets out a loud, high-pitched beep to warn your parent, and it sends an instant alert directly to your phone.

You don't need any tools or a degree in engineering to install them. You just drop them on the floor in high-risk zones and connect them to the home's Wi-Fi. The batteries last for years.

  • Under the kitchen sink: Put it right near the garbage disposal and the main pipes.
  • Behind the toilets: Place it directly under the water supply valve.
  • By the water heater: Older tanks are famous for rusting out and failing all at once.
  • Next to the washing machine: Rubber hoses crack easily under constant pressure.

If you want absolute peace of mind, you can pair these sensors with an automatic shut-off valve on the main water line. If any sensor in the house gets wet, it signals the main valve to shut down the water supply to the entire house in seconds.

Why Your Thermostat Needs Digital Guardrails

Our bodies get worse at regulating temperature as we age. It's why you'll often see seniors wearing sweaters in the middle of July. They are highly vulnerable to heatstroke in the summer and hypothermia in the winter, yet managing a modern thermostat can be incredibly frustrating for someone with cognitive decline.

Osvaldo constantly fought with his old dial thermostat. On freezing winter nights, he would crank the heat up to eighty-five degrees, get overheated, and then turn it completely off—forgetting to turn it back on when the house dropped into the fifties. In the summer, he’d turn the air conditioning off to "save a few pennies," leaving his living room dangerously hot.

This is where a smart thermostat becomes a lifesaver, specifically through a feature called "temperature bounds."

Instead of locking your parent out of the system completely—which feels disrespectful and irritating—you simply set a safe operating range. You program the system so that the house can never drop below sixty-four degrees or climb above seventy-eight degrees, no matter what buttons your parent presses on the wall.

The Digital Guardrail
This isn't about taking away their control. It's about setting a safety net. Your parent can still bump the heat up a few degrees when they feel chilly, but the smart thermostat ensures they can't accidentally turn the house into an oven or a freezer.

Even better, you can check the temperature of their home from your own phone. If you notice it’s ninety degrees outside on a Tuesday afternoon and their thermostat is set to "off," you can turn the AC on yourself and call to check on them. It might just save you a trip to the emergency room.

Chapter 4: The Rhythm of Daily Living: Tracking Routines with Passive Activity Sensors and Smart Dispensers

How many times have you called your aging parent today, just to check if they ate lunch or, God forbid, forgot their morning pills? If you're like most adult children I know, the answer is probably too many. And if you're honest, those phone calls rarely go well. Nobody likes to feel micromanaged, especially not a parent who spent decades looking after you. When you ask, "Dad, did you eat?" or "Did you take your heart medicine?" it doesn't sound like caring. It sounds like an interrogation. They get defensive. You get stressed. Before you know it, that relationship, once built on mutual respect, starts to feel like a struggle between a warden and an inmate. No one wants that.

Consider Isidora and her eighty-two-year-old father, Harlan. Harlan lived alone in a charming ranch home, about three hours away from Isidora. His independence? He guarded it fiercely. He still loved puttering around with woodcraft projects in his garage, still insisted on making his own morning coffee. But Harlan was also starting to forget things. He'd missed his blood pressure medication twice in one month, a lapse that sent him on a frightening trip to urgent care. Isidora was terrified. Her immediate, instinctual response? Call him three or four times a day.

The result was, predictably, disastrous. Harlan started ignoring her calls. "I'm not a child, Isidora," he snapped during one tense Sunday conversation. "I don't need a babysitter checking if I opened the fridge." Isidora was caught in that classic caregiver trap: she absolutely needed to know her father was safe. But her constant checking was slowly eroding his dignity, fraying the edges of their relationship. Cameras were out—a massive invasion of his privacy. She just needed a way to verify his daily habits without actually watching his every move.

This is exactly where passive activity sensors and smart dispensers come in. Think of them as simple, low-cost secret agents. They offer that crucial middle ground, letting you monitor your parent's daily rhythms silently. You get peace of mind. They keep their independence. Everyone wins.

### 💡 Pro-Tip: The "No-Camera" Rule
Many aging parents flatly refuse smart home technology because they associate it with cameras. To get your parent on board, make a firm promise: No cameras in private spaces. Explain that passive sensors only track actions (like a door opening), not images or video. This simple boundary almost always reduces their resistance.

The Magic of Contact Sensors: Invisible Peace of Mind

Forget about expensive home security systems or high-definition cameras. You don't need them to know if your parent is active and healthy. In fact, one of the most powerful tools in smart caregiving costs less than twenty dollars and is roughly the size of a pack of gum. We call it a contact sensor.

A contact sensor is pretty simple: two small plastic pieces. One holds a magnet, the other a tiny wireless transmitter. You stick one piece on the door frame, the other on the door itself. When the door’s shut, they sit side-by-side, completing a magnetic circuit. Crack the door open, the magnet pulls away, the circuit breaks, and the sensor shoots a silent wireless signal to your smart home hub or smartphone. Easy.

For Harlan, Isidora carefully placed these sensors in two key spots: the refrigerator door and the medicine cabinet in his main bathroom. She didn’t need to see what he was eating. She certainly didn’t need to watch him brush his teeth. She just needed a quiet confirmation that he was up, moving, and accessing the things he needed to get through his day.

When Harlan opened the refrigerator at 8:00 AM for his milk, the sensor noted it. Twenty minutes later, the medicine cabinet opened, and another tiny digital tick registered. No flashing lights, no loud noises, no intrusive lenses. To Harlan, absolutely nothing had changed. But to Isidora, those two silent ticks were gold. Proof that her father was awake, upright, and tackling another day.

These sensors are ridiculously easy to install. Most just use simple double-sided tape—no screwdriver required. They run on tiny coin-cell batteries that often last a year or two, and your smartphone app will even warn you when the battery's getting low. Couldn't be simpler.

Setting Up Smart Alerts: The "Quiet Warning" System

Okay, so sensors in your parent's home? That's only half the battle. If your phone buzzed every single time your dad opened the refrigerator, you'd quickly go crazy. Trust me. That's 'notification fatigue,' and within a few days, you'd start ignoring the alerts entirely, completely defeating the purpose of the system. We don't want that.

To make this system actually work, you have to flip your thinking about alerts. Instead of wanting to know when your parent does something, you only want to know when they don't do something. This is what we call exception-based alerting.

Isidora, smart cookie that she is, set up her smart home app with a simple rule: Alert me only if the medicine cabinet doesn't open between 7:00 AM and 11:00 AM.

Isolate the Tech with a Guest Network

To keep your parent's home truly secure, you need to keep their smart gadgets separate from their personal life. If a cheap smart light bulb gets hacked, you do not want that hacker getting a straight path to the iPad your mom uses to check her bank balance or medical portal.

The easiest fix? Create a guest network.

Almost every router made in the last ten years can do this. Think of a guest network as a separate, blocked-off lane on the highway. Devices on this network can talk to the internet, but they cannot talk to the computers on your main network.

By putting every smart camera, plug, and voice assistant on a dedicated guest network, you build an instant firewall. If a cheap, off-brand motion sensor has a security flaw, the damage is boxed in. The intruder is stuck in the digital guest house, staring at a blank wall, unable to touch your parent's sensitive data.

How to Set Up the Guest Network

1. Log into the Router: Find the physical sticker on your parent's router. It will have an IP address (something like `192.168.1.1`) and an admin password. Type that address into any web browser while you are connected to their Wi-Fi. 2. Find the Guest Settings: Look for a menu option labeled "Guest Network" or "Secondary SSID." 3. Turn it On: Enable the network and name it something clear, like OtisSecureTech. 4. Lock It Down: Make sure the security protocol is set to at least WPA2. Create a tough password that is easy for you to remember but impossible for a bot to guess. 5. Move the Gadgets: Reconnect every smart camera, sensor, plug, and smart speaker to this new guest network. Keep only their primary computer, phone, and tablet on the main home Wi-Fi.

When the Grid Goes Down

Blizzards, heatwaves, or even a tripped circuit breaker can instantly kill a smart home. When the power cuts out, your parent’s digital lifeline goes black. Worse, when the power finally turns back on, many smart devices get confused. They fail to reconnect automatically, leaving your parent unprotected even after the lights are back on.

You can fix this by addressing the two biggest weak points: the router and the recovery settings.

The router is the brain of the entire operation. If it loses power, everything dies. The simplest fix is an Uninterruptible Power Supply (UPS). A UPS is just a heavy battery backup strip. By plugging the modem and the router into a UPS, your Wi-Fi will keep broadcasting for hours during a blackout. This gives battery-powered sensors and cameras the power they need to send warning alerts straight to your phone.
