The Quiet Between Us

A sharp spike of heat bloomed right behind Cosima Theriot’s breastbone, rushing up her neck like spilled hot tea.

Mid-November in the Blue Ridge Mountains. Outside, it was barely above freezing, but inside her rattling Volvo, Cosima was roasting. She cursed, slamming the window crank down with one hand. The damp, pine-heavy mountain air slapped her face.

Her forty-four-year-old body was throwing another tantrum. For six months now, these sudden, suffocating flushes had been arriving uninvited—vivid reminders of a shifting tide she couldn't stop. She tugged at the collar of her cheap cotton sweater. She wanted to peel off her own skin.

Just breathe. She gripped the steering wheel. In for four. Out for four.

The asphalt wound higher, clinging to the rocky lip of the pass before dropping into the valley where Maple Hills slept. It was a postcard-perfect mountain town. The kind of place tourists fled to when they wanted to escape their lives.

Cosima wasn’t escaping. She was just out of options.

Three months ago, she’d been the principal designer of a thriving boutique firm in Atlanta. Twenty years of sweat. She’d drawn blueprints with her left hand while her baby daughter napped in a playpen by her drafting table, sacrificing sleep, sanity, and any semblance of a social life to carve out her name.

Then Gregory happened.

He was her partner. Her fiancé. The man she trusted with her heart, her business, and her checkbook.

As it turned out, Gregory was also a thief. He systematically drained the firm's accounts, forged her signature on unauthorized loans, and vanished to South America with a twenty-six-year-old yoga instructor. When the dust settled, Cosima had zero dollars, a stack of lawsuits from furious vendors, and a reputation so thoroughly wrecked that even her oldest clients wouldn't return a text.

She sold the house. She sold the furniture, the jewelry, everything but her pride and her car, just to pay off the immediate debts. This job in Maple Hills—restoring the historic Crestview Theater—was her last line of defense against a cardboard box. It paid next to nothing, but the eccentric owner, Hester Dumont, was throwing in free room and board, plus a tiny bonus if she finished on time.

Cosima pulled the Volvo to the curb.

The Crestview was a massive, brick-and-stone Art Deco beast that had once been the crown jewel of the county. Now, its marquee was missing half its letters. THE RE TIE ER, it read. The grand arched windows were boarded up with ugly gray plywood.

She turned the key. The engine shuddered, gave a wet hiss, and died.

She caught her reflection in the rearview mirror. Gray-streaked brown hair pulled back in a messy, impatient knot. Hazel eyes shadowed by deep, bruised circles. There was a smudge of grease on her jaw from checking her oil three towns back.

"Well," she muttered to the quiet dashboard. "You're still breathing."

She grabbed her leather portfolio—the only expensive thing she had left—and stepped into the biting cold.

The heavy oak doors groaned as she pushed them open. The air inside smelled of water damage, old popcorn, wet wool, and cold plaster. The scent of a building waiting to die.

"Cosima? That you?"

The voice rattled out of the lobby's shadows. A woman stepped from behind a curved ticket booth.

Hester Dumont looked exactly like her letters: a force of nature. She had to be seventy, sporting a crown of thick, silver curls and a sweeping wool coat in a shade of plum that bordered on scandalous. Her fingers were thick with turquoise rings, and she leaned on a silver-tipped cane that she used more for dramatic punctuation than actual support.

"Hester," Cosima said, reaching out to shake hands. "Good to finally meet you."

Hester ignored the hand, lunging forward to wrap Cosima in a brief, surprisingly tight hug. She smelled of lavender soap and stale cigarette smoke.

"Let me look at you," Hester said, pulling back and squinting through oversized, tortoiseshell reading glasses. "Yes. Good. You have the eyes of someone who’s actually taken a beating. I can't stand those young decorators they send up from the city. Smooth faces, smooth minds. No grit."

Cosima managed a dry smile. "I've got grit. Mostly because I can't afford anything else."

Hester cackled, rapping her cane against the terrazzo floor. "Even better. Come on. Let me show you the disaster."

They walked through the double doors into the main theater.

Cosima stopped. Despite the decay, the space caught in her throat. A massive domed ceiling loomed above, painted with faded gold-leaf constellations. The balcony swept along the back in a graceful concrete curve, though several of the velvet seats had been ripped out, leaving rusty metal bolts sticking out like broken teeth.

"The roof leaked for a decade before I bought it," Hester said, pointing her cane toward the ceiling. "Plaster's crumbling on the east wall, the stage floor is rotting, and the dressing rooms downstairs look like a crime scene. But look at the bones, Cosima. Just look at them."

"They’re beautiful," Cosima whispered. Her designer’s brain was already spinning, calculating plaster restoration, wondering if she could salvage the gold leaf with a chemical wash instead of a full, budget-killing replacement.

"I have a shoestring budget," Hester warned, her sharp eyes boring into Cosima's. "I know what Gregory did to you. The design world is small, and the gossips are loud. I don't care about that. I care that you know how to make a dollar look like a hundred. Can you do it?"

A familiar, cold knot of anxiety tightened in Cosima's stomach. "I can. But I need to know who’s handling the structural work. If the foundation isn't right, any cosmetic work I do will just peel off in a year."

"I’ve got one man," Hester said. "He’s doing the structural work, the stage rebuilding, and the woodwork. He’s a genius, but he’s... particular."

"Particular how?"

"He doesn't talk."

Cosima stared. "Excuse me?"

"He doesn't use his voice," Hester said simply. "Not a word. Hasn't spoken in years. If you want to talk to him, write it down on a pad, point, or learn to read his face. He’s been working here for a month, living in the old projectionist’s room upstairs to keep an eye on the place."

Before Cosima could ask anything else, a loud, resonant crack echoed through the space.

It came from the stage.

A man was kneeling near the arch, using a pry bar to lift a rotted section of oak flooring. He was broad-shouldered, wearing a faded gray thermal shirt with the sleeves pushed up to his elbows, revealing thick, muscular forearms dusted with pale sawdust.

As if sensing them, he stopped. He laid the crowbar down with a soft, metallic clink and stood up.

Cosima’s breath caught.

He wasn't some old, wizened mountain carpenter. He was in his mid-thirties, possessing a raw, quiet gravity that seemed to pull the air right out of the room. His face was striking, with a sharp, square jawline covered in a thick, dark brown beard, and eyes so dark they looked almost black under the dim stage lights. He had the heavy, grounded presence of someone who had survived a storm and chosen to stay in the quiet aftermath.

"Darius," Hester called out, her voice booming in the empty theater. "Come down here. I want you to meet our designer."

Darius didn't rush. He climbed down the wooden steps at the side of the stage with a slow, deliberate grace. He wore heavy work boots, dark canvas pants covered in faint paint splatters, and a leather tool belt slung low over his hips.

As he approached, Cosima felt a sudden, ridiculous wave of self-consciousness. Her forty-four-year-old body felt clumsy, worn down by the long drive and the heavy weight of her failures. She felt the gray in her hair like a brand.

Darius stopped three feet away. He was tall, easily six-foot-two, and he smelled of fresh pine shavings, copper, and cold rain.

His dark eyes found hers. No polite, superficial blink. No fake social smile. He just looked at her, his gaze heavy and intensely focused. It felt like he was reading the lines on her forehead, the tension in her shoulders, the exhaustion in her eyes.

"Darius, this is Cosima Theriot," Hester said. "She’s the boss of the look. You two are going to have to work together to make sure the structural repairs align with her historical designs."

Darius reached into his back pocket and pulled out a small, leather-bound notebook and a carpenter's pencil. With quick, fluid strokes, he wrote something, tore the page out, and handed it to Cosima.

His fingers brushed hers as she took the paper. His skin was warm and calloused, sending a sudden, sharp jolt of static electricity straight up her arm.

Cosima looked down at the paper. In neat, block letters, he had written:

WELCOME TO THE RUINS.

She looked back up, her eyes meeting his. "Thank you, Mr. Kincaid. I’ve seen the photos of your structural work on the balcony support. It’s incredibly precise."

Darius gave her a single, slow nod. His expression didn't change, but his eyes softened slightly, a faint glimmer of appreciation warming their dark depths.

"Darius is working on the orchestra pit next," Hester said, pulling a pack of cigarettes from her pocket and then remembering where she was, putting them back with a sigh. "Cosima, I’ve set you up in the old office on the second floor. It has a cot, a small stove, and a sink. It’s not the Ritz, but it’s dry and it’s free."

"It’s perfect," Cosima said, though her heart sank slightly at the prospect of sleeping on a cot. At her age, her lower back was already a daily negotiation.

"I’ll leave you two to get acquainted with the space," Hester said, turning toward the exit. "I have a board meeting with the town historical society. They want to turn this place into a parking lot. We have six months to prove them wrong. Don't waste time."

With a wave of her cane, Hester was gone, the heavy lobby doors swinging shut behind her.

The silence that settled over the auditorium was massive.

Cosima stood at the foot of the stage, her portfolio clutched to her chest like a shield. She could hear the faint drip of water somewhere in the rafters, the distant rumble of a truck on the main road, and the sound of her own breathing.

Darius hadn't moved. He stood there, his hands resting on his tool belt, watching her with a quiet patience that felt almost physical.

"Well," Cosima said, her voice sounding thin in the vast space. "I suppose we should start by looking at the stage. Hester mentioned the joists underneath are rotting?"

Darius nodded. He gestured with his hand, inviting her to follow him up the steps.

As they walked up onto the stage, Cosima was acutely aware of the contrast between them. Darius moved with an easy, fluid power, his boots making solid, confident thuds on the old wood. She felt stiff, her knees aching slightly from the cold dampness of the theater.

He walked over to the section of floor he had been prying up. He knelt down, pointing his flashlight into the dark void beneath the stage.

Cosima leaned over, trying to see. To get a better look, she had to drop to her knees. A sharp pain shot through her left patella as it hit the hard oak floor, and she let out a soft, involuntary hiss of air.

Immediately, a warm, heavy hand settled under her elbow, easing her down so she didn't drop her weight on her joints.

Cosima froze. Darius’s hand was large, his fingers wrapping completely around her forearm, holding her with an effortless strength. Through the fabric of her sleeve, his palm radiated an intense, steady heat.

She looked up. He was kneeling right next to her, so close she could see the silver threads hiding in his dark beard, the faint fan of laugh lines around his eyes, and the pulse beating steady in his neck.

He didn't pull his hand away immediately. He looked at her knees, then at her face, his brow furrowing in a silent, questioning look.

"I'm fine," she said, her voice a little too quick, a little too breathless. "Just... forty-four-year-old knees. They don't like cold floors."

A faint, lazy curve touched the corner of Darius’s mouth beneath his beard. It wasn't a mocking smile; it was warm, almost intimate. He slowly released her arm, then pointed the flashlight beam down into the hole.

"See?" Cosima said, forcing her mind back to the professional track. "The support beam there is completely split. Is that water damage from the roof, or did the plumbing leak?"

Darius reached into his pocket for his notebook, but before he could write, Cosima leaned closer to see the angle of the split. Her shoulder brushed his arm.

A sudden, fierce hot flash hit her like a wall of fire.

The heat bloomed instantly, a suffocating, red-hot wave that made her skin prickle and her breath catch. Sweat broke out at her hairline. She felt dizzy, her heart hammering against her ribs. She gripped the edge of the stage floor, trying to fight through the sudden wave of nausea.

Not now, she pleaded with her body. Please, not right in front of him.

She pulled back, sitting on her heels, her breath shallow. She began to fan herself with her hand, her face burning. "I'm sorry. I just... I need a minute. It’s very warm in here."

The theater was freezing, their breath forming faint plumes in the air.

Darius didn't write anything. He didn't look confused or uncomfortable. He simply set the flashlight down, took off his heavy flannel overshirt, and laid it gently across the dusty floor next to her. Underneath, his gray thermal shirt clung to the broad planes of his chest and shoulders.

He stood up, walked to the side of the stage, and came back a moment later with a clean, unopened bottle of water. He knelt down, unscrewed the cap, and handed it to her.

Cosima took the bottle, her fingers trembling. "Thank you," she whispered.

She took a long, cold drink, letting the water cool her throat. She pressed the cold plastic bottle against her burning cheek, closing her eyes. She felt exposed, stripped of her professional armor, reduced to a collection of failing, middle-aged parts in front of a man who looked like he had been sculpted out of granite.

When she opened her eyes, Darius was watching her. There was no pity in his face. There was only a deep, respectful attention.

He took his notebook and wrote quickly:

NO NEED TO APOLOGIZE. THE THEATER CREAKS. SO DO WE.

Cosima read the note, and a sudden, wet laugh escaped her lips. "Some of us creak more than others, Mr. Kincaid."

He took the notebook back and wrote:

DARIUS.

"Darius," she corrected herself, the name feeling heavy and rich on her tongue. "I'm Cosima."

He gave her that slow, deep nod again. He picked up his flannel shirt from the floor, shook the dust off it, and instead of putting it back on, he folded it carefully and placed it under her knees, providing a thick, soft cushion between her joint and the hard wood.

The simple, unasked-for kindness made a tight knot form in Cosima's throat. She wasn't used to being taken care of. For twenty years, she had been the one doing the taking care of—managing her daughter's life, carrying her clients' anxieties, and shielding Gregory from his own incompetence until he finally ruined her.

To have this silent, powerful man look at her, see her physical vulnerability, and quietly accommodate it without making her feel small... it was a sensation she didn't know how to process.

"Thank you," she said, her voice softer now, more honest.

Darius pointed his flashlight back into the hole, his shoulder resting lightly against hers as they both looked down into the dark.

For the next hour, they worked in a strange, rhythmic harmony. Cosima would point to a section of the blueprinted stage layout she had drawn from the old archives, and Darius would write a brief note—support is good, needs sister joist, or must replace entire section.

He didn't need words. His silence wasn't a void; it was a space filled with intention. He communicated with the tilt of his head, the sharp focus of his dark eyes, and the precise, confident movements of his hands.

Cosima found herself watching his hands. They were large, with square palms and long, blunt fingers. They were stained with walnut dye and bore a few small, faint scars near the knuckles. They looked like hands that could tear down a wall, but when he handled the delicate, eighty-year-old brass floor vents, he did so with a gentle, almost reverent touch.

"We need to preserve these vents," Cosima said, tracing the delicate, stylized leaf pattern of the brass with her finger. "They’re original Art Deco. If we polish them up, they’ll be beautiful against the dark oak."

Darius reached out, his hand stopping just above hers. He waited until she looked up, then he touched the brass next to her finger. He looked at her, his eyes dark and intense, and gave a slow, firm nod.

He took his notebook and wrote:

I WILL CLEAN THEM MYSELF. BY HAND.

Cosima smiled, and for the first time in months, the weight in her chest felt a little lighter. "You're a perfectionist, aren't you, Darius?"

He looked at her, his lips twitching into a full, genuine smile that completely transformed his rugged face, making him look younger, warmer, and dangerous to her peace of mind.

He wrote:

ONLY FOR THINGS WORTH SAVING.

Cosima’s heart skipped a beat. The words felt too heavy, too loaded for a simple conversation about brass vents. She looked away, her cheeks warming again, though this time it had nothing to do with her hormones.

"I... I should probably go up and look at my quarters," she said, standing up from his flannel cushion. Her knees felt much better, thanks to his care. "And get my bags from the car."

Darius stood up immediately. He didn't write anything this time. He simply walked off the stage, headed out to the lobby, and disappeared through the front doors.

By the time Cosima gathered her portfolio and walked out to the street, Darius was already lifting her two heavy suitcases and her rusted toolbox out of the Volvo’s trunk. He carried them as if they weighed nothing, his shoulder muscles tensing under his gray shirt.

"You don't have to do that," she called out, though she was secretly relieved. Her back had been twingeing since Virginia.

Darius didn't stop. He gave her a brief, sideways glance that told her, quite clearly, that he wasn't going to let her carry her own luggage.

She followed him back inside and up the grand, winding staircase to the second floor.

The old theater office was located at the end of a long, dusty corridor. Darius pushed the door open with his foot and stepped inside, setting her bags down on the wooden floor.

The room was small, but clean. A single iron cot stood in the corner, made up with crisp white sheets and a heavy wool blanket. A small wood-burning stove sat against the brick wall, a neat stack of split birch logs piled beside it. A small sink and a pine table with a single chair completed the space.

"It’s cozy," Cosima said, walking over to the window. It looked out over the main street of Maple Hills, the mountains rising up like dark giants in the twilight.

She heard a soft rustle behind her.

Darius was kneeling by the wood stove. He had opened the iron door and was quickly, expertly building a fire with kindling and birch bark. Within minutes, a bright, cheerful crackle filled the room, and the sweet scent of burning birch began to warm the cold space.

He stood up, brushing his hands off on his pants.

"Thank you, Darius," Cosima said, standing near the table. "For everything. The fire, the bags... the flannel."

He walked toward her, stopping just a foot away. In the small room, his presence was overwhelming. The warmth of the stove was rising, but the heat coming off him felt more immediate, more electric.

He looked down at her, his dark eyes searching hers in the dimming light. He raised his hand, his fingers hovering near her face for a fraction of a second, before he gently, carefully reached out and tucked a stray lock of silver-streaked hair behind her ear.

His fingertips brushed the sensitive skin of her temple.

Cosima’s breath hitched. Her skin tingled where he touched her, a sudden, wild spike of desire blooming deep in her belly. It had been years since a man had looked at her like this—not as a partner, not as a mother, not as an investment, but as a woman.

Darius’s gaze slid down to her lips, then back to her eyes. His expression was fierce, intense, and completely silent.

He stepped back, giving her a small, respectful nod.

He walked to the door, paused with his hand on the frame, and looked back at her one last time before slipping out into the quiet corridor, closing the door softly behind him.

Cosima stood in the middle of the room, her hand rising to touch her temple where his fingers had just been. Her skin was warm. Her heart was racing.

She walked over to the cot and sat down, the old springs groaning under her weight. She looked at her hands, worn and lined, and then out at the dark mountains.

She had come to Maple Hills to rebuild her life from splinters. But as she watched the firelight dance across the ceiling, Cosima realized that the quiet between them might just be the most dangerous thing she had ever encountered.

Cosima Theriot stood in the dead center of the lobby, her cheap heels sinking into the threadbare red carpet. She tugged at her collar. The stiff, off-the-rack linen was a discount-store buy from three weeks ago—a far cry from the tailored silk she used to wear before the lawyers and the liquidators finished stripping her life down to the studs. Every time that scratchy fabric bit into her collarbone, it felt like Gregory's hand. Or rather, the cold absence of it.

Gregory. Twenty years of trust, her savings, and her reputation as an architectural conservator, all gone overnight. Now she had a bruised ego and a dry-mouthed panic that kicked her awake every morning at four.

She checked her watch. 9:10 AM. Hester Dumont had promised the master carpenter would be here by nine sharp. Hester held the purse strings for this entire restoration, which meant Cosima played by Hester's rules. Even when those rules meant working with a crew she hadn't vetted.

"Hello?" Cosima called out.

The hollow space of the auditorium swallowed her voice instantly.

The only reply was a steady, rhythmic scritch-scrape. A hand plane, somewhere near the stage.

She marched down the sloped aisle, her heels clicking against the concrete. The theater was beautiful, even with the rot. The plaster moldings along the proscenium arch crumbled like stale wedding cake, and the velvet seats had faded to a dusty, sunburned rose. But the bones were magnificent. She felt that familiar, stupid ache in her chest—the urge to save things nobody else cared about. Maybe because she felt like one of those things herself lately. At forty-four, she was done being the bright young thing people noticed. Her left knee now predicted rain twenty minutes faster than her phone, and the silver at her temples was a permanent fixture she couldn't afford to dye anymore.

She reached the orchestra pit.

A man stood on a low platform near the stage apron, working a massive piece of mahogany wainscoting. Broad shoulders. A faded gray T-shirt, damp and stuck to the curve of his spine. His hair was dark, salted heavy with white at the temples, cropped close to a skull that looked like it had survived a bar fight or two. He didn’t blink when her shoes clattered near the edge of the pit.

"Excuse me," she said, louder this time. "Are you Kincaid?"

Nothing. The plane slid down the wood. A perfect, curling ribbon of mahogany fell to the floor, smelling of old sap and cedar.

"Sir?" Cosima climbed the side steps onto the stage. The heat up here was a wall. It smelled of mineral spirits, kerosene, and ninety-degree dust. "I’m Cosima Theriot. The lead conservator. We had a nine o'clock to go over the structural reinforcement."

He didn't look up. He took a rag, wiped the grain, and ran a thick, scarred thumb over the surface.

Heat flared in her chest. Not just from the room. She’d spent the last twelve months being dismissed by banks, sneered at by former colleagues, and ignored by landlords. She was done being invisible.

She stepped right into his space, stopping two feet from his sawhorse, and smacked her clipboard against the wood.

His eyes were a cold, pale gray. The color of winter water. He was forty-six, according to Hester's notes, but he looked carved from something hard and heavy. A thin white scar split his left eyebrow, and his jaw was dark with silver-and-black stubble.

"I've been calling your name," Cosima said, her voice tight. "We have a schedule. Hester wants the dry rot report by Friday, and I can't write it if you're—"

He didn't look at her eyes. He was watching her mouth.

Before she could finish, he reached into his back pocket, pulled out a small leather-bound notebook and a stubby carpenter's pencil, and scribbled something. He tore the page out and thrust it at her.

His hand was huge. Calloused, yellowed, stained with walnut oil. When her fingers brushed his to take the note, the heat of him felt like a radiator left on in July.

I am deaf. I read lips. Speak slower. Stop shouting.

The blood rushed to her face so fast it stung. The anger evaporated, leaving her hollow and deeply embarrassed. She looked from the paper to his face. His gray eyes were flat, watching her with a calm, patient detachment that made her feel about six inches tall.

"I'm sorry," she said. She forced her lips to move deliberately, pointing to her chest. "I didn't know."

Darius gave a short, curt nod. He took the notebook back, wrote another line, and flipped it open.

Hester told me you were coming. You're late.

She let out a sharp, dry laugh. "By ten minutes. The bridge was backed up to the highway."

He didn't write back. He just tapped the face of his scratched diving watch, then pointed at the mahogany. My clock is running.

"Look," she said, taking a slow breath. Her mind usually ran at eighty miles an hour, her words stumbling over themselves to keep up. She had to force the brakes on. "We have to work together. The columns on stage left are compromised. I need you to shore them up before my assistants start on the rigging."

He watched her lips. His eyes dropped briefly to her throat, where a bead of sweat was tracing a slow line down her collarbone, then flicked back up.

He raised his hands. His fingers moved with a sudden, sharp fluidity. Thick, scarred knuckles cutting through the dusty light, shaping shapes she couldn't read.

"I don't... I don't know sign," she muttered.

Darius stopped. A tiny twitch at the corner of his mouth looked suspiciously like amusement. He picked up the pencil.

You talk too much. The wood tells me what's broken. I don't need a report.

"The city inspector does," she said, stepping closer. The smell of him hit her—no cologne, just cedar, sweat, and the sharp tang of kerosene. It was raw. Real. It made her stomach do a strange, long-forgotten loop. "And I'm the one signing the permits. No shore-up, no work."

Darius stared. The silence between them felt thick, heavy with the weight of the empty room. He didn't look mad; he looked like he was sizing up a difficult piece of timber. He walked right past her, his shoulder brushing hers, the heat of his skin radiating through his shirt.

He stopped near the stage left wing where the huge vertical beams of the proscenium met the floor. He pointed down.

Cosima followed. She wanted him to see she wasn't some city bureaucrat who only knew how to use a red pen. She stepped onto the raised wooden platform where the old pulley ropes hung.

"See this?" she said, pointing to the dark rot near the base. "It's not just surface damage. It's—"

His hand shot out, palm flat, warning her to freeze.

Too late. She took one step too many, and her heel caught the edge of a loose scaffold board.

The wood groaned. The temporary iron piping underneath shifted with a sharp, heavy clack.

Cosima's balance vanished. She gasped, her clipboard flying out of her grip, her papers raining down into the black abyss of the orchestra pit.

She braced herself for the concrete.

Instead, she hit a wall of muscle.

Two arms, hard as oak, locked around her ribs.

Darius moved with the kind of instant, heavy reflex you only get from years of manual labor. He caught her mid-air, his chest slamming against her back to stop the fall. His hands clamped onto her hips, pinning her back against him.

The breath knocked out of her. She was pressed flat against him—every line of his chest, the hard plane of his stomach, the solid columns of his thighs. She could feel his heart thudding through his wet T-shirt, heavy and steady.

For five seconds, neither of them breathed. The dust motes drifted in the amber sunbeam from the high windows. The silence wasn't empty anymore; it was hot, charged, and crowded.

Cosima's hands were wrapped around his forearms. His skin was dark, hairy, the muscle beneath her palms like concrete. A strange, sharp heat bloomed in her gut—a sudden, electric reminder of her own body.

It had been years since anyone had held her like this. Not with the polite, transactional touch of her marriage, and certainly not with Gregory's cold avoidance at the end. This was heavy. Direct. Darius’s fingers were dug into her hips, his palms burning hot through her pants. He was holding her like she was about to fall off a cliff.

She turned her head, her breath hitching.

His face was inches away. Up close, his skin was lined from years of outdoor glare, his nose slightly crooked. His jaw was set, his nostrils flaring as he took her in. She smelled of old paper and cheap lavender; he smelled of timber and sweat.

His gray eyes dropped to her mouth.

Her heart hammered. Her hips shifted back against him, an old, instinctual response she couldn't stop if she tried. A small, quiet sound caught in her throat.

Darius didn't move, but his grip tightened. His thumbs pressed hard into her hip bones—a heavy, deliberate pressure that sent a shiver straight down her back. His eyes went dark, the color of a storm rolling in over the water. There was no polite hesitation there. Just two forty-something adults standing in the ruins of an old theater, realizing exactly what was happening.

Slowly, he eased her back onto her feet.

But he didn't pull his hands away. One hand stayed on her hip, light but solid. He looked down at her feet, then up to her face, raising an eyebrow. Steady?

She nodded. Her legs felt like wet cardboard. She kept her hand on his chest just to stay upright, feeling the heavy thump of his heart under her palm.

"Thank you," she breathed.

He watched her lips. Then, instead of reaching for his notebook, he took her hand off his chest.

He lifted it. He pressed her open palm flat against the side of his neck, right over his jugular. He held her fingers there until she felt the hard, rhythmic jump of his pulse.

Then, he took his other hand and laid it gently against her throat, his thumb resting over her own racing heartbeat.

He looked deep into her eyes, his thumb brushing the soft skin of her neck.

Feel that? his eyes said. Same speed.

Cosima let out a shaky, ragged breath, her eyes closing for a second as she leaned into his hand. Her skin felt raw, electric. When she opened her eyes, she saw a slow, knowing satisfaction in his gaze.

He let his hand slide down her neck, his thumb tracing her collarbone before he finally stepped back. The sudden rush of cold air made her shiver.

Darius picked up his pencil from the floorboards. He didn't look at her as he flipped his notebook open, but his movements were slower now, less aggressive. He wrote one last line and handed it to her.

I will shore up the stage left columns today. You stay off the scaffolding.

Cosima read the messy handwriting. A real smile—the first one in a year—tugged at her mouth. She looked up, making sure he was watching.

"Thank you, Darius."

He didn't nod. He just held her gaze for three long seconds, his gray eyes steady, before turning back to his bench. He picked up his plane and went back to work.

But as Cosima walked down the stage steps to gather her papers, the rhythmic scritch-scrape behind her was different. Slower. More deliberate.

And she knew, for the first time in a long time, she wasn't invisible.
