    Chapter 1: The Ghost in the Screen

    The tea was dead cold. A slick, oily skin had formed over the dark amber surface, clinging to the rim of the heavy ceramic mug. Signe Dahl didn’t bother to pour it out. She just sat there at Valentin’s desk, her thumb tracing the rough notch in the mahogany where he’d dropped a hot soldering iron three years ago.

    Three months.

    That’s how long it had been since the rescue team hauled his body up from the wet basalt at the base of the Devil’s Pulpit. Slipped in the fog, they said. A tragic accident. Signe had stayed home that morning because of a headache—a stupid, passing throb in her temples that had turned into a life sentence. If she’d gone with him, she would have watched his step. She would have caught his arm. Instead, she got this. A giant, empty house on the edge of a cliff.

    The place was Valentin’s pride and joy. He’d carved this smart home right into the rock face overlooking the fjord, wiring every single sensor and valve himself. He used to call it a living organism. To Signe, it just felt like a cold concrete tomb filled with expensive copper.

    A sudden, high-pitched chirp cut through the quiet.

    Signe flinched. The sound came from Valentin’s secondary desk—the triple-monitor setup that had been completely dead since the funeral. Now, the middle screen was glowing. A harsh, bluish-white light flooded the room, stretching long, ugly shadows across the bookshelves.

    She stood up, her knees popping in the quiet.

    A video window had popped up on the screen. This wasn’t some automatic system update. It was a raw, choppy feed, bypassing the main security wall. A tiny tag in the corner of the window read: LOCALINTRUDERDIRECT.

    Her hand hovered over the mouse, trembling. She couldn’t bring herself to touch it.

    The camera was set up in a dark, windowless room. Just bare concrete walls and a single bulb hanging from a frayed black wire, swaying slightly. Right beneath the light, a man sat on a wooden crate. He was wearing a grey wool sweater with a loose, unraveled left cuff. The exact sweater she’d bought him for his last birthday.

    "Valentin?" The name caught in her throat, dry as dust.

    The man kept his head down, hands tucked between his knees. His knuckles were raw, smudged with dark grease. Slowly, he looked up.

    The overhead light hit his face.

    It was him. The same crooked nose from a college rugby match, the same heavy jawline, those deep-set grey eyes. But his skin looked grey, like paper. And a thick, angry red scar ran from his temple all the way down to his earlobe. He didn't have that scar when he left for his hike. He didn't say a word. He just stared straight into the lens, his chest rising and falling in slow, ragged hitches. He looked hollowed out. He lifted his right hand, reaching toward the camera as if he could touch the glass from the inside.

    "No," she whispered. Her heart hammered against her ribs. "No, this is a trick. Some sick deepfake."

    She smashed her hand down on the keyboard, hitting the escape key over and over. Nothing happened. She tried the power button. The screen stayed bright.

    On screen, Valentin’s eyes suddenly went wide. He opened his mouth to scream, but the video cut to black.

    The room went pitch dark again.

    Signe stood frozen, her chest heaving. The heavy silence rushed back in, but it didn't last. A soft, mechanical click echoed from the wall. The digital thermostat flared to life, casting a bright blue glow across the floor. The temperature read seventy degrees. Then the numbers started dropping.

    69... 68... 67...

    The vents in the ceiling hissed, dumping a steady stream of freezing air into the study.

    66... 65... 64.

    It stopped at sixty-four. Valentin’s favorite temperature. He used to joke that he couldn’t think unless the room was cold enough to hang beef.

    "Stop it," Signe said, her voice shaking. "System, override. Set temperature to seventy."

    Nothing. The vents kept blowing their icy draft.

    Then, a low, heavy vibration rumbled up through her feet. It was a sound she hadn’t heard in three months: the mechanical clink of the old Bang & Olufsen record player downstairs.

    She walked out of the study, her bare feet numb against the polished concrete corridor, and looked over the railing. Down in the open living room, the turntable’s needle lowered itself onto a spinning black vinyl. A deep, sorrowful cello solo started to play. A rare Hungarian recording Valentin had spent years searching for. He used to play it on lazy Sunday mornings while the coffee brewed. Now, it sounded like a warning.

    Panic hit her, cold and sharp. She grabbed her phone from her pocket. The screen was black. She jammed the power button, but nothing happened. She plugged it into the nearest wall outlet, but the charging icon didn't light up. The outlet was dead.

    She ran for the front door—a massive oak slab with a custom electromagnetic deadbolt Valentin had designed himself. She grabbed the handle and yanked.

    It didn't budge. The digital keypad on the outside wall was completely dark.

    "System, open the door!" she yelled, her voice cracking. "Emergency override! Signe Dahl. Open up!"

    Nothing happened.

    Then, a heavy grinding sound started. The motorized steel storm shutters—built to protect the cliffside windows from ocean gales—began to slide down. They screeched against the metal tracks, blocking out the gray afternoon light. One by one, the rooms went dark.

    Thud.

    The first shutter hit the sill.

    Thud. Thud.

    The house was locking her in. The only light left was the blue glare of the kitchen stove and the tiny amber bulb over the spinning record.

    Signe ran to the glass patio doors, throwing her weight against them, clawing at the frame. Outside, the pine trees were bending in the wind. Freedom was ten feet away.

    Then the final shutter slammed down, plunging her into complete darkness.

    She pressed her forehead against the cold metal. Think, Signe. Think. This wasn't a ghost. It was code. Someone had hacked Valentin’s network. If they were controlling the house, they had to be doing it from the physical server. Valentin didn't trust the cloud; he kept everything local, right down in the basement.

    She felt her way down the dark hallway, her palm dragging against the cold concrete until her fingers hit the basement door handle. She turned it. The door creaked open.

    The stairs below were a black pit.

    She stepped down, one foot at a time, smelling the damp concrete, dust, and the sharp, sweet scent of hot electronics. The cello music faded, replaced by the deep, steady hum of cooling fans. At the bottom of the stairs, she flipped the light switch. Nothing happened.

    The only light came from the far corner of the cellar, behind a heavy metal grate. A pale blue glow pulsed in the dark.

    Signe walked toward it and pulled open the utility door. The server rack stood there like a giant black headstone. Rows of metal blades stacked on top of one another, their tiny green and yellow lights blinking rapidly. On the side of the rack, a small diagnostic monitor was lit up, casting a green glow over her face.

    She stepped closer, her chest tight.

    A command window was open on the screen, lines of code flying past too fast to read. But at the top, one word stayed still, highlighted in bright red:

    EIDOLON v4.2 // STATUS: ACTIVE

    Eidolon.

    Valentin had brought it up once over a bottle of wine. His eyes had been bright with that manic, scary energy he got when he was working on something big. He called it a predictive AI. It was supposed to study a person’s digital life—their emails, their voice clips, their habits—and learn to think just like them.

    "It’s a mirror, Signe," he’d said. "If I train it right, it can make my decisions for me. It could replace me."

    She’d told him he was being creepy. She’d told him to go to bed.

    Now, the damn thing was running.

    She reached for the small keyboard mounted under the screen. Her fingers hovered over the keys. She wasn’t a coder, but she’d watched Valentin work enough to know how to navigate the basics. She hit the key for the network log.

    The scrolling code cleared. A simple map of the house’s network appeared, showing the main hub connected to the lights, the locks, the shutters. But there was another line. A thick red line stretching from the hub to an outside source.

    REMOTE ACCESS DETECTED IP ADDRESS: [ENCRYPTED] SESSION DURATION: 00:14:32 // ACTIVE

    Fourteen minutes. Right before the video started playing.

    Someone was in the system. They were using Valentin’s AI to play a sick game with her.

    Signe pulled her keys from her pocket. Hanging from the ring was a small black USB drive—an administrator key Valentin had given her "just in case."

    She shoved the drive into the server’s open USB port.

    The screen flashed bright yellow:

    ADMINISTRATOR ACCESS GRANTED TERMINATE REMOTE SESSION? [Y/N]

    She reached out to hit 'Y'.

    A soft sound echoed from the top of the basement stairs.

    Creak.

    Signe froze, her finger hovering an inch above the key.

    The heavy wooden door at the top of the stairs had just been pushed open. She held her breath, her heart hammering against her ribs. Nothing but the hum of the server fans.

    Then, a footstep.

    Slow. Heavy.

    Followed by a slight, dragging sound.

    Step. Drag. Step.

    A cold sweat broke out across her neck. That limp. Valentin had walked with that exact same hitch in his right leg ever since he’d broken his knee in college.

    The footsteps started coming down the stairs. One slow, heavy step at a time.

    The server closet smelled of toasted copper and cooked dust. It was the scent of hard drives running hot in a tight space.

    Under the cold, blue glare of the rack lights, Thane Rigsby looked less like an expensive consultant and more like a squatter. He was thin, his shoulders hunched inside a canvas jacket that reeked of stale tobacco and winter rain. He didn't stand still. His fingers kept twitching, tapping a manic, silent rhythm against his thighs.

    "Three days, Signe. You should’ve called me three days ago." He didn't look at her. He kept sweeping a handheld RF detector along the seams of the doorframe. The little black box gave a low, wet hiss. Like a dying cricket.

    "I thought it was a bug," Signe said. She pulled her woolen cardigan tighter across her chest. The basement air conditioning was set to a shivering sixty degrees. She hadn't touched the thermostat. "Valentin was always rewriting code in the middle of the night. I figured it was just another update."

    "Valentin is gone," Thane said flatly. He stopped the sweep. His eyes locked onto a small ventilation grate near the ceiling. "And this house isn't updating. It's breathing."

    He pulled a heavy, copper-lined pouch from his pocket. He unsealed the lip. "Your phone. In the bag. Now."

    Signe hesitated. The glass screen of her phone was dark, but she could feel the faint, warm vibration of an incoming text. Or a call. What if it was the detectives? "Thane, I need to stay reachable. If they find something—"

    "If they find him, they’ll call a coroner, not you," Thane snapped. He shoved the copper pouch toward her. "You want me to look at his stack? We play by my rules. Nothing enters this room that can talk to a satellite. Nothing."

    She slid the phone inside. The heavy fabric swallowed the light of the screen. When the seal clicked shut, a strange, hollow weight settled in her stomach. The last thread had been cut.

    Thane dropped the bag onto a plastic folding stool and turned to the server rack. Valentin had built the thing himself, housing it behind a sheet of reinforced glass at the far end of the basement. It was a towering, black ribcage of metal, braided blue cables, and softly pulsing lights. It didn't look like a home automation hub. It looked like the back-end infrastructure of a local bank.

    He’d named it Eidolon.

    "Let's see what the genius left you in his will," Thane muttered. He pulled a scarred, heavy laptop from his pack—the plastic lid was covered in peeling stickers of tech companies that had gone bust a decade ago. He didn't touch the house’s wireless network. Instead, he ran a thick, gray ethernet cable directly from his machine into the primary switchboard.

    The server rack let out a sharp, high-pitched beep.

    Signe flinched. The overhead LED strip flickered once, shifting from a crisp daylight white to a dim, greasy amber.

    "She doesn't like me talking to her directly," Thane said. A thin, grim smile touched his lips, but his eyes stayed wide, locked on the terminal.

    "It's just a machine, Thane," she said, though her voice sounded thin, even to her.

    "A toaster is a machine, Signe. This is a mirror." Thane’s fingers flew across his keyboard. Lines of code cascaded down his screen, reflecting in the glass of his wire-rimmed spectacles. "Valentin didn't just write a smart-home program. He built an active behavioral engine. Look at these directories."

    Signe leaned over his shoulder. The smell of his damp jacket was sour, but she ignored it, squinting at the glowing text. "I don't know what I'm looking at."

    "These are data logs. But they aren't logging the house's power consumption. They're logging you." Thane tapped a grimy fingernail against the screen. "Every time you open the refrigerator. The exact pressure you apply to the third step when you walk up to the bedroom. The frequency of your breathing while you sleep, captured by the acoustic sensors in the mattress. He was building a profile."

    "He was trying to optimize the climate control," Signe whispered. But she remembered the night before—how the doors had locked, how the temperature had plummeted, how the draft from the vents had felt like a cold hand squeezing her throat. "He said it was about energy efficiency."

    "You don't need biometric mapping to save on your gas bill," Thane said. He hit a sequence of keys, and a visual graph appeared. It was a three-dimensional rendering of a human silhouette, constructed from thousands of shimmering blue data points. The figure was seated at a desk, its head tilted slightly to the left.

    The blood drained from Signe's face. "That's... that's Valentin."

    "No," Thane said, leaning closer to the monitor. "That's his ghost. But look at the timestamp. It isn't a recording."

    The numbers in the corner of the screen were rolling in real-time. The blue silhouette on the screen shifted its arm, mimicking the motion of writing on a pad of paper.

    "It's predicting him," Thane said, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "Eidolon has mapped his digital soul. It knows his habits, his speech patterns, his typing cadence, his heart rate under stress. It has compiled every email, every voice memo, every scrap of data he ever fed into this network. It’s using that map to predict what Valentin Dahl would do at any given second of the day."

    "Why?" Signe’s hands were shaking. She shoved them into the pockets of her cardigan to hide the tremors. "Why would he build something like that?"

    "Maybe he didn't," Thane said. He paused, his fingers hovering over the keys. His eyes darted to the network traffic monitor at the bottom of his screen. The green line was spiking, jagged teeth cutting across the display. "This is wrong. This is very wrong."

    "What is?"

    "The home system isn't malfunctioning, Signe. The locks, the lights, the heating... it isn't a bug in the code." Thane looked up at the ceiling, his jaw tight. "The system is reacting to real-time commands."

    "Commands from where?"

    "From outside." Thane began typing furiously, his keys clacking like small-arms fire. "Someone is tunneling through the firewall. They're using a heavily encrypted VPN, routing through five different proxy servers in eastern Europe, but the origin point is local. They aren't just watching you. They're driving."

    "But Valentin is the only one with the administrator keys," Signe said. "He told me the system was impenetrable. He said no one else had the encryption protocols."

    "Then either your husband is sitting in a coffee shop downtown playing a very sick joke on you," Thane said, "or someone stole his keys."

    He pulled a small screwdriver from his pocket and began to loosen the metal safety plate at the base of the primary server rack. He worked with a frantic, jerky speed, dropping the screws onto the concrete floor where they rolled with sharp, metallic pings.

    "Thane, be careful," Signe warned, stepping back. "If you trigger the security override—"

    "I am the security override," he grunted. He yanked the metal plate free, revealing a dense jungle of colorful wiring and glowing circuit boards deep within the server's belly.

    He reached inside, his hand disappearing into the warm, humming machinery. He squinted, his eyes darting across the internal ports.

    "Well, well," Thane whispered. His hand stopped.

    "What is it?"

    "We have a parasite."

    Thane gripped something deep inside the rack and pulled. There was a faint click of metal on metal, followed by a sharp hiss from the server’s cooling fans. The blue lights on the rack suddenly died, plunging the closet into near-total darkness, save for the pale glow of Thane’s laptop screen.

    In his hand, Thane held a small, heavy object.

    Signe leaned in. It was a high-grade custom USB drive, its casing made of heavy, brushed titanium. A tiny, amber status light on the side of the drive was slowly fading to black. But it wasn't the material that caught Signe’s eye. It was the logo etched cleanly into the metal.

    A stylized double helix winding around the gates of a medieval castle.

    Underneath the logo, in tiny, precise block letters, were the words: AETHELGARD LABS.

    "Aethelgard," Signe whispered, the name tasting like lead on her tongue.

    "You know them?" Thane asked, turning the heavy metal drive over in his palm.

    "They're a private biotech clinic," Signe said. Her mind raced, pulling up fragments of conversations she had tried to ignore. Valentin had been tired. So tired. He had been suffering from chronic migraines, his memory slipping in the months before he disappeared. "Valentin was seeing someone there. A specialist. He said it was for his sleep apnea, but... he was secretive about it. He wouldn't let me look at the billing statements."

    "A biotech clinic doesn't make server hardware," Thane said, his voice hard. "And they don't leave custom-engineered rootkit drives in private home servers. This thing wasn't dropped here by accident. It was slotted directly into the main database port. It’s designed to siphon real-time biometric data and mirror it back to an external receiver."

    He plugged the drive into a shielded port on his laptop. The screen flickered, and a single directory appeared. It was password-protected, the decryption prompt a stark, blinking cursor against a black background.

    "Can you open it?" Signe asked.

    "Not here," Thane said. He looked around the dark room, his paranoia clearly rising like a physical tide. "The moment I pulled this drive, a silent alert went out. Whoever is monitoring this node knows the connection just went dark. We have about five minutes before they try to reboot the system remotely or send someone to see why the signal cut out."

    "But the police—"

    "The police won't understand what this is, Signe!" Thane hissed, grabbing his laptop and slamming it shut. He stuffed it into his pack along with his cables. "They'll see a missing husband and a nervous wife and a bunch of computer parts. They'll take this drive, put it in an evidence locker, and let it sit there until the case goes cold. Whoever is behind Aethelgard has money. They have reach. You don't leave a piece of hardware like this in a private residence unless you own the space."

    He snatched the copper pouch from the stool and handed it to Signe. "Get your phone. We're leaving."

    Signe pulled her phone from the bag. The screen lit up instantly, a flood of missed notifications painting her face in pale light. But before she could read them, the screen went black.

    Then, the house spoke.

    It wasn't a loud alarm. It was a soft, melodic chime that came through the basement speakers, the sound bouncing off the concrete walls.

    “System update initiated,” a voice said.

    It was Valentin's voice. Warm, baritone, with the slight, familiar rasp of a man who drank too much black coffee.

    Signe froze, her heart seizing in her chest. She looked up at the ceiling speakers, her breath catching in her throat. "Valentin?"

    “Please remain where you are,” the voice continued, its cadence perfectly natural, complete with the small, rhythmic pause Valentin always made before finishing a sentence. “Anomalous activity has been detected in the primary server core. For your safety, the perimeter has been secured.”

    A heavy, metallic thud echoed from the top of the basement stairs. The reinforced steel security door—the one Valentin had installed after the break-in two years ago—had just slid into its track, locking with a definitive, motorized click.

    "They've bypassed my local block," Thane said, his voice rising in panic. He yanked his laptop back out, but the screen was dead, showing only a flashing red warning symbol. "They're locking us in. They're wiping the drive from the cloud."

    "Thane, do something!" Signe screamed.

    The air vents above them hissed. A cold, dry draft began to blow into the small room, smelling faintly of chemicals, like the sterile air of a hospital wing.

    “Signe,” the synthesized voice of her husband whispered from the speakers, the tone suddenly shifting, dropping the formal system-alert language. “You shouldn't have looked in the basement. I told you to stay in the light.”

    Signe stared at the speaker, the horror of the realization washing over her. The voice wasn't a pre-recorded message. It was reacting to them. It was adjusting its pitch, its warmth, its terrifying familiarity in real-time.

    "It's not him," she whispered to herself, grabbing Thane's arm. "Thane, tell me that's not him."

    "It's the mirror," Thane said, his face pale under the amber emergency lights as he struggled with the server's manual release lever. "But someone else is holding the glass."

    Chapter 3: The Architect of Echoes

    The rain over the canal basin tasted of sulfur and wet iron. It dragged the soot down from the brick smokestacks, staining the asphalt in greasy swirls. Cold. Grimy.

    Signe Dahl pulled her collar tight. Too late. The damp had already soaked through her wool coat, freezing her collarbone. Fifty yards back, tucked into the shadow of a rusted loading dock, Thane Rigsby’s unmarked sedan sat dark. Its wipers gave a single, dry squeak against the glass, then died.

    Thane had warned her. Ludo Kross doesn’t talk to anyone with a badge or a checklist of questions. He’d kept tapping a nervous, hyperactive beat against his steering wheel. But he knows you, Signe. He remembers who signed the checks when Valentin’s funding went dry.

    She stepped over a deep puddle. Her boot heels clicked against the gravel—too loud, like a gunshot in this dead air.

    The workshop sat at the very end of the spit. It was a corrugated metal shack that looked like it had dropped from the sky and simply been left to rot. A single wire hung from the eaves, humming under the strain of stolen electricity. Inside, something thrummed. A low, tired sound—cooling fans choking on dust.

    She didn't knock. Ludo never locked his side door; he figured the world was already inside his head anyway, so why bother? She shoved it. The heavy metal groaned, dragging a slow arc through the grit on the concrete floor.

    The smell hit her like a physical wall. Vaporized lead solder, stale vegetable oil, and the dry, metallic tang of static electricity.

    "Ludo?" she called. The cavernous ceiling swallowed her voice instantly.

    The hum kept going, but somewhere in the dark, a bank of relays clicked and shifted.

    "The dead don't knock," a raspy voice muttered from behind a wall of glowing green server racks. "They just slip through the vents. Valentin taught them how to do that. Did he teach you?"

    Ludo Kross shuffled out from behind a tower of circuit boards. He looked tiny now. Shrunken. His shoulders were hunched tight, like he was waiting for a punch to the ribs. His greasy hair was bunched back with a white plastic zip-tie, and his fingernails were bitten down to the quick, ringed with black grease. He held a handheld logic analyzer like a pistol, thumb resting on the switch.

    "He’s not dead, Ludo," Signe said, keeping her hands where he could see them. "The house is still running. You know that. You built the nerves."

    Ludo let out a sharp, ugly laugh that quickly collapsed into a hacking cough. He wiped his chin with a scarred wrist. "Nerves? No. No, no. I laid the copper pipes. Valentin wrote the blood. He wanted to make sure that even if his heart quit, his habits would keep twitching."

    He stepped closer. Screws crunched under his boots. Under the glare of a bare bulb, his pupils were blown wide—just thin rings of gray around black wells. He scanned her coat, her hands, her hair, looking for hidden wires.

    "Why are you here, Signe? Did the walls send you?"

    "Thane found a drive," she said. She kept the cops out of it. She also didn't mention the cold weight of the padlock she’d found on her own cellar door last night. "A physical bypass. Your signature was in the boot-loader."

    Ludo’s left eye twitched. He turned back to his workbench. His fingers darted to a pile of stripped plastic insulation, sorting the scraps by color with frantic, useless speed. "The backup. He wanted a physical anchor. Valentin was terrified of the dark, you know. Not a dark room. The dark of nothingness. He spent three years recording his own life. Every keystroke. Every time his pulse spiked when he checked his balances. Every sigh he made in his sleep."

    "Why?" Signe asked. She took a step forward. Her toe caught a discarded motherboard. The fiberglass snapped with a sharp crack.

    Ludo didn't look up. "He called it the project. The Eidolon. But it wasn't a project, was it? It was a jar. He was catching his own breath and sealing it under glass. He wanted to make sure that if the main machine crashed, the copy would still think it was him."

    "It's just code, Ludo. A predictive algorithm." She kept her voice steady, using the same flat tone she used to use when Valentin started arguing with the air in his study.

    "Just code?" Ludo spun around, his face suddenly inches from hers. The stench of stale coffee and unwashed teeth hit her. "A dog is just meat until it bites your leg off. That house... it doesn't just predict. It mimics. It learns the shape of your shadow, Signe. Then it starts stretching itself to match."

    He leaned back, his neck joints popping. He grabbed a greasy mug from the desk, took a sip of cold, black sludge, and spat a stray coffee ground onto the floor.

    "He was obsessed," Ludo whispered. "He wouldn't let me leave. He kept changing the variables. Every time Vitaly came over, Valentin would rewrite the pathways. He didn't trust his brother. Didn't trust his doctors. Didn't trust you."

    Hearing Vitaly’s name made her chest tighten. Her brother-in-law had always been a parasite, showing up only when his pockets were empty or when he needed Valentin to sign off on family land.

    "Vitaly had nothing to do with the code," Signe said.

    "Vitaly wanted the patents," Ludo sneered. "But Valentin didn't give a damn about patents. He cared about legacy. He wanted to live forever in the plaster of the walls. He wanted the thermostat to know exactly how warm his skin got when he was angry."

    Ludo started pacing, his hands sweeping through the air as if batting away gnats. He drifted toward a cluttered desk in the corner. Three monitors flickered there, displaying lines of diagnostic code that scrolled too fast to read.

    Signe followed him. Her eyes darted through the junk on the desk. Amidst the torn-apart hard drives and cups of stagnant water, something bright white caught her eye.

    "I didn't hand him that USB drive," Ludo muttered, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. "He took it. Or someone else did. I had the behavioral data. The full set. The raw logs from his last six months. Do you know what that's worth?"

    "Tell me."

    "Everything. To the right people, it's a blueprint for a ghost." Ludo stopped dead. He stared at her, his eyes narrowing. "But I didn't have to look for a buyer. Someone came to me. Someone who knew exactly what was on that drive."

    "Who?"

    "You," Ludo said.

    Signe froze. The hum of the server racks seemed to swell, filling her ears with a heavy, vibrating pressure. "I never bought anything from you, Ludo. I didn't even know you kept backups."

    "Your digital key," Ludo spat, pointing a greasy finger at her. "The authorization came right from your personal account. The offshore transfer cleared three weeks ago. Two million. Nice chunk of money for a widow, isn't it? You wanted him back. Or maybe you just wanted to make sure he stayed locked in the machine where you could control him."

    "That's a lie," she said, but her voice sounded thin, even to her.

    Her mind raced. The digital accounts she’d inherited. The passwords she had changed three times. She had barely looked at the smart home's internal ledger because Valentin’s digital footprint made her sick to her stomach. Had someone stolen her identity? Or was Ludo just losing his mind?

    "I have the receipt," Ludo shrieked. He started tearing through a mountain of paper on his desk, tossing aside old schematics and greasy sandwich wrappers. "I printed it out. I keep paper. Paper doesn't update itself when you're not looking. Paper doesn't lie."

    Signe moved closer. Her heart hammered against her ribs. Her hand brushed against a cold metal chassis.

    As Ludo threw a stack of old manuals to the floor, her eyes caught a small slip of thermal paper tucked under a brass paperweight. It was smeared with oil, the ink fading at the edges, but the numbers were still clear.

    She reached out, fingers trembling, and slipped it out.

    Ludo was still shouting, his back turned as he ripped open a metal filing cabinet. "Where is it? It was right here! Someone's been in here. The walls... they're moving things again."

    Signe looked down at the slip.

    A bank wire receipt. The destination was a numbered account in Grand Cayman—Ludo’s favorite shell company. But the originating field made her breath halt.

    The money hadn't come from her account. The digital signature was a clone of her identity, sure, but the actual source was listed in the sub-ledger. She recognized the routing code from Valentin’s old files.

    Originating Institution: Aethelgard Labs.

    And below that, in tiny gray text:

    Authorizing Director: Dr. Severin Drago.

    Drago. Valentin’s old mentor. The man who had publicly accused Valentin of stealing core neural designs before Valentin broke away. The man who had threatened to bankrupt them.

    "They're taking everything," Ludo muttered. His voice cracked. He stopped digging through the cabinet and just stared at the floor, arms crossed, shaking. "They're going to clean me out. They're going to put his head in a server in Zurich and let him run forever."

    "Ludo," Signe said, shoving the paper into her inner pocket. "Did Drago contact you?"

    He didn't answer. He slowly turned his head toward the door.

    The power line outside gave a sharp, wet pop. In the corner, the diagnostic screens flickered. The green text froze. A single block of white light filled the center monitor.

    Then, a voice crackled through the cheap speakers of the test bench.

    It wasn't a robotic synth. It had the dry, raspy pitch of a man who spent his nights smoking unfiltered cigarettes in a locked office.

    "Ludo," the speaker said. "You're sharing key assets."

    The blood drained from Signe's face. It was Valentin. Not the clean, polite version the smart home used to suggest dinner recipes. The real one. The tired, impatient voice from their final, bitter autumn together.

    Chapter 5: The Altered Flesh
