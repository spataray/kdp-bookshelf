    Cold Horizon

    The metal skin of the 1953 DeHavilland Beaver was cold enough to strip the skin right off your palms.

    Eight o’clock in the morning in Whitehorse. The sun was nothing but a pale, watery smudge bruised against the jagged peaks of the horizon. Twenty below zero. Every breath Avery Blackwood took tasted like broken glass.

    She grunted, throwing her whole weight against the propeller blade to pull it through.

    One.

    Two.

    Three.

    It was a miserable, backbreaking chore. She had to clear the thick oil from the radial engine’s bottom cylinders before she even dared to turn the starter. Her leather mitts were black with grease and stiff from the frost, but she kept moving. If she stopped, she froze.

    "Come on, sweetheart," she muttered. Her breath puffed out in thick white clouds, freezing instantly on the collar of her worn canvas parka. "Don't do this to me today."

    Then, without warning, the furnace lit.

    It didn't start in the engine. It started at the base of her neck—a sudden, blinding wave of heat that rushed upward like a grass fire. Within seconds, she was boiling. Underneath her heavy wool base layer, her thermal shirt, and her thick fleece sweater, her skin broke into a slick, itching sweat. The freezing wind that had been biting at her cheeks a moment ago suddenly felt like a joke.

    "Damn it," Avery whispered. She leaned her forehead against the cold metal of the propeller.

    The aluminum sucked some of the heat from her skin, but the sudden shift made her head spin. At forty-four, her body was playing by a new set of rules. Rules she had never agreed to. Perimenopause in the Yukon was a cruel, sick joke. One minute she was shivering so hard her teeth clicked, and the next she was peeling off layers in the middle of a snowdrift, feeling like she might spontaneously combust.

    She stood there for a full minute, eyes closed, waiting for the tide to recede.

    She didn't have time for this. She had a business to run. Or, more accurately, a business to drag kicking and screaming back from the edge of the grave.

    Twelve months ago, the divorce papers had cleared. Her ex-husband—a slick Vancouver lawyer who had spent fifteen years chipping away at her self-worth—had walked away with their home, their savings, and her dignity. He had left her with Blackwood Aviation. A money-bleeding charter company in the far north, and this single, temperamental airplane. He’d figured she would sell the Beaver, crawl back to the city, and beg him for alimony.

    He didn't know the Yukon. And he sure as hell didn't know Avery.

    She pulled off her mitts. Her bare fingers trembled as she wiped the cold sweat from her forehead. Her skin was flush, her heart hammering too fast. She looked down at her hands. They were the hands of a woman who worked. The knuckles were thick. The skin was dry and lined from windburn and aviation fuel, with a small white scar across her left thumb from a slipped wrench. They weren't the soft, manicured hands she’d kept during her years in Vancouver, trying to play the part of the perfect corporate wife.

    These hands belonged to her. They knew how to fly, how to survive, and how to rebuild.

    She pulled her mitts back on, took a deep, freezing breath that cleared the lingering heat from her lungs, and climbed up onto the cabin step.

    The Beaver was her sanctuary. Inside the cockpit, the smell was a comforting mix of old leather, hydraulic fluid, and the faint, sweet scent of gasoline. Up in the air, there were no lawyers. No bank notices. No whispers about a woman her age trying to make it in a man's game. Up there, it was just her, the wind, and the machine.

    She turned the key. The engine coughed—a heavy, metallic rattle that vibrated right through the soles of her boots. It cleared its throat with a puff of blue smoke, then settled into a deep, roaring rumble that shook the entire fuselage.

    Avery smiled. The tension in her shoulders eased just a fraction.

    "Good girl," she murmured, patting the instrument panel.

    The office of Blackwood Aviation was a small, cedar-sided shack attached to the main hangar. Inside, a drum-shaped wood stove glowed a dull, comforting red in the corner, filling the room with the sharp, sweet scent of burning birch.

    Beryl Blackwood was sitting behind the laminate desk, a steaming mug of black tea clutched between her weathered hands. At sixty-eight, Avery’s aunt looked like she had been carved out of Yukon spruce. She wore a thick flannel shirt, her grey hair pinned back in a messy bun. Her eyes, sharp as a hawk's, tracked Avery the moment she stepped through the door.

    "You look like hell," Beryl said, her voice a gravelly drawl earned from decades of northern winters. "The Beaver start?"

    "She's warming up now," Avery said. She shed her heavy parka and hung it on the wooden peg by the door. She immediately felt another faint ripple of heat across her collarbone, but she ignored it, grabbing the kettle from the top of the stove to pour herself some hot water. "Any calls?"

    Beryl sighed. It was a heavy, deflated sound that made Avery’s stomach drop.

    "The bank called. Miller. He was polite, which is always worse. Says if we don't make the quarterly payment on the hangar lease by Friday, they're going to start the repossession."

    Avery closed her eyes, letting the steam from her mug warm her face. "We have four days."

    "And about three hundred dollars in the operating account," Beryl added gently. "Unless a miracle walks through that door with a fat wallet and a need to fly into the high country, we're done, Avery. I'm sorry."

    "No," Avery said, her jaw tightening. "We are not done. I didn't let that bastard strip me of my life in Vancouver just to lose this. I'll find a client. I'll haul freight for the mining camps. I'll fly timber surveys in a blizzard if I have to."

    "The mining camps have their own contracts, Avery. And the big outfits down south are squeezing out the independents." Beryl looked at her niece, her expression softening. "You've worked yourself to the bone this year. You're exhausted. I see you up at three in the morning, checking the weather maps, drinking cold coffee. You can't keep running on fumes."

    "I have to," Avery said softly.

    She sat down on the squeaking vinyl chair opposite Beryl, cradling her tea. Her body ached. Her lower back was a constant, dull throb from the vibration of the pilot's seat, and her joints felt stiff in the mornings. Sometimes, she felt every single one of her forty-four years. She felt the weight of her failed marriage, the quiet emptiness of her life now that her daughter was off at university in Toronto, and the terrifying realization that she was entirely on her own.

    There was no safety net. No one was coming to save her.

    She looked out the small, frosted window. The runway was a long, white scar cutting through the dark green of the pine forests. Beyond it, the mountains rose like sleeping giants, their peaks jagged and white against the pale sky. It was beautiful, but it was a brutal, unforgiving kind of beauty. It didn't care if she succeeded or failed. It didn't care if she froze.

    Suddenly, the crunch of heavy tires on hard-packed snow broke the silence.

    Avery stood up, peering through the frosted glass. A brand-new, charcoal-grey Ford F-350 had pulled into the gravel lot. It was immaculate, devoid of the mud and salt that coated every local vehicle. The doors were customized, the engine purring with a quiet, expensive power.

    "We expecting anyone?" Avery asked, her pulse spiking.

    "Not unless the tax man bought a sixty-thousand-dollar truck," Beryl muttered, leaning forward.

    The driver’s side door opened, and a man stepped out into the biting cold.

    Avery’s breath caught.

    He did not look like he belonged in the Yukon. He wore a dark, heavy wool coat that cut a sharp, tailored silhouette against the snow, and leather boots that cost more than Avery’s monthly fuel budget. He wasn't wearing a hat, and the wind stirred his dark, thick hair, which was cut short and neat.

    But he wasn't some delicate city boy. As he turned toward the office, Avery took in the sheer breadth of his shoulders, the solid, heavy frame that carried itself with a quiet, powerful confidence. He was tall—easily six feet two—and he moved with an athletic, easy grace that spoke of strength, energy, and a body that had never known the slow, wearing aches of manual labor but was built for power nonetheless.

    He was young. Not a boy, by any means, but a man in his absolute prime. He looked thirty-four, maybe thirty-five, and the contrast between his youthful vigor and her own lived-in, seasoned maturity felt instantly stark.

    "Well, well," Beryl murmured, a slow grin spreading across her face. "Maybe that miracle just walked in."

    The office door opened, and a gust of freezing air swept into the room, bringing with it the scent of him. He smelled of expensive cedarwood, cold leather, and something uniquely clean, like winter rain on stone.

    He stepped inside, closing the door behind him with a solid click. He paused, his dark eyes scanning the small, cluttered office before settling on Avery.

    For a second, the world seemed to tilt. Avery felt the air leave her lungs. His eyes were a deep, piercing hazel, framed by thick lashes and set beneath a strong, heavy brow. He had a rugged, incredibly handsome face, with a jawline that looked like it had been carved from granite, shadowed by a neat, dark stubble.

    But it was the way he looked at her that made her blood run hot.

    It wasn't the dismissive, looking-past-her glance she usually got from male clients who expected a male pilot. It wasn't the polite, distant respect of a stranger. His gaze was slow, deliberate, and incredibly intense. It swept down her body, taking in her faded flannel shirt, her grease-stained jeans, and her heavy work boots, before coming back up to lock onto her eyes.

    A heavy, electric silence filled the room. Avery felt a sudden, familiar warmth bloom in her chest—but this wasn't a hot flash. This was a deep, liquid heat that pooled lower, in the pit of her stomach, a sensation she hadn't felt in so long she had almost forgotten what it was.

    "Can I help you?" Avery asked, her voice steadier than she felt.

    The man stepped forward, pulling off his leather gloves. His hands were large, his fingers long and strong. "I'm looking for Avery Blackwood."

    His voice was a deep, rich baritone that vibrated through the small room, sending a shiver straight down her spine. It was a voice used to giving orders, a voice that expected to be obeyed.

    "You're looking at her," Avery said, crossing her arms over her chest, trying to shield herself from the sheer force of his presence. "And you are?"

    "Kieran Brooks," he said.

    Avery felt Beryl stiffen beside her.

    Even in the remote Yukon, the name Brooks meant something. Kieran Brooks was the head of Brooks Industries, a massive, ruthless Manhattan-based conglomerate that had been buying up mineral rights across the north. He was a corporate titan, a man known for hostile takeovers and stripping companies down to their bones. He was young, fabulously wealthy, and completely merciless.

    "Mr. Brooks," Avery said, her tone cooling instantly. She knew his type. Power, money, and a belief that everything in the world was for sale. "What brings a Manhattan billionaire to a dirt-floor hangar in Whitehorse?"

    Kieran’s lips curved into a slow, dry smile. It was a dangerous, seductive look that made him look younger, yet somehow more formidable. "I need a pilot. And I was told you're the best bush pilot in the territory."

    "I am," Avery said without hesitation. "But I don't usually fly corporate suits who don't know the difference between a spruce and a pine."

    Kieran took a step closer. The heat radiating from him was palpable, or maybe it was just her own body reacting to his proximity. He was close enough that she could see the gold flecks in his hazel eyes, the faint, intriguing scar just above his left eyebrow.

    "I don't need to know the trees, Ms. Blackwood," he said, his voice dropping to a low, intimate murmur that seemed meant for her ears alone. "I need someone who can fly me into the MacMillan Pass in a blizzard and get me out alive. I'm told you're the only one with the guts to do it."

    *

    Avery did not back down. She held his gaze, her chin tilted up, refusing to be intimidated by his height, his wealth, or the raw, masculine energy that seemed to fill every inch of the small office.

    "The MacMillan Pass is treacherous this time of year," she said, her voice hard. "The weather changes in seconds. It’s a death trap for anyone who doesn't respect the mountain."

    "I respect the mountain," Kieran said, his eyes locked onto hers. "But more importantly, I respect competence. And I'm willing to pay for it."

    He reached into his breast pocket and pulled out a sleek leather wallet. He drew out a check, pre-signed, and slid it across the laminate desk toward Beryl.

    Beryl looked down at the check, and Avery saw her aunt's eyes widen. Her fingers trembled slightly as she picked it up. She looked at Avery, her face pale, and nodded slowly.

    "Ten thousand dollars," Beryl whispered. "For a three-day charter."

    It was more than enough to cover the hangar lease. It was enough to keep them afloat for the next three months. It was a lifeline.

    But Avery felt a cold dread settle in her stomach. A man like Kieran Brooks did not throw that kind of money around out of the goodness of his heart. He wanted something. He wanted control. And Avery had spent the last year fighting with every breath in her body to ensure no man would ever control her again.

    "Why me, Mr. Brooks?" Avery asked, ignoring the check. "There are bigger outfits in town. Great Northern has a fleet of modern turboprops. They have heated hangars and pilots who wear uniforms."

    Kieran’s gaze did not waver. He stepped even closer, until he was standing right in front of her. The scent of him—that clean, expensive, powerful scent—wrapped around her like a blanket.

    "Great Northern refused to fly," Kieran said. "They said the weather window was too tight. They said it was too risky."

    "And you think I'm stupid enough to take a risk they won't?"

    "I think you need the money," Kieran said softly.

    The honesty of his words was like a slap in the face. It was brutal, direct, and entirely true. He had researched her. He knew she was struggling. He knew about the divorce, the debts, the desperate state of her business. He was using her vulnerability against her.

    Avery’s temper flared, a hot, liquid anger that burned away her hesitation. She stepped into his space, her chest almost touching his. She had to look up to meet his eyes, but she did not feel small.

    "Listen to me, Mr. Brooks," she hissed, her voice low and dangerous. "I don't care how many billions you have in the bank. Up in that sky, I am the captain. My word is law. If I say we turn back, we turn back. If I say we don't fly, we don't fly. You do not command me. You do not buy my judgment. Do you understand?"

    Kieran did not flinch. In fact, his eyes darkened, a sudden, fierce spark of desire flaring in his hazel depths. He looked down at her mouth, his breath warm on her face. Avery’s heart hammered against her ribs, her body betraying her with a sudden, violent throb of anticipation.

    He liked it. He liked that she was fighting him.

    "I understand perfectly, Avery," he said, using her first name for the first time. His voice was a low, rough caress. "I wouldn't have it any other way."

    He held out his hand.

    Avery looked at his palm. It was wide, strong, the skin of a man who was young and powerful, yet there was a hardness to it that surprised her. Slowly, she reached out and took his hand.

    The moment their skin met, an electric shock ran up her arm. His hand was warm, wrapping completely around hers, his grip firm and steady. Her smaller, rougher hand felt alive in his grasp. She felt the calluses on her own palms rubbing against his smooth, warm skin, a contrast that felt intensely, vibrantly sensual.

    She wanted to pull away, but she couldn't. She stood there, frozen, staring into his eyes, her hand clasped in his, feeling the heat of him seep into her bones.

    "We leave in one hour," she said, her voice a little breathless.

    "I'll be ready," Kieran replied, his gaze lingering on her lips for one more second before he let her go.

    The cold air of the hangar was a relief. Avery stood by the wing of the DeHavilland Beaver, checking the fuel levels with a wooden dipstick. Her hands were shaking, and it wasn't from the cold.

    She could hear Kieran behind her, unloading his gear from the back of his truck. He was moving heavy metal cases with an easy, fluid strength that she couldn't help but watch. He had taken off his heavy wool coat, now wearing a thick, dark grey cashmere sweater that hugged the contours of his chest and shoulders. The fabric clung to his back as he bent over, revealing the play of muscles beneath.

    He was thirty-four. He had his whole life ahead of him, a mountain of wealth and power at his fingertips. And she was a forty-four-year-old divorced pilot with a failing business and a body that felt like it was constantly changing on her.

    Why is he looking at me like that? she thought, a sudden wave of insecurity washing over her.

    She wasn't the kind of woman men like Kieran Brooks looked at. She didn't wear makeup, her hair was a sun-bleached brown pulled into a practical ponytail, and her face bore the fine lines of a life lived in the sun and wind. She was mature. She had lived.

    But when he looked at her, she did not feel old. She felt... wanted.

    "Where do you want these?"

    Kieran’s voice broke her out of her thoughts. He was standing right behind her, carrying two heavy aluminum gear cases as if they weighed nothing. He was so close she could feel the heat radiating from his body in the chilly hangar.

    Avery cleared her throat, pointing to the rear cargo door of the plane. "In the back. Secure them with the straps. I don't want anything sliding around if we hit turbulence."

    "Got it," he said.

    He stepped past her, his shoulder brushing against hers. Even through their thick clothing, the contact felt like a spark of electricity. Avery watched him climb into the cargo bay, his long legs disappearing into the dark interior of the plane.

    She followed him, standing at the door to supervise.

    The space inside the Beaver was tight. Kieran was too big for the cabin, his head nearly touching the ceiling as he maneuvered the cases. He knelt on the metal floor, his thighs stretching the dark fabric of his trousers. As he reached up to pull a cargo strap, his sweater rode up, revealing a strip of lean, tanned skin at his waist.

    Avery’s breath hitched.

    She stared at the smooth, hard muscle of his lower back, the way his spine curved as he worked. She felt a sudden, wild urge to reach out and touch him, to run her rough hands over that smooth, warm skin. It was an urge so primal, so intense, it frightened her.

    She had spent years in a marriage where she had been treated like a piece of furniture, a decorative asset to be shown off and ignored in private. She had forgotten what it felt like to want someone this way. She had forgotten what it felt like to be a physical being, a woman with desires of her own.

    Kieran finished securing the cases and turned around. He was still kneeling, placing his face at her eye level. The cargo bay was dim, the only light coming from the open door where she stood.

    His hazel eyes locked onto hers, dark and intense.

    "Is that to your satisfaction, Captain?" he asked, his voice low, a playful but heavy undercurrent running through his words.

    Avery stood in the doorway, her hands resting on the frame. She felt her body warming up again, but she didn't care. She looked down at him, her heart thumping against her ribs.

    "It'll do," she said softly.

    He did not move to get up. He stayed there, looking at her, his gaze dropping to her chest, which was rising and falling with her shallow breathing. He could see how she was affecting him. He could see the flush on her neck, the way her fingers clutched the door frame.

    "You're shivering," he said, his voice softening, a sudden, unexpected warmth in his tone.

    "I'm not cold," Avery said, and it was the truth.

    Kieran slowly stood up, stepping closer to her in the cramped cabin. He had to bend his head slightly to avoid the ceiling. He was so close she could feel the air shifting between them, the heavy, electric pull that seemed to draw them together.

    He reached out. For a second, Avery thought he was going to touch her face. Her breath caught, her eyes widening.

    But he only reached past her to grab the edge of the door, his arm brushing against her side.

    "Let's get this show in the air," he murmured, his eyes holding hers for one more long, burning moment before he climbed down onto the snowy hangar floor.

    Avery stood in the cabin, her heart racing, her skin tingling where he had brushed against her. She looked out at the cold, white horizon of the Yukon, the mountains waiting for them in the distance.

    She was walking into a storm, she knew that. But as she watched Kieran walk toward the copilot's seat, she realized the real danger wasn't the weather.

    It was the fire he had just lit inside her.

    The takeoff was rough. The wind was howling down the valley, throwing sheets of dry, powdery snow across the runway. Avery sat in the pilot's seat, her hands firm on the yoke, her feet working the rudder pedals with practiced ease.

    Beside her, Kieran sat quiet, his long legs cramped into the small copilot’s space. He wasn't looking at the instruments; he was looking at her.

    "Hold on," Avery called out over the roar of the engine.

    She pushed the throttle forward. The Beaver surged, the old radial engine screaming as it fought the drag of the snow. The plane bumped and rattled, the skis sliding over the ice, before the lift took hold and they broke free of the earth.

    The ground fell away, a white, frozen wilderness opening up beneath them.

    Avery leveled the plane, setting their course toward the dark, jagged teeth of the mountains. The air was turbulent, the plane bouncing in the updrafts. She held the yoke steady, her forearm muscles tensing under her sleeve.

    "You're good," Kieran said through the headset, his voice clear and deep in her ears.

    "Of course I'm good," Avery replied, not looking at him. "I told you, I'm the best."

    "I like a woman who knows her worth," Kieran said.

    There was a heavy, intimate weight to his words that made her throat dry. She looked over at him. He was leaning back in his seat, his gaze relaxed but intense, studying her profile.

    "Why are you really going to the MacMillan Pass, Kieran?" she asked, using his name for the first time. "A man like you doesn't risk his life in a bush plane just to look at some rocks."

    Kieran’s smile faded, his expression turning serious. "There's a gold deposit up there. The largest untapped vein in the territory. My competitors want it. I want it more."

    "And you don't care who you have to step on to get it?"

    "I do what needs to be done," he said simply. "That's how you survive."

    "That's not survival," Avery said, her voice quiet. "That's greed."

    Kieran looked at her, his eyes searching her face. "And what about you, Avery? Why are you still here? You could sell this plane, go back to Vancouver, live a comfortable life. Why stay in the cold?"

    Avery looked out the window at the vast, empty sky, the white mountains stretching out as far as the eye could see.

    "Because down there, I was nothing," she said, her voice thick with emotion. "I was a wife, a hostess, a shadow. Up here, I'm free. Nobody owns me. Nobody tells me who to be."

    She looked back at him, her eyes fierce and proud.

    "I'd rather freeze to death up here than go back to being a ghost."

    Kieran stared at her, his expression unreadable. But there was something in his eyes—a deep, burning respect, a raw admiration that made her chest tighten. He did not see a struggling, middle-aged woman. He saw a survivor. He saw a warrior.

    He reached out, his large, gloved hand resting on the center console, just inches from hers.

    "I don't want to own you, Avery," he said softly through the static of the headset. "I just want to see how high you can fly."

    The words hung in the air, heavy and loaded with a promise that made her blood run hot.

    Ahead of them, the mountains loomed, dark and majestic, a cold horizon waiting to be conquered. Avery gripped the yoke, her heart wild and free, and flew straight into the storm.

    Cold didn't care about leather. The socket wrench bit straight through Avery Copeland's work gloves, icy and relentless. She leaned her entire weight into the tool, bracing her boots against the aluminum stepladder. A hard yank. The stubborn bolt on the Cessna’s engine cowling finally gave way with a sharp, metallic crack. Avery let out a long, ragged breath that blossomed into a ghost of steam in the drafty hangar.

    Forty-four wasn't old, but the Yukon winters had a way of adding interest to every past injury. A heavy, dull ache throbbed across her shoulder blades—yesterday’s souvenir from hauling fuel drums across frozen gravel. She didn't mind it. It was a clean sort of pain. Honest. She’d earned every single ache keeping Copeland Air aloft in a country that actively tried to kill anything stupid enough to stay.

    She smeared grease across her cheek with her sleeve, tucking a stray lock of silver-streaked hair behind her ear. The hangar was too quiet. Ever since Wren had boarded the flight to Vancouver for college three months ago, the silence had a weight to it. The cabin up on the ridge was worse. No floorboards vibrating with bass. No half-dead mugs of herbal tea abandoned on the windowsills. Just... nothing. Nineteen years of living entirely for someone else, suddenly hollowed out. Shrunk down to a single pair of boots clicking on concrete.

    Avery liked the quiet. Or she lied to herself and said she did. It kept things simple.

    The latch on the side door slammed. The hangar door shrieked open, throwing a spray of dry, needle-sharp snow across the floorboards. The wilderness rushed in with it—the sharp sting of crushed spruce, frozen granite, and the iron smell of an incoming blizzard.

    And then came the man.

    He didn't ask permission to be there; he just occupied the space like he owned the deed. Avery didn't budge from her ladder. She kept her wrench in her hand and watched him pull off a pair of shearling-lined gloves. His hands were pale and clean. No calluses. No grease under the nails. A city man's hands, yet they didn't look soft.

    He wore a dark, tailored overcoat that probably cost more than her fuel dock’s entire winter inventory. His boots were pristine leather, the kind of designer gear meant for walking from a town car to a heated lobby, not wading through northern slush. Under a heavy, dark brow, his eyes were cold as slush—a sharp, critical gray that mapped the hangar in three seconds flat before locking onto her.

    "You Avery Copeland?" His voice had the fast, clipped edge of New York. Impatient. Unused to waiting.

    Avery stayed on her rung. Why give up the three feet of height? "I am. And you're tracking snow onto my floor."

    He didn't apologize. Instead, he reached into his inner coat pocket, pulled out a heavy cardholder, and flicked a gold-lettered card onto her workbench. "Heath Radcliffe. I need a pilot."

    Avery glanced down at the card, then back up. Heath Radcliffe. The name had been rattling around the local radio broadcasts all week—some high-finance shark who’d bought out the majority shares of the Blackwood mine. Up close, the corporate-predator label fit. He had a hard, blunt jaw shadowed by a day's worth of dark stubble, and his mouth was a flat, tight line. He looked like something built in a factory to break things.

    "Mr. Radcliffe," Avery said, resting her forearm on the cowling. "The hangar is closed. There's a wall of white rolling in from the pass, and nothing with wings is leaving Whitehorse today."

    Radcliffe took three long strides and stopped at the base of her ladder. Up close, he smelled of expensive cedar wood, cold wool, and the kind of high-end cologne that didn't belong within five hundred miles of the Yukon.

    "I didn't ask for a weather report, Ms. Copeland. I need a flight to the Blackwood Ridge lodge. There's a board meeting tomorrow at dawn. If I'm not on that mountain, a three-hundred-million-dollar buyout falls apart."

    "Then let it fall apart," Avery said. She took one step down the ladder, putting her eyes right at his level.

    He had tight, exhausted lines bracketed around his eyes and a tense pulse ticking in his temple. He was clearly a man who got his way by raising his voice or writing a check. But Avery had spent twenty years flying through mountain gaps that didn't care about his net worth.

    Their eyes locked. The air between them went instantly still, thick and heavy. It wasn't the usual irritation she felt with pushy tourists. It was something sharper. More electric. His gaze dropped for a split second, tracking the line of her throat down to the open collar of her flannel shirt, before snapping back to her eyes.

    Avery felt a sudden prickle of heat under her collar. It had been a long time since anyone had looked at her like that—not as a reliable local mechanic, not as Wren's tired mother, but simply as a woman.

    She swallowed hard, refusing to let him see she was rattled. "The canyon is already a washing machine of snow. Wind shear is hitting forty knots at the ridge. My Cessna can take a beating, but I don't fly suicide missions."

    Radcliffe took another step. He was close enough now that she could feel the heat radiating off his wool coat. "Ten thousand dollars."

    Avery let out a dry bark of a laugh. "No."

    "Fifteen."

    "You don't get it, do you?" She stepped down from the last rung, landing right in his space. Even with her thick-soled boots, he towered over her. "Out here, your money doesn't buy the barometric pressure. It doesn't stop the ice from freezing our wings. If we engine-stall in that pass, we don't get a late fee. We freeze to death in thirty minutes. And nobody finds our bones until May."

    "I've spent my entire life betting against the odds, Ms. Copeland," Radcliffe said, his voice dropping to a quiet, rough murmur. "I don't lose. And I don't hide in a hangar."

    "Then you're a fool," she shot back, her pulse hammering. It felt good to say it. For nineteen years she’d been the sensible one, the cautious mother, the reliable pilot who never took risks. Fighting this arrogant bastard felt like throwing a match into a dry forest. "A rich, stubborn fool who thinks his checkbook can override aerodynamics."

    Radcliffe’s eyes tracked down to her lips, then slowly back up. He didn't look angry at all. If anything, his mouth twitched with something like amusement. "You're not afraid of me."

    "I've flown through active volcanic ash clouds, Mr. Radcliffe. A man in a four-thousand-dollar coat doesn't even make my top ten."

    A slow, crooked smile broke through his stubble. It completely changed his face, taking the cold, corporate edge off him and replacing it with something far more dangerous: charm. "Good. I hate dealing with people who scare."
