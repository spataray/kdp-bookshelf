A Mycologist's Guide to Murder

Chapter 1: The Ring of Death

The fog didn’t drift through the hemlocks; it crawled. Wet wool. It clung to the skin and tasted of salt, diesel exhaust from the distant logging road, and old rot. Beneath the canopy of the Olympic Peninsula, the world was reduced to shades of slate and bruised lichen.

Tressa Penhallow hitched her ash-splint pack higher on her shoulder. The woven wood groaned against her oilskin coat. Every breath hurt her lungs, coming out in gray, ragged plumes. Beside her, Truffle—a shaggy Lagotto Romagnolo with mud-clotted curls—kept his snout glued to the forest floor. He wasn't looking for beauty; his damp black nose was vacuuming up the sharp, mineral stink of rain-soaked basalt, wet needles, and decomposing cedar.

Most people see this valley as a chaotic wall of green. They get lost, they panic, they die. To Tressa, it was a ledger. She read it in spores, threads, and rot. She’d spent her life tracking the subterranean empire that held this wet rock together.

But today, the woods felt off-key. The air was too heavy, carrying a flat, greasy sweetness that didn't belong to late October.

"Find the gold, boy," she muttered, her voice a flat rasp against the damp bark of a cedar. "Show me the chanterelles."

Truffle didn't head toward the rotting alder logs where the yellow caps usually gathered. Instead, he froze. His tail, usually a cheerful, mud-tipped flag, dropped between his legs. A low, vibrating rumble started deep in his chest—a sound Tressa had heard only once before, when a cougar had shadowed them along the high ridge of the Sol Duc.

"Truffle? What is it?"

The dog didn't bolt. He stepped backward, pressing his wet flank against her knee, his gaze fixed on a dense thicket of huckleberry and devil’s club.

The wind shifted. The greasy sweetness hit her fully then. It was a smell she knew from her brief, miserable stint in Seattle, working the back-alley salvage routes before the rain called her back. It was the scent of iron. A butcher’s shop left out in the sun.

Tressa unclasped her curved Opinel knife from her belt. The wooden handle, worn smooth by her own palm and oiled with beeswax, felt cold but familiar. She gripped it, thumb resting on the safety ring, and pushed through the thorny ribs of the devil’s club.

The brush gave way to a small, cathedral-like clearing dominated by a towering Western Hemlock. The tree was a giant, its trunk six feet wide, its shaggy bark weeping gray moss.

And there, cradled in the deep moss between two massive root buttresses, sat Huxley Draxler.

He looked almost comfortable, propped up against the ancient wood as if he had stopped to rest. Except his eyes were wide, milky, and locked onto the gray canopy fifty feet up. The expensive Gore-Tex of his hunting jacket was torn at the shoulder, revealing pale, bloodless skin underneath. His hands, usually busy signing land-clearing permits or waving away the protests of the valley locals, were folded neatly across his chest, fingers interlaced like a man in a casket.

Tressa stopped breathing. Her heart hammered against her ribs, a frantic, trapped bird.

Huxley Draxler. The man who had spent the last six months trying to buy up the Penhallow homestead, who had called her a "moss-backed squatter" at the last town council meeting, and who had promised to run her out of the county if she didn't sell her foraging rights.

Now, he was just another piece of forest floor.

But it was not the body that made Tressa’s throat go dry. It was what surrounded him.

Encircling the corpse in a flawless, mathematical geometry was a ring of pure white.

They rose from the dark moss like tiny, elegant umbrellas carved from bone. They were beautiful, pristine, and utterly lethal.

Penhallow's Field Guide (Mental Index):
Amanita bisporigera.* The Destroying Angel.
Toxin:* Amatoxins.
Effect:* A single cap contains enough poison to shut down a grown man's liver and kidneys in forty-eight hours. No cure. No reversal. Just a slow, agonizing slide into organ failure while you watch your skin turn yellow.

Tressa took a slow, deliberate step forward, her boots sinking into the spongy moss. Her professional curiosity—the cold, analytical shield she used to distance herself from the world—took over. She knelt, her knees dampening instantly as the moisture seeped through her canvas trousers.

"Stay, Truffle," she commanded softly. The dog remained at the edge of the clearing, whining, his ears pinned flat.

She leaned in, her face inches from the pale gills of the nearest specimen. The cap was smooth, satiny, and possessed a faint, sickeningly sweet odor that mimicked honey but ended in a chemical sting.

Then, she saw the first anomaly.

Mushrooms do not grow in perfect, unbroken fairy rings around Western Hemlocks. Not these mushrooms. Amanita bisporigera is strictly mycorrhizal with deciduous trees—broadleaf hardwoods like oak, birch, and beech. Their underground threads, the hyphae, must fuse with the roots of those specific trees to exchange nutrients. A coniferous tree like a Western Hemlock could not host them. It was biologically impossible.

Someone had brought them here.

Tressa reached out with the tip of her knife, gently nudging the base of the nearest stalk. It wasn't rooted in the soil. It had been carefully inserted into the moss, propped up with tiny twigs to make it look as if it were growing naturally from the earth.

She leaned closer to the stem of another cap.

Her breath hitched.

The bottom of the stipe had not been plucked or torn from its original bed. It had been severed. The cut was clean, precise, and made at a sharp, forty-five-degree angle.

Tressa’s fingers tightened on the handle of her own Opinel.

In the valley, every forager had their own way of harvesting. Most of the commercial pickers—the rough-and-tumble crews who worked for the big timber-land distributors—simply ripped the mushrooms out of the dirt, ruining the delicate mycelial mat below. But Tressa had been taught by her father, and her father by his. She always cut. And she always cut at exactly forty-five degrees, a clean slice that allowed the remaining stem to shed water and prevent rot from invading the underground colony. It was her signature. The "Penhallow Cut."

She had taught that very technique to dozens of students in her weekend workshops. She had demonstrated it to the local conservation club. She had even shown it to Sheriff Rufus Cloutier when he had accused her of over-harvesting the state park lands two years ago.

The cut on the stem before her was identical to her own work. It was a mirror.

A cold sweat broke out across the back of her neck, freezing in the mountain wind.

Someone is telling a story, she thought. And I am the main character.

The past, that heavy, suffocating ghost she had tried so hard to bury, clawed its way back to the surface. Three years ago, a tourist had died after eating a wild mushroom soup served at a local lodge. Tressa had supplied the chanterelles for that kitchen. Even though the autopsy later revealed the victim had harvested his own deadly lookalikes from his campsite, the whisper had never left the valley. Tressa's basket. Tressa's poison. It had ruined her father's health, hastening his descent into the quiet dark of dementia, and it had turned her into an outcast, a witch of the woods who lived on the margins.

If Cloutier found this... if he saw these cuts...

"You shouldn't be here, Tressa."

The voice was low, dry, and came from the shadow of a large cedar behind her.

Tressa spun around, her knife raised before she could think. Her boot slipped on a wet root, and she barely kept her balance.

A man stepped out of the fog. He wore a heavy, grease-stained oilskin coat that matched hers, but his trousers were tucked into high leather boots that looked older than the state itself. Llewelyn Pryce. The valley’s resident hermit, a man who claimed to have lived in the high woods since the timber mills shut down in the eighties. His beard was a tangle of gray and pine needles, and his eyes, deep-set and watery, held a strange, feverish light.

"Llewelyn," Tressa said, her voice shaking despite her efforts to keep it steady. She did not lower the knife. "You frightened me."

"He's cold," Llewelyn said, pointing a dirty, thick-nailed finger at Draxler’s body. "The forest took him. The forest doesn't like concrete. It doesn't like iron."

"The forest didn't do this," Tressa said, her eyes darting to the dirt path behind him. "Did you see anyone? When did you find him?"

Llewelyn smiled, revealing yellowed, uneven teeth. "I see everything. I see the wind through the needles. I see the voles under the snow. I saw him walking. He was looking at his little map. His little papers. He wanted to cut down the grandmother tree. The one we're standing under."

He took a step closer, and Truffle let out a sharp, warning bark. Llewelyn stopped, his gaze dropping to the ring of white mushrooms.

"Beautiful," he whispered. "The circle of the white ladies. They dance around him. Did you make them dance, Tressa?"

"No," she said sharply. "They don't grow here, Llewelyn. You know that. They belong in the hardwood draws near the river. Someone brought them."

"Someone who knows how to cut," Llewelyn murmured, his eyes shifting to her hand, where her Opinel knife still gleamed in the dim light. "Someone who cuts clean. Like you."

"Llewelyn, listen to me," she said, stepping forward, her heart pounding. "You can't tell anyone you saw me here. Not yet. I need to..."

What did she need to do? Run? Destroy the evidence? If she gathered the mushrooms, she would destroy the crime scene, making herself look even more guilty. If she left them, Cloutier would have her in handcuffs before the sun set.

"The Sheriff is already at the logging road," Llewelyn said, his voice dropping to a conspiratorial whisper. "He's got his big truck. He’s looking for Draxler. Draxler’s fancy wife called him. Said her husband didn't come home last night."

Tressa’s blood ran cold. The logging road was less than half a mile away. If Cloutier was already searching, it was only a matter of time before his deputies—or his dogs—found this clearing.

"Llewelyn, please," she said.

But the old man was already backing into the fog, his figure dissolving into the gray mist like a ghost made of vapor. "The white ladies don't like secrets, Tressa. They always tell. They always tell."

And then, he was gone. Not even the sound of a snapping twig marked his departure. He knew the woods too well; he moved like the moisture itself.

Tressa stood alone in the clearing with the dead man.

She looked down at Draxler. On his mud-splattered leather boot, there was a tiny smear of yellowish clay. It was a specific, sticky clay—the kind found only in the deep cuts of the old copper mine on the north side of the ridge, miles from here. There was no such clay near the Western Hemlock.

She knelt again, forcing her trembling hands to remain steady. She didn't touch the body, but she leaned in close to his jacket. There was a small, dried leaf caught in the zipper of his pocket. Not a hemlock needle. Not a huckleberry leaf.

It was a leaf from Quercus garryana—the Oregon white oak.

The oak was a hardwood. The natural habitat of Amanita bisporigera.

The pieces of the puzzle began to click together in her mind, cold and sharp. Draxler had been killed elsewhere—likely in the oak groves near the old copper mine—and transported here. The killer had harvested the Destroying Angels from the oak grove, brought them to this hemlock tree, and meticulously laid them out to frame her.

But why here? Why under this specific tree?

She looked up at the trunk of the Western Hemlock. High up, near the first major branch junction, something glinted. It was small, metallic, and barely visible through the thick growth of moss.

Before she could investigate, a sharp sound shattered the silence of the forest.

It was the distinctive, high-pitched crack of a dry fir branch breaking. And it was close. Less than fifty yards away, down the slope.

Truffle bolted toward the sound, his hackles raised, barking furiously.

"Truffle, no! Come back!" Tressa hissed.

She scrambled to her feet, her boots slipping on the damp hemlock needles. She couldn't be found here. Not like this. Not with her knife in her hand and her signature cut on the stems of the very poison that had killed the town's most prominent figure.

She grabbed Truffle's collar, hauling him back into the shadow of a massive cedar.

Through the dense screen of ferns, she saw a flash of orange. A high-visibility vest.

"Over here!" a voice shouted. It was rough, gravelly, and instantly recognizable. Sheriff Rufus Cloutier. "I think I heard something up by the old boundary marker!"

Tressa's breath caught. She had seconds.

She looked at her basket, then at the ring of white death. If she fled, they would find her tracks. If she stayed, she was a murderer.

With a desperate, silent prayer, she slid her knife back into its sheath, grabbed Truffle by the harness, and retreated into the thickest part of the devil's club, moving deeper into the shadows of the forest where the fog was thickest.

Behind her, the heavy thud of boots grew louder, marching steadily toward the clearing of the hemlock. And above, hidden in the moss of the ancient tree, the metallic object caught the dying light of the afternoon, waiting to be found.

Chapter 2: Spores and Suspicions

It was the peppermint that gave him away first. That, and the heavy, sour stench of wet, rotting wool.

You could hear Rufus Cloutier’s boots long before you saw him. He climbed the ridge with a slow, rhythmic crunch, branches groaning under his bulk as he shoved his way through the weeping hemlocks, dumping fat, icy drops of condensation onto the moss below. Tressa Penhallow didn't move. She stayed kneeling in the dirt beside what was left of Huxley Draxler. Her hands were shoved deep into the pockets of her waxed coat, fingers curled into tight fists to keep them from shaking.

Beside her, Llewelyn Pryce crouched like a waterlogged crow. His ragged coat-tail dragged in the black muck. He was muttering under his breath, his yellow fingernails scraping at the soft, rotten bark of a fallen cedar.

"Back off, Tressa," Cloutier barked, crashing through the final screen of damp ferns like an old bull. He paused to catch his breath, his massive chest heaving under a brown uniform that was missing its bottom brass button. His small, pale eyes dropped to Draxler's white, rigid face, then shifted to her. "And you, Llewelyn. Get those filthy claws away from him."

Llewelyn spat a brown stream of tobacco juice into the pine needles, scrambling backward on all fours. "He ain't a gentleman anymore, Sheriff. Just wet meat. Fast rot. The woods are already starting on him."

Cloutier ignored him. He squatted by the body, his leather gun belt creaking like a ship’s timber in a gale. He smelled of stale tobacco, cheap mints, and the grease of the station house stove. He didn't touch the corpse. Instead, he studied the neat, pale circle of Amanita bisporigera—the Destroying Angels—that ringed the dead man’s head like a skeletal crown.

"A miserable way to go," Cloutier muttered. He reached into his breast pocket, pulled out a silver mechanical pencil, and used the tip to nudge one of the smooth white caps. "Very bad. They say your insides turn to soup before the heart finally gives up. That true, Tressa?"

"The amatoxins destroy the liver first," Tressa said. Her voice was too tight, too thin. She kept her eyes fixed on the rough gray bark of the hemlock behind Cloutier’s head. "Then the kidneys. It takes days. By the time you actually start vomiting, the damage is done. There is no cure."

"Days," Cloutier repeated. He leaned down, his nose nearly touching the damp earth. His pencil slid down the stem of a harvested mushroom. He froze. "Well, now. Look at this."

A cold needle of sweat pricked the back of Tressa’s neck.

"Someone’s been harvesting," Cloutier said, tapping the sheared stalk of a mushroom cut clean at the base. "A perfect, beveled forty-five-degree slice. So the rain doesn't collect in the remaining mycelium and rot the underground colony." He looked up, his eyes narrowing into wet, dark slits. "Now, who was it went and lectured the whole village on that exact angle? Down at the parish hall two winters back?"

From the Field Journals of Arthur Penhallow (1884):
I. Never pull. To tear the mycelial thread is to murder the future.
II. The hawkbill blade must slide at forty-five degrees, clean as a surgeon's steel.
III. If the cut is jagged, the rain will settle, the rot will enter, and the colony dies.

Llewelyn let out a sharp, rattling laugh from the shadows. "You cut 'em clean, Tressa. Just like you always do. No tearing. No ripping. A clean slice for a clean death."

"Be quiet, Llewelyn," Tressa snapped, her heart hammering against her ribs.

"He's not wrong, though, is he?" Cloutier stood up, his massive frame blotting out what little gray light filtered through the canopy. "You’re always preaching about the 'sanctity of the spore.' You insisted on the forty-five-degree angle. Said it was the only civilized way to forage."

"It’s basic science, Rufus," Tressa said, rising to her feet. Her knees were stiff from the cold damp. She had to tilt her chin up to meet his eyes. "Anyone who has read a field guide knows to cut, not pull. It’s not a secret."

"Maybe," Cloutier said, his voice dropping to a low, suggestive rumble. "But not many folks in this valley carry a blade sharp enough to make a cut this clean. Most use pocketknives with dull edges that tear the stalk. This..." He pointed the silver pencil at the stump. "This was done with something razor-sharp. Curved, probably. A specialist's tool."

The memory hit her like a physical blow—the weight of her father’s disgraced name. Ten years ago, the village had whispered the exact same things when the old vicar fell ill after eating what he thought were chanterelles. They blamed her father’s sloppy sorting, his failing eyesight, his arrogance. He had died in disgrace, his reputation ruined by a single bad batch she had failed to double-check. Now, the whispers were returning, but this time, the knives were sharper.

"I didn't do this, Rufus," she said softly.

"I didn't say you did," Cloutier replied, though his tone suggested he had already written her name on the arrest warrant. "But your tools are well-known. And your hatred of Draxler isn't exactly a secret, is it? Not after he threatened to evict you from the cottage to clear the south woods for his new timber mill."

"He was going to destroy the ancient mycelial networks," Tressa said, her voice rising. "He was going to kill the forest."

"And now he's dead," Cloutier said simply. "Saves you the trouble of moving."

He turned back to the body, his boots squelching in the mud. He reached down to lift one of Draxler’s legs, inspecting the dead man’s footwear. Draxler had been wearing expensive riding boots, the black calfskin polished to a mirror shine before his death—though now they were caked in filth.

"Look here," Cloutier muttered, more to himself than to them.

Tressa stepped closer, despite her fear. Her eyes, trained for years to spot the minutest variations in soil and flora, focused on the thick muck clinging to the tread of Draxler’s left boot.

It was not the dark, spongy, red-brown hemlock loam of this grove.

The mud on Draxler’s boots was a pale, greasy, slate-gray clay. It was thick and sticky as lard, packed tightly into the crevices of the sole.

"That's Blue Lias," Tressa whispered, her scientific training momentarily overriding her panic.

Cloutier glanced at her. "What's that?"

"The clay," Tressa said, pointing a gloved finger. "It’s Blue Lias clay. It doesn't exist in this part of the woods. The soil here is highly acidic, rich in iron and decayed pine needles. It’s dark brown, almost red. That gray clay only occurs in two places in the county."

"And where would those be?" Cloutier asked, his pencil hovering over his small leather notebook.

"The old abandoned brickworks on the eastern boundary of Euphemia Skeet's land," Tressa said, her mind racing. "And the tidal salt marshes down by the estuary, near where Dr. Ignatius Clade keeps his brackish water specimens."

Cloutier grunted, writing the names down with slow, deliberate strokes. "Euphemia’s place is three miles from here. Clade’s laboratory is even further. What was Draxler doing walking through gray clay before he came up here to die?"

"Maybe he didn't walk here," Llewelyn muttered from the shadows. He was rocking back and forth on his heels, his dirty hands tucked into his armpits. "Maybe he flew. Or maybe someone carried him. Carried the dead weight of a bad man."

"Shut up, Llewelyn," Cloutier barked, but he was staring at the gray clay with genuine confusion. "Draxler’s horse was found tied up by the lower gate. That’s a mile away, and the path is dry gravel. If he walked from the gate, his boots should be covered in red dust, not river mud."

"He was killed elsewhere," Tressa said, the realization settling over her like a cold shroud. "He was poisoned somewhere else, and his body was brought here to frame me. The Destroying Angels... the clean cuts... it’s too perfect. Someone wanted you to find him here, Rufus. In my territory. Surrounded by my signature."

Cloutier closed his notebook with a sharp snap. He looked at Tressa for a long, agonizing moment. The wind sighed through the hemlocks, dropping a spray of cold water onto his hat.

"That's a pretty story, Tressa," the sheriff said, his voice flat. "But until I find another explanation, you don't leave this valley. You understand me? I’m going to have the doctor look at the body, and I’m going to have some people look into your movements yesterday. Don't go wandering off to harvest any more 'specimens.'"

"I have work to do," Tressa said, her voice trembling.

"Your work can wait," Cloutier said. "Go home. Sit by your stove. And pray I don't find your fingerprints on any more dead men."

Tressa didn't wait for him to change his mind. She turned and walked away, her boots slipping on the wet pine needles. Behind her, she could hear Llewelyn’s soft, mocking laughter and the heavy, wet crunch of Cloutier’s boots as he began the tedious process of hauling Huxley Draxler’s heavy corpse out of the hemlock grove.

The air inside Tressa’s drying shed was thick with the scent of dried boletes, earth, and old cedarwood. Bundles of herbs hung from the rafters, casting long, skeletal shadows across the wooden walls in the fading afternoon light. Usually, this place was her sanctuary. Today, it felt like a cage.

She closed the heavy oak door behind her and slid the iron bolt into place. Her breath came in short, ragged gasps.

The cuts, she thought. The forty-five-degree cuts.

She needed to check. She needed to prove to herself that her tools were where they belonged.

She walked quickly to the long workbench beneath the window. The table was cluttered with the tools of her trade: brass magnifying glasses, jars of alcohol, spore-print papers in varying shades of blue and white, and a small, leather-bound chest where she kept her knives.

Her hand shook as she reached for the chest.

She flipped the brass latch. The lid creaked open.

Inside, the velvet lining showed the empty, dusty imprints of her tools. Her small paring knife was there. Her bone-handled spade was there. But the center slot—the one carved specifically to fit the curved cherry-wood handle of her hawkbill foraging knife—was empty.

Tressa’s stomach dropped.

She tore through the drawers of the workbench, knocking over a jar of dried chanterelles that shattered on the floor, sending yellow fragments skittering across the floorboards. She searched her canvas foraging bag, turning it inside out. She checked the pockets of her spare coats hanging by the door.

Nothing.

The hawkbill knife was gone.

It was a unique tool, forged by a local blacksmith for her father thirty years ago. The blade was curved like a bird’s beak, designed to slip under the caps of delicate fungi without bruising the flesh. The handle was dark cherry-wood, worn smooth by decades of her father’s hands, and then her own. Everyone in the village knew that knife.

If Cloutier found it—or if someone had used it to cut those Amanita stems and left it near the scene—she was as good as hanged.

She leaned against the workbench, her head in her hands. The smell of the spilled chanterelles rose from the floor, dusty and earthy, reminding her of her father’s hands, of the day he had given her the knife.

“A tool is an extension of your eye, Tressa,” he had told her, his voice rough with age and cider. “Treat it with respect, and it will never lie to you.”

But someone had stolen it. Someone who knew its value, and its significance.

Through the small, dusty window of the shed, she looked out toward the mist-shrouded valley. The forest was turning a dark, bruised purple as the sun dipped below the ridge.

The gray clay.

That was the key. Whoever had framed her had been somewhere with Blue Lias clay before they brought Draxler's body to the hemlock grove.

Tressa straightened her spine, her panic hardening into a cold, sharp resolve. She could not sit by her stove and wait for Cloutier to find her knife. She had to find it first. And she had to find out who had been walking in the gray mud.

She reached for her spare coat, her fingers tight and determined as she buttoned it up to her chin. She had to visit Euphemia Skeet. Or perhaps Dr. Ignatius Clade.

She stepped out into the damp evening air, the latch of her door clicking softly behind her. The forest was silent, save for the dripping of the trees and the distant, lonely cry of an owl. She took a deep breath, the cold air burning her throat, and set off down the path, her boots sinking into the dark, red-brown loam.

Chapter 3: The Root of All Evil

Scorched vinegar left a taste like a copper coin pressed flat against the back of the tongue. Sour. Metallic. The smell of an old woman trying to scrub the damp out of her own skull.

Inside the greenhouse, the air didn't move. Decades of lead-soldered glass held the rot captive, sweating against the cold October mist rolling off the Ghyll. Outside, the world was nothing but grease and charcoal smudge. Tressa Penhallow stood by the door, her boots sinking into the grey, slate-colored silt of the path.

Scritch. Scritch. Scritch.

Euphemia Skeet didn’t look up. She sat hunched on a three-legged milking stool, her spine curved like a dried willow branch. Her forearms were deep in a shallow tub of grey water, yellowed fingernails digging into the bristles of a horsehair brush. It was a dry, scraping sound. A wood-boring beetle in a dead beam.

"You’re tracking," Effie spat, not stopping the brush. "I don't keep soil I didn't invite, Tressa."

"It’s only sand from the high lane," Tressa said, her eyes fixed on the old woman's wrists.

"Sand is just stone that gave up," the old woman muttered. She pulled her hands from the basin, dripping.

Under the nails, where the water couldn't reach, was a greasy crescent of black. It wasn’t the pale, river-washed silt of the garden lanes, nor the dry peat Effie kept in her seedling boxes. It was heavy. Oily. It had the distinct, sour sheen of decomposed hemlock needles and deep-woods loam—the sort of dirt you only found in the bottom of the ravine where Huxley Draxler’s body had been dragged from the weir eighteen hours ago.

"The Sheriff’s been asking after you, Effie," Tressa said. She reached out, her fingers finding a dried poppy head hanging from the rafters. She rolled the paper-thin crown between thumb and forefinger. Inside, the seeds gave a dry, hollow rattle. Like teeth in a tin box. "He wants to know if you’ve been down by the water lately."

Effie let out a wet, rattling cough that might have been a laugh. "Rufus Cloutier couldn’t find his own backside in a snowstorm without a map and three deputies. Let him ask. Let him bring his boots onto my land. I’ve got nettle tea waiting for his kidneys."

"He’s not looking for tea. He’s looking for whoever put Amanita in Draxler’s morning oats."

The horsehair brush stopped.

The silence went thick. In the corner of the glasshouse, an iron pipe let go a single, rust-colored drop. Plink. Tressa knew that silence. She’d heard it six years ago in the green-walled ward at Bristol, standing over her cousin Ismay while the girl’s skin turned the color of lard. Ismay had survived the Galerina Tressa had failed to spot in the orchard pasture, but her kidneys had shriveled to leather. That was the cost of a bad eye. Nature didn’t argue, and it didn't do refunds.

"The developer is dead," Effie said, her voice dropping into a flat, drone-like register. "The earth has a way of digesting its pests. You spend your life watching things turn to mold, Tressa. You ought to appreciate the efficiency."

"I watch them transform," Tressa said. "Draxler didn’t transform. He suffocated on his own spit while his liver turned to jelly. It's a hard way to die."

"He wanted to bring the iron-tracked engines," Effie hissed, her wet hands clenching into fists against her apron. "He had the papers. He had the signatures from the town council. He was going to clear the old elder grove to put a gravel road straight through my sweet-cicely. Three hundred years of medicine in this soil, Tressa. My grandmother took fever-few from this ground when the plague was in the valley. Draxler wanted to cover it in tar."

"So you cured him?"

"I don't need to cure what the ground already hates," Effie snapped. She stood up, her knee joints popping with a wet, wooden sound. She was small, but she had the sharp, territorial look of a kestrel on a fence post. "If you're asking if I shed a tear for him, save your breath. I’ve been right here. Ask Llewelyn. He saw me yesterday morning, tending the winter savory."

"Llewelyn Pryce doesn’t see anything unless it’s through the bottom of a green glass bottle."

"He sees enough," Effie whispered, stepping closer. She smelled of elder-vinegar, old wool, and the bitter, green scent of crushed leaves. "He sees who goes up the high path after dark. He sees the doctor’s carriage staying late at the manor. Why don’t you ask Ignatius Clade what he was doing with Draxler’s ledger? Or ask the Sheriff why his own brother-in-law held three shares in the timber mill?"

Tressa didn't answer. Her eyes had drifted to the floorboards.

Near the heel of Effie’s left boot, half-stuck to the leather, was a tiny purple-brown scale. A bract from a western red cedar. The only red cedars in the valley grew down in the spray of the waterfall—exactly where Draxler had taken his last breath.

"I have work to do," Effie said, her tone turning sharp as a pruning knife. "The frost is coming early, and the valerian needs lifting before the ground goes hard as iron. Get out of my light, Tressa."

Tressa stepped back, letting the poppy pod swing on its twine. "Don't wash your hands too clean, Effie. Rufus Cloutier has a magnifying glass now. He thinks it makes him a detective."

"Let him look," the old woman said, turning her back. Scritch. Scritch. The horsehair brush went back to work, scraping at the black loam that refused to leave her skin.

*

The air outside tasted of wet iron and oncoming rain. Tressa walked the boundary line, where Effie’s neat beds of angelica gave way to the tangled, briar-choked chaos of the public path. The wire fence between them was old, sagging under the weight of dead nettles.

Here, the ground was torn up. Someone had scrambled up the clay bank, leaving deep, sliding gouges in the wild ramsons.

Tressa knelt, her skirts soaking up the cold moisture of the leaves. Under a rotting birch log, half-buried in fern fronds, something caught the light. She reached out, her fingers brushing past the cold, slippery caps of a cluster of sulfur tufts—bitter, toxic things, but not the killer.

Her fingers closed around cold metal.

She pulled it free. It was a heavy, sterling silver pocket flask, tarnished to the color of wet lead. There was no engraving, but the metal was dented near the hinge, as if it had been trodden on by a heavy boot.

Tressa unscrewed the cap.

The scent rose instantly. It smelled of peppermint, loud and sharp, but underneath was that greasy, sweet stink of crushed cherry-laurel. Benzaldehyde. The doctor's masking scent, used to cover the bitter, metallic taste of raw alkaloids.

She turned the flask over. A smudge of the same black, hemlock-rich loam she had seen under Effie’s fingernails was dried to the base.

"Find anything good, Penhallow?"

The voice came from the hazels. Tressa didn't startle; she simply let her fingers slide the flask deep into her coat pocket before she stood.

Llewelyn Pryce was leaning against a hazel trunk, looking like something the tide had left behind. His wool coat was stiff with grease, his eyes red-rimmed but sharp. He held a peeled hazel switch, tapping it against his boot. He smelled of woodsmoke, wet dog, and spruce beer.

"You're up early, Llewelyn," Tressa said.

"The birds are loud when there’s a carcass in the woods," the hermit said, stepping closer to the wire. "They don’t like the smell of him. Even the crows are turning up their noses."

"Did you see Effie yesterday?" Tressa asked. "She says you saw her tending her savory."

Llewelyn leaned his chin on his hands, which were clasped over the top of his hazel rod. He smiled, showing yellowed teeth that looked too large for his sunken face. "Effie says a lot of things. She says the moon is made of old tallow and that the adders speak to her in the dry season. You believe her?"

"I believe she hates Draxler enough to have helped him along."

"We all hated him," Llewelyn said simply. The mocking edge left his voice, leaving it flat and cold. "The man was a cancer. He looked at a five-hundred-year-old oak and saw nothing but floorboards and silver shillings. He wanted to drain the marsh. Do you know what lives in the marsh, Tressa?"

"The local orchids. The bog rosemary."

"The water-snakes," Llewelyn corrected. "And the old things. The things that were here before the church had a roof. He was going to dig them up. He was going to put his cellar right where the old well-house stood."

"That doesn't justify murder, Llewelyn."

The old man spat into the brambles. "That’s city talk. Out here, if a fox comes into the hen-house, you don't call the town council. You break its neck and hang it on the wire."

"And did you?" Tressa asked, stepping closer to the rusted fence. "Did you break his neck, or did you let Effie brew the tea?"

Llewelyn’s eyes drifted past her, toward the glass roof of the greenhouse where Effie’s silhouette was still visible, a dark shadow against the steamed glass.

"Effie’s got no teeth left," he said softly. "But she’s got a long memory. You want to look at flasks, Tressa, you should look at the ones they keep in the library at the manor. The ones with the silver crests."

"The doctor?"

"He’s got a whole cabinet of 'em," Llewelyn said, his voice dropping to a whisper as the wind stirred the hazel branches above them. "All lined up like little tin soldiers. He was up at the manor three nights ago, arguing with Draxler. I heard 'em from the path. Draxler was shouting about some land deed. Said Clade was a fraud. Said he’d have his license before the month was out."

Before Tressa could press him, the sound of a horse’s hooves echoed from the lane below—sharp, rhythmic, and heavy.

"The law's coming," Llewelyn muttered, his eyes narrowing. "Rufus is on the scent. He’s like a dog with a bone he doesn’t know how to chew."

Without another word, the hermit turned and melted back into the hazel thicket. He moved with surprising speed for a man of his years, his grey coat blending instantly with the wet bark and the fog until he was gone.

Tressa walked back toward the cottage entrance just as Sheriff Rufus Cloutier dismounted his bay gelding. The horse was steaming, its breath rising in long, white plumes in the cold air.

Cloutier was a large man, his leather coat stretched tight across his broad shoulders. He looked out of place among the delicate, pale herbs of Effie’s garden, his heavy riding boots crushing a clump of winter-browned sage as he walked up the path.

"Penhallow," he said, nodding once. His face was red from the wind, his mustache damp with mist. "I thought I’d find you here. You’re like a magpie when there’s something dead in the brush."

"I was visiting a friend, Sheriff," Tressa said, her hands tucked deep into her pockets. Her right hand closed around the cold, heavy curve of the flask.

"A friend who threatened to put a curse on the deceased three weeks ago at the town hall?" Cloutier stopped, looking up at the greenhouse. "Effie Skeet is seventy years old, but she’s got enough spite in her to poison a regiment. What was she doing yesterday?"

"She was here," Tressa said. "Scrubbing her pots."

"Llewelyn says otherwise," Cloutier said, his eyes tracking Tressa’s face with a slow, heavy suspicion. "He was down at the tavern last night, mumbling about old women gathering roots by the Ghyll after dusk."

"Llewelyn was drunk, Rufus. He’s always drunk."

"Maybe," Cloutier said. He took a step closer, his eyes dropping to the wet clay on Tressa’s boots, then to her coat. "What’s that in your pocket, Tressa? Looks heavy."

Tressa’s fingers tightened on the silver flask. If she showed him now, he would arrest Effie by nightfall. The soil on the flask matched the Ghyll, and the smell of peppermint would be enough for a jury of local farmers who already feared the old herbalist’s cabinets. But if Effie had been in her greenhouse all day, whose flask was it? And why did the soil match the deep ravine?

"My moss tin," Tressa said, pulling her left hand from her pocket. She showed him the little rectangular tobacco box she used for liverworts. It was rusted at the hinge.

Cloutier stared at it, his jaw working under his wet mustache. He didn't ask her to open it. The Penhallow name still carried enough weight to keep him from reaching into her pockets, even if the manor house was falling to pieces.

"Don't go getting too close to this, Tressa," he warned, his voice low. "Draxler was a powerful man. He had friends in London. Important friends. They’re sending a coroner down from Leeds on the morning train, and he won’t be as polite as I am."

"I'm just a mycologist, Sheriff," she said. "I only care about the fungi."

"The fungi killed him," Cloutier said, turning back toward his horse. "And whoever picked 'em is going to hang from the oak by the crossroads. Tell Effie I’ll be back with a warrant if I find so much as a single gill of that white mushroom in her kitchen."

Tressa watched him mount his horse and ride down the lane, the gravel crunching beneath the hooves until the sound was swallowed by the fog.

She pulled the flask out again, holding it up to the grey, salt-white light of the sky.

Near the neck, under a smudge of dried clay, was a tiny, stamped mark: a five-pointed star surrounding a serpent-wrapped rod. The Apothecaries' Guild. Not Effie's. It was the sort of silver they gave to medical students at Edinburgh or London when they passed their boards.

She looked back at the glasshouse. Through the condensation, Effie’s crooked shadow was still scrubbing, her arms moving back and forth, back and forth, trying to wash the woods off her skin.

Chapter 4: The Toxicologist’s Riddle

The air in Dr. Ignatius Clade’s basement surgery didn't drift. It sat. It was a thick, greasy soup of boiled vinegar, coal tar, and that distinct, sweetish rot of old iron that clings to the back of your throat for hours after you leave.

Tressa Penhallow kept her hands shoved deep into her boiled-wool pockets. Her right fingers were wrapped so tight around the cold, heavy cylinder of the silver flask she'd lifted from the hemlock grove that her knuckles ached. To keep it from clinking against her specimen tin, she had stuffed her spare woolen glove down first, wedging the silver into the grease-stained yarn. Every time she took a breath, the hard rim of the metal nudged her lower ribs. A silent, bruising accusation.

Beside her, Sheriff Rufus Cloutier stood with his boots planted wide, taking up half the narrow room like a fallen oak blocking a pasture gate. He had one thumb hooked into his waistcoat pocket; the other hand idly turned a small pocketknife over and over. Click. Snap. Click. The sound was like a clock ticking down to an arrest.

"You’re quiet, Penhallow," Cloutier said. He didn't look at her. His eyes stayed glued to the long pine table in the center of the room, where a white linen sheet rose over a shape that was too long and far too still.

"I am listening, Sheriff," Tressa said, her voice dry as winter bark. "And hoping the doctor’s chimney draws better than his lamps."

"The lamps are clean enough," a voice muttered from the shadows behind the table.

Dr. Ignatius Clade stepped into the yellow pool of lamplight. He was a small, wire-stringy man with his sleeves rolled to his elbows, exposing forearms as pale and hairless as blanched celery. He wore a heavy apron of black oilcloth that caught the light with a greasy sheen. He didn't look like a man of science; he looked like a butcher who had taken a sudden, grim interest in human anatomy.

"The chimney, however, is choked with jackdaw nests," Clade continued, wiping his fingers on a gray rag that smelled faintly of turpentine. "I have told the parish council three times. They tell me the dead do not complain of the draft."

"We didn't come for a lecture on masonry, Ignatius," Cloutier said, his knife snapping shut with a sharp, metallic clink. "What did the belly say?"

“Amanita bisporigera. The Destroying Angel. Two drams of the expressed juice will rot a man’s liver from the inside out, yet he will walk, talk, and thank you for his tea until the third day, when the yellow death takes him.”
— Extract from The Penhallow Herbal, 1842.

Clade pulled a pair of wire-rimmed spectacles from his waistcoat, hooked them behind his ears, and turned his back to them. He reached for a tall glass jar sitting on a shelf packed with blue-labeled apothecary bottles. Inside, a grayish, folded mass suspended in yellowish spirit drifted like a drowned jellyfish.

Tressa’s stomach turned. She’d seen sheep slaughtered, had seen her father prepare specimens for his work, but the clinical coldness of this basement always made her feel like she was the one under the glass.

"The belly said very little," Clade murmured. "Because the belly had nothing to do with it."

Tressa stepped forward, her boots scraping on the grit of the stone floor. "What do you mean? The ring of Destroying Angels—the stems were cut. He had to have eaten them."

"He may have had them in his teeth, or perhaps some fool smeared the juice of them on his lips after the breath had already left him," Clade said. He turned, holding the jar up between his face and the lamp. His eyes became enormous, watery discs behind his lenses. "But Huxley Draxler’s liver is as pink and healthy as a newborn pig's. Fatty from brandy, sure, but entirely intact."

Tressa felt a cold splash of relief, followed instantly by a sharper, more dangerous curiosity. "The amatoxins in Amanita don't kill quickly. They take their time."

"Exactly," Clade said, nodding once. His spectacles slipped down the bridge of his thin nose. "The human liver is a stout house. It takes forty-eight hours, sometimes three days, for the poison to tear down the rafters. First the choleraic phase—the purging, the cramping. Then the false recovery, when the patient thinks he’s mended and asks for broth. Only then do the kidneys fail, the skin turns the color of an old lemon, and the blood thickens to sludge. Draxler was walking the north ridge yesterday morning. He was dead before noon."

"He was found in the evening," Cloutier grunted. "Llewelyn found him."

"Llewelyn found a corpse that had already gone cold," Clade corrected. "If Draxler had eaten the mushroom, he would have been sweating, vomiting, crawling through the dirt for water hours before his heart stopped. He’d have torn his fingernails to stubs on the gravel. But look at him."

With a sudden, indifferent flick, Clade pulled the sheet back to the chest.

Huxley Draxler lay under the yellow light. His skin had the leaden hue of wet clay, but his face was peaceful. Too peaceful. Just a slight bluish tint around his nostrils and the corners of his mouth. His hands, resting at his sides, were clean. The nails were trimmed, pale, and free of dirt.

"No struggle," Tressa whispered.

"None," Clade said. "The cardiovascular system simply... folded its tents. Lungs congested, full of dark, uncoagulated blood. The heart stopped mid-beat, paralyzed. It is the signature of a very different guest."

"What guest?" Cloutier asked. He’d stopped playing with his knife. His hand had drifted down to the heavy leather belt around his middle.

Clade set the jar down with a heavy thud. He reached for a smaller container—a shallow porcelain dish containing a few drops of a dark, oily liquid that smelled intensely, suffocatingly of stale pipe dregs and burnt cabbage.

"I found this in the dregs of his stomach," Clade said, holding the dish toward them. "And a similar, much stronger trace upon his tongue. It is not mushroom. It is an alkaloid. Highly concentrated. Soluble in water, but far more stable in spirits."

Tressa leaned in. Her nose wrinkled as the vapor hit her. "Nicotine."

Clade looked at her, his eyes narrowing with a small, grudging flicker of respect. "The girl knows her poisons. Yes. Not the mild smoke of a pipe, mind you. To get a dose this clean, this lethal, you’d have to boil down several pounds of coarse leaf, press the juice, and distill it with potash until it becomes a concentrated oil. A single drop of the pure stuff on the skin will kill a dog. Two drops in a cup of cider will stop a man’s heart before he can finish his sentence."

"A distillate," Cloutier said. He looked at Tressa, his eyes small and hard. "That takes equipment. Glass tubes. Retorts. A stove that can hold a steady heat for twelve hours."

Tressa felt the silver flask in her pocket grow heavier, colder. Her mind raced back to the clearing. The circle of white caps had been so perfectly placed. It was a stage set. A theatrical performance designed to evoke an old herbalist’s warning.

Someone wanted me to take the blame, she thought. Or they wanted the parish to look at the woods while they looked somewhere else.

"The mushrooms were a performance," she said, keeping her voice steady. "If he died of cardiovascular collapse from nicotine, the Amanita was placed around him afterward. The stems were cut with a small, straight blade—I saw the clean edges. But if he had died from the mushroom, there would have been no need for the ring. No one dies of mushroom poisoning sitting upright with their clothes neat and their hair combed."

"Unless they were poisoned by someone who didn't understand how the fungus works," Cloutier suggested. He stepped closer, his heavy boots loud on the stones. "Or someone who did understand, and wanted to make sure we looked at the local gatherers. Who around here knows the woods, Penhallow? Who knows what a Destroying Angel does to a man?"

"Everyone in the valley knows to avoid them," Tressa said, looking him in the eye. "My father taught half the parish. And Euphemia Skeet has her own books."

"Effie’s books are about curing the croup, not stopping hearts," Cloutier scoffed. "And Llewelyn? He’s got three stills in the high rocks. He knows how to boil down mash. He’s got the copper worms for it."

"Llewelyn distills rye and sour apples," Tressa said. "If he distilled tobacco, he’d go blind from the fumes before he could sell a pint. And he had no reason to kill Draxler."

"Draxler was going to evict him," Cloutier said flatly. "The estate papers were in Draxler's pocket when we found him. He’d signed the notice of ejectment three days ago. Llewelyn’s been squatting on that ridge since the old lord died. He didn't want to move."

Tressa’s mind went back to Llewelyn's face when he had spoken to her by the creek. You cut 'em clean, Tressa. Just like you always do.

The old man had been terrified. Was it the terror of a framed man, or the cunning of an old wolf who had already sprung the trap?

"Where is the bottle?" Tressa asked.

Clade looked up. "What bottle?"

"The vessel he drank from," Tressa said, her fingers twitching inside her pocket, touching the cold silver through her glove. "If he took a massive dose of liquid nicotine, it had to be in something. A man doesn't carry a distilled poison in his bare hand. Was there a cup near the body? A flask?"

Cloutier’s face darkened. "We searched the grass for twenty yards around. Found nothing but his walking stick and his hat. He must have had a flask, though. His housekeeper, Mrs. Garrow, said he never walked the ridge without his brandy. A silver flask, engraved with his family crest—a hound’s head."

Tressa felt her breath hitch. Her thumb traced the raised metal on the side of the flask in her pocket.

It was a hound’s head. She had felt the shape of the snout and the ears through the wool of her glove when she first picked it up.

"If there was a flask," she said, her voice dropping, "and it isn't there now, then someone took it."

"The killer," Cloutier said. "To hide the dregs. If we find that flask, we find the person who filled it."

He turned his gaze back to Clade. "How long do we have before the body has to go in the ground? The family in Bristol is already sending a carriage."

"In this weather? Two days," Clade said, turning back to his jars. "Though if you leave him on my table much longer, I’ll start charging you for the vinegar. The smell of him is beginning to fight with my tobacco."

Cloutier grunted. He reached into his pocket, pulled out a thick plug of black leaf, and sliced off a corner with his knife. He popped the leaf into his cheek, his jaw working the leather-dark lump with a slow, rhythmic crunch.

"Nicotine," the Sheriff muttered, spitting a dark stream into Clade’s iron coal bucket. "Dirty way to go. A gentleman’s poison, but a poor man’s weed."

He turned his small, grey eyes back to Tressa. "You look pale, Penhallow. More than usual. You been spending too much time in the damp?"

"I am fine, Sheriff," she said, her hand tightening on the hidden flask until the metal bit into her palm. "I'm only thinking of the timeline."

"What about it?"

"If Draxler died before noon, as Dr. Clade says, then he must have met his killer on the ridge between his breakfast and his midday meal," Tressa said. "Who saw him? Who was on the high trail yesterday morning?"

"Llewelyn says he was checking his rabbit snares," Cloutier said, his voice dripping with suspicion. "But Effie Skeet says she saw Llewelyn down by the old lime kiln around ten o'clock. And she says he was carrying a brown earthenware jug."

"Llewelyn always carries a jug," Tressa said. "It’s how he keeps his tea hot."

"Or his poison cold," Cloutier said. He took a step toward the door, his heavy boots echoing under the low brick ceiling. "I'm going back up to the ridge. I want to see if Llewelyn’s stills have been running lately. And you, Penhallow—stay close to your cottage. Don't go wandering off after any more of your little white angels. This case has turned sharp, and I don't want to find you face-down in the gorse with a blue mouth."

"I have no intention of dying, Sheriff," Tressa said.

"Good. Because if you do, I’ll have to ask Clade here to open you up, and he’s already complained about the bills."

With a low, dry chuckle that sounded like stones rattling in a tin cup, Cloutier went up the wooden stairs, his heavy tread shaking the floorboards overhead until the outer door slammed shut, cutting off the draft from the street.

The basement surgery grew very quiet. The only sound was the steady, rhythmic drip-drip of water from the zinc washing basin in the corner and the dry scrape of Clade’s pen as he began to write his report on a sheet of yellowed parchment.

Tressa stood still, her heart thumping against her ribs like a trapped sparrow.

"He’s a stupid man," Clade said without looking up from his writing. "But he has the persistence of an old dog. If he smells grease on your coat, he’ll follow you until you drop."

Tressa’s fingers stiffened around the flask. "I don't know what you mean, Doctor."

Clade paused his pen. He turned his head slowly, his spectacles catching the lamp’s yellow glare so that his eyes were hidden behind twin silver coins.

"You have a habit of keeping your hands in your pockets when you are lying, Tressa," he said softly. "Your father did the same thing. Whenever he brought me a specimen he knew he shouldn't have harvested from the lord's private woods, he’d stand right where you are standing now, with his hands buried in his coat and his shoulders hunched like a cold crow."

Tressa did not move. "My father was an honest man."

"Your father was a genius who died with sixty pounds of debt and a cellar full of dried hemlock," Clade said. He set his pen down on the desk with a precise, wooden tap. "And he knew that the line between a medicine and a murder is nothing but a matter of measurement. He taught you that, didn't he?"

Tressa let her hand release the flask. She pulled her right hand out of her pocket, showing him her bare, calloused palm. "He taught me to respect the specimens. He taught me that the forest doesn't lie."

"The forest doesn't," Clade agreed, his voice dropping to a dry whisper. "But the people who walk in it do. If you have something that belongs to Huxley Draxler, Tressa, you had best make sure Cloutier doesn't find it on you. He’s already looking for a reason to clear those cottages on the ridge. If he can hang Llewelyn or lock you away for the murder of the estate’s heir, the new landlord will have those hills cleared and sheep-run before the hay is cut."

"Is that what you think this is about?" Tressa asked. "Land?"

"It is always about land," Clade said, turning back to his parchment. "Or the things that grow upon it. Now go. The smell of this room is doing you no favors, and I have a bladder to examine."

Tressa didn't wait for another warning. She turned and walked quickly up the steep wooden stairs, her boots loud in the narrow shaft.

When she stepped out into the lane, the cold autumn air hit her like a wet towel. The sky was the color of dirty dishwater, and a thin, greasy mist was rolling down from the high tors, turning the slate roofs of the village black.

She walked fast, her chin tucked into her collar, her arm pressed tight against her side to keep the flask from moving.

She didn't go back to her cottage. Instead, she took the narrow path that led behind the churchyard, where the old nettles grew high and the ground was soft with rotted leaves.

She needed to see the flask. She needed to see what was inside it before Cloutier’s men came searching her shelves.

Behind the sexton’s hut, under the dripping boughs of a massive yew tree that had stood since the Danes, she stopped. She looked around. The lane was empty; the only sound was the distant, dull clink-clink of the blacksmith’s hammer from the village square.

With trembling fingers, she pulled the silver cylinder from her pocket and shook off the woolen glove.

The flask was heavy, solid silver, tarnished to a dull pewter-grey around the seams. On the face of it, the engraved head of a bloodhound stared out with blank, ridged eyes.

Tressa unscrewed the cap.

The threads were clean, but as the seal broke, a smell drifted out—sharp, oily, and intensely sweet.

It was not brandy. Or rather, it had been brandy once, but someone had added something that smelled of burnt sugar, almonds, and the bitter, suffocating greenness of crushed tobacco stalks.

She tilted the flask slightly. A dark, syrupy liquid clung to the inner rim.

Suddenly, a voice spoke from the shadow of the yew tree.

"You shouldn't have that, Tressa."

Tressa gasped, her hand jerking as she nearly dropped the silver. She turned, her back slamming against the rough bark of the yew.

Euphemia Skeet stood in the narrow gap between the churchyard wall and the sexton's shed. She was wrapped in a heavy, grease-spotted shawl of faded green, her gray hair escaping from her bonnet in wild, damp wisps. Her hands were tucked into her apron, and her face was pale, her eyes dark and hollow in the twilight.

"Effie," Tressa breathed, her heart racing. "You startled me."

"I saw you come out of Clade's," Effie said, her voice low and raspy, like dry leaves scraping across a stone. "And I saw the way you were holding your coat. You always did have your father's walk when you were carrying something you shouldn't."

Tressa quickly slipped the flask back into her pocket, but she knew it was too late. Effie had seen the silver glint.

"It’s Draxler’s," Tressa said, watching the older woman’s face closely. "I found it by the trail."

"Then you should have given it to Rufus," Effie said. She stepped closer, her boots sinking into the wet moss. "He’s a hard man, Tressa. If he thinks you're hiding things, he’ll have you in the lock-up before the sun sets. And he won't care about your father’s books or your mother’s grave."

"If I give it to him now, he’ll ask why I didn't give it to him yesterday," Tressa said. "He’ll say I took it to clean it. Or to fill it."

Effie looked at her for a long, silent moment. The wind stirred the branches of the yew overhead, sending a shower of cold drops down upon them.

"He died of the weed-water, didn't he?" Effie whispered suddenly.

Tressa’s eyes narrowed. "What do you know about weed-water, Effie?"

"I know what my grandmother taught me," Effie said, her voice trembling slightly. "You boil the green leaves in a copper pot until the water turns black as coal tar. You use it to kill the green-flies on the roses. But you never, ever touch it with your bare skin. It’s a devil’s broth, Tressa. It makes the heart leap like a hooked trout."

"Did you make some, Effie?"

The old woman’s face went stiff. Her chin quivered, and she pulled her shawl tighter around her shoulders. "I have roses, yes. But I haven't boiled leaf since the frost came. And I didn't give none to Huxley Draxler."

"But someone did," Tressa said, stepping toward her. "Someone who knew he drank brandy on the ridge. Someone who knew exactly where he’d be."

"It was Llewelyn," Effie said quickly, her voice rising to a thin squeak. "I saw him, Tressa! I told the Sheriff. He was down by the lime kiln yesterday morning with his brown jug. He had that look on him—that wild, shifting look he gets when the spirits are in him."

"Llewelyn’s looked that way for twenty years," Tressa said. "That doesn't make him a poisoner."

"He had the motive!" Effie cried, her hand reaching out to clutch Tressa’s sleeve. Her fingers were cold, the skin dry and rough as sandpaper. "They were going to take his cabin. They were going to throw his traps into the beck. If they took his ridge, he’d have nowhere to go but the workhouse in Exeter. And he’d die there, Tressa. You know he would."

Tressa looked down at the hand on her sleeve. Under the dirt of the nails, she noticed a faint, greenish-black stain, thickest around the cuticles—the kind of stain that came from handling wet, bruised leaves.

She looked up, meeting Effie’s watery, desperate eyes.

"The Sheriff is going to Llewelyn's cabin now," Tressa said softly. "If he finds the distillate there, Llewelyn will hang."

"Then it will be over," Effie whispered. "It will be over, and we can all go back to our work."

"No," Tressa said, pulling her arm gently but firmly from Effie’s grasp. "It won't be over. Because Llewelyn didn't stage that ring of Destroying Angels."

"Why not?"

"Because Llewelyn knows the woods," Tressa said. "He knows that a Destroying Angel takes three days to kill a man. He would never have placed them there to make it look like a foraging accident, because he knows I would have seen the lie in an instant. The only person who would stage that ring is someone who wanted me to see it. Someone who wanted the village to think the Penhallow girl had finally gone mad like her father."

Effie stared at her, her mouth slightly open, revealing her yellowed, uneven teeth.

For a second, the silence between them was so heavy that Tressa could hear the steady, rhythmic dripping of the yew tree onto the flat stones of the old grave beside them.

Then, Effie took a step back, her face hardening into something cold and old.

"You always were too clever for your own good, Tressa Penhallow," she said, her voice no longer trembling. "Just like your father. But cleverness didn't keep him from the churchyard. And it won't keep you from the rope if Rufus finds that silver in your pocket."

Without another word, Effie turned and hobbled quickly away, her green shawl fluttering behind her like the wings of a great, bruised beetle as she disappeared into the gathering fog.

Tressa stood alone under the yew tree.

The flask in her pocket felt heavier than ever. She knew Effie was right about one thing: if Cloutier found the silver on her, the story was finished.

She needed to get to Llewelyn’s cabin before the Sheriff did. Not to hide the old man, but to find out if the brown jug Effie had seen really contained tea—or if someone had left a gift of black leaf distillate on his shelf to ensure he never spoke again.

Chapter 5: A Blade in the Dark

The wind off the salt marshes always tasted of rusted iron and rotting kelp. Tonight, though, as Tressa Penhallow reached her kitchen door, the air had a cold, oily edge to it. Hemlock. Specifically, the sharp, medicinal tang of crushed needles ground into the damp flagstones of her step. Someone had stood here. Recently.

She didn't touch the brass handle immediately. She just stared at it. The heavy oak door was swollen from three solid weeks of autumn rain, which meant it required a violent, upward lift of the latch to catch properly. It was a miserable little trick only she knew. But right now? The thumb-latch sat perfectly level. Resting.

She gave it a tentative push. It didn't fight her. The door swung back into the pitch-black hallway with a dry, screaming creak that made her jaw clench.

The cold inside was different from the cold outside; it was stagnant, smelling of dead ash, soot, and wet dog-hair from her old rugs. Tressa stood on the threshold, her head tilted, letting her eyes adjust to the gloom. The only sound was the dry, rhythmic clack-clack of the pendulum clock in the parlor and, far off, the heavy, thudding boom of the tide tearing itself apart against the basalt cliffs.

No breathing. No rustle of wool.

She reached into her heavy wool coat. Her hand brushed the cool, flat curve of her pewter flask—spirit-gum and cheap gin—before wandering down to her hip. Her belt was light. The leather sheath where her curved hawkbill knife usually lived hung empty, limp against her thigh. A stupid mistake. She'd dropped it three days ago, and the loss still felt like a missing tooth.

She scraped a sulfur match against the rough stone of the chimney breast. The blue flare hissed, filling her nose with the stink of rotten eggs before settling into a smoky yellow flame. She touched it to the greasy wick of her tallow lamp.

The shadows fled, retreating into the high rafters where her dried bundles of mugwort, pennyroyal, and black trumpets hung like shriveled, leathery wings.

She carried the lamp toward the long pine workbench where she did her sorting.

And then she froze.

The hawkbill knife was sitting in its rack.

Her heart did a slow, heavy roll in her chest.

The knife was slotted neatly into its customized leather loop, its curved horn handle pointing up. It shouldn't have been there. She’d spent the last seventy-two hours crawling through the mud and rotting leaf mold of the deep woods, looking for the damn thing. She’d lost it the exact moment she found Huxley Draxler’s bloated, greyish-yellow face staring up at her from the roots of the old hemlock. In her panic, she’d dropped it. She was certain of it.

She set the lamp down. Her fingers trembled just enough to make the glass chimney rattle.

She pulled the blade from its loop.

It didn't smell of forest rot or river mud. It smelled of clean, mineral oil and high-grade whetstone paste. Someone had found it, scraped the dried clay from the horn handle, and ground the nicked steel down to a terrifyingly clean, hair-shaving edge. It was pristine. It was a work of absolute, obsessive precision.

Who the hell does something like that?

A memory flickered: Sheriff Orson Cloutier’s hands. Big, thick-jointed paws with grease permanently wedged under the thumbnails, smelling of horse sweat and plug tobacco. But the man was an experienced trapper. She’d once watched him skin a fox with three clean strokes, his fingers moving with the delicate, terrifying efficiency of a lace-maker. He could have found it in the mud, cleaned it, and put it back just to watch her squirm when she realized he knew.

Then there was Dr. Gabriel Clade. The parish coroner. Clade’s hands were long, bloodless, and always smelled of carbolic soap and vinegar. He kept his scalpels in velvet-lined boxes, polished to a mirror sheen. If Clade had found it near the corpse, he’d have cleaned it out of sheer, sterile habit. But Clade didn't creep through wet gardens at midnight. He was too proud, too delicate for wet boots.

Or Maura Skeet. Old Maura, whose fingers were permanently stained ink-black from her ledgers. She had the eyes of a hawk and the spite of a starved ferret. If Maura wanted to rattle Tressa, she wouldn't have cleaned the blade. She would have left a smudge of Huxley’s dried blood on the horn to see if Tressa would scream.

And Gwilym Pryce, the old marsh-man. He’d been cackling in the gorse when they dragged the body out. Gwilym’s hands were like bark, but he knew the value of good Sheffield steel.

She laid the blade on the pine table. The soft clink of the steel felt too loud. This wasn't a return of property. It was a receipt. A quiet, terrifying way of saying: I can walk through your walls whenever I please.

She checked the bolt on the back kitchen door. Latched. The small sash window by the larder was still pegged shut from the inside. Nothing was broken, nothing was forced.

She didn't go to bed. She couldn't. Instead, she dragged her wicker armchair to the corner by the dead stove, her fingers locked around the horn grip of the hawkbill, tracing the smooth, cool bone. She sat there in the dark, her eyes wide, watching the door until the tallow lamp sputtered, choked on its own wick, and died.

The dark that followed was thick enough to swallow her whole.

Sometime around three, the storm finally hit.

It started as a soft, spitting hiss against the slate tiles, then deepened into a violent, drumming roar that made the old timbers of the cottage groan.

Tressa's head jerked up. Her neck was stiff, a sharp ache radiating down her shoulder from sitting hunched in the wicker chair.

Underneath the rhythmic drumming of the rain, there was a different sound.

A wet, splintering crack.

It came from the back. The lean-to workshop where she dried her herbs.

She didn't breathe. Her heart beat so hard against her collarbone she was sure anyone in the room would hear it. She didn't light a match. That would be suicide—a tiny, bright signal flare marking her exact position.

She knew this house better than her own skin. Six paces to the pantry door. Avoid the third floorboard from the wall because the joist was rotten and squeaked like a stepped-on mouse.

She slid out of the chair, her bare feet silent on the rag rug. The cold of the stone floor bit through her wool stockings, sharp as ice.

Another sound. A low, scraping drag. The sound of a heavy wooden drawer being pulled too far out of its housing.

The workshop was a drafty, dirt-floored lean-to at the end of the pantry. It smelled of peat, dried boletus, and the vinegar she used for pickling spore prints.

She crept into the narrow pantry passage. The air was thick with the scent of dried elderberry and sour jar-sealers. At the far end, the low door to the workshop was ajar by three inches. A thin sliver of grey, watery moonlight—greasy and weak behind the rain clouds—cut across the floor.

She pressed her back against the pantry shelves. A jar of pickled chanterelles pressed cold against her shoulder blade.

A shadow cut across the sliver of light.

It was a large, heavy silhouette, bulked out by a thick oiled coat. The intruder was moving with a slow, heavy-footed confidence that made her blood run cold. This wasn't a frantic kid looking for silver spoons. The movements were methodical.

A drawer slid open. The small pine desk where she kept her household accounts.

My cash box, she thought.

There were forty-two silver pounds in an iron tin in the bottom drawer. Her entire life's savings, earned shilling by shilling from selling dried medicinal roots and dried chanterelles to the apothecaries down in the valley.

She waited for the metallic rattle of the keyhole being forced.

It didn't come.

The drawer was pushed shut with a quiet, dull click. Then the top drawer—the one with her personal letters, her spore charts, and her journals—was pulled out.

The dry rustle of paper.

They weren't after her money.

Tressa’s grip on the knife handle went slick with sweat.

The shadow moved, and for a fraction of a second, the intruder unhooded a small brass dark-lantern, casting a thin, pencil-sharp beam of yellow light onto the desk.

She saw a hand.

It was small, stubby-fingered, but encased in a wet, dark leather glove. It didn't hesitate. It reached past her personal letters and pulled out a thick, square book bound in scuffed green calfskin.

Her throat went completely dry.

The foraging log.

It wasn't just a list of mushrooms. It was seven years of her life mapped out in minute, obsessive detail: surveyor's pacing notes, compass bearings, limestone elevations, and secret paths through the deep swamps. And on the very last page, written in her own distinct, sloping hand just four days ago, were the exact coordinates to the hollow hemlock roots where Huxley Draxler’s body had been stuffed.

From the split-lightning oak, forty paces north-northeast, down the limestone ledge.

If the sheriff found that book, she wouldn't even get a trial. It was a written confession in her own hand, mapping out the grave before the body was even cold.

She couldn't let them leave with it.

Tressa stepped through the door, the knife raised level with her ribs. "Leave it," she rasped, her voice cracking but hard.

The shadow spun.

The dark-lantern flared wide, a blinding, white-hot beam catching her straight in the eyes. Tressa flinched, her vision dissolving into a smear of purple glare.

She lunged forward anyway, striking out with the hawkbill toward where she thought the chest should be, but her blade met nothing but damp wool and empty air.

A heavy shoulder slammed into her sternum.

The impact knocked the wind out of her in a wet, choking gasp. She went flying backward into her drying racks. The thin cedar trays collapsed with a deafening splintering crash, burying her under a shower of dried birch polypores, pine needles, and dust.

The knife slipped from her fingers, clattering into the dark.

She scrambled on her hands and knees through the debris, her chest burning, but she was too slow.

The exterior workshop door was kicked open. A gust of freezing rain whirled into the room, stinging her eyes.

Through the doorway, she saw the silhouette of the coat disappearing into the black wall of the hemlock woods, the green calfskin book tucked tight under its arm. The storm swallowed the sound of their boots instantly.

Tressa lay there in the dirt, her chest heaving, her palms coated in grey mold and sawdust.

She didn't run after them. To go out into those woods in a pitch-black gale without a lantern was a good way to end up with a broken neck at the bottom of a limestone sinkhole. They knew that. They were counting on it.

She dragged herself up, her ribs aching with every breath. She groped through the debris until her fingers closed around the cold steel of the hawkbill.

The cash box on the desk was untouched. The key still dangled from its little brass hook behind the door.

They didn't want her silver. They wanted her neck in a noose.

She walked to the open door, the freezing rain drenching her hair and face, staring out into the black, shifting wall of the forest.

By tomorrow morning, Cloutier would get his anonymous tip. Or maybe he’d just happen to find the green book tucked under a stone near Huxley’s body. Once the law had those hand-drawn maps, nothing she said would matter.

She shut the heavy door and slid the iron bolt home, but the wood felt about as thick as paper now.

She wasn't just a bystander anymore. She was the scapegoat, and the trap was already swinging shut. She had to find Dr. Clade or get to Gwilym's cabin before the sun came up, but how could she trust either of them when one of them had likely just stood in her kitchen, polishing her own murder weapon?

The rain fell in deafening sheets, washing away the intruder's tracks, washing away the mud, washing away any chance of proving she was innocent.

Tressa tightened her grip on the horn handle of the knife. It was clean now. But she knew, before the storm broke, it would be filthy again.

She stepped out into the black, weeping woods, and let the cold dark take her.

Chapter 6: The Poison Garden

The glasshouse rose from the rotting leaf-mold of the lower valley like a ribbed, crystalline carcass. Its iron skeleton had long since rusted to the dull purple of dried blood.

Tressa Penhallow stopped where the hemlocks gave out. Across the boundary line of Dr. Ambrose Clade’s private grounds, the air changed. The clean, sharp scent of peat and cold rain died, replaced by a thick, yeasty rot and the bitter sting of bruised nightshade. It hung in the stagnant air. You could feel it on your skin—a greasy film that coated the tongue if you breathed through your mouth.

She yanked her woolen shawl tighter. Beneath her wool coat, tucked deep inside an inner pocket, the hard silver flask bit into her ribs. A constant, heavy reminder. Sheriff Rufus Cloutier was looking for a scapegoat, and he wasn't particularly picky. He wanted an end to the affair—a simple, tidy story that wouldn't upset the delicate, shifting politics of the valley's landowners. What better fit than a solitary herb-gatherer with soot on her skin and mud on her boots?

She knew what happened to scapegoats.

Her mind drifted back to the winter she turned nineteen. Her father was already slipping into the gray fog of senility, leaving her to run the apothecary drawers. She’d prepared a simple syrup of wild cherry for a young boy from the downs. But she’d harvested the bark too late—after the hard frost had set in—failing to account for the bitter almond scent that signaled a lethal dose of cyanide. The boy survived. Barely. But the image of his blue, gasping lips and his mother’s shrieking accusations had never left her. That was her ghost. It still sat on her chest in the dark hours before dawn, whispering that she was no healer. Just a poisoner who hadn't slipped up lately.

She shook the memory free and stepped onto the gravel path.

When she reached the heavy oak door of the conservatory, she knocked. The sound was flat, swallowed instantly by the moss-grown brickwork.

"Come," a voice called from within. It was dry as dry beech leaves scraping across stone.

Tressa lifted the iron latch and stepped inside.

The heat hit her like a wet towel. It was thick with the stench of boiled vinegar and rotting peat. Beneath the towering glass roof, a jungle of toxic flora grew in structured, manicured torment. Slate tables held rows of terra-cotta pots containing specimens that would make any parish beadle look for a rope. There were the nodding, purple hoods of Aconitum napellus—wolfsbane, capable of halting a human heart in minutes—and the pale, trumpet-like flowers of Datura, their seedpods spiked like medieval maces.

In the middle of this green asylum sat Dr. Ambrose Clade.

He was swaddled in a heavy tartan blanket that reached his chest, his frame dwarfed by a high-backed wheelchair of dark walnut and iron. A small charcoal brazier burned at his side, throwing off a dry, red glow that did little to color his gray, papery skin.

"Ah, Tressa." Clade didn’t look up. His gaze remained fixed on the brass tube of a microscope. "The parish is whispering. They say Rufus Cloutier spent his morning stomping through your kitchen gardens, looking for hemlock juice."

"The sheriff looks for his keys under the lamp-post because that’s where the light is," Tressa said, her voice low. She moved down the aisle, her boots clicking softly on the damp flagstones. "He found Huxley Draxler in the hemlock grove. Naturally, he thinks of me."

"And did you?" Clade’s eyes flicked up. They were watery, yellowed at the edges, but sharp as broken glass. "Did you rid us of that modern plague?"

"I collect fungi, Dr. Clade. I don't assassinate people."

"A pity," the old man muttered. He reached for a small porcelain cup of tea. But when his hand emerged from the tartan wrap, Tressa froze.

His fingers were a horror. The joints of his knuckles were swollen to the size of walnuts, pale and shiny where the skin stretched tight over the inflamed bone. They didn't bend; they curled inward like dried talons, locked in a permanent, agonized clench. It took him three attempts just to hook his index finger through the handle of the cup. When he finally lifted it, the porcelain rattled violently, spilling amber tea over his desk.

Tressa watched the liquid seep into the wood.

"You’re staring at my hands," Clade said, his voice dropping to a dry, bitter whisper. "The Great Art. The gout and the rheumatics have done what the University Senate never could. They’ve disarmed me."

"They look painful," she said softly.

"They are a prison." He slammed the cup down. "A man of my mind, reduced to watching his own gardens grow wild because he cannot squeeze a pair of shears. If I could have strangled Draxler myself, Tressa, I would have. The man was a pig. A greedy, unlettered pig who wanted to turn the ancient oak-stand into a coal-wharf and a row of brick tenements. He laughed at my injunction. Called my legal filings the dry scribblings of a dead era."

"The injunction was denied?"

"Two days ago," Clade spat. "The clerk in Bodmin sent the notice. Draxler had bought the surveyors, you see. He had bought the parish council. He was going to begin clearing the lower woods next week. He told me he would start with the wild nightshade beds, just to clear the air."

Tressa stepped closer to the long workbench behind his chair.

Her gaze was drawn to a large, double-necked glass retort sitting on a sand bath. It was connected by a lead pipe to a brass condenser. The equipment was pristine, far more advanced than anything she possessed in her cottage. Inside the glass neck, a faint, yellowish, oily film clung to the walls.

She leaned closer.

Beneath the heavy scent of vinegar, there was another smell.

Sharp. Alkaline. Intensely concentrated.

It smelled of stale tobacco leaves boiled down to a dark, tarry syrup.

Pure nicotine.

A single drop of that yellow oil, absorbed through the wet skin of a wrist or slipped into a flask of cider, would trigger instant muscle spasms, respiratory failure, and death within five minutes. A perfect, untraceable poison. And Clade had the Nicotiana rustica growing right in the corner—their broad, coarse leaves showing the jagged, clean scars of a recent harvest.

"You’ve been busy with the still," Tressa remarked, keeping her voice level. She reached out, her finger hovering just above the glass neck of the retort.

"A scholar does not cease his study merely because his hands fail him," Clade said. His voice was too quick, too loud. "I was preparing a wash for the aphids. They are devastating the winter cabbage."

"An expensive wash," Tressa said. "And a highly concentrated one. If Rufus Cloutier were to bring his deputies here—"

"Let him," Clade snapped. "The sheriff doesn't know the difference between a condenser and a chimney pot. He would look at this laboratory and see nothing but glass jars."

Tressa paced around the table. As she walked, her toe caught on something hard beneath the hem of the green baize table cover. She looked down.

A pair of heavy leather boots sat tucked under the bottom shelf. They were caked in dry, crumbly clay. Not just any clay—it was the distinct, pale blue Lias clay that lay only in the deep bed of the hemlock grove, near the creek where Draxler’s body had been discovered.

She felt her breath hitch, a cold spike of adrenaline shooting through her chest.

She bent down, pretending to adjust the buckle of her shoe, and reached out to touch the leather. The clay was dry on the surface, but when she pressed her thumb into the heel, it was still soft, damp, and greasy underneath. The boots had been worn into the grove within the last twenty-four hours.

"Your boots are muddy, Dr. Clade," she said, rising slowly.

The old man laughed, a harsh, dry bark. "Of course they are. A garden is not a parlor, Tressa."

"This clay," she said, her voice dropping to a whisper. "This is blue Lias. It doesn't exist on this side of the ridge. It only comes from the marshy dip near the ancient hemlock stand. The place where they found Huxley Draxler yesterday morning."

Clade's face did not pale; instead, a mottled, angry red crept up his thin neck. "Are you accusing me, girl? You, who have every reason to want that developer dead? You, whose father's lands he seized for the right-of-way?"

"I am looking at the evidence," Tressa said, her heart hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird. "The nicotine in your retort. The blue clay on your boots. You hated him. You had the knowledge, the tools, and the motive."

"And how, pray tell, did I accomplish this grand feat?" Clade gestured wildly to his gnarled, distorted hands, then to the heavy oak wheels of his chair. "Look at me! I cannot even lift my own teapot without spilling it! I cannot walk three paces without my legs buckling beneath me like rotten pine! Do you think I wheeled myself through two miles of knee-deep forest mud, carrying a dead man weighing fifteen stone on my lap?"

Tressa stared at him. The sheer absurdity of the image crushed her theory.

He was right.

She looked at the wheels of his chair. They were fitted with smooth, black rubber tires, designed for the flat flagstones of the conservatory and the level gravel of his terrace. They were clean. There was no blue clay in the treads. There was no mud on the oak spokes.

More than that, she had seen the spot where Draxler's body had been dragged. The earth had been torn up, the deep black loam scarred by the weight of a heavy corpse being hauled over the rough roots of the hemlocks. It had taken two of Cloutier's strongest deputies, sweating and cursing, to carry the body up the steep, slippery embankment to the cart.

For Dr. Clade, it would have been physically impossible. Even if he had used a hand-cart, the narrow, rocky paths through the ravine were impassable for anyone without the full, vigorous use of their limbs. If he had tried to drag Draxler himself, he would have collapsed from the exertion before he reached the estate boundary.

The clues were there, but they did not fit the man.

"You didn't do it," Tressa murmured, her mind spinning. "But the boots..."

"The boots are mine," Clade said, his chest heaving as he struggled for breath, his eyes darting toward the glass doors that led to the dark, dripping shrubbery outside. "Or rather, they were mine before my feet swelled to the size of turnips. They have sat in the porch cupboard for three years."

"Then who wore them?" Tressa took a step closer, her voice urgent. "Who took them, Dr. Clade? Someone who wanted the blue clay on them? Or someone who wanted to help you?"

The old man did not answer. He turned his face away, staring out into the gray, rain-streaked garden where the rhododendrons shivered in the wind. His jaw was set, a hard, obstinate line that told her more than any confession.

"You aren't the only one who hated Draxler," Tressa said, her voice rising. "The whole valley wanted him gone. But someone came here. Someone who knew you had the nicotine. Someone who knew how to use it."

"Go away, Tressa," Clade whispered. "Go back to your mushrooms and your silence. You are playing with forces you do not understand."

"My father always said that the most dangerous poison is the one you don't recognize until you've already swallowed it," Tressa said, her fingers tightening around her shawl. "If Cloutier finds those boots, he won't care about your arthritis. He will hang you first and ask questions when the grave is already green."

She turned toward the door, her boots clicking on the flagstones, but paused before she reached the latch.

On the glass pane of the conservatory door, at eye level, there was a small smudge. It was faint, almost invisible against the gray light of the afternoon, but to Tressa's trained eye, it was unmistakable. It was a smear of yellow wool grease—lanolin—mixed with a tiny speck of raw, unwashed gray fleece.

The kind of wool that only came from the spinning wheels of the local weavers.

The kind of wool that Euphemia Skeet processed in her cottage at the edge of the parish line.

Tressa stood frozen, her hand on the cold iron latch. The heat of the greenhouse suddenly felt like a suffocating shroud, thick with the scent of boiled tobacco and old secrets.

Behind her, Dr. Clade remained motionless in his chair, his gnarled hands hidden once more beneath the heavy tartan blanket, his eyes fixed on the empty glass of his microscope.

"The forest has a way of weeding itself, Tressa," the old man said softly to the empty room. "Sometimes, the gardener only has to watch."

Tressa pushed the door open and stepped out into the cold rain, the wind catching her cloak as she hurried back down the gravel path, the weight of the silver flask pressing hard against her ribs.

Chapter 7: Under the Canopy

Vinegar and cold lard. That was the smell of a south wind in this valley, a greasy sourness that leaked through the floorboards and clung to the corners of the kitchen. Tressa Penhallow worked a yellow block of raw beeswax into the dry ash of her table. She rubbed until her palms burned, until the heat of her skin melted the wax deep into the grain. It was a stupid, mindless distraction, but silence in this cabin was a dangerous thing to leave unfilled. Outside, the sky had bruised. A heavy, purplish black, low enough to scrape the hemlock tops, pressed down on the canopy like a wet wool blanket.

She stopped. A sharp, hot throb flared right where her thumb met the wrist tendon. She listened. The woods weren't singing today. They weren't even whispering. They were holding their breath, the hemlocks frozen stiff, their needles shivering only when a sudden, cold draft spilled down from the high ridges.

Then, the gravel crunched. It wasn't Llewelyn Pryce—Llewelyn moved like a heavy-set badger, all scuff and light, erratic hops through the brush. This was a heel-first, deliberate thud. The stride of a man who owned the dirt he walked on. Or thought he did.

She knew who it was before the iron latch even rattled. She didn't stand up. Instead, she slowly ran a thumb around the sticky, dust-furred rim of her scrap-wax jar. Her throat went dry. Bitter. It felt exactly like the time she’d accidentally inhaled a cloud of yellow spores from a ruptured puffball.

The knock didn't request entry; it shook the door in its frame.

"Tressa," called Sheriff Rufus Cloutier. His voice sounded thick through the heavy cedar, flat and heavy-laden. He sounded like a man who’d spent three hours getting yelled at by a town council desperate for someone—anyone—to blame. "Open up. We have business."

She pulled her gray wool shawl tight, letting the rough, unwashed fibers scrape against her collarbone to keep her focused, and lifted the latch.

The wind that barged past Cloutier tasted of river mud and rotting fern. The Sheriff had aged five years since yesterday morning. His greatcoat was unbuttoned, his collar ringed with a yellow band of dried sweat, and the mist had turned his muttonchops into damp grey wool. Behind him, looming near the dripping elderberry bush, stood Euphemia Skeet. Euphemia wouldn't look at her. She kept her eyes glued to the mud by the porch, her thin lips pinched into a white seam of pure, holy satisfaction. She held her black umbrella before her like a guard’s pike.

"Sheriff," Tressa said. "You're a long way from the gravel road."

Cloutier didn’t wait for an invitation. He just stepped over the sill, his broad shoulders instantly shrinking her narrow entryway. He brought the smell of stale pipe tobacco and wet horsehair into the room. He took off his hat, but instead of setting it on the bench, he kept it in his thick hands, turning it round and round by the grease-stained brim.

"The council’s been sitting since seven this morning, Tressa," he said. He wouldn't look at her. His eyes drifted up to her ceiling joists, where bundles of black trumpet mushrooms hung from hemp strings like dried, shriveled ears. "Dr. Clade spent most of it explaining how a woman with your... let's call it particular education... could have brewed the draft that finished Huxley Draxler. He had some grand words for it. 'Premeditation.' 'Systematic toxicity.' Rubbish like that."

"Dr. Ignatius Clade wouldn't know a chanterelle from a dog’s ear," Tressa said, her fingers digging into the edge of the door. "He steals his taxonomy from old French texts and hopes nobody in this province can read the language. If he thinks I had anything to do with Huxley’s heart stopping—"

"It’s not just Clade, Tressa," Cloutier cut in. His voice dropped, weary and flat. He finally looked at her, his grey eyes heavy under bushy brows. "Euphemia swears she saw Llewelyn Pryce coming out of your lane two nights back. Said he was carrying a burlap coal-sack. The same kind Huxley used for his field specimens."

"Llewelyn was bringing me dry ash-logs for the stove," Tressa said. "And if Euphemia spent half as much time weeding her own turnips as she does peering over my hedge, her cellar wouldn't be rotting out from under her."

Outside, Euphemia’s chin gave a sharp, offended jerk, but she didn't open her mouth. She just stood there, her heavy boots sinking millimeter by millimeter into the wet leaf-mold.

"I’ve got a warrant," Cloutier said. He sounded tired of his own voice. "The council wanted me to bring you down to the lockup in Blackwood in the open cart. I told them the paperwork wasn't in order, but... I have to go through the place. For the record. To clear you, if we can."

A cold, copper tang rose in the back of Tressa’s mouth. The silver flask she’d found in the clearing—the one now wrapped in an old oily rag beneath the loose joist under her dry-sink—felt like it was burning through the floorboards. She’d checked her apron pockets three times before they arrived, making sure nothing clinked, but the mere memory of the metal made her skin itch.

"Search, then," she said, stepping back from the door. "You’ll find dirt and winter herbs. Nothing else."

Cloutier went about it with a slow, heavy-handed thoroughness that made her teeth ache. He didn't smash anything; he wasn't that kind of man. He just took his time. He used his thick thumbs to turn over her dried sage bundles, peered into the stoneware jars of willow bark, and even lifted the linen cover off her yeast-pot, wrinkling his nose at the sour, bready stink of the fermenting mash.

Tressa stood with her shoulders pressed against the cool glass of the window, arms locked over her chest. Watching him made her skin crawl with a ten-year-old memory. Bristol. A gray, rain-slicked afternoon in her tutor’s study, standing exactly like this while a man with yellowed teeth and gold-rimmed spectacles ransacked her writing slope. He’d found her drawings of Claviceps purpurea—the black, crescent spikes of rye ergot—and claimed she was compiling a poisoner's handbook. All because she’d corrected his translation of Dioscorides during a lecture. "A hedge-witch," he’d called her. "A disturber of the natural order." The university gates had slammed shut behind her before nightfall. That was the ghost that lived in her ribs: the absolute certainty that when a respectable man tells a convenient lie, the world will happily hang an inconvenient woman to make it true.

Cloutier was drifting toward the dry-sink.

Tressa’s breath caught in her windpipe, sharp and dry. She squeezed her fingers together until her knuckles clicked. If he kneels. If his boot catches that loose board.

But the Sheriff didn't look down at the floor. His eyes settled on the pine scrap-box beside the coal-scuttle. It was full of kitchen refuse—peelings, cold wood ash, and the slimy, worm-bitten stalks of some old boletes she’d rejected for her stew three days ago.

He reached down. He didn't want to get his hands dirty, so he pulled a graying handkerchief from his pocket to push aside the potato skins.

Something dark and square lay nestled in the wet ash.

Tressa didn't feel her heart race; it simply seemed to stop, leaving her chest hollow and cold.

Cloutier fished it out.

A small, pocket-sized ledger bound in grease-darkened pigskin, the corners stained with what looked like walnut juice. The spine was cracked. A few loose sheets of thick paper sagged from the bottom. Even from five feet away, she recognized the big, sprawling ink-strokes on the loose margin: H.D. – Aug 14. High ridge near the mine.

Huxley’s foraging book.

"This," Cloutier said, his voice dropping to a gravelly whisper, "wasn't in here when I stopped by yesterday, Tressa."

"I’ve never seen that book before in my life," she said. The words tasted like dry sand. "You know someone planted that. The window latch was loose this morning. I told you—someone was in the garden last night. I found the grease—"

"Euphemia!" Cloutier barked, his voice cracking under the weight of his badge.

The door creaked wide. Euphemia Skeet didn't wait; she stepped straight into the kitchen, her eyes instantly latching onto the pigskin book in Cloutier’s handkerchief. A tiny, ugly twitch of a smile showed at the corner of her mouth before she smoothed it away.

"Is that it?" Euphemia’s voice sounded like dry brush dragging over gravel. "Huxley’s book? The one with his maps for the black truffle beds on the north face?"

"It is," Cloutier said. He looked at Tressa, his eyes full of a heavy, professional pity. "And it was sitting under her potato skins."

"She did for him to get it," Euphemia whispered, though she made sure her voice carried right out to the trees. "She wanted those slopes. The Penhallows always thought they owned every spore that grew under these hemlocks. She couldn't stand that Huxley was about to lease the lumber rights to the mill."

"That’s a lie," Tressa said, taking a step toward her. Her boot-heels cracked hard against the boards. "Huxley Draxler was a fool who couldn't tell a truffle from an oak gall. If I wanted his locations, I could have walked ten yards behind him on any Tuesday morning and watched him trip over his own shoelaces. I didn't need his book."

"That’s enough," Cloutier snapped, raising a beefy hand. His face had gone dark, the color of old beef. "Tressa, I’m not hauling you down to Blackwood today. Not yet. But the council has a right to secure the parish's peace until the circuit judge gets down from the county seat next week."

"What's that mean?"

"It means you stay right here," Cloutier said. "Under guard. If I catch you past the old boundary stone by the creek, or if Llewelyn Pryce is spotted within fifty yards of this porch, I’ll have the deputy put the irons on you myself and throw you in the back of the coal-wagon. Clear?"

Tressa looked at Cloutier’s grim jaw, then at Euphemia’s small, twitching mouth. Her skin felt suddenly cold under her shift. "And my gathering? The season's done in a week, Sheriff. The autumn storms are starting today. If I don't get the yellow-legs off the north slope before Friday, they’ll turn to black slime under the pine needles. My winter money—"

"Your winter money is the least of your problems," Cloutier said. He stuffed the pigskin journal into his coat pocket. He didn't look at her again. "Keep inside. I’ll have Deputy Miller sitting at the bottom of the lane."

They left. The heavy door clicked shut behind them, the iron latch dropping into the keeper with a sharp, dry clack that sounded exactly like a rifle being cocked.

Tressa stood still in the middle of the kitchen. The silence came back slowly, filling the corners like cold water rising in a cellar.

Through the glass, she watched them go. Euphemia was nearly trotting, her black umbrella bobbing with her stride, desperate to be the first to tell the story at the dry-goods counter and the parish house. Dr. Clade would be waiting, of course. He'd be sitting by the stove, drinking his weak tea and nodding his sharp little chin while the village spent the afternoon turning Tressa Penhallow into a local witch.

And then the sky split open.

It wasn't a gentle drizzle to start. A single, hollow crack of thunder shook the rafters—sounding exactly like a dry fir splitting down the middle—and then the sky simply collapsed. Great, gray sheets of water lashed the roof, drumming against the cedar shingles with a deafening roar.

She ran back to the window.

The rain was a solid wall now, turning the edge of the hemlock grove into a blurry smear of dark slate. She looked down at the mud. Cloutier’s deep boot-prints were already washing away, the sharp edges of his heels melting into yellow puddles, the clay dissolving back into a smooth, featureless slick.

She pressed her forehead against the cold glass until it hummed with the vibration of the storm.

The blue clay. The greasy tuft of wool snagged on the window-latch. The silver flask hidden under her floor. And that tiny, metallic glint she’d spotted sixty feet up in the old hemlock near where they found Huxley’s body—it was all out there, naked under the canopy, getting scrubbed clean by a hundred thousand gallons of water. By tomorrow morning, the forest would be blank. The clay would be flat mud; the wool would be beaten down into the moss; the tracks of whoever had unlatched her window and slipped that pigskin journal into her trash-box would be gone.

They weren't just framing her. They were washing her out of existence.

Her hands were shaking when she pulled away from the glass. She stared back at the pine scrap-box.

How did they manage it? She’d been right here all night, sleeping on the narrow cot three feet from the stove. True, the wind had been howling through the eaves, but she wasn't a heavy sleeper. Her father had trained her to wake if a buck stepped through the dry brush thirty yards from their tent.

She crossed the room and dropped to her knees on the bare floorboards.

The wet, sour smell of the potato skins was there, yes, but there was a sharper note underneath. Something chemical. Something that didn't belong in a kitchen that smelled of beeswax and woodsmoke. She reached into the box, her fingers sliding past the cold, slimy peelings until they scraped the dry pine at the bottom.

When she pulled her hand back, her fingers were slick. Not with water. Not with the grey slime of rotting potatoes.

She held her fingers up to the gray light from the window. Her index finger was smeared with a thick, yellowish-brown grease.

She sniffed it.

Sheep-dip. That unmistakable, choking stench of cheap coal-tar and sulfur they used to scrub scabies off the hill-flocks before the spring shearing.

Her breath went shallow.

The person who dropped that book into her waste-basket had the grease of the sheep-pens on their skin. And there was only one place within five miles of this valley that bought that particular, foul-smelling coal-tar dip: the tenant farm on the edge of the common land. A farm rented by Llewelyn Pryce’s brother, but managed since midsummer by Dr. Clade’s nephew—a sullen, red-eared boy who spent his wages on cheap gin at the back of Euphemia Skeet’s shop.

She stared back at the window, her heart hammering against her ribs like a bird in a chimney.

She was trapped. A deputy on the road, a storm washing away every shred of physical proof in the valley, a silver flask rotting under her floorboards, and a dead man's notebook in the Sheriff's pocket. The village wanted a monster, and they’d built one out of her.

But she looked down at the grease on her fingertips again.

The rain kept roaring against the roof, a great, heavy thrum that sounded like the canopy closing its doors to her for good. Yet, the logic didn't fit. If whoever planted that book knew her movements well enough to know she’d been in the clearing yesterday... they must have watched her find the silver flask. They’d seen her take it.

So why leave it under her sink? Why not plant the flask too and finish the job?

Chapter 8: The Symbiotic Clue

The stench of acetic acid was a clean, sharp blade. It sliced right through the heavy, wet-rot stink of hemlock and coal-tar that had nested in Tressa Penhallow’s wool skirts since she’d fled the grove.

Inside the pantry—tight, windowless, smelling of damp stone—the red glass sleeve of her lantern threw a thick, currant-colored wash over the wooden counter. Amber bottles of potassium bromide and sodium hyposulfite looked like rows of dark, coagulated blood. She held the glass plate negative by its dry corners. Her fingertips were stained a deep, permanent walnut brown from the silver nitrate. A working woman's brand. The glass was ice-cold.

She lowered the plate into the washing tray with a slow, deliberate tilt. The water rippled. Under the surface, the silver-gray image of the hemlock’s gnarled roots wavered, then settled.

She’d taken the shot yesterday morning. Before Sheriff Cloutier’s heavy boots had chewed the soil into mush. Before Dr. Clade had looked at Huxley Draxler’s bloated tongue and pronounced it a simple case of a greedy fool eating the wrong wild mushroom for supper. To the Sheriff, it was open and shut. Nature was dangerous; foragers were stupid.

But the camera didn't lie. It just took its time.

Tressa slid the plate into the drying rack. Her temples throbbed—a dull, chemical ache from the ether fumes. There was no time to lie down. Cloutier’s suspicion was a physical weight in the village, and her own silver flask, still smelling of bitter almonds, sat tucked beneath the loose floorboard in her kitchen. If they came back with a warrant, this darkroom wouldn't look like a laboratory. It would look like a poisoner's nest.

She took the dry positive print into her study. Cold here. No fire—smoke would only invite questions from the road. She trimmed the wick of the brass lamp, tilting the reflector until a hard, white wedge of light split her oak desk.

She laid the print flat. The bellows camera was a beast to haul, but it didn't miss a thing.

Tressa leaned down with her brass-rimmed thread-counter. Her nose nearly touched the paper. Her breath fogged the glass; she wiped it on her sleeve and looked again.

There. Amanita bisporigera. The Destroying Angels.

They rose from the leaf-drift like pale, perfect hands reaching out from a grave. Clean. Untarnished by the heavy rains that had flattened everything else earlier in the week.

She frowned.

In Bristol, old Professor Albright had stood before the blackboard and dismissed her notes on fungal association as "feminine fancy." He’d told her mushrooms were erratic, lawless things that sprouted wherever a stray spore took a notion to land. Then he’d closed her sketchbook and suggested she stick to watercolors.

He was wrong. Nature doesn't do chaos. It runs on contracts.

The tree in the shot was a hemlock—Tsuga canadensis. Its fallen needles make the soil sour, thin, and sterile. Nothing grows under a hemlock canopy.

But Amanita bisporigera is a parasite of partnership. It's an obligate ectomycorrhizal fungus. Around here, its underground threads can't feed themselves; they have to clasp the roots of living oaks or beeches. Sugars for minerals. A life-for-life trade.

"You don’t belong there," she whispered.

There wasn't an oak tree within half a mile of that grove. The floor was nothing but conifer needles. For those white caps to spring up naturally at the base of that hemlock was a biological lie. They would have starved in the dirt.

They’d been moved.

Someone had dug them up from the deep oak woods on the northern ridge, carried them down the slope, and replanted them in the hemlock needles. And they’d done it with a surgeon's touch—keeping the fragile, cup-like volva at the base of the stems completely intact. A staged scene. A botanical forgery meant to explain why Draxler lay dead in the mud.

Who had that kind of patience?

Llewelyn Pryce knew the woods, but his hands were scarred and stiff from winter wood-cutting. He would have torn the delicate veils.

But Dr. Clade? Clade’s fingers were light enough to thread a needle in the dark. He knew anatomy. And he’d been awfully quick to declare the death accidental, shutting down her questions with that dry, patronizing smile.

Tressa reached for the small, latch-top tin hidden behind her dried ferns. She popped the brass clasp. Inside, resting on damp moss, was the single cap she’d managed to slip into her pocket before Cloutier chased her off.

She transferred it to a slide with bone-handled forceps. It was bruising now, a yellow-gray rot creeping along the edge, but the structure remained.

She leaned into her brass microscope. The cold metal rim bit into her eyebrow. She turned the thumb-screw, focusing down, bypassing the lethal spores, straight to the gills where they met the stalk.

A speck of bright, emerald green.

Not hemlock. Not oak-humus. It was a tiny, feathery leaf-structure, smaller than a grain of salt.

She adjusted the mirror, bouncing the lamp-light up from below. The green speck flared. The cells were arranged like tiny, double-convex lenses, catching the weak light and throwing it back.

Schistostega pennata. Goblin’s Gold.

Her heart gave a hard, sudden thump.

The moss only grew in absolute dark. It died in the sun. It withered under trees. You only found it in the wet, clay-packed depths of the old slate quarries and sandstone caves beneath the northern ridge.

But Destroying Angels didn't grow in caves. They couldn't.

"You were stored," she said to the slide.

The whole trap fell into place. Mushrooms rot. In forty-eight hours, an Amanita turns into a black, melting puddle of stink. If the killer had harvested them ahead of time, waiting for Draxler to walk into the woods, they couldn't have kept them in a kitchen drawer or a hot barn.

They needed a cellar.

The old limestone quarries stayed at forty degrees all year. Wet, dark, cold. A natural refrigerator. Down there, the caps would stay firm and deceptive for days. And while they waited, the spores of the cave-moss had drifted down from the wet stone ceiling, sticking to the damp gills of the poison.

Tressa stood up. The legs of her chair shrieked against the stone floor.

The blue clay.

The smear she’d found on her kitchen latch after the rainstorm—the same greasy, pale-blue Lias mud she’d scraped off her own boots after visiting the quarry flats. She’d ignored it. But the valley’s topsoil was iron-red. Only the deep subterranean tunnels held that pale blue clay.

Whoever had put the silver flask under her sink had come straight out of the earth.

She crossed to the window, pulling the heavy wool drape aside by a fingernail's width. The valley was a pool of ink. The pines were black teeth against the sky. No lights in the village.

Then, a wet, heavy crack from the garden.

She froze. Her fingers bunched into the wool.

It wasn't the wind. It was the distinct, sucking slide of a boot shifting in the mud by her well-house.

Someone was out there. Waiting. Watching the thin yellow line of her lamp through the shutters.

She let the curtain drop, her back sliding against the wood. Alone. Her nearest neighbor was Euphemia Skeet, half a mile off, and Euphemia's loyalty went to the highest bidder.

Tressa's hand slipped into her apron, wrapping around her small, horn-handled pocketknife. The little blade she used to scrape lichens off maple bark. It felt like a toy.

She looked at the microscope. The green speck was still shining on the slide. She had the proof. But to make Cloutier believe a woman over a doctor, she’d have to show him where the moss lived.

She was going to have to go into the dark.

Chapter 9: The Silt Quarry Confrontation

The sash was stuck fast, swollen fat by three weeks of autumn rain. Tressa Penhallow leaned into it, pressing the heel of her hand against the top rail until the old pine groaned. Outside, the valley fog pressed flat against the glass, a wet, sightless eye. One slip, one hard knock with a tool, and the pane would shatter. That would bring Deputy Higgins off his stool on the porch below faster than a gunshot.

Her throat was dry, tasting of cold iron and nerves. She shoved again, hard, using her shoulder this time.

The paint gave way with a wet, tearing sound. A draft of cold air, thick with the smell of waterlogged hemlock and rotting leaf mold, slid into the room. Tressa froze. She held her breath, hand still glued to the frame, listening. Down below, the porch boards creaked. Higgins shifted his weight, his boots scraping the gravel before he settled back into his dull vigil.

She didn't look back at the bedroom. It wasn't her home anymore; it was just a wooden cage. Hoisting her heavy wool skirts above her knees, she swung her leg over the sill. The rough granite of the foundation ledge bit straight through her stockings, but she didn't care. She let herself drop, her boots sinking into the peat of her mother’s ruined flowerbed with a soft, dead thud.

The silver flask was a heavy, freezing weight against her ribs, wrapped tight in a scrap of lint-free specimen cloth to keep it from clinking against the copper tin in her pocket.

She avoided the lane entirely. Instead, she took the deer path, vanishing into the shoulder-high ferns that choked the northern throat of the valley, where the limestone ran deep and cold.

Down in the dip, the very nature of the air shifted. The sweet, compost-heavy smell of the woods vanished, replaced by something sharper. Metalled. It was the scent of ancient silt, water-swept slate, and the faint, rotten-egg stink of sulfurous groundwater. The silt quarry was a raw, jagged split in the side of the ridge, the only spot for miles where the greasy, blue-grey sheets of the Blue Lias clay lay bare to the sky. It was also the only place where the overhangs stayed dark and damp enough for the goblin gold to breathe. The moss needed that absolute, suffocating humidity to cast its strange, emerald glow.

She didn't light her lantern. She didn't need to. She knew this path by the tilt of the wet stone under her soles and the rhythmic drip of water from the fern roots above.

Then, right at the lip of the pit, a new smell cut through the mineral cold.

Petroleum. And the sweet, oily stink of scorched rag paper.

Tressa slowed, her boots sliding in the slick grey silt at the entrance. The fog was heavier down here, trapping the smoke, holding it low like a dirty blanket. A dull orange light pulsed against the sheer clay walls, making the shadows of the hemlocks look like long, thin fingers reaching down into the earth.

She crept closer, her palm skimming the wet, gritty face of a limestone boulder.

In the center of the quarry floor, right beside the rusted carcass of an old crane, stood Llewelyn Pryce.

He didn't look like the harmless, wool-gathering eccentric who usually spent his days wandering the hedges. In the orange glow of the burning iron drum, his face looked smaller, sharper, as if he’d been carved from the same hard slate as the cliffs. He was wearing his heavy oil-skin coat—the one with the hem caked in the dry, chalky grey clay she’d found on the floorboards after Huxley Draxler’s death.

He held a thick ledger in his gloved hands. He was tearing the pages out three at a time, crumpling them, and feeding them to the oil-fire.

"The ink doesn't burn when the pages are shut, does it?" Tressa said. Her voice sounded thin but remarkably clear over the dry crackle of the flames.

Llewelyn didn't startle. He simply froze, a fistful of white paper hovering just inches above the heat. Slowly, he turned his head. The firelight caught his eyes, turning them into two wet, dark holes.

"You ought to be in your parlor, Tressa," he said. His voice had lost its sing-song lilt. It was flat, dry, and perfectly educated—the voice of a man who had run offices in Cardiff, not a local rustic. "Rufus is a patient man, but he won't take to you wandering the hills at midnight."

"Rufus only looks where you tell him to," she said, taking a slow step forward. The silt beneath her boots was slick as lard. "He looked at my drying racks, he looked at my garden, and he looked at the dirt on my floor. But he never thought to ask for the books, did he?"

Llewelyn looked down at the paper in his hand. The edges were already curling brown, turning to ash before they even touched the coals. "There's nothing in here but old numbers. Draxler & Pryce, Lime and Stone. Just a dull record of a dull trade."

"A trade you were robbing blind," Tressa said. "For three years, the shipments from the lower kiln have been light. I saw the old invoices in Euphemia’s shop—she keeps everything, Llewelyn. The coal-tar, the stone lime. You were selling a third of this quarry’s yield under a false name to the Cardiff builders. Huxley didn't know his own partner was bleeding him until he started the autumn audit."

Llewelyn’s hand dropped to his side, but his fingers stayed clamped around the paper, his knuckles turning a stark, bloodless white against the leather of his glove. "You've got a wild mind, girl. Too many hours spent talking to the weeds."

"The weeds don't have a reason to lie," Tressa said, her eyes dropping to his grease-stained sleeve. "The night Huxley died, he didn't go to the hemlock grove to meet some poacher. He came to the old kiln to find you. He had the Cardiff drafts in his pocket. He had the proof."

A sudden gust of wind dropped into the pit, swirling the greasy smoke between them. It carried the chemical bite of cheap sheep-dip.

"You struck him there, by the kiln," Tressa said. "But the road is too close. Anyone could have passed by. So you brought him here, down into the deep silt, thinking the water would rot him before the spring. But you forgot about the moss."

Llewelyn’s eyes flicked toward the dark crack in the rock face behind the crane.

"The goblin gold," she whispered. "It only grows in the deepest, wettest crevices of this pit. It has cells like little lenses—it catches the tiniest scrap of light and reflects it back. When you dragged Huxley’s body through the gap, his wool coat scraped the wet ceiling of the cleft. The spores stuck to his wool. They stuck to his collar."

She reached into her pocket, her fingers closing around the cold silver flask.

"And then there's this."

She didn't pull it out fully, just enough for the silver rim to catch the orange glare of the drum.

Llewelyn’s chest rose and fell in a quick, shallow hitch. "Where did you get that?"

"Where you dropped it," she said. "You wanted Rufus to find it near my garden. A silver flask with Draxler’s initials, tucked into the hemlock roots. But you were in a hurry, Llewelyn. You used the coal-tar sheep-dip from Euphemia’s yard to clean the blood off your boots before you planted it. The silver is tarnished with the residue. You can't wash that smell out."

She took another step. "You kept him in the cold crevice for two days while you figured out how to frame the local herb-wife. The strange girl who lives alone and knows which roots can stop a heart. Then you moved him under the cover of the Tuesday storm."

Llewelyn’s face seemed to harden into stone. The firelight carved deep, black hollows under his cheekbones. When he spoke again, the last trace of the country neighbor was gone.

"Huxley was a fool," he said, tossing the rest of the ledger into the iron drum. The flames roared up, white-hot and greedy, reaching for the damp air. "He sat in Bristol in his dry boots while I spent twenty years in this ditch, breathing lime until my lungs felt like dry mortar. He wanted to call the law. He wanted to lock me in a cage because I took my share."

He reached behind him, his fingers wrapping around a heavy iron poker that had been resting against the drum. Its tip was a dull, pulsing red.

"And who’s going to believe you, Tressa?" Llewelyn asked, his voice dropping to a low, gravelly rattle. "A girl under arrest for poisoning. A girl who broke her bail and ran into the night. If they find you at the bottom of the silt pit, they'll just say you came to hide your jars. They'll say you lost your footing in the dark."

"They might," Tressa said. She didn't back away. "If I were the only one who came."

Out of the deep shadow by the rusted crane, a figure moved.

The firelight glinted off the brass buttons of a heavy wool coat and the long, blue-steel barrel of a carbine.

Sheriff Rufus Cloutier didn't look like a man who had just been roused from his sleep. His jaw was set like iron, his boots already grey with the wet silt of the quarry floor. Behind him, laboring down the narrow clay path, came Dr. Montgomery Clade, his spectacles completely fogged by the steam rising from the pit.

"I told you, Llewelyn," Cloutier said, his voice flat and heavy as a grave marker. "The girl has too much of her mother’s eye. But she's right about the clay."

Llewelyn’s arm went limp. The heavy iron poker sank until its red tip hissed in the wet mud at his feet. "Rufus... you don't know what he was going to do. He was going to sell the whole north ridge to the Cardiff syndicate. We’d have been left with nothing. Both of us."

Tressa looked from Llewelyn to the Sheriff, her heart knocking against her ribs like a trapped bird.

Cloutier didn't lower the rifle. His eyes weren't on Llewelyn; they were fixed on the burning drum, where the last of the white pages were turning to grey, fragile flakes that drifted up into the fog.

"The Cardiff drafts," Cloutier said, almost to himself. "Are they gone, Llewelyn?"

"Most of them," Llewelyn whispered. "The old ones from eighty-eight are dust. The rest... there's nothing left to read."

Tressa felt the cold, wet stone of the quarry wall against her back. Her skin went cold. The Sheriff hadn't been searching the woods for her. He hadn't been asleep at all. He had been waiting for the fire to finish its work.

"You knew," Tressa said. "You knew he was stealing the stone."

Cloutier didn't look at her. "The valley is a small place, Miss Penhallow. We don't need Bristol men coming down here to tell us how to divide our own earth."

He shifted his grip on the carbine. The barrel swung, slow and steady, until it pointed directly at her apron.

"The papers are gone," Cloutier said to Llewelyn. "But she still has the flask."

"Rufus," Dr. Clade’s voice came from the dark behind them, high and thin with panic. "We... we agreed. No more of this. The girl was supposed to take the blame and be done with it. We didn't agree to this."

"The girl should have stayed in her parlor, Montgomery," Cloutier said. He took a long, heavy step toward her, his boots crunching in the shale. "She has a bad habit of digging up things that are meant to rot."

Tressa pressed her palms against the wet clay of the wall, but there was no purchase. Behind her lay only the deep, narrow crevice—and the cold, green light of the goblin gold, waiting in the dark.

Chapter 10: The Spore Print of Guilt

Water dripped from the limestone overhead. Cold, regular drops that hit the quarry floor with the flat, metallic tick of a cheap clock.

Tressa Penhallow kept her lantern low. The yellow wick threw long, shuddering rib-shadows across the wet stone, smelling of bad tallow and lime dust. Ahead of her stood Llewelyn Pryce. His jaw was locked so tight the tendons in his neck looked like dry hemp. He didn’t look like the village soft-wit anymore—the harmless old eccentric who spent his afternoons muttering to the crows and gathering kindling. His spine was straight. His eyes, usually clouded with a theatrical squint, were clear, cold, and entirely blank.

"You’ve a soft head, Tressa," Pryce said. The singsong lilt he used for the villagers was gone, replaced by something dry and flat. "A judge doesn't care for fairy-ring lore or kitchen-gossip. You can babble about gills and caps until the bailiff kicks the barrel out from under you, but a court of law deals in iron. In deeds. In blood."

He took a step forward. His heavy boots ground the loose shale into grey flour. "A gentleman like Huxley Draxler dies of a bad liver or a sudden stroke, and the parish finds a handful of white toadstools nearby. That is what they see. They don’t see your invisible threads of mold, girl. They see a strange, solitary woman who spends her nights in the dark of the wood with a basket of poisons."

Tressa didn't budge. In her coat pocket, her hand was clenched around something heavy and wrapped in wool. Her thumb traced a hard, dented seam through the flannel.

"They see what they’re shown, Llewelyn," she said, keeping her voice level. "Right up until someone holds a candle to the grease they left on the windowpane."

Pryce spat—a quick, dry sound that died instantly in the damp air. "You have nothing. Drawings. Jars of dirt."

"I have the earth," she said. "And the earth doesn't lie to please a landlord."

A boot slipped on the wet shale behind the central pillar. A sharp, gritty scrape, followed by a wet, rattling breath that smelled of stale chicory and cheap tobacco.

Pryce froze. His gaze flicked over Tressa’s shoulder toward the dark mouth of the cavern.

Sheriff Rufus Cloutier stepped into the lantern’s reach. He looked old. His heavy shoulders were hunched under a grease-stained wool coat, and his face had the grey, doughy look of a man who’d spent the night dragging himself through briars. He didn’t even look at Tressa. His small, watery eyes were locked on Pryce.

"I’ve tracked beasts through these hills for forty years, Llewelyn," Cloutier said. His voice was a low, gravelly rumble that seemed to vibrate in the very stone. "I know the sound of a man walking when he thinks he’s alone, and I know the sound of a man trying to disappear. I followed her from her cabin because I thought she was running. But she wasn't. She was hunting."

Pryce’s hand twitched toward his belt.

Before he could touch it, Cloutier’s fist was resting on the heavy brass butt of the pistol tucked into his waistcoat. No rush. No hesitation. Just the quiet weight of a man who had shot dogs and men alike.

"Don’t," Cloutier said.

"This is madness, Rufus," Pryce said. The hard edge in his voice cracked, sliding back into the familiar, wheedling drawl of the local herbalist. "You’re going to take the word of an outsider? A woman who spent her youth in the smoky alleys of Bristol? She came here to turn our young men's heads and poison our wells. She’s desperate. She’s throwing dirt in your eyes to save her own neck."

"The dirt is already on your hands," Tressa said.

She pulled the flannel-wrapped parcel from her coat. She didn't rush. She unfolded the grey wool fold by fold, revealing the silver flask she had pulled from the hemlock grove. Its polished sides were scarred with deep, jagged gouges where it had scraped against the ravine stones.

"This was found near the trail where Draxler's horse was tethered," Tressa said, holding it high enough for the yellow light to catch the faint, hand-scratched initials near the cap: L.P. "It belongs to you, Llewelyn. You dropped it the night you waited for him by the boundary stone."

"A common flask," Pryce hissed. "Lost during the lambing. Half the men in the valley carry silver if they’ve had a decent year. It proves nothing."

"The silver is nothing," Tressa agreed. Her voice went cold and dry, the tone she used when she still sat in the back of London lecture rooms before the doors were locked against her. "It’s what’s inside the neck. When I unscrewed the cap, I smelled peppermint. Strong, bitter stuff, used to hide something far worse."

She tilted the flask. "But peppermint oil doesn’t leave a greasy, brownish-yellow crust around the screw-threads. That is the residue of nicotine sulfate. Sheep-dip. The highly concentrated poison Dr. Ignatius Clade keeps in his locked dispensary—the dispensary you cleaned every Tuesday evening while the doctor was visiting his patients in the lower valley."

Pryce’s jaw worked. A muscle in his cheek twitched like a buried beetle. "Clade’s dispensary is open to anyone. The man is careless."

"He doesn't drink peppermint tea," Tressa said. "But you do, Llewelyn. Euphemia Skeet sold you three ounces of dried peppermint leaf last Tuesday morning. She wrote it down in her ledger. She also noted that you paid her with a coin that smelled of the coal-tar sheep-dip you’d been handling at the home farm."

The Sheriff’s gaze shifted to Pryce’s face. "The girl’s right about the tea, Llewelyn. I’ve sat in your kitchen enough to know the smell of your kettle."

"Peppermint isn't a hanging offense," Pryce muttered, his chest rising and falling in quick, shallow jerks. "And Draxler died of the white caps. The doctor said so himself. He saw the yellowing of the skin. He saw the black vomit. You cannot put a rope around my neck for a weed that grows wild in the ditches."

"He did not die of the white caps," Tressa said. She stepped closer, her boots splashing through a puddle of mineral-heavy water. "The Amanita bisporigera is a slow, cruel death. It takes twelve hours for the first cramp to hit the stomach, and two days for the liver to turn to mush. But Huxley Draxler was seen alive and well at seven o'clock on Thursday evening, leaving the tavern. By midnight, his horse was riderless, and his body was cold before dawn."

She shook her head. Her eyes were hard. "No fungus on earth kills a grown man in five hours and leaves his pupils dilated to the very edge of the iris. That is the signature of nicotine. You met him at the boundary stone. You offered him a draft from your flask—perhaps to seal the bargain for the sale of the common land, or perhaps to toast the clearing of the ancient woods he wanted for his limestone kilns. He drank. He choked on his own spit within minutes. And then you had to hide the body."

The silence in the quarry cave grew heavy, thick with the smell of wet earth and old rot.

"You carried him," Tressa said, pointing toward the dark recesses behind them. "You’re a strong man, Llewelyn. Stronger than you let the village believe. You dragged his body to the hemlock grove, and there you staged his death. You gathered the white caps from the wet ditch near the sheep-fold. You crushed them. You smeared their juices on his lips and forced the pieces down his throat after his heart had already stopped. But in your haste, in the dark, you made a mistake that no naturalist would ever make."

She gestured toward the cave walls around them, where the lantern light caught a faint, emerald-green luminescence clinging to the damp crevices of the rock. It looked like cold fire, or the eyes of small, buried beasts.

"Look at the stone, Sheriff," Tressa said. "What do you see?"

Cloutier squinted at the glowing fissures. "The green mold. The stuff the boys call goblin gold."

"It is Schistostega osmundacea," Tressa said. "A rare, luminous moss that grows only in the deepest, most undisturbed limestone caverns where the light is near to nothing. It dies within hours if exposed to the dry wind of the forest."

She reached into her small canvas specimen bag and pulled out a glass slide, holding it up between the lantern and the Sheriff's face. On the glass was a faint, greenish-brown smear.

"When I examined the white caps found near Draxler's body, I found they were not growing from the pine needles of the grove as they appeared to be. They had been severed at the base with a clean, upward slice of a small knife—just like the knife you use to trim your hazel hurdles. And clinging to the soil at the very bottom of those severed stalks were the microscopic protonema of this exact moss."

She pointed her finger directly at the damp rock wall behind Pryce. "This cave is the only place within thirty miles where this strain of Schistostega grows. You did not gather the mushrooms from the woods, Llewelyn. You grew them here, in the dark of this quarry, away from the dry winds, using the moist limestone and the sheep-manure you hauled from the pens. You staged the entire scene. But when you pulled the mushrooms from the damp floor of this cave, the moss clung to them. You carried the cave out to the woods on the very evidence you used to frame me."

Pryce’s face did not harden into stone; instead, it seemed to liquefy, the muscles of his mouth drooping, his eyes widening with a sudden, animal panic. He looked at the Sheriff, then at the narrow opening of the cave behind Tressa.

"She’s a witch," Pryce whispered, his voice cracking. "She’s woven a web out of nothing. You can't hang a man on the color of some dirt on a mushroom, Rufus. The circuit judge will laugh you out of the county."

"The circuit judge is a man who understands a silver flask with your name on it," Cloutier said. He stepped forward, his heavy boots splashing through the water. "And he is a man who understands why a poor laborer suddenly has thirty gold sovereigns hidden under his hearthstone—the very sovereigns Huxley Draxler withdrew from the bank in town the afternoon before he died."

Pryce took a quick, desperate breath, his eyes darting to the lantern in Tressa’s hand. He lunged forward, his hand clawing for the hot brass of the lamp, intending to plunge them all into the suffocating black of the cavern.

But Tressa was already moving. She stepped back, swinging the lantern low and wide, the bright yellow beam sweeping across the wet floor.

Cloutier moved with a surprising, heavy speed. He grabbed Pryce by the collar of his rough wool coat, his massive forearm locking around the smaller man's throat. Pryce thrashed, his boots kicking uselessly against the shale, his fingernails digging into the leather of the Sheriff's sleeves. But the old man’s grip was like iron. With a grunt, Cloutier threw Pryce against the limestone wall, pinning him there until the fight went out of him and he slid down the stone, gasping for breath.

"It's over, Llewelyn," Cloutier said, his breathing heavy, his chest heaving under his waistcoat. He pulled a pair of heavy iron manacles from his pocket, the metal clinking with a cold, final sound. "You’ve spent too much time in the dark. You forgot that even the dirt has eyes."

Tressa watched as the iron cuffs clicked shut around Pryce's wrists. The prisoner did not speak again. He sat on the wet stone, his head bowed, his hands resting between his knees like two dead birds. The arrogance had drained from him, leaving only the hollow, grey shell of a man who had sold his neighbors and his forest for a handful of gold he would never spend.

The Sheriff turned to Tressa. He looked at her for a long time, his gaze moving from her muddy boots to the glass slide still held between her fingers.

"You’re a strange woman, Miss Penhallow," he said, and for the first time, there was no suspicion in his voice. Only a tired, profound respect. "Most people look at the woods and see nothing but firewood and shade. You see things the rest of us walk right over."

"The forest does not hide its secrets, Sheriff," Tressa said, her fingers tightening around the strap of her specimen bag. "It only waits for someone to learn its language."

Three days later, the rain finally quit.

The sky over the valley was the flat, cold grey of wet slate. But the wind was clean. The heavy, sulfurous humidity that had choked the village all summer was gone, swept away by a sharp northern draft.

Tressa stood on the high ridge above her cabin, her old woolen shawl pinned tight against her chest. Below her, the ancient hemlock grove stretched out like a dark green sea. Its deep, tangled roots anchored the slopes; its canopy remained unbroken by the grey scars of limestone kilns or the timber trails Huxley Draxler had planned to cut through its heart.

The land was safe. The estate would pass to some distant cousin in the north—a man who, the lawyers said, cared nothing for lime-burning or sheep-pens and was content to let the timber stand as it had for three centuries.

A quiet, cool peace settled over her. It was a strange feeling. For years, she had been driven by a desperate, burning need for vindication—first in the lecture halls of Bristol, where they laughed at her drawings and called her a dilettante playing with weeds while the real work of science was done by men in velvet coats.

But here in the damp, quiet north, her weeds had spoken. They had saved her life. And in doing so, they had saved themselves.

She reached into her coat pocket and pulled out her leather-bound field notebook. On the open page, she had laid out a fresh spore print from an Amanita cap she’d found that morning. The spores had fallen in a perfect, white, radial star across the dark paper—a clean, silent geometry that no man’s grease or gold could ever alter.

She closed the book, tucked it away, and started down the trail toward her cabin. Her boots were quiet on the damp, living earth.
