The Hawaii Local Grandma Cookbook: Multi-Ethnic Island Recipes from Our Ohana to Yours

Chapter 1: The Island Pantry

If you want to understand Hawaii, don’t look at the postcard beaches. Look at the dented aluminum pot bubbling on the back burner of a garage stove.

The local food here isn't some static, museum-packaged cuisine. It is a messy, beautiful, living history of survival and shared kitchens. When the plantation era threw Chinese, Japanese, Portuguese, Filipino, and Korean workers together in the sugarcane fields, their metal lunch tins became the ultimate diplomat. A scoop of white rice here, a piece of sweet Portuguese sausage there, a splash of soy sauce over everything. It was survival first. Then, over generations of backyard parties and borrowed recipes, it became comfort.

Go into any local home—and I don’t mean the sleek, air-conditioned condos in Waikiki, but the old, single-wall wooden houses in Kalihi or Hilo—and you’ll find the same sacred space: a pantry packed to the gills with jars of dark liquids, salty seeds, and pickled roots. These are the building blocks that make island cooking incredibly fast, deeply savory, and comforting. Before you even think about firing up a grill for a main course, you have to get these foundational stocks, marinades, and condiments right. They are the quiet workhorses of the kitchen.

### The Five Essentials of the Island Pantry
- The Savory Backbone: Clean, sea-kissed dashi and clear, hours-simmered chicken stocks.
- The Sweet and Salty Sweet Spot: Dark shoyu and brown sugar glazes that caramelize over open flames.
- The Acid Relief: Quick, crunchy pickles that cut straight through the richness of fried meats.
- The Slow-Fried Aromatics: Garlic and shallots, fried low and slow until golden.
- The Liquid Fire: Homemade chili pepper waters, resting on every kitchen table next to the napkins.

The Golden Rule of the Island Kitchen

Here is the thing most people miss: local food is not delicate. It is bold, aggressive, and balances on a razor's edge of sweet, salty, and sour. If you try to use fancy, low-sodium French ingredients here, the whole dish falls apart. Buy the cheap, local brands. They exist for a reason.

Master Dashi Stock (Ichiban Dashi)

Listen, I know those little green boxes of instant Hon-Dashi powder are sitting in your cupboard. No judgment. We all use them when we're in a rush. But if you want to understand the soul of real saimin broth or a clean miso soup, you need to know how to make this from scratch. It takes twenty minutes and tastes like a cool morning on the coast.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: Makes 4 cups

Ingredients

  • 1 piece Kombu (dried kelp, about 4x4 inches)
  • 4 cups Cold water
  • 2 cups Katsuobushi (tightly packed dried bonito flakes)

Instructions

1. Gently wipe the kombu with a damp paper towel to remove any loose dirt. See that white, powdery dust on the surface? Leave it alone. That isn’t mold—it is pure, crystallized marine umami. Don't wash your money down the drain. 2. Toss the kombu and cold water into a medium saucepan. Let it sit there at room temperature for at least 30 minutes. You want the kelp to bloom and start giving up its flavor before you apply any heat. 3. Place the pan over medium heat. Watch it like a hawk. You want a very gentle simmer. The moment you see bubbles starting to break the surface, pull the kombu out immediately. If you let it boil, the kelp releases a mucilaginous slime that makes the broth bitter and cloudy. 4. Dump the katsuobushi flakes into the hot water. Let the liquid rise back to a bare simmer, then turn off the stove immediately. 5. Let the flakes steep undisturbed for about 10 minutes. Don't stir them. Just let them slowly sink to the bottom of the pot. 6. Strain the stock through a fine-mesh sieve lined with cheesecloth into a clean glass jar. Do not squeeze the flakes to get the last drops out—that just forces bitter solids into your crystal-clear broth.

Chef's Tip

Don't throw away those used kelp pieces and bonito flakes. Keep a bag in your freezer and save them to make "Niban Dashi" (second stock). It's a heavier, rougher broth that works perfectly in rustic stews where you don't need a pristine, delicate flavor profile.

Double-Boiled Chinese Chicken Stock

Most Western recipes tell you to boil your chicken bones violently to emulsify the fat. Don't do that here. This traditional Chinese method relies on gentle, indirect heat to yield a stock as clear as water but packed with deep chicken flavor. This is exactly why your favorite won ton min broth tastes so clean yet incredibly rich.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 3 hours | Servings: Makes 6 cups

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs Chicken carcasses or bone-in chicken backs, rinsed well
  • 8 cups Cold water
  • 2 inches Fresh ginger, sliced and lightly bruised with the flat of your knife
  • 3 whole Green onions, cut into 3-inch lengths
  • 2 tbsp Shaoxing rice wine
  • 1 tsp Kosher salt

Instructions

1. Dump your chicken bones into a large pot, cover them with cold water, and bring the whole thing to a rolling boil over high heat. Let it boil for 5 minutes. You'll see a gray, nasty foam rise to the top. That's the impurities. 2. Pour the whole mess into a colander in the sink. Rinse the bones thoroughly under cold running water, scrubbing away any coagulated dark bits. 3. Wash your pot out completely. Put the clean, blanched bones back in, and pour in 8 cups of fresh cold water, along with the ginger, green onions, and Shaoxing wine. 4. Bring the liquid to a bare, lazy simmer over low heat. If you see big bubbles breaking, turn it down. Violent bubbling emulsifies the fat and ruins the clarity of the stock. 5. Cook uncovered at a bare simmer for 3 hours, skimming any stray fat or foam that rises to the surface every 30 minutes or so. 6. Stir in the salt, then gently ladle the stock through a fine-mesh strainer lined with double-layered cheesecloth.

Chef's Tip

When straining, never tip the pot upside down to dump it. That stirs up the sediment at the bottom and makes all your hard work pointless. Use a ladle and gently scoop the broth out layer by layer.

Basic Shoyu-Sugar Marinade

If you’ve ever spent a warm Saturday afternoon driving past a beach park in Hawaii, you’ve smelled this. It’s the scent of sweet, garlic-heavy soy sauce hitting red-hot kiawe wood. This is the ultimate, all-purpose marinade for teriyaki chicken, kalbi short ribs, or even charred tofu.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: Makes 2 cups

Ingredients

  • 1 cup Aloha brand shoyu (do not use dark Chinese soy sauce or heavy Japanese tamari)
  • 3/4 cup Light brown sugar, packed
  • 1/2 cup Water
  • 2 tbsp Mirin
  • 2 tbsp Fresh ginger, peeled and grated on a microplane
  • 4 cloves Garlic, finely minced
  • 1 tbsp Toasted sesame oil

Instructions

1. Get a medium saucepan and combine the shoyu, brown sugar, water, and mirin over medium-low heat. 2. Whisk the mixture until the sugar has completely dissolved. You want it warm, not boiling. 3. Pull the pan off the heat. Stir in the grated ginger, minced garlic, and toasted sesame oil. 4. Let the marinade cool completely before you pour it over your meat. If you pour warm marinade over raw chicken, you'll start cooking the edges and ruin the texture. 5. Marinate your proteins for at least 4 hours, or better yet, leave them in the fridge overnight.

Chef's Tip

Brand choice actually matters here. Grab a bottle of Aloha brand shoyu. Standard Japanese or Chinese soy sauces are much saltier and more fermented. Aloha shoyu is milder, sweeter, and matches the local palate perfectly.

Homemade Chili Pepper Water

This is the undisputed king of the island table. It sits right next to the salt shaker in every home. A few shakes over a plate lunch, poke, or a bowl of beef stew cuts right through the grease and wakes your tongue up. It's thin, salty, sharp, and absolutely addictive.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: Makes 2 cups

Ingredients

  • 15 fresh Hawaiian chili peppers (or red bird's eye chilies), stems popped off
  • 1 1/2 cups Water
  • 1/2 cup Distilled white vinegar
  • 3 cloves Garlic, peeled and smashed with your palm
  • 1 tbsp Coarse Alaea (Hawaiian red sea salt)

Instructions

1. Wear gloves for this. Slice the chili peppers in half lengthwise to let the seeds loose, and drop them into a clean, sterilized pint-sized glass jar. 2. Add the smashed garlic and Alaea salt to the jar with the chilies. 3. Bring the water and white vinegar to a hard boil in a small saucepan. 4. Carefully pour the boiling liquid over your chilies and garlic, leaving about half an inch of space at the top of the jar. 5. Screw the lid on tight, give it a vigorous shake to dissolve the salt, and let it cool on the counter. 6. Stash the jar in the back of your fridge for at least 3 days before you use it. It needs time to develop that slow, creeping heat.

Chef's Tip

Never use a metal lid directly on the jar. The high concentration of salt and vinegar will corrode it in a matter of weeks, leaving a rusty mess. Put a piece of plastic wrap over the mouth of the jar before screwing the metal ring on.

Toasted Sesame-Salt Seasoning (Gomasio)

Brought to the islands by Japanese sugar workers, this simple mix is how you elevate plain white rice when there's nothing else in the fridge. It adds a nutty, savory crunch that balances out fatty proteins.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: Makes 1 cup

Ingredients

  • 1 cup Raw white sesame seeds
  • 1 1/2 tbsp Coarse Alaea salt (or coarse sea salt)

Instructions

1. Put a dry, heavy skillet (cast iron is best) over medium-low heat. Don't add oil. 2. Toss in the sesame seeds. Keep the pan moving constantly. Sesame seeds can burn in the blink of an eye. 3. Toast them for 3 to 5 minutes. You'll know they're ready when they start popping like tiny popcorn, turn a warm golden-blonde color, and make your kitchen smell like a bakery. 4. Throw the sea salt into the hot pan during the last minute of toasting. This dries out any trace moisture in the salt crystals so the seasoning stays dry and shelf-stable. 5. Pour the hot mixture into a mortar and pestle. Grind it gently. You want to crush about half the seeds to release their aromatic oils, but leave the rest whole for texture. 6. Let it cool completely before storing it in an airtight jar.

Chef's Tip

Resist the temptation to use a spice grinder or mini food processor. The high-speed blades generate heat, which will turn the seed oils into a sticky, clumping paste instead of a light, free-flowing powder.

Crispy Garlic and Shallot Oil

This is the ultimate shortcut of the island kitchen. When you make this, you end up with two incredible things: a fragrant cooking oil that makes everything taste better, and a crunchy, savory topping that you'll want to spoon over literally everything.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: Makes 1.5 cups

Ingredients

  • 10 cloves Garlic, finely minced
  • 4 medium Shallots, peeled and sliced into paper-thin rings
  • 1 1/2 cups Neutral oil (like canola or peanut oil)
  • 1/2 tsp Kosher salt

Instructions

1. Add the minced garlic, sliced shallots, and oil to a cold saucepan before you turn on the stove. This is key—starting in cold oil lets the moisture escape slowly so the aromatics crisp up instead of burning on the outside. 2. Place the pan over medium-low heat. As the oil warms up, you'll see tiny bubbles form around the garlic and shallots. Give it a stir occasionally. 3. Let it cook slowly for 12 to 15 minutes. Keep a close eye on it as the water cooks out and the shallots begin to shrink. 4. The moment the garlic and shallots turn a pale golden-blonde, pour the oil through a metal fine-mesh strainer into a heat-safe bowl. Do not wait for them to look dark brown in the pan; they will keep cooking in the hot oil and burn. 5. Spread the hot, crispy garlic and shallots onto a plate lined with paper towels to drain, and sprinkle them with salt. 6. Once everything is cold, store the infused oil in one jar and the crispy bits in another. Keep the crispy bits at room temperature so they stay crunchy.

Chef's Tip

The transition from golden-brown perfection to bitter, black charcoal happens in about five seconds. Put your phone away and watch the pot during those final minutes.

Quick Pickled Ginger (Gari)

That pink ginger served with your plate lunch isn't just a pretty garnish. It’s an essential tool. When you are deep into a mountain of heavy mac salad and fatty kalua pork, your palate needs a rescue mission. This sharp, sweet ginger is the perfect reset button.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: Makes 1.5 cups

Ingredients

  • 8 oz Fresh young ginger root (look for skin that is thin as paper with pink tips)
  • 1 tsp Kosher salt
  • 1/2 cup Rice vinegar
  • 1/3 cup Granulated sugar
  • 2 tbsp Water

Instructions

1. Use the edge of a spoon to scrape the thin skin off the ginger. Using a mandoline or your sharpest knife, slice the ginger as paper-thin as you possibly can. 2. Put the ginger slices in a bowl, sprinkle them with the salt, and rub it in with your fingers. Let it sit for 30 minutes. This draws out the excess water and tames the raw, burning bite of the root. 3. Bring a small pot of water to a boil. Drop the salted ginger in for exactly 30 seconds to blanch it, then drain it immediately. 4. Let the ginger cool, then use your hands to squeeze as much water out of the slices as you can. Really wring it out, then pack the dry ginger into a clean glass jar. 5. Combine the rice vinegar, sugar, and water in a small pan over medium heat. Stir until the sugar is dissolved, then pour the hot brine directly over the ginger. 6. Seal the jar and pop it in the fridge.

Chef's Tip

If you can't find young pink ginger and have to use mature ginger from the regular grocery store, your pickles will stay pale yellow. If you want that classic pink color, drop a tiny sliver of raw red beet into the jar with the warm brine.

Atchara (Pickled Green Papaya)

Brought over by Filipino immigrants, this bright, tangy pickle is the natural partner to anything fatty, greasy, or deep-fried. The sweet-and-sour crunch of raw papaya cuts through rich pork belly like nothing else.

Prep time: 25 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: Makes 3 cups

Ingredients

  • 1 medium Green papaya (unripe, rock-hard to the touch), peeled, seeded, and shredded
  • 2 tbsp Kosher salt
  • 1 cup White vinegar
  • 1 cup Granulated sugar
  • 1 inch Fresh ginger, peeled and cut into matchsticks
  • 4 cloves Garlic, sliced thin
  • 1/2 medium Red bell pepper, cut into matchsticks
  • 1 small Carrot, peeled and sliced into thin rounds
  • 1 tbsp Whole black peppercorns

Instructions

1. Shred your peeled and seeded green papaya on a box grater. Throw the shreds into a large bowl. 2. Sprinkle the papaya with the 2 tablespoons of salt and toss it well. Let it sit for 45 minutes. The salt will draw out a surprising amount of water. 3. Wrap the salted papaya in a clean kitchen towel. Twist the towel and squeeze it with everything you’ve got. You want to wring out every last drop of moisture. 4. Pack the bone-dry papaya, red bell pepper, carrots, and peppercorns tightly into clean glass jars, layering them so they look colorful. 5. In a medium pan, bring the vinegar, sugar, ginger, and garlic to a boil for 2 minutes. 6. Pour the boiling brine over the packed papaya, making sure everything is submerged. Seal the jars and let them cool before placing them in the fridge. Give it 24 hours to let the flavors meld before you dig in.

Chef's Tip

Do not skip the squeezing step. If you leave water in the green papaya, it will dilute your pickling brine, and your Atchara will turn soggy and limp within a few days.

Chapter 2: Pupus & Starters

Go to any backyard in Hawaii on a sticky Saturday evening—a kanikapila where three uncles are arguing over who gets to tune the ukulele, or a graduation party where the pop-up tents stretch across three driveways. What is the real centerpiece? It is never the main course. It is the pupu table.

In Hawaii, we do not just eat; we graze, we share, and we linger. The word pupu technically translates to appetizer or starter, but on the islands, it is an entire lifestyle. It is a culinary handshake, an open invitation to sit down and talk story. Our pupus are edible history lessons, shaped by the waves of immigrant plantation workers—Japanese, Filipino, Chinese, Korean, and Portuguese—who shared their lunch tins under the shade of sugar cane stalks. They swapped soy sauce for fish sauce, traded ginger for chili pepper water, and created a fusion cuisine long before "fusion" became a high-priced buzzword in metropolitan restaurants.

Here is the honest truth about making a great local pupu: if you are stressing over perfect plating, you are doing it wrong. These dishes are designed for sticky fingers, paper plates, and casual company. They are bold, unapologetically salty-sweet, and meant to be washed down with a freezing cold beer.

### Aunty Lani's Golden Rule
“You never walk into someone’s house empty-handed. Even if they tell you, ‘Just bring yourself,’ you show up with a warm foil pan of something savory. To arrive without a pupu is to arrive without aloha.”

Classic Spam Musubi

The undisputed king of local convenience. Born from a beautiful clash of Japanese onigiri and American military surplus, this is the ultimate grab-and-go fuel. It is salty, sweet, carb-heavy, and absolutely perfect. If you’ve never had one warm from a beachside cooler, you haven't lived.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 2 cups uncooked short-grain sushi rice
  • 2 cups water
  • 3 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 2 tbsp sugar
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1 can (12 oz) Spam, cut into 8 even slices
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp white sugar (for the glaze)
  • 1 tbsp mirin
  • 4 sheets nori (seaweed), cut in half lengthwise
  • 2 tbsp furikake seasoning

Instructions

1. Get the rice right. Rinse your sushi rice repeatedly until the water runs clear. Don't skip this, or your rice will turn into paste. Cook it with 2 cups of water. While it's steaming hot, gently fold in the rice vinegar, sugar, and salt. Set it aside to cool slightly—you want it warm, not hot. 2. Whisk the glaze. In a small bowl, stir together the soy sauce, sugar, and mirin until the sugar completely dissolves. 3. Sear the meat. Heat a dry, non-stick skillet over medium-high heat. Fry the Spam slices for about 3 minutes on each side. You want those edges brown, caramelized, and slightly crispy. 4. Glaze it. Pour your soy-mirin glaze directly over the Spam in the pan. Turn the slices to coat them evenly. Let it bubble and reduce for a minute until the glaze clings to the meat like a shiny jacket, then kill the heat. 5. Set up your station. Lay a strip of nori, shiny-side down, on a clean cutting board. Place your musubi mold right in the middle of the seaweed. 6. Pack the rice. Press about 1/2 cup of your seasoned rice into the mold. Use the press tool to pack it down firmly. Give it a generous shake of furikake. 7. Stack and wrap. Place a slice of that glazed Spam on top of the rice, and press down hard once more. Carefully slide the plastic mold upward while keeping the press held down. 8. The tuck. Fold one side of the nori strip tightly over the Spam, then wrap the other side over to seal it. Wet your finger with a dab of water to glue the seam shut if it feels stubborn. Repeat with the remaining slices.

Chef's Tip

No musubi mold? No problem. Wash out the empty Spam can, line it with plastic wrap, press your rice and meat inside, and use the plastic wrap handles to lift out a perfectly shaped block.

Shoyu Ahi Poke

Do not call this a "poke bowl" and do not put quinoa in it. Real local poke is simple, clean, and entirely focused on the quality of the fish. It is raw yellowfin tuna tossed with sweet onions and sesame, tasting exactly like a clean ocean breeze.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 lb sushi-grade ahi tuna, cut into 3/4-inch cubes
  • 1/4 cup sweet Maui onion, finely chopped
  • 3 stalks green onions, thinly sliced
  • 3 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame oil
  • 1 tsp Hawaiian sea salt (alaea salt)
  • 1 tsp toasted white sesame seeds
  • 1/2 tsp crushed red pepper flakes
  • 1 tbsp finely chopped fresh ogo (seaweed)

Instructions

1. Prep the fish. Pat your cubed ahi dry with a paper towel. Excess moisture is the enemy of a good marinade. Put the fish into a chilled glass bowl. 2. Add the crunch. Toss the chopped Maui onion, green onions, and fresh ogo into the bowl with the tuna. 3. Dress it. Drizzle the soy sauce and toasted sesame oil directly over the fish. 4. Season. Sprinkle the Hawaiian sea salt, toasted sesame seeds, and red pepper flakes over the bowl. 5. Fold, don't stir. Using a rubber spatula, gently fold the ingredients together. Be tender—you don't want to bruise or mash the delicate tuna. 6. Rest. Cover the bowl and put it in the fridge for 30 minutes. This gives the salt and soy sauce time to penetrate the fish without curing it. Serve cold.

Chef's Tip

Your poke is only as good as your fish. Go to a real fishmonger. The tuna should look vibrant, translucent, and deep red. If it looks dull, pink, or gray around the edges, walk away.

Crispy Pork Lumpia

Brought to the islands by Filipino sugar workers, these shatteringly crisp, slender spring rolls disappear within seconds of being set down. They are the undisputed life of the party.

Prep time: 30 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground pork
  • 1/2 cup carrots, minced
  • 1/2 cup water chestnuts, minced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 cup green onions, finely chopped
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tsp salt
  • 1/2 tsp ground black pepper
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 30 lumpia wrappers (or spring roll wrappers)
  • 4 cups neutral frying oil

Instructions

1. Mix the filling. In a large bowl, work the ground pork, minced carrots, water chestnuts, garlic, green onions, soy sauce, salt, and pepper together with your hands until the mixture is fully combined and pasty. 2. Prep the wrappers. Peel your lumpia wrappers apart gently. Keep them under a damp paper towel while you work so they don’t turn into brittle autumn leaves. 3. Roll them thin. Lay a wrapper flat with one corner pointing toward you (like a diamond). Place about 1.5 tablespoons of the pork filling near the bottom corner, shaping it into a thin, tight pencil-like log. 4. Wrap it tight. Fold the bottom corner up over the meat, roll it over once tightly, fold in the two side corners, and keep rolling upward. 5. Seal the deal. Brush the top corner of the wrapper with your beaten egg wash and press it firmly to seal. 6. Heat the oil. Heat your oil in a deep skillet or wok to 350°F. 7. Fry in batches. Drop the lumpia carefully into the hot oil. Fry for 5 to 7 minutes, turning them occasionally, until they are deep golden-brown and the pork inside is completely cooked. 8. Drain. Rest them on a wire rack—never on paper towels, which trap steam and make the bottoms soggy. Serve hot with sweet chili sauce.

Chef's Tip

Keep your lumpia thin. If you make them fat like Chinese egg rolls, the wrapper will burn before the raw pork inside has finished cooking.

Garlic Soy Edamame

The ultimate sticky, finger-licking bar snack. You will find this at every local dive bar and izakaya, usually served alongside a cold pitcher of draft beer. Have a discard bowl ready for the empty pods.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 lb frozen edamame (in pods)
  • 1 tbsp sesame oil
  • 5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 1 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 tsp chili garlic sauce
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame seeds

Instructions

1. Boil the pods. Drop the frozen edamame into a pot of heavily salted boiling water. Cook for 4 to 5 minutes until they are tender but still retain a slight bite. Drain them thoroughly and dry them with a clean kitchen towel. 2. Mix the sauce. Whisk the soy sauce, oyster sauce, brown sugar, and chili garlic sauce in a small bowl until the sugar dissolves. 3. Sauté the aromatics. Heat the sesame oil in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Toss in your minced garlic and cook for about 1 minute. Watch it like a hawk—burnt garlic tastes bitter and ruined. 4. Glaze it. Pour the sauce mixture into the pan with the garlic. Let it bubble up for a minute until it looks thick and glossy. 5. Toss. Toss the dried edamame into the pan. Stir vigorously for 2 minutes to coat every nook and cranny of the pods in that sweet, garlic-flecked glaze. 6. Serve. Transfer to a bowl, top with toasted sesame seeds, and serve warm with plenty of napkins.

Chef's Tip

Water is the enemy of this glaze. If your edamame pods are still wet when you toss them in the pan, the glaze will slide right off and pool at the bottom of the bowl.

Crispy Fried Wontons

At family gatherings, folding wontons is a team sport. An entire generation of kids in Hawaii grew up sitting around kitchen tables, folding these little triangles while the adults heated up the oil.

Prep time: 25 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 lb ground pork
  • 1/2 lb raw shrimp, peeled, deveined, and minced into a paste
  • 2 stalks green onions, finely chopped
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
  • 1 tsp grated fresh ginger
  • 1/2 tsp white pepper
  • 1 package square wonton wrappers
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 4 cups cooking oil for frying

Instructions

1. The filling. Combine the pork, minced shrimp, green onions, soy sauce, sesame oil, ginger, and white pepper in a bowl. Mix until the filling becomes sticky and holds together. 2. Assemble. Place a wonton wrapper flat on your palm. Spoon a single teaspoon of filling right into the center. Don't overdo it, or it will blowout in the oil. 3. Fold. Lightly brush the edges of the wrapper with the beaten egg. Fold the wrapper diagonally to form a triangle. 4. Pinch. Press out any trapped air around the filling, then pinch the edges firmly to seal. 5. Heat the oil. Heat your oil in a heavy-bottomed pot to 360°F. 6. Fry. Fry the wontons in small batches of five or six. Cook for 3 to 4 minutes, turning them occasionally, until they are light golden and puffy. 7. Drain. Scoop them out with a slotted spoon and let them drain on paper towels. Serve warm with sweet and sour dipping sauce.

Chef's Tip

Air pockets are your enemy here. If you trap air inside the wonton, it will expand in the hot oil, pop, and fill your kitchen with hot, spitting grease.

Korean-Style Fried Chicken Wings

Forget standard hot wings. This local island favorite features a glass-like crunch, a double-fried crust, and a sticky, sweet-and-spicy lacquer that will ruin all other wings for you.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs chicken wings, split
  • 1 tbsp grated fresh ginger
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/2 tsp black pepper
  • 1/2 cup potato starch
  • 4 cups oil for deep frying
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup honey
  • 2 tbsp brown sugar
  • 1 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tbsp gochujang (Korean chili paste)
  • 1 tbsp toasted sesame seeds
  • 2 stalks green onions, sliced

Instructions

1. Marinate. Toss your chicken wings with the fresh ginger, garlic, salt, and pepper. Let them sit at room temperature for 15 minutes to soak up those flavors. 2. Coat. Dust the wings in potato starch. Shake off any excess; you want a super-thin, paper-like coating, not a heavy batter. 3. First fry. Heat your oil to 325°F. Fry the wings in batches for 7 to 8 minutes until they are cooked through but still look pale. Remove them and let them drain on a wire rack for 5 minutes. 4. Make the sauce. While the chicken rests, combine the soy sauce, honey, brown sugar, rice vinegar, and gochujang in a small pot over medium heat. Bring to a boil, then simmer for 3 minutes until the sauce coats the back of a spoon. 5. Second fry. Turn the frying oil heat up to 375°F. Drop the wings back in for another 2 to 3 minutes. This second pass is where the magic happens—it drives out any remaining water and turns the skin into glass. 6. Sauce and toss. Toss the screaming-hot wings directly into a large bowl with the warm glaze. Coat them thoroughly, then garnish with sesame seeds and green onions.

Chef's Tip

Do not try to skip the double-fry. The first fry cooks the meat; the second fry creates the crunch. Skip it, and your wings will turn soggy five minutes after you sauce them.

Baked Char Siu Manapua

Cantonese immigrants brought cha siu bao to the plantations, but Hawaii transformed them into manapua—oversized, pillowy, sweet-dough buns packed to the brim with sticky barbecue pork.

Prep time: 2 hours | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 8

Ingredients

  • 1 cup warm milk (110°F)
  • 2 and 1/4 tsp active dry yeast
  • 1/4 cup sugar
  • 1 large egg
  • 3 cups all-purpose flour
  • 4 tbsp unsalted butter, softened
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1 and 1/2 cups char siu (Chinese barbecue pork), finely diced
  • 1/2 cup chicken broth
  • 1 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp hoisin sauce
  • 1 tbsp sugar
  • 1 tbsp cornstarch dissolved in 2 tbsp water
  • 1 egg beaten with 1 tbsp water (egg wash)
  • 1 tbsp honey mixed with 1 tsp warm water

Instructions

1. Activate the yeast. Whisk the warm milk, yeast, and sugar in a mixer bowl. Let it sit for 10 minutes until it looks frothy and smells like fresh bread, then beat in the egg. 2. Knead the dough. Add the flour and salt. Mix with a dough hook until shaggy. Drop in the softened butter one tablespoon at a time, kneading for 8 minutes until the dough is smooth and springy. Place it in a greased bowl, cover, and let it rise in a warm spot for 1 hour. 3. Cook the filling. Bring the chicken broth, oyster sauce, soy sauce, hoisin, and sugar to a simmer in a small pan. Stir in your cornstarch slurry and cook until the mixture thickens into a heavy paste. Stir in the diced barbecue pork and let it cool completely. 4. Divide. Punch the risen dough down and cut it into 8 equal pieces. Roll each into a ball, cover with a damp cloth, and let rest for 10 minutes. 5. Fill and shape. Roll a ball into a 4-inch circle, keeping the center slightly thicker than the edges. Put 2 generous tablespoons of the cold pork filling in the center. 6. Seal. Gather the edges of the dough over the meat, pinching and twisting the top tight to seal it shut. Place the buns seam-side down on a parchment-lined baking sheet. 7. Second rise. Let the buns rise for 30 minutes. Preheat your oven to 375°F. 8. Bake. Brush the tops with the egg wash. Bake for 15 to 18 minutes until the buns are deep golden-brown. 9. Glaze. The second they come out of the oven, brush the tops with the honey-water mixture for that sweet, sticky bakery shine.

Chef's Tip

Never use warm filling. If the char siu mix is warm when you wrap it, it will create steam inside the raw dough, causing the buns to burst or leak in the oven.

Furikake Garlic Fries

A fixture at local drive-ins and outdoor theaters. This recipe elevates standard frozen french fries with a dump of garlic butter and salty, umami-packed seaweed seasoning.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 lb frozen high-quality French fries
  • 4 cups cooking oil (if frying)
  • 3 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 4 cloves garlic, very finely minced
  • 1/4 cup furikake seasoning
  • 1 tsp coarse sea salt

Instructions

1. Cook the fries. Deep-fry or bake your French fries until they are exceptionally crispy. If they aren't crunchy, they won't hold up to the wet garlic butter. 2. Make the garlic butter. While the fries cook, melt your butter in a small pan over low heat. Add the minced garlic and sauté gently for 2 minutes. You want the garlic fragrant and soft, but not browned. 3. Toss immediately. Put the hot, freshly cooked fries straight into a large, pre-warmed bowl. 4. Coating. Drizzle the warm garlic butter over the hot fries and toss them quickly. 5. Season. Dump the furikake and sea salt over the wet fries, tossing continuously so the green and black flecks of seaweed and sesame stick to every single fry. Serve immediately.

Chef's Tip

Timing is everything. If you let the fries cool down even slightly before tossing them, the butter won't absorb, the seasoning will sink to the bottom of the bowl, and you’ll end up with soggy fries.

Chapter 3: Plate Lunch Classics

You smell it before you see it. It’s the sharp, sweet tang of caramelized shoyu scorching on a flat-top grill, the pungent kick of garlic sizzling in hot butter, and the unmistakable, sweet char of cheap cuts of meat cooked over fire. It hangs thick in the heavy salt air.

If you pull off the road at any rusted lunch wagon or concrete drive-in from Hilo to Hanalei, you will find the soul of Hawaii’s everyday cooking: the plate lunch. This isn't high-concept culinary art. It was born in the late nineteenth century out of pure necessity in the sugarcane and pineapple fields. Japanese, Chinese, Filipino, Portuguese, Korean, and Native Hawaiian laborers worked side-by-side under a brutal sun. At noon, they would sit in the dirt and open their metal tiffin tins. They traded. A scoop of rice here, a piece of Korean kalbi there, a handful of Portuguese sausage to top it off.

What started as a backyard trade evolved into a legendary, carb-heavy cuisine. The modern plate lunch is built on a rigid, unpretentious foundation that locals will defend to the death.

The Three Pillars of the Plate

  • Two Scoops of White Rice: It must be short-grain or medium-grain Calrose rice. If you serve jasmine or basmati, you’ve missed the point entirely. It needs to be steamed until it’s sticky enough to hold together in a single, clean clump on your fork.
  • One Scoop of Macaroni Salad: Cold, creamy, heavily salted, and drenched in Best Foods mayonnaise. It acts as the cooling element to balance the screaming-hot, fatty meats next to it.
  • The Protein: This is where the magic happens. It’s always generous, usually fatty, and always packed with garlic, soy, or sugar.
Local Rule of Thumb: If the paper plate isn’t bending under its own weight when the Aunty hands it to you over the counter, you aren't doing it right. Grease stains on the cardboard are a badge of honor, not a flaw.

Loco Moco

This is the ultimate hangover cure and post-surf recovery meal. Legend has it a bunch of hungry teenagers created it at the Lincoln Grill in Hilo back in 1949 because they wanted something cheap and fast. It is a massive pile-up of hot rice, a thick beef patty, heavy brown gravy, and a fried egg. When you pierce that yolk, it runs into the salty gravy and creates a rich sauce that coats every single grain of rice.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs Ground beef (80/20 blend—don’t go lean here, you need the fat)
  • 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tsp Garlic powder
  • 0.5 tsp Salt
  • 0.5 tsp Black pepper
  • 2 tbsp Unsalted butter
  • 1 large Yellow onion, thinly sliced
  • 2 cups Beef broth
  • 1 tbsp Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 2 tbsp Cornstarch
  • 2 tbsp Cold water
  • 4 large Eggs
  • 8 cups Steamed Calrose rice (hot)
  • 2 Green onions, finely sliced for garnish

Instructions

1. Grab a bowl and drop in the ground beef, Worcestershire sauce, garlic powder, salt, and black pepper. Use your hands to mix it, but keep it brief. If you overwork the meat, you'll end up with rubbery, tough hockey pucks. Shape them into four thick, heavy patties. 2. Get a large skillet screaming hot over medium-high heat. Sear those patties for about 4 to 5 minutes on each side. You want a dark, crispy crust on the outside while keeping the center juicy. Remove the patties and set them on a warm plate. Do not wipe out the pan. 3. Drop a tablespoon of butter directly into that hot beef fat left in the skillet. Toss in the sliced onions and cook them down for about 5 minutes until they’re soft, brown, and sweet. 4. Pour the beef broth and shoyu right into the skillet with the onions. Use a wooden spoon to scrape up all those delicious, caramelized brown bits stuck to the bottom of the pan. Bring it to a rolling simmer. 5. In a small cup, stir the cornstarch and cold water together until smooth. Whisk this slurry directly into the bubbling broth. Let it simmer for 2 to 3 minutes. Watch it transform into a thick, glossy gravy. 6. In a separate non-stick skillet, melt the remaining tablespoon of butter over medium heat. Fry your eggs sunny-side up. You want the whites completely cooked and opaque, but the yolks must remain liquid and runny. 7. To build the plate, slap two scoops of hot rice onto a plate. Place a beef patty right on top. Ladle a massive spoonful of the hot onion gravy over the meat, and crown the whole thing with a fried egg. Scatter some green onions on top and eat it immediately.

Chef's Tip

To get that beautiful, glossy sheen on your gravy, make sure the water is ice-cold when you mix it with the cornstarch. If you use warm water, the gravy will turn out cloudy and lumpy.

Crispy Chicken Katsu

Go to any drive-in on Oahu, and you’ll hear the rhythmic crunch of katsu being sliced in the kitchen. We take boneless chicken thighs, pound them flat, dredge them in flaky panko, and fry them until they turn a beautiful golden brown. It's served with a tangy, sweet-and-sour dipping sauce that cuts right through the richness of the fried meat.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs Boneless, skinless chicken thighs
  • 0.5 cup All-purpose flour
  • 2 large Eggs, beaten
  • 1.5 cups Panko breadcrumbs
  • 1 tsp Garlic salt
  • 0.5 tsp Black pepper
  • Vegetable oil, for shallow frying
  • 0.5 cup Ketchup
  • 0.3 cup Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tbsp Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 1 tbsp Sugar

Instructions

1. Sandwich your chicken thighs between two sheets of plastic wrap. Grab a meat mallet (or the bottom of a heavy cast-iron skillet) and pound them down until they are an even half-inch thick. Season both sides with garlic salt and black pepper. 2. Line up three shallow bowls for your dredging station: flour in the first, beaten eggs in the second, and panko in the third. 3. Work in a neat line. Dredge a chicken thigh in the flour and shake off the excess. Dip it in the egg, then press it hard into the panko. Make sure the breadcrumbs coat every single nook and cranny. 4. Fill a heavy skillet with about a half-inch of vegetable oil. Bring it up to 350°F (175°C) over medium-high heat. 5. Slide the chicken into the hot oil. Fry in batches—don't crowd the pan, or the oil temperature will drop and make the crust soggy. Fry for 5 to 6 minutes per side until the crust is deeply golden. Drain the cooked chicken on a wire rack. 6. While the chicken cools slightly, whisk the ketchup, Worcestershire, shoyu, and sugar together in a small bowl until the sugar dissolves. 7. Slice the katsu into thick, finger-sized strips. You should hear a loud, satisfying crunch with every slice. Serve with the sauce on the side or drizzled right over the top.

Chef's Tip

Don't just gently toss the chicken in the panko. Use the palms of your hands to physically press the breadcrumbs into the wet egg coating. This guarantees the crust locks onto the meat and won't slip off when you slice it.

Slow-Cooker Kalua Pork and Cabbage

Traditionally, kalua pig is cooked in an imu—an underground pit lined with hot lava rocks and sweet banana leaves. Since most of us don't have a trench dug in our backyard, this slow-cooker method brings that same smoky, tender shred to your kitchen with zero digging required. The addition of cabbage at the end adds a sweet, clean crunch that cuts the fat.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 8 hours | Servings: 8

Ingredients

  • 5 lbs Pork butt (pork shoulder, bone-in or boneless)
  • 1.5 tbsp Hawaiian red alaea sea salt (or coarse kosher salt)
  • 1.5 tbsp Liquid smoke (hickory or mesquite)
  • 1 medium head Green cabbage, chopped into 2-inch chunks
  • 0.5 cup Water

Instructions

1. Take a sharp paring knife and poke deep, inch-wide slits all over the pork roast. Rub the coarse sea salt over every inch of the meat, making sure you push some of that salt deep into the cuts you just made. 2. Place the seasoned pork into your slow cooker. Pour the liquid smoke and water down the sides of the pot so you don't wash the salt off the meat. 3. Pop the lid on and walk away. Cook on the low setting for 8 to 10 hours. Your kitchen is going to smell incredible. The meat is done when it completely falls apart at the touch of a fork. 4. Carefully lift the pork out of the slow cooker and transfer it to a large cutting board. Leave the juices in the pot. Shred the meat using two forks, discarding any large, unrendered chunks of fat. 5. Drop the shredded meat back into the slow cooker so it can soak up all those salty, smoky, fatty drippings. 6. Pile the chopped cabbage right on top of the shredded pork. Cover the cooker, turn the heat up to high, and let it steam for 20 to 30 minutes. You want the cabbage tender but still holding a bit of its crunch. 7. Fold the sweet cabbage into the pork and serve it steaming hot.

Chef's Tip

Hawaiian red alaea salt gets its color from volcanic clay, which adds a distinct, subtle earthiness. If you can't find it, coarse sea salt works perfectly. Just steer completely clear of standard table salt—it's too fine and will make the pork inedibly salty.

Hawaiian-Style Shoyu Chicken

This is the dish bubbling on the back burner of every home kitchen in Hawaii when the afternoon rain starts coming down. It is pure, simple comfort food. Bone-in chicken thighs are simmered in a sweet, ginger-heavy soy glaze until the meat is falling off the bone and stained a beautiful, deep mahogany color.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 40 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 3 lbs Bone-in, skin-on chicken thighs
  • 1 cup Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 1 cup Packed light brown sugar
  • 1 cup Water
  • 1-inch piece Fresh ginger, sliced thick and smashed with the side of a knife
  • 4 cloves Garlic, smashed
  • 1 tbsp Cornstarch (optional)
  • 1 tbsp Cold water (optional)
  • 3 Green onions, chopped for garnish

Instructions

1. Trim away any loose, flapping pieces of excess fat from the chicken thighs. In a large, heavy-bottomed Dutch oven, stir together the shoyu, brown sugar, water, smashed ginger, and garlic until the sugar dissolves. 2. Lay the chicken thighs in the pot, skin side down. Bring the liquid to a rolling boil over medium-high heat. 3. Drop the heat down to low, cover the pot with a tight-fitting lid, and let it simmer gently for 20 minutes. 4. Flip the chicken thighs over so they are skin side up. Cover the pot again and simmer for another 15 to 20 minutes. The meat should be incredibly tender. 5. If you want a thick, sticky glaze rather than a thin broth, lift the chicken out of the pot and set it aside on a plate. Whisk the cornstarch and cold water together in a small cup, then pour it into the boiling sauce. Let it bubble for 2 minutes until it thickens. 6. Return the chicken to the pot and roll it around in that sweet, glossy glaze. Scatter the green onions over the top and serve.

Chef's Tip

Do not use boneless, skinless breasts for this. You need the bones and the skin. The bone releases gelatin into the braising liquid, which gives the sauce its rich body, and the dark meat of the thighs stays incredibly juicy through the long simmer.

Teriyaki Beef

This is the speed demon of the plate lunch. Instead of thick steaks, we use paper-thin slices of beef marinated in a heavy soy-ginger mixture and seared in a smoking-hot pan. The high sugar content in the marinade caramelizes instantly, giving you charred, crispy edges and juicy centers in under two minutes.

Prep time: 15 mins (plus marinating) | Cook time: 8 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs Flank steak or ribeye
  • 0.5 cup Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 0.5 cup Sugar
  • 2 tbsp Mirin (Japanese sweet rice wine)
  • 2 cloves Garlic, finely minced
  • 1 tsp Fresh ginger, finely grated
  • 1 tbsp Sesame oil
  • 1 tbsp Toasted sesame seeds, for garnish
  • 2 Green onions, thinly sliced

Instructions

1. Pop your beef into the freezer for about 30 minutes before you start slicing. It won't freeze solid, but it will firm up enough to let you slice it paper-thin across the grain without the meat sliding around under your knife. 2. Whisk the shoyu, sugar, mirin, minced garlic, grated ginger, and sesame oil together in a bowl until the sugar is fully dissolved. 3. Submerge the sliced beef in the marinade, making sure every single strip of meat is coated. Cover it and stick it in the fridge for at least 2 hours. Overnight is even better. 4. Remove the beef from the bowl, letting the excess marinade drip off. Throw away the leftover liquid. 5. Get a large cast-iron skillet or wok over high heat until it starts to smoke. Pour in a tiny splash of a high-smoke-point oil (like canola or peanut oil). 6. Cook the beef in small batches. If you dump it all in at once, the meat will steam in its own juices instead of searing. Sear the strips for 1 to 2 minutes per side until they are beautifully browned with charred, crispy edges. 7. Pile the hot beef onto a platter, scatter the sesame seeds and green onions over the top, and serve immediately.

Chef's Tip

Always slice your beef against the grain—perpendicular to the visible lines of muscle fiber. If you slice with the grain, the beef will be tough and chewy. Slice against it, and it will practically melt in your mouth.

Hamburger Steak with Brown Gravy

Don't confuse this with a Loco Moco. There is no egg here, and the patties are packed with seasoned breadcrumbs and sweet onions, making them incredibly tender. This is comfort food at its most basic: thick beef patties smothered in a rich, velvety onion gravy.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs Ground beef (80/20 blend)
  • 1 cup Yellow onion, minced
  • 1 large Egg
  • 0.5 cup Panko breadcrumbs
  • 2 tbsp Milk
  • 1 tsp Garlic powder
  • 0.5 tsp Salt
  • 0.5 tsp Black pepper
  • 2 tbsp Unsalted butter
  • 1 large Yellow onion, sliced into rings
  • 2 cups Beef broth
  • 1 tbsp Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 2 tbsp Cornstarch
  • 2 tbsp Cold water

Instructions

1. In a large mixing bowl, combine the ground beef, minced onion, egg, panko, milk, garlic powder, salt, and black pepper. Use your hands to mix it gently until just combined. 2. Form the mixture into four thick, oval-shaped patties. Use your thumb to press a shallow indentation into the center of each patty. This keeps them from bulging up into footballs while they cook. 3. Melt a tablespoon of butter in a large skillet over medium-high heat. Sear the patties for 5 minutes on each side until they have a deep, caramelized crust. Lift them out and set them aside. 4. Melt the second tablespoon of butter in the same pan. Throw in the onion rings and cook them for 5 to 6 minutes, stirring often, until they are soft, golden, and sweet. 5. Pour the beef broth and shoyu into the pan, scraping the bottom of the skillet with a wooden spoon to release all the flavor. Bring it to a boil. 6. Stir the cornstarch and cold water together in a small bowl, then pour it into the bubbling broth. Lower the heat to medium-low and stir until the gravy thickens up. 7. Put the beef patties back into the gravy, spooning the rich onion sauce over the top. Cover the pan and let it simmer for about 5 minutes until the patties are cooked through.

Chef's Tip

Letting the panko breadcrumbs soak in the milk for five minutes before you add them to the ground beef creates a panade. This binds the moisture and keeps the beef incredibly tender, preventing it from drying out as it sears.

Kahuku-Style Garlic Shrimp

If you take the drive up Oahu’s windward coast, you’ll eventually hit Kahuku, a town famous for its roadside shrimp trucks. This recipe brings that experience home. We use fresh, shell-on shrimp fried in an ungodly amount of butter and sweet, toasted garlic. It is messy, sticky, and absolutely incredible.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs Large shrimp (shell-on, easy-peel)
  • 2 heads of Garlic (yes, the whole heads—about 20 cloves), finely minced
  • 0.5 cup Unsalted butter, cut into small cubes
  • 2 tbsp Olive oil
  • 0.25 cup All-purpose flour
  • 1 tbsp Paprika
  • 0.5 tsp Cayenne pepper (optional, for a kick)
  • 0.25 cup Dry white wine (or chicken broth)
  • 1 Lemon, cut into wedges for serving

Instructions

1. Pat your shrimp completely dry with paper towels. In a large bowl, whisk the flour, paprika, and cayenne pepper together. Toss the shrimp in the seasoned flour until they are lightly coated. 2. Melt half of the butter with the olive oil in a large skillet over medium-low heat. 3. Dump the mountain of minced garlic into the skillet. Cook it slowly for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly. You want the garlic to soften and turn a pale golden color. Keep the heat low—if you burn the garlic, it will turn bitter and ruin the dish. 4. Crank the heat up to medium-high. Add the floured shrimp to the pan in a single layer. Let them cook undisturbed for about 2 to 3 minutes until the undersides turn pink and slightly crispy. 5. Flip the shrimp over. Pour in the white wine (or chicken broth) and drop the remaining butter cubes into the pan. 6. Let it cook for another 2 to 3 minutes. Shake the pan to help the melting butter and wine emulsify into a thick, creamy sauce. 7. Pour the shrimp along with every single drop of that garlic butter sauce onto a platter. Serve with plenty of lemon wedges.

Chef's Tip

Do not peel the shrimp. Cooking them in their shells keeps the meat sweet, tender, and juicy. Plus, the crispy shells act like a sponge, trapping all that garlicky butter so you get a burst of flavor with every bite.

Mochiko Chicken

This is the Japanese-style fried chicken you will find at every local potluck. Instead of a standard flour batter, we use mochiko (sweet rice flour). It creates a light, incredibly crunchy crust that has a slightly chewy texture when you bite into it. It is sweet, salty, ginger-heavy, and addicting.

Prep time: 20 mins (plus marinating) | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs Boneless, skinless chicken thighs, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 0.25 cup Mochiko (sweet rice flour)
  • 0.25 cup Cornstarch
  • 0.25 cup Sugar
  • 0.25 cup Shoyu (Japanese soy sauce)
  • 2 large Eggs, beaten
  • 3 cloves Garlic, finely minced
  • 1 tsp Fresh ginger, finely grated
  • 3 Green onions, chopped
  • Vegetable oil, for frying

Instructions

1. Whisk the mochiko, cornstarch, sugar, shoyu, beaten eggs, garlic, and grated ginger together in a large bowl until you have a smooth, thick batter. 2. Toss the chicken pieces into the batter, coating every piece. Cover the bowl and refrigerate for at least 4 hours. If you can let it sit overnight, the flavors will penetrate deep into the meat. 3. Pour vegetable oil into a deep, heavy skillet or Dutch oven until it is about 1.5 inches deep. Heat the oil to 350°F (175°C). 4. Give the chicken and batter a final stir. Working in batches, carefully drop the chicken pieces into the hot oil one by one so they don’t stick together. 5. Pile the hot chicken onto a platter, throw the chopped green onions over the top, and dig in.

Local Style Saimin

Ask any local what they crave on a rare, chilly, rainy night in Hilo, and the answer is always saimin. It’s our ultimate homegrown comfort soup—a beautiful, messy culinary collision of Japanese dashi, Chinese wheat noodles, and Filipino green onions. This isn't ramen. It’s lighter, cleaner, and deeply nostalgic. The secret is the broth: a delicate, smoky whisper of dried shrimp and ocean kelp that tastes like old-school diners and late-night drives.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 4 packs (approx. 5 ounces each) fresh saimin noodles (or fresh ramen/yakisoba wheat noodles)
  • 6 cups water
  • 1 piece (4-inch) konbu (dried kelp)
  • 1/2 cup dried shrimp (dried baby prawns)
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce (shoyu)
  • 1 teaspoon dashi powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup kamaboko (Japanese fish cake), sliced into half-moons
  • 1/2 cup char siu (Chinese barbecue pork), sliced thin
  • 4 stalks green onions, finely sliced
  • 2 hard-boiled eggs, halved

Instructions

1. Get a medium pot and toss in the water, konbu, and dried shrimp. Bring it to a gentle simmer over medium heat. Let it do its thing for 15 minutes to pull out those deep, ocean flavors. Whatever you do, don't let it boil vigorously, or you'll end up with a cloudy broth. 2. Fish out the konbu and throw it away. Strain the broth through a fine-mesh sieve to catch the dried shrimp, then pour that beautiful, clear gold back into the pot. 3. Stir in the soy sauce, dashi powder, and salt. Keep the broth hot over low heat. 4. Cook your saimin noodles in a separate pot of boiling water. It only takes about 2 to 3 minutes. Drain them thoroughly—nobody wants watery soup. 5. Divide the hot noodles among four deep bowls. 6. Ladle the hot broth over the noodles until they are completely submerged. 7. Lay the slices of kamaboko, char siu, and half a hard-boiled egg on top of each bowl. 8. Toss on a handful of green onions and serve it immediately while it's piping hot.

Chef's Tip

Want to eat this like a real local? Serve your saimin with a small side dish of hot Chinese mustard mixed with a splash of soy sauce. Use it as a dipping sauce for the noodles and char siu between slurps.

Maui-Style Dry Mein

Go to Maui, and you’ll hear people talk about dry mein with a reverence bordering on the religious. Invented on the Valley Isle, this is saimin’s rebellious, water-free cousin. The noodles are boiled, slammed into a blazing hot wok, and tossed with an intense, savory glaze of shoyu and garlic oil that coats every single strand. It is rich, salty, and incredibly satisfying. And yes, you always serve a tiny cup of scalding hot broth on the side. You sip, you bite, you repeat.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 20 ounces fresh saimin noodles (or thin chow mein noodles)
  • 2 tablespoons peanut oil (or vegetable oil)
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1/2 pound pork loin, sliced into thin strips
  • 1/2 cup bean sprouts
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce (shoyu)
  • 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
  • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
  • 1/2 cup char siu (Chinese barbecue pork), sliced thin
  • 1/2 cup kamaboko (Japanese fish cake), julienned
  • 4 stalks green onions, sliced into 1-inch pieces
  • 2 cups hot chicken or dashi broth (served on the side)

Instructions

1. Drop the fresh noodles into boiling water for 2 to 3 minutes until they are al dente. Drain them, shake the colander hard to get rid of excess water, and set them aside. 2. Crank your stove to high and heat the peanut oil in a large wok. Toss in the garlic and pork, stir-frying for 3 to 4 minutes until the meat is cooked through and gets a little color on the edges. 3. Throw the bean sprouts into the wok. Give them a quick 30-second toss. They should soften slightly but still keep their crunch. 4. Drop the heat to medium and dump the drained noodles into the wok. 5. Drizzle the soy sauce, oyster sauce, and sesame oil right over the noodles. Use tongs or chopsticks to quickly lift and toss everything. You want every single noodle glossy and coated. 6. Toss in the char siu, kamaboko, and green onions. Cook for one more minute just to heat them through. 7. Divide the dry mein into four bowls. 8. Serve immediately with a small cup of seasoned chicken or dashi broth on the side.

Chef's Tip

Here is the thing most people miss: if your noodles aren't bone-dry when they hit the wok, the oil won't stick, and you'll end up with a soggy, bland mess. Shake that colander like you mean it.

Beef Chow Fun

This is the king of Chinese-Hawaiian takeout. A mountain of wide, chewy, glossy rice noodles, charred beef, and crisp bean sprouts. But here is the catch: making a great beef chow fun is a high-wire act. Those fresh rice noodles are incredibly delicate. If you handle them like a brute, you’ll end up with a plate of broken, mushy paste. You need the "flip-and-fold" technique. Let the wok do the work.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 pound wide, flat fresh rice noodles (Ho Fun)
  • 1/2 pound beef flank steak, sliced thin against the grain
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch
  • 3 tablespoons soy sauce (divided)
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil (divided)
  • 1 tablespoon oyster sauce
  • 1 teaspoon dark soy sauce (for rich color)
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 2 cups fresh mung bean sprouts
  • 1 small yellow onion, sliced thin
  • 3 stalks green onions, cut into 2-inch pieces

Instructions

1. Toss the sliced beef, cornstarch, and 1 tablespoon of soy sauce into a bowl. Mix it with your hands and let it marinate at room temperature for 15 minutes. 2. If your fresh rice noodles are cold and stiff from the fridge, microwave them for 60 seconds to soften them up, then gently peel the strands apart so they don't clump in the pan. 3. Whisk the remaining 2 tablespoons of soy sauce, oyster sauce, dark soy sauce, and sugar in a small bowl. Keep it close. 4. Heat 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil in your wok over high heat until you see wisps of smoke. Lay the marinated beef in a single layer. Let it sear undisturbed for 1 to 2 minutes per side to get a deep, dark crust, then remove the beef and set it aside. 5. Add the remaining tablespoon of oil to the wok. Toss in the yellow onions and stir-fry for 1 minute. 6. Dump in the separated rice noodles. Let them sit on the hot metal for 30 seconds to get a quick sear before you even think about moving them. 7. Pour the sauce mixture over the noodles. Using a wide spatula and tongs, gently lift and fold the noodles to distribute the sauce without tearing them apart. 8. Slide the beef back into the wok, along with the bean sprouts and green onions. Toss everything together for 1 final minute over high heat until the sprouts just begin to wilt. Serve immediately.

Chef's Tip

This is exactly why we use dark soy sauce. It doesn't add much salt, but it gives the noodles that gorgeous, deep mahogany color that makes your mouth water before you even take a bite.

Crispy Cake Noodles with Seafood Gravy

Walk into any legacy Chinese restaurant in Honolulu's Chinatown on a Sunday morning, and you’ll hear the crunch of cake noodles echoing across the dining room. It’s pure drama on a plate. We take fresh egg noodles, press them into a skillet until they form a thick, golden pancake—crispy as potato chips on the outside, soft and chewy in the middle—and then drown them in a thick, savory seafood gravy.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 12 ounces fresh thin egg noodles
  • 4 tablespoons vegetable oil (divided)
  • 1/2 cup shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • 1/2 cup squid, cleaned and scored
  • 1/2 cup fish cake, sliced thin
  • 1 cup broccoli florets
  • 1/2 cup sliced carrots
  • 1/2 cup straw mushrooms
  • 2 cups chicken broth
  • 2 tablespoons oyster sauce
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1/2 teaspoon sesame oil
  • 2 tablespoons cornstarch dissolved in 3 tablespoons water (slurry)

Instructions

1. Boil the egg noodles for exactly 2 minutes. Drain them, rinse with cold water to stop the cooking, and spread them out on a clean kitchen towel. Let them dry for 10 minutes. 2. Heat 2 tablespoons of vegetable oil in a flat-bottomed skillet over medium heat. Press half of the dried noodles into the pan to form an even, flat cake. 3. Don't touch it. Let it cook undisturbed for 5 to 7 minutes until the bottom is deeply golden and crispy. Carefully flip the noodle cake and cook the other side for another 5 minutes, adding a splash of oil if the pan looks dry. Repeat with the second half of the noodles. Cut the cakes into quarters and place them on serving platters. 4. Crank up the heat and warm 1 tablespoon of oil in your wok. Throw in the shrimp, squid, and fish cake. Stir-fry for 2 minutes until the seafood is just cooked, then scoop it out and set aside. 5. Add the last tablespoon of oil to the wok. Stir-fry the broccoli, carrots, and mushrooms for 3 minutes until they are tender but still have some bite. 6. Pour in the chicken broth, oyster sauce, soy sauce, and sesame oil. Bring the liquid to a boil. 7. Toss the seafood back into the wok. Give your cornstarch slurry a quick stir, then pour it in slowly, stirring constantly. Let it bubble for 1 to 2 minutes until the gravy is thick, glossy, and coat-the-back-of-a-spoon rich. 8. Pour the bubbling hot gravy and seafood right over the crispy noodle cakes and serve immediately.

Chef's Tip

Patience is everything here. If you try to flip that noodle cake too early, it will fall apart into a heap of greasy noodles. Wait for that solid, golden crust to form.

Pancit Bihon

If you throw a party in Hawaii and there isn't a massive, foil-covered aluminum pan of pancit bihon sitting on the table, did the party even happen? Brought over by Filipino plantation workers, these razor-thin rice vermicelli noodles are the ultimate crowd-pleaser. They are incredibly light and act like a sponge, soaking up every drop of garlic, chicken broth, and citrus.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 8 ounces dried rice vermicelli noodles (bihon)
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1/2 pound boneless, skinless chicken thighs, sliced thin
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 medium onion, sliced thin
  • 1 cup carrots, julienned
  • 2 cups green cabbage, shredded thin
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 2 cups chicken broth
  • 1 tablespoon fish sauce (patis)
  • 1/2 teaspoon black pepper
  • 3 calamansi limes (or 1 regular lemon), cut into wedges for serving

Instructions

1. Submerge the dried rice vermicelli in a large bowl of warm water. Let them soak for 10 minutes to soften, then drain them well. Use kitchen shears to snip the noodles a few times so they aren't impossibly long to eat. 2. Heat the vegetable oil in a large wok over medium-high heat. Add the sliced chicken and sear for 4 to 5 minutes until it's nicely browned and cooked through. 3. Throw in the garlic and onion, sautéing for about 2 minutes until your kitchen smells incredible and the onions are soft. 4. Toss in the carrots and cabbage. Stir-fry for 2 to 3 minutes until the veggies just start to soften. 5. Pour in the soy sauce, chicken broth, fish sauce, and black pepper. Bring the whole thing to a rolling boil. 6. Add the drained rice noodles to the wok. Using two wooden spoons or tongs, constantly lift and toss the noodles. They will rapidly drink up all that seasoned broth. 7. Cook for 3 to 5 minutes until the pan is dry and the noodles are tender but still springy. 8. Dump the pancit onto a large platter. Pile fresh calamansi wedges around the edges and squeeze that bright, acidic juice over the noodles right before you take your first bite.

Chef's Tip

Never use boiling water to soak your rice vermicelli. It turns them to mush before they even hit the wok. Warm tap water is all you need to prep them for absorbing the real flavor in the pan.

Kimchi Spam Fried Rice

This is the undisputed champion of late-night kitchen-sink cooking. It’s salty, spicy, funky, and unapologetically loud. We fry cubed Spam until the edges get shattered and crispy, let the fat render out, and then toss in tangy, sour, heavily fermented kimchi. Top it with a fried egg—whites crispy, yolk dangerously runny—and you have a masterpiece.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 4 cups cooked jasmine rice (dry, day-old rice works best)
  • 1 can (12 ounces) Spam, cut into 1/2-inch cubes
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 1 cup napa cabbage kimchi, chopped (reserve 2 tablespoons of kimchi juice)
  • 1 tablespoon gochujang (Korean chili paste)
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon sesame oil
  • 4 large eggs
  • 2 stalks green onions, sliced thin
  • 1 tablespoon toasted sesame seeds
  • 1 sheet nori (dried seaweed), crumbled

Instructions

1. In a small bowl, whisk the reserved kimchi juice, gochujang, and soy sauce together until smooth. 2. Heat the vegetable oil in your wok over medium-high heat. Toss in the cubed Spam and fry for 5 to 6 minutes, stirring occasionally, until every side is deeply browned and crispy. 3. Dump the chopped kimchi into the wok. Cook it for 2 to 3 minutes, letting it caramelize in the hot Spam fat. 4. Crank the heat to high. Add the cold jasmine rice, using the back of your spatula to break up any stubborn clumps. 5. Pour the gochujang and kimchi juice mixture evenly over the rice. Toss and fold continuously for 4 to 5 minutes. Every single grain of rice should be stained red and steaming hot. 6. Kill the heat. Stir in the sesame oil and half of your green onions. 7. In a separate non-stick skillet, fry your eggs in a splash of oil. You want crispy, lacy edges and a completely runny yolk. 8. Scoop the fried rice into bowls, slide an egg onto each, and shower them with the remaining green onions, sesame seeds, and crumbled nori.

Chef's Tip

When you actually do this, make sure your kimchi is old and sour. Fresh, sweet kimchi won't cut it. You want that sharp, fermented funk to cut through the rich, salty grease of the Spam.

Garlic Lup Cheong Fried Rice

Lup cheong is the unsung hero of the Hawaii pantry. This sweet, cured Chinese sausage is packed with fat that melts as it hits the hot metal, releasing an aroma that will bring anyone in the house running to the kitchen. When you combine that rendered sweet fat with a mountain of toasted, crispy garlic, you get a fried rice that is so aromatic it doesn't even need a main dish to stand next to.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 12 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 4 cups cooked jasmine rice (dry, day-old)
  • 3 links Lup Cheong (Chinese sausage), sliced thin on the bias
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 6 cloves garlic, finely minced
  • 3 large eggs, beaten
  • 1 cup frozen peas and carrots, thawed
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce (shoyu)
  • 1/2 teaspoon white pepper
  • 3 stalks green onions, sliced thin

Instructions

1. Heat the vegetable oil in your wok over medium-low heat. Toss in the minced garlic and cook for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring constantly. Keep your eyes on it—garlic turns from perfect gold to bitter black in a heartbeat. Scoop out the crispy garlic with a slotted spoon and set it aside, leaving the garlic-infused oil in the wok. 2. Turn the heat up to medium. Add the sliced Lup Cheong and fry for 3 minutes until the edges curl and get slightly crispy. The sausage will release a sweet, reddish fat. 3. Push the sausage to the outer edges of the wok to create an empty space in the middle. Pour the beaten eggs into the center and scramble them quickly. 4. Throw in the thawed peas and carrots, tossing them with the eggs and sausage for 1 minute. 5. Crank the heat to high. Add the cold jasmine rice, stirring fast to break up the grains. 6. Drizzle the soy sauce and white pepper over the rice. Toss everything together for 3 to 4 minutes until the rice is piping hot. 7. Fold in the reserved crispy garlic and sliced green onions. Give it one last toss and pile it onto a platter.

Chef's Tip

Because Lup Cheong is cured with sugar, it burns incredibly fast. Don't rush it on high heat. Keep the flame at medium to let the fat render out slowly without scorching the sausage.

Japanese Yakisoba

Yakisoba is pure street-food soul. In Japan, it’s cooked on massive flat-top grills at summer festivals. In Hawaii, we’ve adopted it as an essential component of the plate lunch. We toss chewy wheat noodles with sliced pork belly, sweet onions, and crisp cabbage, coating it all in a dark, sweet, tangy sauce. It’s savory, a little messy, and pairs perfectly with a scoop of cold macaroni salad. Yes, carbs on carbs. Don't judge.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 3 packages (approx. 5 ounces each) pre-cooked yakisoba noodles
  • 1/2 pound pork belly (or boneless pork chops), sliced into thin bite-sized strips
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1/2 sweet yellow onion, sliced
  • 1 cup carrots, sliced into matchsticks
  • 2 cups green cabbage, chopped into bite-sized pieces
  • 4 tablespoons yakisoba sauce (or 2 tbsp Worcestershire sauce, 1 tbsp oyster sauce, 1 tbsp ketchup, 1 tsp soy sauce, 1 tsp sugar mixed together)
  • 1 tablespoon pickled red ginger (beni shoga) for garnish
  • 1 tablespoon aonori (dried seaweed powder) for garnish

Instructions

1. If your pre-cooked yakisoba noodles are stiff and stuck together, put them in a colander and run hot tap water over them. Gently loosen the strands with your fingers, drain thoroughly, and set aside. 2. Heat the vegetable oil in a large wok over high heat. Add the pork belly and cook for 4 to 5 minutes until the fat renders and the pork gets beautifully browned. 3. Toss the yellow onions and carrots into the wok. Stir-fry for 2 minutes. 4. Throw in the cabbage and cook for another 2 minutes. The cabbage should be tender-crisp, not mushy. 5. Drop the loosened yakisoba noodles into the wok and turn the heat down to medium-high. 6. Pour the yakisoba sauce evenly over the noodles and vegetables. 7. Use tongs to lift and toss the ingredients continuously for 3 to 4 minutes. You want the sauce to caramelize slightly onto the noodles. 8. Divide the noodles among four plates. Garnish with a shake of aonori and a pinch of pickled red ginger on the side.

Chef's Tip

If you don't have bottled yakisoba sauce, don't panic. The quick blend of Worcestershire, oyster sauce, ketchup, shoyu, and sugar listed in the ingredients is incredibly close to the real thing and takes thirty seconds to mix.

Chapter 5: Soups, Stews & Simmered Comforts

You do not look at a calendar in Hawaii to know when winter has arrived.

Instead, you listen. You listen for the sudden, heavy downpours rattling the corrugated tin roofs in the valley. You feel the trade winds shift, bringing a damp, cool chill that creeps down from the volcanic ridges. And if you walk into any local home on one of these rainy afternoons, you will see a massive, seasoned stockpot bubbling away on the back burner.

Comfort on an island is spelled in broth.

The food we cook in these pots is a living map of how we got here. When families from China, Portugal, Japan, the Philippines, and Korea arrived to work the sugar cane plantations in the late nineteenth century, they had very little. But they had their cooking pots. They shared whatever they had across backyard fences. A Japanese dashi met Chinese won tons. Portuguese sausage slipped into the beef stew. Filipino tamarind brightened up the pork. It was survival cooking that became, over generations, the very definition of comfort.

### The Rule of the Back Burner
Never rush a simmer. The depth of island comfort food doesn’t come out of a spice jar or a flavor packet. It comes from the quiet patience of letting bones, ginger, and garlic break down over hours of gentle heat until the water itself becomes rich and velvety. If your kitchen doesn't smell like ginger for at least three hours, you're doing it wrong.

You don’t need to stress over hard-to-find ingredients here. If you can't get your hands on a specific local green, use cabbage. Use spinach. What actually matters is the scale of the operation. You do not make a small pot of stew. You make enough to feed your family, the neighbors, and whoever happens to walk through your kitchen door with an empty stomach.

The Real-Deal Oxtail Soup

Forget the pristine, clear consommés of high-end restaurants. Real oxtail soup is rustic, intensely beefy, and unashamedly fatty. It is a Chinese-style masterclass in patience. The magic isn’t just in the soup itself, but in the ritual of eating it: you dip the tender, gelatinous meat into a small saucer of raw, fiery grated ginger and soy sauce, wash it down with the hot broth, and crunch on the boiled peanuts.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 180 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 3 pounds oxtails, trimmed of excess outer fat
  • 1 piece (3 inches) fresh ginger, sliced and thoroughly smashed
  • 4 whole star anise pods
  • 1 strip dried tangerine or orange peel (about 2 inches)
  • 1/2 cup raw, skin-on peanuts
  • 6 dried shiitake mushrooms, soaked in hot water and halved
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 bunch Chinese mustard greens (gai choy), washed and chopped into thick bite-sized pieces
  • 1 cup fresh cilantro, rough chopped
  • 1/2 cup green onions, sliced
  • For serving: Lots of grated fresh ginger and shoyu (soy sauce)

Instructions

1. The Parboil: Drop the oxtails into a massive pot. Cover them with cold water, crank the heat to high, and let them boil hard for 10 minutes. Don't skip this. You want to pull all the grey scum and impurities out of the bones. 2. The Rinse: Drain the oxtails completely. Wash the meat under cold water to clean off any remaining grit, and wash out the pot itself. 3. The Slow Simmer: Return the clean meat to the clean pot. Pour in 12 cups of cold water. Toss in the smashed ginger, star anise, orange peel, raw peanuts, and the rehydrated shiitakes. Bring it to a boil, then immediately drop the heat to low. Cover and let it simmer gently for 2.5 to 3 hours. You want the meat fork-tender, but not completely falling off the bone yet. 4. The Seasoning: Stir in the soy sauce. Taste the broth. Add salt as needed. Use a ladle to skim off the heavy slick of fat floating on top. 5. The Greens: Just before you call everyone to the table, stir in the chopped mustard greens. Cook them for 3 minutes. They should wilt but still keep their bitter, peppery kick and bright green color. 6. The Assembly: Ladle the soup into deep bowls. Make sure everyone gets a mix of meat, peanuts, and mushrooms. Pile the green onions and cilantro on top. Serve with individual dipping bowls of grated ginger and shoyu.

How to Execute This Perfectly

Do it the day before. Seriously. Build the soup, let it cool, and put the whole pot in the fridge overnight. The fat will solidify into a hard white disk on top. Pop that disk off with a spoon the next morning, and you are left with a perfectly clean, gelatinous, hyper-flavorful broth that is ready to reheat.

Portuguese Bean Soup

If you grew up in Hawaii, you have eaten this soup out of a paper cup at a school carnival or a backyard graduation party. It is thick, smoky, and heavy enough to stand a spoon up in. The base relies on smoky linguica sausage and smoked ham hocks that cook down until they dissolve into a rich, tomato-infused gravy.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 120 mins | Servings: 8

Ingredients

  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 12 ounces Portuguese sausage (linguica), sliced thick
  • 1 pound smoked ham hocks
  • 1 large yellow onion, diced
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 2 stalks celery, sliced
  • 3 medium carrots, peeled and sliced into thick coins
  • 2 medium russet potatoes, peeled and cubed
  • 1 can (15 ounces) red kidney beans, rinsed
  • 1 can (15 ounces) tomato sauce
  • 6 cups beef broth
  • 1/2 small head of green cabbage, shredded thin
  • 1 cup dry elbow macaroni
  • Salt and freshly cracked black pepper

Instructions

1. Brown the Sausage: Heat the vegetable oil in your heaviest stockpot over medium heat. Drop in the sliced Portuguese sausage. Sear it until the edges are crispy and the oil turns a deep red-orange, about 5 minutes. Use a slotted spoon to pull the sausage out, but leave all that glorious fat in the pot. 2. The Aromatics: Toss the onion, garlic, celery, and carrots into that hot sausage fat. Sauté for 6 minutes until the onions go translucent and the kitchen smells incredible. 3. The Long Braise: Throw the sausage back into the pot. Add the smoked ham hocks, cubed potatoes, kidney beans, tomato sauce, and beef broth. Bring it to a rolling boil, then drop the heat to low. Cover and let it simmer for an hour and a half. The ham hock meat should be slipping off the bone. 4. Shred the Meat: Pull the ham hocks out onto a cutting board. Shred the tender, smoky meat off the bone. Throw away the bones and fat, and stir the meat back into the soup. 5. The Pasta and Cabbage: Turn the heat up slightly and add the dry macaroni and shredded cabbage. Simmer uncovered for 10 to 12 minutes until the pasta is cooked and the cabbage is soft. Season generously with black pepper and salt.

How to Execute This Perfectly

If you can't find Portuguese sausage: Do not use bland breakfast sausage. Grab a highly smoked, spicy Andouille or a firm Spanish chorizo instead. You need that deep, smoky paprika oil to build the base of the soup.

Red-Gravy Beef Stew

Let's get one thing straight: this is not the pale, watery beef stew you find in school cafeterias. This is a plate lunch legend. It is thick, sweet, savory, and characterized by its deep red color. You do not eat this out of a bowl with crackers; you ladle it directly over two giant scoops of hot, sticky white rice.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 120 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds beef chuck roast, cut into 1.5-inch chunks
  • 1/4 cup all-purpose flour
  • 2 tablespoons vegetable oil
  • 1 large yellow onion, cut into thick wedges
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 can (6 ounces) tomato paste
  • 4 cups beef broth
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce (shoyu)
  • 1 tablespoon Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tablespoon brown sugar
  • 2 bay leaves
  • 3 large carrots, peeled and cut into big chunks
  • 3 medium russet potatoes, peeled and cut into big chunks
  • 2 stalks celery, cut into 1-inch pieces
  • Salt and black pepper

Instructions

1. Dredge and Sear: Season the beef chunks heavily with salt and pepper, then toss them in the flour until coated. Heat the oil in a Dutch oven over medium-high heat. Sear the beef in batches. Do not crowd the pan, or the meat will steam instead of brown. You want a hard, dark crust. Transfer the beef to a plate. 2. The Paste: Turn the heat down to medium. Sauté the onion wedges and minced garlic for 3 minutes. Plop the tomato paste into the center of the pan. Cook it, stirring constantly, for 2 minutes until it turns a dark brick-red color. This caramelizes the sugars and gets rid of the raw metallic taste. 3. The Liquid: Slowly pour in the beef broth, scraping up all the caramelized flour and beef bits from the bottom of the pot. Stir in the shoyu, Worcestershire, brown sugar, and bay leaves. 4. The Simmer: Put the beef back in. Bring to a boil, cover, and drop the heat to low. Let it cook for 1 hour. 5. The Veggies: Stir in the carrots, potatoes, and celery. Cover and simmer for another 45 minutes to an hour. The beef should yield to a fork, the potatoes should be soft, and the gravy should be thick and glossy. Taste and add salt or pepper if needed.

How to Execute This Perfectly

Do not chop your vegetables too small. If you dice your carrots and potatoes, they will dissolve into mush during the long simmer. Keep them in large, rustic chunks so they hold their shape and structure against the heavy beef.

Chicken Long Rice

Despite what the name says, there is no rice in this dish. It is a soup made of clear bean thread noodles (which we call "long rice") that act like sponges, soaking up a heavily gingered chicken broth. It is light, simple, and absolutely mandatory at any backyard party.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 40 mins | Servings: 6

``` THE SAIMIN VS. WON TON MIN HIERARCHY

[ Saimin ] The simple foundation. Curly wheat noodles, hot dashi-shoyu broth, a slice of pink kamaboko, and some green onions. Late-night comfort in its purest form. │ ▼ [ Won Ton Min ] The upgrade. Take that same hot bowl of saimin and drop in 4 to 5 plump pork-and-shrimp won tons, along with sweet roasted char siu pork and tender baby bok choy. ```

Ingredients

  • 8 ounces dry bean thread noodles (long rice)
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 2 pounds bone-in, skinless chicken thighs
  • 1 piece (3 inches) fresh ginger, peeled and sliced into thin matchsticks
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 6 cups chicken broth
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon Hawaiian salt (or kosher salt)
  • 4 green onions, cut into 1-inch lengths
  • Black pepper

Instructions

1. Soak the Noodles: Put the dry bean thread noodles in a large bowl and cover them with warm water. Let them sit for 15 minutes until they are soft and pliable. Drain them, then use kitchen shears to snip them into shorter, manageable lengths (about 6 inches). 2. The Aromatics: Heat the oil in a large pot over medium heat. Throw in the ginger matchsticks and garlic. Cook for 2 minutes until your kitchen smells like a ginger workshop. 3. The Chicken: Add the chicken thighs and sear them slightly for 5 minutes. Pour in the chicken broth, soy sauce, and salt. Bring to a boil, then cover, drop the heat to low, and simmer for 25 minutes until the chicken is tender. 4. The Shred: Pull the chicken out of the pot. Let it cool for a minute, then use two forks to shred the meat off the bones. Throw the bones away and put the shredded meat back into the bubbling broth. 5. The Finish: Drop the soaked noodles into the pot. Simmer uncovered for 5 to 7 minutes. The noodles will absorb the broth and turn completely translucent. Stir in the green onions, season with black pepper, and serve hot.

How to Execute This Perfectly

Watch the noodles closely. Bean thread noodles are incredibly thirsty. If you let them sit in the hot pot after cooking, they will absorb every single drop of broth. If you are making this ahead of time, keep some extra chicken broth on hand to loosen things up when you reheat.

Stove-Top Pork Lau Lau

If you go to a traditional luau, you will eat pork lau lau that has been steamed for hours in an underground pit oven (imu) wrapped in ti leaves. This kitchen version uses your stove-top steamer to deliver that exact same smoky, melt-in-your-mouth fat without you having to dig up your backyard.

Prep time: 30 mins | Cook time: 240 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds pork butt, cut into 2-inch chunks
  • 1/2 pound salted butterfish (or black cod), cut into 6 pieces
  • 2 tablespoons Hawaiian sea salt (alaea salt)
  • 1 tablespoon liquid smoke
  • 30 fresh taro leaves (luau leaves), washed well and stems removed
  • 12 fresh ti leaves, washed and center ribs shaved flat (or heavy-duty foil)
  • Kitchen twine

Instructions

1. Season the Pork: Toss the pork butt chunks in a bowl with the Hawaiian salt and liquid smoke until fully coated. 2. Build the Bundle: Lay out two ti leaves in an "X" shape on your counter (if you don't have ti leaves, use a large square of heavy aluminum foil). Layer 5 taro leaves in the center, overlapping them to make a solid green mat. 3. The Filling: Place a few chunks of seasoned pork and one piece of salted fish in the very center of the taro leaves. 4. The Wrap: Fold the taro leaves tightly over the meat and fish like a little package. Then, wrap the outer ti leaves (or foil) around that package to make a waterproof bundle. Tie it tightly with kitchen twine. Repeat until you have 6 bundles. 5. The Long Steam: Set up a large pot with a steamer basket. Make sure the water level is just below the bottom of the basket. Pack the bundles inside. Cover with a tight lid, bring the water to a boil, and then drop the heat to low. Steam them for 4 full hours. Check the water level every hour and add boiling water as it evaporates. 6. To Serve: Snip the twine and unwrap the outer ti leaves (remember: the outer ti leaves/foil are not edible, but the inner taro leaves are soft, delicious, and taste like rich spinach).

How to Execute This Perfectly

Do not cut the steaming time short. Taro leaves contain tiny calcium oxalate crystals that can cause a highly uncomfortable scratchy, itchy feeling in your throat if they aren't fully cooked. Four hours of steam completely breaks these down, making the greens incredibly soft and safe to eat.

Pork Sinigang

This Filipino staple is a sour-savory punch to the senses. The sharp, lip-smacking acidity of tamarind cuts through the rich, buttery fat of pork belly, while the vegetables absorb the complex broth. It is the ultimate restorative soup when you are feeling run down.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 60 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds pork belly or pork spare ribs, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 1 medium yellow onion, sliced
  • 2 medium tomatoes, cut into wedges
  • 1 packet (1.4 ounces) tamarind soup mix (Sinigang mix)
  • 8 cups water (or rice-washing water)
  • 1 medium daikon radish, peeled and sliced into rounds
  • 10 yard-long beans, cut into 2-inch lengths
  • 1 medium eggplant, sliced
  • 2 cups baby bok choy, halved
  • 2 long green finger chilies
  • 2 tablespoons fish sauce (patis), to taste

Instructions

1. The Sear: Heat the oil in a large, deep pot over medium-high heat. Sear the pork belly pieces until they get some color and start to render their fat, about 6 minutes. 2. The Base: Throw in the onion and tomato wedges. Sauté for 3 minutes until the tomatoes soften and begin to break apart. 3. The Simmer: Pour in the water. Bring it to a boil, then drop the heat to low. Cover the pot and let the pork simmer gently for 40 minutes until it is tender. 4. Go Sour: Stir in the tamarind soup mix until completely dissolved. Drop in the daikon radish and the whole green finger chilies. Simmer for 5 minutes. 5. The Veggies: Stir in the long beans and eggplant. Cook for another 5 minutes. 6. The Greens: Fold in the baby bok choy and the fish sauce. Cover the pot, turn off the heat completely, and let it sit on the warm burner for 2 minutes to wilt the greens before serving with hot rice.

How to Execute This Perfectly

Use rice water. When you wash your raw white rice before cooking, don't dump the cloudy water down the drain. Save it and use it as the liquid base for this soup. The starches in the rice water give the sour broth a silky, velvety body that water can't match.

Saimin-Style Won Ton Min

A legendary local diner mashup. Saimin is Hawaii’s native noodle soup, but when you want to go all out, you order Won Ton Min. It pairs curly wheat noodles with plump, handmade pork-and-shrimp won tons in a light, clean dashi-chicken broth.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 package (12 ounces) fresh saimin or ramen noodles
  • 1 package won ton wrappers
  • For the Filling:
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 1/4 pound raw shrimp, peeled and finely chopped
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon toasted sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon grated fresh ginger
  • 2 green onions, minced
  • For the Broth:
  • 4 cups chicken broth
  • 4 cups dashi stock
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • For the Toppings:
  • 1/2 cup sweet sliced char siu pork
  • 1/2 roll kamaboko (pink fish cake), sliced
  • 1 cup baby bok choy, blanched
  • 1/2 cup sliced green onions

Instructions

1. Make the Filling: In a bowl, vigorously mix the ground pork, chopped shrimp, soy sauce, sesame oil, ginger, and minced green onions with your hands until the mixture becomes sticky and paste-like. 2. Fold the Won Tons: Lay a wrapper on your hand. Put a teaspoon of filling in the center. Wet the edges of the wrapper with water, fold it diagonally into a triangle, and press the air out to seal. Grab the two long bottom corners of the triangle, fold them toward each other, overlap them, and pinch them together with a drop of water. Repeat to make 16 to 20 won tons. 3. The Broth: Heat the chicken broth, dashi, and soy sauce in a pot until simmering. Keep it hot on low. 4. Cook the Elements: Bring a separate pot of water to a boil. Drop the won tons in and cook for 4 to 5 minutes until they float and the pork is cooked. Scoop them out. In that same boiling water, cook your noodles for 2 minutes, then drain. 5. Assemble: Divide the hot noodles into four large bowls. Top each with 4 cooked won tons, slices of char siu, fish cake, and bok choy. Ladle the hot broth over everything and cover with green onions.

How to Execute This Perfectly

Do not cook your noodles in the soup broth. If you boil the fresh noodles directly in your dashi-chicken broth, the starch from the noodles will bleed into the soup, making it cloudy, thick, and pasty. Always boil and drain the noodles separately before combining.

Miso Soup with Fresh Clams

This is not the standard, powdery instant miso soup you get as an afterthought. This version uses fresh, sweet clams to release their briny liquor directly into a delicate white miso base. It is incredibly clean, fast, and satisfying.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 pound fresh Manila clams, scrubbed
  • 4 cups dashi stock
  • 3 tablespoons white (shiro) miso paste
  • 1/2 block silken tofu, cut into neat small cubes
  • 3 green onions, green parts only, thinly sliced

Instructions

1. Purge the Clams: Put the scrubbed clams into a bowl of cool, lightly salted water. Let them sit for 15 minutes in a dark spot so they spit out any hidden sand. Rinse them well. 2. The Steam: Bring the dashi stock to a gentle simmer in a saucepan. Drop the clean clams in, cover the pot, and let them cook for 3 to 5 minutes. As soon as they pop open, turn the heat down to low. Throw away any clams that refuse to open. 3. The Miso: Never boil miso. It ruins the delicate, live flavors. Instead, scoop 1/2 cup of the hot dashi into a small bowl. Add your miso paste and whisk it with a fork until it is smooth and lump-free. Pour this mixture back into the pot and stir. 4. The Finish: Drop in the silken tofu cubes. Let them warm through in the hot soup for 1 minute. Ladle the clams, tofu, and rich broth into bowls and scatter the green onions on top.

How to Execute This Perfectly

The Sand Trick: Want to make sure you never bite into grit? Toss a single tablespoon of cornmeal into the salt water while the clams are purging. The clams will eat the cornmeal and push out every last grain of sand hiding inside their shells.

That was the year the first Sakadas—Filipino plantation workers—stepped off the boats and into the sugar cane fields of Hawaii. They didn't have much. But their wives and mothers, our beloved Babays and Nanays, possessed a rare kind of culinary genius. This isn't dainty tweezers-and-garnish food. It’s survival food elevated to high art. It’s about the grit of a woman who could feed twenty hungry field hands with nothing but a handful of backyard yardlong beans, a bottle of shoyu, and sheer willpower.

"If your hands don't smell like garlic and vinegar by the time dinner is ready, you didn't make it with enough love." — Aunty Gloria, Waipahu

To cook like a local Filipino grandma, you have to throw away your measuring spoons. Seriously, put them in a drawer. Her style of cooking is guided entirely by instinct, a heavy hand with the seasoning, and three essential pillars:

The Acid: Sharp cane vinegar (sukang maasim*) or standard white vinegar to cut through the heavy, rich fats. The Umami: A splash of salty patis (fish sauce) or a spoonful of funky, fermented bagoong* (shrimp paste) to add deep, savory funk.

  • The Aromatics: Not a clove or two, but entire heads of smashed garlic, fat coins of fresh ginger, and thick slices of yellow onion.

Chicken Adobo

The unofficial national dish of the Philippines, adobo is a masterclass in preservation and flavor balance. This local Hawaii version balances salty shoyu with tangy cane vinegar and plenty of crushed garlic. It tastes even better the next day after the flavors have melded together in the refrigerator.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 40 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 3 lbs chicken thighs, bone-in and skin-on
  • 1 head garlic, peeled and thoroughly smashed
  • 1/2 cup soy sauce (shoyu)
  • 1/2 cup Filipino cane vinegar
  • 1 cup water
  • 3 dried bay leaves
  • 1 tbsp whole black peppercorns
  • 1 tbsp brown sugar
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil

Instructions

1. Grab a heavy pot or a seasoned Dutch oven and slick the bottom with the oil over medium-high heat. Lay the chicken thighs in skin-side down. Don’t crowd them. Let them sear until the skin is beautifully golden and crisp—about 5 to 6 minutes—then flip them to brown the other side for another 3 minutes. Transfer the chicken to a plate for now. 2. See all that rendered chicken fat in the pot? Don't throw it away. Keep about a tablespoon of it in there, turn the heat down to medium, and throw in your mountain of smashed garlic. Stir it around until your kitchen smells incredible and the garlic starts to turn a pale gold. Don't let it burn. 3. Put the chicken back in, along with any juices that pooled on the plate. Pour in the soy sauce, water, bay leaves, whole black peppercorns, and the brown sugar. 4. Now, pour the cane vinegar right over the top. Do not stir. Seriously, leave the spoon alone. Bring the liquid to a hard boil uncovered for 3 to 4 minutes. Why? You need to let the raw, eye-watering sting of the vinegar boil off. If you stir it too early, you'll end up with a harsh, sour mess. 5. Once that initial vinegar punch has mellowed, lower the heat to a gentle simmer, cover the pot, and let it ride for 25 minutes until the meat is practically falling off the bone. 6. Take the lid off and crank the heat back to medium-high. Let the sauce bubble away and reduce for 5 to 10 minutes, stirring occasionally, until it thickens into a glossy, dark glaze that clings to every inch of the chicken.

Grandma's Rule of Thumb

Resist every urge to stir the pot the second you pour in the vinegar. Letting it boil undisturbed allows the harsh acid to transform into a sweet, tangy glaze. It's the difference between a mediocre adobo and a legendary one.

Pork Guisantes

Pork Guisantes is a beloved Hawaii-Filipino comfort stew made with tender pork, sweet green peas, and pimientos in a savory tomato broth. Seasoned with fish sauce, it is a staple at local graduation parties and family gatherings. The bright contrast of the peas makes it as beautiful as it is delicious.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs pork shoulder, cut into 1/2-inch cubes
  • 2 tbsp cooking oil
  • 1 medium yellow onion, diced
  • 5 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, minced
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (patis)
  • 1 can (8 oz) tomato sauce
  • 1 cup chicken broth
  • 1 cup frozen sweet green peas
  • 1 jar (4 oz) sliced pimientos, drained
  • 1/2 tsp ground black pepper
  • 1/2 tsp salt

Instructions

1. Slick a deep skillet or a wide pot with the cooking oil over medium-high heat. Drop in your pork cubes in a single layer. Let them get a deep, gorgeous sear on all sides—give it about 8 to 10 minutes. 2. Push the pork to the outer edges of the pan to create a little clearing in the center. Toss in your diced onion, garlic, and ginger. Sauté them right in the middle until they soften and release their oils, which should take about 3 minutes. 3. Splash in the fish sauce and stir everything together, using your wooden spoon to scrape up all those delicious brown bits stuck to the bottom of the pan. 4. Pour in the tomato sauce and chicken broth. Bring it to a rolling boil, then slap the lid on and drop the heat to low. Let it simmer gently for 30 minutes, or until the pork yields to the gentlest pressure of a fork. 5. Take the cover off and fold in the frozen green peas and sliced pimientos. Let it simmer uncovered for another 5 minutes, just until the peas turn a vibrant green and the sauce thickens up. 6. Give it a quick taste, season with a pinch of salt and a grind of black pepper, and ladle it hot over a mountain of white rice.

Grandma’s Secret Weapon

Want to know how to take this from everyday home cooking to party-ready decadent? Stir in two tablespoons of creamy peanut butter or a splash of heavy cream right at the end. It rounds out the sharp tomato acidity and creates a velvety sauce that will have people scraping the pot.

Sari-Sari Vegetable Stew

"Sari-sari" translates to "variety," and this rustic stew is the ultimate way to use up whatever fresh vegetables are growing in the backyard garden. Simmered in a savory broth accented with shrimp and pork, it is a wholesome, comforting meal that honors the thrifty spirit of plantation-era grandmas. It is best enjoyed hot over a mound of white rice.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 25 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 lb pork belly, sliced into thin strips
  • 1/2 lb medium shrimp, peeled and deveined
  • 1 tbsp cooking oil
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 medium onion, sliced
  • 2 roma tomatoes, sliced
  • 2 cups kabocha pumpkin, cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 1 bunch yardlong beans (sitaw), cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 medium eggplant, sliced into rounds
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (patis)
  • 2 cups water
  • 1/2 tsp ground black pepper

Instructions

1. Heat your cooking oil in a large pot over medium heat. Drop in the pork belly slices and let them cook slowly, rendering out their fat until the edges get nice and crispy, about 5 minutes. 2. Throw in your garlic, onions, and sliced tomatoes. Use the back of your spoon to crush the tomatoes as they cook, letting them bleed their juices into the pork fat until everything is soft and aromatic. 3. Toss in the shrimp and stir-fry them just until they flash pink, which takes about 2 minutes. Now, pull the shrimp out and set them aside on a plate. Nobody likes rubbery, overcooked shrimp. 4. Pour the water and fish sauce into the pot, then drop in the cubed kabocha pumpkin. Bring the whole thing to a boil, cover, and let it simmer for about 8 minutes until the pumpkin just starts to yield. 5. Add the yardlong beans and eggplant slices. Cover it back up and simmer for another 6 to 8 minutes until the vegetables are tender but still hold their shape. 6. Slide the cooked shrimp back into the pot, crack some black pepper over the top, and give it one final, gentle stir. Let it warm through for a minute, then serve it piping hot.

The Golden Rule

Don't peel that kabocha pumpkin! The skin is completely edible, holds its shape during the simmer so your stew doesn't turn into baby food, and adds a great earthy bite.

Pinakbet

Pinakbet is a northern Filipino vegetable masterpiece defined by its rich, savory seasoning of fermented shrimp paste (bagoong). This Hawaii version features local favorites like bitter melon, eggplant, and yardlong beans steamed together with crispy pork belly. It is an earthy, robust dish that captures the authentic taste of the countryside.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 30 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 lb pork belly, cut into 1/2-inch thick slices
  • 2 tbsp fermented shrimp paste (bagoong alamang)
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 medium onion, sliced
  • 2 large tomatoes, chopped
  • 1 cup water
  • 2 cups kabocha pumpkin, cut into cubes
  • 1 medium bitter melon (ampalaya), cored and sliced
  • 1 medium eggplant, sliced
  • 10-12 pieces fresh okra
  • 1 bunch yardlong beans, cut into 3-inch pieces
  • 1/4 tsp black pepper

Instructions

1. Start with a cold, dry wok or a deep pot. Lay your sliced pork belly in it, then turn the heat to medium. Let the pork slowly heat up and render its own fat, stirring occasionally, until the meat is beautifully crispy and golden, about 10 minutes. 2. Spoon out most of the rendered fat, leaving just about a tablespoon in the pan. Toss in your garlic, onion, and chopped tomatoes. Sauté them until the onions turn translucent and the tomatoes melt down into a jammy paste. 3. Stir in the fermented shrimp paste and let it fry in the hot fat for 2 minutes. Yes, it’s going to smell incredibly strong. That’s exactly what you want. 4. Pour in the water and bring it to a boil. Drop in your kabocha pumpkin cubes, cover the pot, and let them cook for 5 minutes. 5. Here’s the trick: lay the rest of your vegetables on top of the pumpkin in layers. First the yardlong beans, then the bitter melon, okra, and eggplant. Do not stir. 6. Clamp the lid on tight and drop the heat to medium-low. Let the vegetables steam in the rising, savory steam for 12 to 15 minutes. Every now and then, give the pot a gentle shake by the handles. Once the vegetables are tender but still vibrant, dust with black pepper and serve.

Grandma’s Secret Weapon

If bitter melon makes you squeamish, rub the sliced pieces with a generous tablespoon of salt and let them sit for 10 minutes. Rinse them thoroughly in cold water and squeeze them dry before cooking. This draws out the harsh, mouth-puckering bitterness, leaving behind a clean, pleasant bite.

Pork Dinuguan

Often playfully referred to as "chocolate meat" to encourage reluctant children to try it, Pork Dinuguan is a rich, savory stew simmered in a vinegar-infused pork blood gravy. This traditional delicacy balances deep, earthy notes with a sharp tanginess and a hint of heat from green chili peppers. It is traditionally paired with sweet, steamed puto (rice cakes) or warm rice.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 50 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs pork belly, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 1 tbsp cooking oil
  • 1 head garlic, minced
  • 1 medium yellow onion, diced
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, minced
  • 1/2 cup white vinegar
  • 1 1/2 cups water
  • 10 oz frozen pork blood, thawed completely
  • 2 tbsp fish sauce (patis)
  • 1 tbsp brown sugar
  • 2 green banana peppers (sili mahaba)
  • 2 dried bay leaves
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

1. Slick a large, heavy pot with cooking oil and set it over medium heat. Drop in the pork belly and let it cook until it renders its fat and turns a deep golden brown, about 10 to 12 minutes. 2. Toss in your garlic, onion, and ginger. Sauté them in the pork fat for about 3 minutes until they’re soft and highly aromatic. 3. Pour in the white vinegar and bring it to a rolling boil. Let it simmer uncovered for 3 to 5 minutes. Do not stir it. You want that sharp, harsh alcohol bite to evaporate. 4. Pour in the water, bay leaves, and fish sauce. Bring the pot back to a boil, then turn the heat down to low, cover, and let it simmer for 20 minutes until the pork belly is meltingly tender. 5. While that's simmering, grab a whisk and thoroughly beat your thawed pork blood in a bowl to break up any lumps. Slowly drizzle the blood into the simmering pot, whisking or stirring the stew constantly. This is crucial—if you don't stir, the blood will curdle and clump. 6. Drop in the green banana peppers and brown sugar. Simmer uncovered over low heat for another 15 minutes, stirring frequently, until the gravy transforms into a thick, glossy, near-black sauce. Season with a pinch of salt and pepper to taste before serving.

The Golden Rule

Whisk a tablespoon of vinegar directly into the raw pork blood before it ever touches the hot pot. It acts as an insurance policy, keeping the blood perfectly smooth and preventing it from clotting during cooking.

Beef Caldereta

Beef Caldereta is a festive, Spanish-influenced stew that features melt-in-your-mouth beef simmered in a rich tomato sauce thickened with liver spread. Local grandmas love to add a handful of grated cheese at the end to bring an unexpected creaminess and savory depth to the dish. It is a hearty favorite reserved for special family celebrations and Sunday dinners.

Prep time: 25 mins | Cook time: 1 hour 30 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 lbs beef chuck, cut into 1.5-inch cubes
  • 3 tbsp cooking oil
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 medium onion, chopped
  • 1 can (8 oz) tomato sauce
  • 3 cups beef broth
  • 1 can (3 oz) liver spread (such as Underwood)

  • 2 medium carrots, peeled and sliced into thick rounds

  • 2 bay leaves
  • 1/2 tsp chili flakes (optional)
  • Salt and pepper to taste

Instructions

1. Heat 2 tablespoons of oil in a heavy-duty Dutch oven over high heat. Sear your beef cubes in batches. If canned liver spread isn't sitting on your grocery store shelf, don't panic. Grab a high-quality, smooth pork or chicken liver pâté from the deli counter. It does the exact same job, giving the sauce that signature velvety texture and deep, rich undertone.

Local Meat Jun

This dish is a true Hawaii-Korean hybrid. You won't find it on menus in Seoul, but it is legendary across the islands. Thinly sliced beef is marinated in a sweet soy mixture, dredged in flour, dipped in egg batter, and pan-fried to golden-brown perfection. It is traditionally served with a tangy, spicy soy-vinegar dipping sauce.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 lb flank steak or ribeye, sliced paper-thin against the grain
  • 1/2 cup flour
  • 4 large eggs, beaten
  • 3 tbsp vegetable oil (for frying)

For the Marinade:

  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/4 cup white sugar
  • 2 tbsp sesame oil
  • 4 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tsp minced fresh ginger
  • 2 green onions, finely chopped

For the Dipping Sauce:

  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1 tsp sugar
  • 1 tsp sesame oil
  • 1/2 tsp gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)

Instructions

1. Whisk together the soy sauce, sugar, sesame oil, garlic, ginger, and green onions in a medium bowl until the sugar dissolves. 2. Toss the thinly sliced beef into the marinade. Use your hands to make sure every single slice is coated. Cover it and let it sit in the fridge for at least 30 minutes. Two hours is even better if you have the patience. 3. In a small bowl, whisk the dipping sauce ingredients together. Set it aside so the flavors can mingle. 4. Set up a neat assembly line: flour in one shallow dish, beaten eggs in another. 5. Heat 1 tablespoon of vegetable oil in a large skillet over medium heat. Don't let it get smoking hot, or the egg coating will burn before the beef cooks. 6. Take a slice of marinated beef, shake off the excess liquid, dredge it lightly in flour, and then submerge it in the beaten egg. 7. Lay the battered beef straight into the hot skillet. Fry for about 2 minutes per side. You want the egg coating to turn a beautiful golden brown while the thin beef cooks through. 8. Work in batches. Slice the finished meat jun into bite-sized strips and serve them hot alongside that dipping sauce.

Kitchen Note

Trying to slice raw beef paper-thin is a nightmare. Do yourself a favor: throw the meat in the freezer for 40 minutes beforehand. Once it's firm, your knife will glide through it like butter.

Chicken Hekka

Born in the plantation camps, chicken hekka is Hawaii’s rustic, local-style adaptation of Japanese sukiyaki. It features tender chicken, vegetables, and slippery long rice simmered in a rich ginger-soy broth, traditionally cooked outdoors in a heavy cast-iron wok.

Prep time: 25 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1.5 lbs boneless, skinless chicken thighs, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 2 bundles dried long rice (cellophane/bean thread noodles)
  • 2 tbsp vegetable oil
  • 1 large sweet Maui onion, sliced
  • 1 cup sliced bamboo shoots
  • 1 cup sliced water chestnuts
  • 8 fresh shiitake mushrooms, stems removed and sliced
  • 2 cups fresh watercress, cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 bunch green onions, cut into 1-inch pieces

For the Simmering Broth:

  • 3/4 cup soy sauce
  • 1/2 cup mirin
  • 1/2 cup sake
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar
  • 1 tbsp grated fresh ginger
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced

Instructions

1. Soak the long rice in warm water for about 15 minutes to soften it up. Drain it, then use kitchen shears to cut the noodles into manageable 4-inch lengths. 2. Whisk the soy sauce, mirin, sake, brown sugar, ginger, and garlic together in a bowl until the sugar dissolves. 3. Heat the vegetable oil in a large wok or deep skillet over medium-high heat. Sear the chicken pieces until they get some color on them, about 5 minutes. 4. Toss in the sliced sweet onion and shiitake mushrooms. Cook for another 3 minutes until the onions begin to soften and turn translucent. 5. Pour in the bamboo shoots, water chestnuts, and that sweet, gingery simmering broth. Bring it to a boil, then drop the heat down to medium-low. 6. Fold in the drained long rice. Push the noodles down into the liquid so they can drink up all that savory broth. Simmer for 5 minutes until they turn translucent and tender. 7. Toss the watercress and green onions on top. Fold them in gently for just a minute or two until they wilt. Serve it straight out of the hot pan.

Kitchen Note

Watercress turns bitter and stringy if you overcook it. Keep it brief. You want that peppery, bright green crunch to offset the sweet, heavy noodles.

Sukiyaki is the ultimate Japanese comfort food, perfected for cozy family gatherings. Thinly sliced beef, firm tofu, and fresh vegetables are simmered at the table in a sweet and savory sauce called warishita.

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ribeye, sliced paper-thin

  • 1 bundle shirataki noodles, drained and rinsed
  • 1 bag enoki mushrooms, roots removed
  • 6 fresh shiitake mushrooms, stems removed and caps scored
  • 1 small napa cabbage, cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 large leek, sliced diagonally into 1/2-inch thick rounds
  • 1 tbsp vegetable oil

For the Warishita (Sauce):

  • 1/2 cup soy sauce
  • 1/2 cup mirin
  • 1/2 cup sake
  • 1/4 cup sugar
  • 1/2 cup dashi stock (or water)

Instructions

1. In a small saucepan, combine the soy sauce, mirin, sake, sugar, and dashi stock. Simmer gently over medium heat just until the sugar dissolves. Set it aside. 2. Heat the vegetable oil in a shallow, wide pot or electric skillet over medium-high heat. 3. Lay a few slices of the thin ribeye into the pan. Pork Tonkatsu

Forget greasy, heavy fried food. We're looking for an ear-shattering, clean crunch that gives way to pork so tender it practically dissolves. These panko-encrusted pork cutlets are fried to perfection and served with a tangy, sweet tonkatsu sauce.

Ingredients

  • 4 center-cut, boneless pork loin chops (about 1/2-inch thick)
  • Salt and freshly ground black pepper, to taste
  • 1/2 cup all-purpose flour
  • 2 large eggs, beaten
  • 1.5 cups panko breadcrumbs
  • Vegetable oil, for deep frying
  • 1/2 head green cabbage, shredded very thin (for serving)

For the Tonkatsu Sauce:

  • 1/4 cup ketchup
  • 2.5 tbsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 1 tsp sugar

Instructions

1. Use a meat mallet to gently pound the pork chops to a uniform 1/3-inch thickness. Take a knife and make small snips in the fat and connective tissue along the edges. Set up your breading stations: flour first, then egg, then panko. 4. Dust a pork chop in flour, shake off the excess, submerge it in egg, and then press it firmly into the panko. Pork and Chive Gyoza

These aren't those soggy, frozen potstickers you buy in bulk. These Japanese-style potstickers feature a delicate wrapper stuffed with a juicy, garlic-heavy pork filling. Fried to a crisp golden brown on the bottom and steamed to perfection on top, they are the ultimate crowd-pleaser.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1 lb ground pork
  • 1 cup finely chopped garlic chives (or green onions)
  • 1 cup napa cabbage, finely minced and squeezed dry
  • 2 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tsp grated fresh ginger
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 1 tbsp sake
  • 1 tsp sesame oil
  • 1/2 tsp salt
  • 1/4 tsp black pepper
  • 1 pack round gyoza wrappers (about 40-50 wrappers)
  • 2 tbsp vegetable oil (for frying)
  • 1/2 cup water (for steaming)

For the Dipping Sauce:

  • 3 tbsp soy sauce
  • 2 tbsp rice vinegar
  • 1/2 tsp chili oil (optional)

Instructions

1. Throw the ground pork, garlic chives, minced cabbage, garlic, ginger, soy sauce, sake, sesame oil, salt, and pepper into a large bowl. Mix it vigorously with your hands until the meat changes texture and becomes sticky and cohesive. 2. Place a gyoza wrapper in the palm of your hand. Kimchi Jigae

This spicy, bubbling Korean stew is the ultimate comfort food for a rainy day. Well-fermented kimchi is stewed with pork belly and soft tofu to create a rich, comforting broth packed with complex umami flavor.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 30 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1/2 lb pork belly, cut into bite-sized pieces
  • 2 cups well-fermented, sour kimchi, chopped
  • 1/2 onion, sliced
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 tbsp gochujang (Korean chili paste)
  • 1 tbsp gochugaru (Korean chili flakes)
  • 1 tbsp soy sauce
  • 2 cups anchovy kelp broth (or chicken stock)
  • 1/2 cup kimchi juice (from the jar)
  • 1 block soft or medium tofu, sliced into 1/2-inch thick slabs
  • 3 green onions, cut into 1-inch pieces

Instructions

1. Throw the pork belly into a cold, heavy pot over medium heat. Let it render out its own fat and turn golden brown, about 5 minutes. 2. Toss in the chopped kimchi, sliced onion, and minced garlic. Chapter 8: Chinese-Hawaiian Heritage

The sweet, smoky grease of red-rimmed char siu dripping onto a paper plate next to a mound of salty kalua pig—this is the real flavor of Hawaii. Forget the pineapple-topped pizzas sold to tourists in Waikiki. The true culinary soul of the islands lives in the backyard tents of baby luaus and on the crowded tables of Sunday family gatherings. It is a story written in grease, smoke, and steam, starting back in 1852 when the merchant ship Thetis docked in Honolulu, carrying the first wave of Chinese contract laborers from Guangdong and Hainan.

These workers came to sweat in the sugarcane fields. But once those grueling contracts ended, they did what survivors do: they built something of their own. They packed up their belongings, moved into urban centers like Honolulu’s Chinatown, and opened grocery stores, noodle stands, and humble chop suey houses. In doing so, they completely redrew the map of how Hawaii eats.

The magic of Chinese-Hawaiian cooking isn't found in high-end restaurant kitchens. It lives in the quiet adaptability of home cooks who had to make do. When traditional Cantonese ingredients were impossible to find, grandmas experimented with what was growing in the yard or stocking the plantation co-op shelves. They married Cantonese precision—the delicate art of steaming, poaching, and glazing—with the heavy, rich abundance of the islands. The heavy iron wok became as essential to the local household as the rice cooker. It is a legacy born of sheer resourcefulness, where screaming-hot oil, bruised ginger, and the funk of fermented paste turn cheap, tough cuts of meat into pure comfort.

Do you want to cook like a real island grandma? Then your pantry cannot be an afterthought. You can't just sub out ingredients and hope for the best.

"In a local Chinese home, food is how we speak. We don't say 'I love you'; we put a steaming plate of ginger chicken in front of you and tell you to eat while it's hot." — Aunty Sau Hing

Before you even think about turning on the gas flame, you need to stock these four cornerstones. If you don't have them, wait until you do.

  • Oyster Sauce: Buy the good stuff—the kind where oyster extract is the first ingredient, not water and cornstarch. This is the rich, savory-sweet backbone of every local gravy.
  • Chinese Five-Spice: A heavy, aromatic punch of fennel, cloves, cinnamon, star anise, and Sichuan peppercorns. A pinch goes a long way; a tablespoon will hijack your entire kitchen.
  • Shaoxing Rice Wine: Don't use dry sherry if you can help it. This wine cuts through the fatty richness of pork and coaxes out the aromatics in a smoking hot wok.
  • Red Fermented Bean Curd: Intensely salty, creamy, and deeply savory. This is the secret weapon for Hakka-style slow braises. It smells funky out of the jar, but cooks down into pure gold.

Oven-Roasted Char Siu Pork

This is the sweet, sticky, crimson pork you see hanging in Chinatown windows. In Hawaii, we put it on everything. We slice it into hot bowls of saimin, tuck it into pillowy manapua buns, or—let's be honest—eat it warm right off the cutting board with our fingers. You don't need a professional rotisserie hook to get those charred, caramelized edges. A standard home oven can do the heavy lifting if you treat the meat right.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 3 pounds pork butt (shoulder), sliced lengthwise into 2-inch wide strips
  • 1/3 cup hoisin sauce
  • 1/4 cup honey
  • 1/4 cup soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons Shaoxing rice wine
  • 1 tablespoon brown sugar
  • 1 tablespoon minced garlic
  • 1 teaspoon Chinese five-spice powder
  • 1 teaspoon red food coloring (completely optional, but it gives that nostalgic Chinatown look)
  • 1 tablespoon maltose or honey (for the final, sticky glaze)

Instructions

1. Grab a large bowl and whisk together the hoisin, honey, soy sauce, Shaoxing rice wine, brown sugar, garlic, five-spice, and food coloring until smooth. 2. Toss the pork strips into the bowl. Massage that marinade into every crevice of the meat. Cover it up and let it sleep in the fridge for at least 8 hours. Overnight is even better if you want the flavor to penetrate to the bone. 3. When you're ready to cook, preheat your oven to 400°F (200°C). Line a heavy rimmed baking sheet with aluminum foil—trust me, the sugar in the marinade will burn and ruin your pan if you skip this—and set a metal wire rack on top. 4. Shake off the excess marinade and lay the pork strips on the wire rack. Leave some breathing room between each piece so the hot air can circulate. Keep that leftover marinade; we aren't throwing away flavor. 5. Roast the pork for 25 minutes. While the kitchen starts to smell incredible, pour that reserved marinade into a small saucepan. Bring it to a boil over medium heat and let it bubble for 5 minutes to kill any raw meat bacteria. Stir in the extra tablespoon of maltose or honey to make it thick and glossy. 6. Flip the pork strips over. Brush them heavily with the boiled marinade. Slide them back into the oven for another 15 to 20 minutes. You want the edges to blacken, char, and turn sticky-crisp, and the internal temperature to hit 160°F (71°C). 7. Pull the pan out. Slather on one final layer of the glossy glaze. Let the meat rest for 10 minutes before you even think about slicing it. If you cut it too soon, all those delicious juices will run out onto your board.

The Pro Move

Do not let the pork sit on a flat pan. The metal rack is non-negotiable. It lifts the meat so it roasts instead of boiling in its own rendering fat. That hot, dry air circulation is the only way to get those crispy, charred edges without drying out the interior.

Cold Ginger Chicken

At any local island potluck, this dish is gone within twenty minutes. It is a masterpiece of temperature play: ice-cold, silky-smooth poached chicken topped with a sizzling-hot, intensely aromatic ginger-scallion oil. It sounds incredibly simple, but the clean flavors rely entirely on technique. It is light, refreshing, and absolutely perfect on a muggy Honolulu afternoon.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 45 mins | Servings: 4-6

Ingredients

  • 1 whole chicken (3 to 4 pounds), preferably pasture-raised for better flavor
  • 3 inches fresh ginger, sliced into thick, bruised coins
  • 4 green onions, cut into 2-inch pieces
  • 1 tablespoon coarse salt
  • 1 tablespoon Shaoxing rice wine
  • A large bowl filled with ice water

For the Ginger-Scallion Sauce:

  • 1/2 cup fresh ginger, grated fine (don't skimp here)
  • 1 cup green onions, green and white parts, chopped fine
  • 1/2 cup peanut oil (or any high-heat oil with a neutral taste)
  • 1 1/2 teaspoons salt
  • 1/2 teaspoon ground white pepper

Instructions

1. Clean the chicken inside and out, then pat the skin completely dry with paper towels. Rub the coarse salt all over the skin and inside the cavity. 2. Place the chicken in a large, heavy pot. Stuff the cavity with your bruised ginger coins and green onion pieces. Pour in enough cold water to submerge the bird completely, then stir in the Shaoxing wine. 3. Bring the water to a rolling boil over high heat. The moment it boils, drop the heat to low, clamp a tight lid on the pot, and let it simmer as gently as possible for 35 to 40 minutes. 4. Kill the heat. Leave the pot on the burner, covered, for another 15 minutes. This gentle, residual steaming ensures the breast meat stays tender instead of turning stringy. 5. While the chicken rests, prep your ice bath. Carefully lift the hot chicken out of the pot and plunge it straight into the ice water. Leave it there for 10 minutes. It feels wrong, but this shocks the skin, locking in the natural gelatin and keeping the meat incredibly juicy. 6. Remove the chicken from the cold bath, pat it dry, and lightly rub the skin with a splash of sesame oil for shine. Use a sharp cleaver to chop the chicken through the bone into bite-sized pieces, then arrange them on a platter. 7. To make the sauce, mix the grated ginger, chopped green onions, salt, and white pepper in a heatproof glass or metal bowl. 8. Heat your peanut oil in a small pan until you see the first wisps of white smoke. Carefully pour the hot oil directly over the ginger and scallions. It will hiss and bubble violently, releasing an incredible aroma. Stir the mixture and let it cool slightly before spooning it generously over the cold chicken.

The Pro Move

Do not skip the ice shock. Dropping the hot bird into ice-cold water stops the cooking instantly and transforms the fat beneath the skin into a cool, savory jelly. That gelatinous texture is what separates a true home-cooked Chinese chicken from a generic boiled bird.

Steamed Fish with Soy and Ginger

This dish is pure elegance, symbolizing wealth and abundance at family feasts. Delicate white fish is gently steamed until it just flakes, layered with raw aromatics, and then splashed with smoking-hot oil to flash-fry the ginger and scallions right on top of the plate. It is finished with a seasoned soy sauce that pools around the fish.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 whole snapper or sea bass (1.5 to 2 pounds, cleaned, scaled, with gills removed), or 4 thick, firm white fish fillets
  • 2 inches fresh ginger, peeled and sliced into matchsticks (julienned)
  • 4 green onions, shredded into fine curls
  • 1 cup fresh cilantro leaves
  • 1/3 cup peanut oil

For the Seasoned Soy Sauce:

  • 1/4 cup light soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons water
  • 1 tablespoon Shaoxing rice wine
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 1/4 teaspoon toasted sesame oil

Instructions

1. Pat the fish dry. If you're using a whole fish, make three shallow, diagonal cuts on both sides. This helps the thickest parts of the fish cook at the same rate as the tail. 2. Find a heatproof plate that fits comfortably inside your steamer basket. Lay half of your ginger matchsticks on the plate, place the fish on top, and scatter the rest of the ginger over and inside the fish. 3. Get the water in your steamer to a rolling boil. Carefully set the plate with the fish inside, cover tightly, and steam on high heat for 10 to 12 minutes. Heat your peanut oil in another small pan until it is shimmering and smoking hot. Carefully pour this screaming-hot oil directly over the green onions and ginger. It will crackle, cook the raw herbs instantly, and release their aromatic oils into the soy sauce below. Serve it immediately with plenty of hot rice.

The Pro Move

Keep the seasoned soy sauce off the top of the fish. If you pour it directly over the skin, you'll wash away the heat of the hot oil splash and make your herbs soggy. Keep the sauce in the bottom pool so diners can spoon it up with the fish as they eat.

Honey Walnut Shrimp

Go to any Chinese restaurant in Honolulu on a Saturday night, and you will see a plate of these golden, crispy shrimp on almost every table. It is a glorious, decadent contradiction: light, crispy tempura-style batter tossed in a sweet, tangy condensed milk glaze, topped with crunchy, sweet candied walnuts. It is sweet, savory, and completely addictive.

Ingredients

  • 1 pound large shrimp, peeled, deveined, and patted dry
  • 1/2 cup cornstarch or potato starch
  • 2 large egg whites, whisked until frothy but not stiff
  • Neutral oil, for deep-frying

For the Candied Walnuts:

  • 1/2 cup walnut halves
  • 1/2 cup water
  • 1/2 cup sugar

For the Sweet Creamy Sauce:

  • 1/4 cup Kewpie mayonnaise (use Kewpie—standard mayo is too sour)
  • 2 tablespoons sweetened condensed milk
  • 1 tablespoon raw honey
  • 1 teaspoon fresh lemon juice

Instructions

1. First, make the candied walnuts. Bring the water and sugar to a boil in a small saucepan. Toss in the walnuts and boil for 2 minutes. Standard flour batter turns heavy and soggy under the weight of a mayo-based sauce. Using egg whites and pure cornstarch or potato starch creates a delicate, glass-like shell that stays remarkably crunchy even when coated in sweet glaze.

Beef and Broccoli

This is the ultimate weeknight comfort food. It's a quick, high-heat stir-fry that pairs tender, caramelized strips of flank steak with crisp, vibrant green broccoli. The real secret here isn't the sauce—it’s the classic Chinese technique of "velveting" the beef to make even the cheapest cut melt-in-your-mouth tender.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 pound flank steak, sliced thinly against the grain
  • 3 cups fresh broccoli florets
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced fine
  • 1 teaspoon fresh ginger, grated
  • 2 tablespoons neutral vegetable oil

For the Beef Marinade:

  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon Shaoxing rice wine
  • 1 teaspoon cornstarch
  • 1/2 teaspoon baking soda (the ultimate meat tenderizer)

For the Stir-Fry Sauce:

  • 1/3 cup low-sodium chicken broth
  • 3 tablespoons oyster sauce
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon brown sugar
  • 1 teaspoon toasted sesame oil
  • 1 teaspoon cornstarch

Instructions

1. Slice your flank steak at a sharp angle against the grain. In a bowl, toss the beef slices with the soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, cornstarch, and baking soda. Let it sit at room temperature for 15 minutes while you prep everything else. 2. In a small bowl, whisk together your chicken broth, oyster sauce, soy sauce, brown sugar, sesame oil, and cornstarch. Make sure the starch is completely dissolved so you don't get lumps in your gravy. 3. Bring a small pot of water to a boil. Drop the broccoli florets in for just 60 seconds until they turn a brilliant green. Drain them immediately and run them under cold tap water to stop the cooking. Pat them dry. 4. Get your wok or largest skillet screaming hot over high heat. Pour in 1 tablespoon of oil. 5. Lay the marinated beef in a single layer. Let it sear undisturbed for one full minute to develop a deep, dark crust. Stir-fry for another minute until the meat is browned but still slightly pink in the center, then transfer it to a plate. 6. Drop the heat to medium-high. Add the remaining tablespoon of oil to the wok, then throw in the garlic and ginger. Stir-fry for 30 seconds. Don't let the garlic burn, or it will turn bitter. 7. Return the beef and blanched broccoli to the wok. Give your sauce mixture a quick stir to kick up the starch at the bottom, and pour it in. 8. Toss everything together fast. Within a minute, the sauce will bubble, clear up, and thicken into a glossy glaze that coats every single floret. Plate it up and serve with hot rice.

The Pro Move

That half-teaspoon of baking soda in the marinade is a restaurant secret. It raises the pH on the surface of the meat, making it difficult for the proteins to bond tightly when cooked. This keeps the beef incredibly tender, even if you accidentally overcook it.

Sweet and Sour Pork

Forget the neon-orange syrup you get at mall food courts. This is the real deal: crispy, double-fried chunks of pork shoulder tossed with sweet local pineapple, crisp bell peppers, and pungent onions in a perfectly balanced, tangy vinegar-and-ketchup glaze. It is sweet, tart, and deeply satisfying.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 4-6

Ingredients

  • 1.5 pounds pork shoulder (butt), cut into 1-inch cubes
  • 1 green bell pepper, cut into 1-inch squares
  • 1 medium yellow onion, cut into 1-inch pieces
  • 1 cup fresh pineapple chunks (don't use canned if you can avoid it—fresh has much better acidity)
  • 1 egg, beaten
  • 1/2 cup cornstarch (for dredging)
  • Vegetable oil, for deep-frying

For the Pork Marinade:

  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon Shaoxing rice wine
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 teaspoon ground white pepper

For the Sweet and Sour Sauce:

  • 1/3 cup ketchup
  • 1/3 cup rice vinegar (unseasoned)
  • 1/3 cup packed light brown sugar
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch mixed with 2 tablespoons water (your slurry)

Instructions

1. In a bowl, mix the pork cubes with the soy sauce, Shaoxing wine, salt, and white pepper. Let those flavors sink into the meat for 20 minutes. 2. Whisk the ketchup, rice vinegar, brown sugar, and soy sauce together in a bowl until the sugar is fully dissolved. 3. Heat about 3 inches of oil in your wok or a deep pot until it reaches 350°F (175°C). 4. Pour the beaten egg into your marinated pork and stir to coat every piece. Dredge each piece of pork thoroughly in the cornstarch, pressing it on so it sticks, then shake off any loose powder. 5. Carefully drop the pork into the hot oil in batches. Fry for 4 to 5 minutes until the coating is a light golden brown. Scoop them out and drain on paper towels. 6. Now, pour out all but 1 tablespoon of oil from your wok. Heat it over high heat. Throw in the bell pepper and onion squares. Stir-fry for 2 minutes. You want them to soften slightly but still have a satisfying crunch. 7. Pour your sauce mixture into the wok and bring it to a boil. Give your cornstarch slurry a quick stir and pour it in. Let the sauce bubble and thicken for a minute until it looks like shiny glaze. 8. Toss your fried pork and pineapple chunks into the wok. Turn off the heat and quickly toss everything together so the sauce coats every nook and cranny. Serve immediately before the steam softens that crispy pork crust.

The Pro Move

Want that legendary, shatteringly crisp crunch? Double fry your pork. Fry it once at 350°F to cook the meat through. Right before you toss it into the sauce, crank your oil heat up to 375°F and flash-fry the pork a second time for just 60 seconds. It will stay crunchy much longer.

Kau Yuk (Pork Belly with Taro)

This is a true labor of love. A classic Hakka dish, it features alternating slabs of rich pork belly and earthy local taro, slow-steamed for hours in a savory red bean curd sauce. The taro works like a sponge, drinking up the melting pork fat and rich juices. It is a spectacular centerpiece reserved for Chinese New Year or serious family celebrations.

Prep time: 45 mins | Cook time: 2 hours 30 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 2 pounds pork belly (look for a uniform, square block with even layers of fat and meat)
  • 1 pound fresh taro root, peeled and sliced into 1/2-inch thick slabs
  • 2 tablespoons dark soy sauce
  • Vegetable oil, for frying

For the Braising Sauce:

  • 3 cubes red fermented bean curd (plus 2 tablespoons of the packing juice)
  • 2 tablespoons Shaoxing rice wine
  • 2 tablespoons soy sauce
  • 2 tablespoons brown sugar
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon Chinese five-spice powder
  • 1.5 cups low-sodium chicken broth

Instructions

1. Place your whole block of pork belly in a large pot of cold water. Bring it to a boil and let it cook for 20 minutes to firm up the meat. Drain it, and pat the skin completely dry with paper towels. 2. Use a metal skewer or a fork to prick the pork skin all over. Do this thoroughly; the more tiny holes you make, the better the skin will blister. Rub the dark soy sauce all over the skin and meat. 3. Pour an inch of oil into a deep wok and heat it up. Carefully lower the pork belly, skin-side down, into the hot oil. Cover the wok immediately with a lid. The skin will pop and splatter violently. Fry for 5 minutes until the skin is deeply blistered and dark brown. Flip it and fry the meat side for 2 minutes. Pull it out and submerge it in ice water for 15 minutes, then slice it into 1/2-inch thick slabs. 4. In the same oil, fry your taro slices for 2 to 3 minutes per side until they develop a light, protective crust. This keeps the taro from disintegrating during the long steam. Drain them well. 5. In a medium bowl, mash the red fermented bean curd cubes with their juice, Shaoxing wine, soy sauce, brown sugar, garlic, five-spice, and chicken broth until you have a smooth, rich sauce. 6. Dip each slice of pork and taro into this sauce. In a deep, heatproof bowl, pack the slices tightly, alternating one slice of pork (skin-side down) and one slice of taro. Pour whatever sauce is left over the top. 7. Set the bowl inside a large steamer. Cover tightly and steam over medium-low heat for a full 2 hours. Keep an eye on your steamer's water level and top it off with boiling water as needed. The pork should be completely melt-in-your-mouth tender. 8. To serve, place a deep-rimmed platter face-down over your steaming bowl. In one quick, confident motion, flip both the bowl and plate over. Lift the bowl away; the pork and taro will release onto the plate in a beautiful dome, skin-side up, drenched in that rich mahogany gravy.

The Pro Move

That ice bath shock after frying the pork belly is what creates the legendary "wrinkled" skin of authentic Kau Yuk. It forces the hot, blistered skin to contract rapidly, creating pockets that drink up the braising liquid.

Mapo Tofu

The original Sichuan version of this dish is a fiery, tongue-numbing assault of chili oil and Sichuan peppercorns. But the local Hawaii style is a gentler, warmer kind of comfort. It leans heavily on the deep, savory umami of garlic, ginger, and fermented black beans, making it incredibly cozy spooned over a bowl of hot white rice.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 block (14 ounces) silken or soft tofu, cut into 3/4-inch cubes
  • 1/2 pound ground pork
  • 1 tablespoon fermented black beans (douchi), rinsed and chopped fine
  • 1 tablespoon chili garlic sauce (or Toban Djan for a bit more depth)
  • 3 cloves garlic, minced
  • 1 teaspoon fresh ginger, grated
  • 1/2 cup low-sodium chicken broth
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • 1 tablespoon cornstarch mixed with 2 tablespoons water (slurry)
  • 3 green onions, chopped fine
  • 1 teaspoon toasted sesame oil

Instructions

1. Gently slide your cubed tofu into a bowl of warm, lightly salted water. Let it sit for 5 minutes, then drain it carefully. This simple step seasons the tofu and helps the delicate cubes hold their shape when tossed in the wok. 2. Heat 1 tablespoon of oil in your wok over medium-high heat. Add the ground pork and stir-fry for 3 to 4 minutes, breaking it up into fine crumbles with your spatula, until it is nicely browned. 3. Push the pork to the outer edges of the wok. In the center, add your garlic, ginger, chopped fermented black beans, and chili garlic sauce. Stir-fry for 60 seconds until your kitchen smells amazing. 4. Stir the pork back into the aromatics, then pour in the chicken broth, soy sauce, and sugar. Bring the whole mixture to a gentle simmer. 5. Carefully slide your drained tofu cubes into the wok. Instead of stirring roughly—which will turn your tofu into mush—use the back of your spatula to gently push the tofu through the sauce. Simmer for 3 minutes to heat the tofu through. 6. Give your cornstarch slurry a stir, then drizzle it in slowly. Keep gently moving the tofu until the sauce thickens and turns into a glossy, rich gravy. 7. Turn off the heat. Drizzle with sesame oil and scatter the chopped green onions over the top. Serve it piping hot in a shallow bowl with plenty of rice to soak up the sauce.

The Pro Move

Do not skip the warm salt-water soak for the tofu. Silken tofu is incredibly fragile and full of water. The salt water draws out just enough excess moisture to tighten up the protein structure, meaning your cubes stay whole and beautiful instead of turning into scrambled eggs.

Baked Butter Mochi

If you want to make friends in Hawaii instantly, show up with a warm 9x13 pan of butter mochi. It is the undisputed king of the local potluck. It’s got a wild, contradictory texture—crisp, caramelized edges on the outside, and a chewy, bouncy bite on the inside.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 60 mins | Servings: 24 squares

Ingredients

  • 1 box (16 ounces) glutinous rice flour (Mochiko)
  • 2 cups granulated sugar
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, melted and cooled
  • 4 large eggs, beaten
  • 1 can (13.5 ounces) coconut milk
  • 1 can (12 ounces) evaporated milk
  • 2 teaspoons vanilla extract

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Slick a 9x13-inch baking pan with plenty of butter or nonstick spray. 2. Grab a large mixing bowl and whisk together the Mochiko flour, sugar, and baking powder. Make sure there are no clumps. 3. In a separate bowl, beat the eggs, then whisk in the melted butter, coconut milk, evaporated milk, and vanilla extract. 4. Pour the wet mixture directly into the dry ingredients. Whisk like crazy until the batter is completely smooth. It will look quite wet, but don't worry—that's exactly what you want. 5. Pour the batter into your greased pan, using a spatula to get every last drop out of the bowl. 6. Bake for 55 to 60 minutes. You’re looking for a deep, golden-brown crust on top and edges that are starting to pull away from the sides of the pan. 7. Let it cool completely right in the pan. If you cut it while it’s hot, you'll end up with a sticky mess. Slice it into squares when cool.

Chef's Tip

Here's the trick most people miss: never use a metal knife to cut mochi. It will stick and tear. Use a plastic knife instead. It glides through the sticky, chewy texture like butter.

Haupia (Coconut Pudding)

Simple, elegant, and incredibly refreshing, this traditional Hawaiian coconut pudding is a staple at every single luau. It has a firm, clean bite but relies entirely on cornstarch—not gelatin—to get its signature set.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 16 squares

Ingredients

  • 2 cans (13.5 ounces each) coconut milk
  • 1 cup water
  • 3/4 cup granulated sugar
  • 3/4 cup cornstarch
  • 1/4 teaspoon salt

Instructions

1. Lightly splash an 8x8-inch baking dish with water, dump the water out, and wipe any major puddles with a paper towel. Leaving it slightly damp keeps the pudding from sticking. 2. In a medium saucepan, combine one can of the coconut milk, the sugar, and the salt. Place it over medium-low heat and stir until the sugar completely dissolves. 3. While that warms up, whisk the second can of coconut milk, the water, and the cornstarch in a bowl. Whisk until it’s perfectly smooth. Any lumps here will ruin the final texture. 4. Slowly pour the cornstarch slurry into the warm saucepan, whisking the entire time. 5. Crank the heat up to medium. Don’t walk away. Stir constantly with a wooden spoon, making sure you scrape the bottom and the tight corners of the pot so nothing burns. 6. After about 8 to 10 minutes, the mixture will suddenly thicken, turn glossy, and start to bubble. Keep stirring for another minute to cook out the raw starch taste. 7. Pour the thick, hot pudding into your prepared dish and smooth out the top. 8. Let it cool to room temperature on the counter, then cover it and slide it into the fridge for at least 4 hours. Once it's completely firm, slice it into clean 2-inch squares and serve cold.

Chef's Tip

To take this up a notch, toast some unsweetened shredded coconut in a dry pan until it's golden and fragrant. Sprinkle it over the top of the haupia just before you slice it.

Sugar-Dusted Malasadas

Let’s get one thing straight: a proper malasada is not just a donut. It’s a hot, yeast-risen cloud of fried dough, heavily rolled in sugar, with absolutely no hole in the middle. If you aren't eating them hot enough to singe your fingers, you're doing it wrong.

Prep time: 120 mins | Cook time: 20 mins | Servings: 18 malasadas

Ingredients

  • 1 package (0.25 ounces) active dry yeast
  • 1 teaspoon plus 1/2 cup granulated sugar (divided)
  • 1/4 cup warm water (around 110°F)
  • 6 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1 can (12 ounces) evaporated milk
  • 1/2 cup unsalted butter, melted
  • 6 large eggs, room temperature
  • Neutral oil (like canola or vegetable), for deep frying
  • 1 cup granulated sugar, for coating

Instructions

1. Wake up the yeast: In a small bowl, stir the yeast and 1 teaspoon of sugar into the warm water. Let it sit for 10 minutes until it’s frothy and smells like fresh bread. 2. In a large, deep bowl, whisk together the flour, the remaining 1/2 cup of sugar, and the salt. 3. In another bowl, beat the eggs thoroughly, then stir in the evaporated milk and melted butter. 4. Dump the yeast mixture and the wet ingredients into the flour. Mix with a wooden spoon until you get a very soft, sticky dough. 5. Cover the bowl with a damp kitchen towel. Put it in a warm, draft-free spot and let it rise for about an hour and a half, or until it has doubled in size. 6. Fill a heavy pot with about 3 inches of oil and heat it to 350°F (175°C). Use a candy thermometer—temp control is everything here. 7. Rub a little oil on your hands so the dough doesn't stick to you. Pull off golf-ball-sized portions of the puffy dough, gently stretching them into slightly flattened rounds. 8. Carefully drop the dough into the hot oil. Don't crowd the pot. Fry for 2 to 3 minutes per side until they are a deep, beautiful golden brown. 9. Scoop them out with a slotted spoon and let them drain on paper towels for just a few seconds. 10. While they are still hot, drop them into a bowl of granulated sugar and toss them until they are completely coated. Eat them immediately.

Chef's Tip

Want to elevate these? Poke a hole in the side of a warm malasada with a chopstick and use a pastry bag to squeeze in a generous dollop of coconut haupia pudding or tart passion fruit curd.

Chichi Dango Mochi

You’ve probably seen these pillowy, pastel-pink squares at baby showers or graduation parties, packed into tiny cellophane bags tied with curling ribbon. Aunty Gladys down the street used to make giant batches of these, and we’d walk home with our fingers completely dusted in white starch.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 60 mins | Servings: 36 bite-sized pieces

Ingredients

  • 1 box (16 ounces) glutinous rice flour (Mochiko)
  • 2 cups granulated sugar
  • 1 teaspoon baking powder
  • 1 can (13.5 ounces) coconut milk
  • 1 3/4 cups water
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 3 to 4 drops red food coloring
  • 1 cup potato starch (Katakuriko) or cornstarch, for dusting

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease a 9x13-inch baking pan. 2. In a large bowl, whisk together the Mochiko, sugar, and baking powder. 3. In a separate bowl, whisk the coconut milk, water, vanilla, and red food coloring until you get a pretty, soft pastel pink. 4. Pour the pink liquid into the dry ingredients and whisk until completely smooth. If you see any stubborn lumps, strain the whole batter through a fine-mesh sieve. 5. Pour the batter into your greased pan. Cover the pan incredibly tightly with aluminum foil—this traps the steam, which is what actually cooks the mochi. 6. Bake for 1 hour. When it's done, the mochi should be set and slightly translucent. 7. Let the mochi cool completely in the pan for a few hours. Don't rush this. 8. Dust a large cutting board with a thick layer of potato starch. Turn the cool mochi out onto the starch, then dust the top with more starch. 9. Using a plastic knife or a starch-dusted knife, cut the mochi into bite-sized rectangles. Toss each piece in the extra starch to prevent them from sticking to each other, then gently tap off the excess.

Chef's Tip

Do not try to substitute regular flour for the potato starch (Katakuriko). Raw flour will taste chalky and ruin the treat. Potato starch keeps the mochi dry and soft without altering its delicate flavor.

Shave Ice with Azuki Beans and Condensed Milk

Shave ice is an art form, not a snow cone. If you hear someone call it "shaved" ice with a "d," you immediately know they're from out of town. The texture must be like fresh winter snow—so delicate that it melts the second it hits your tongue.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1 can (15 ounces) sweet azuki bean paste (tsubuan)
  • 4 cups block ice (tempered for shaving)
  • 1/2 cup strawberry syrup
  • 1/2 cup blue Hawaii or lilikoi (passion fruit) syrup
  • 1/2 cup sweetened condensed milk
  • 4 scoops vanilla ice cream (optional)

Instructions

1. Grab 4 wide serving bowls. Drop 2 to 3 tablespoons of sweet azuki bean paste right into the bottom of each bowl. If you're using ice cream, plop a scoop right next to the beans. 2. Shave your ice. You want a high-quality machine that shaves the ice into fluffy, cloud-like ribbons, not crunchy little crystals. 3. Pack the shaved ice gently but firmly over the beans and ice cream, shaping it into a neat dome. Don't pack it too tight, or the syrup won't soak through. 4. Slowly pour your syrups over the dome. Pour one flavor on one half and another flavor on the other half to make a vibrant, colorful split. 5. Drizzle a generous amount of sweetened condensed milk—known locally as the "snow cap"—right over the top. Serve immediately with both a spoon and a wide straw.

Chef's Tip

To get that legendary, ultra-fluffy texture, let your ice blocks sit on the kitchen counter for 5 to 10 minutes before you start shaving. This is called "tempering" the ice. If it's too cold straight out of the freezer, it will shave crunchy instead of snowy.

Guava Chiffon Cake

This is the crown jewel of local island bakeries. It looks like a pink cloud and tastes like a tropical dream. The key is balance: the chiffon itself is incredibly light, while the tart, glossy guava glaze on top brings a bright punch that keeps it from being cloyingly sweet.

Prep time: 30 mins | Cook time: 55 mins | Servings: 12

Ingredients

  • 2 cups cake flour
  • 1 1/2 cups granulated sugar (divided)
  • 1 tablespoon baking powder
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/2 cup vegetable oil
  • 6 large egg yolks, room temperature
  • 3/4 cup canned guava nectar
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla extract
  • 3 to 4 drops pink food coloring
  • 8 large egg whites, room temperature
  • 1/2 teaspoon cream of tartar

For the Guava Glaze:

  • 2 cups canned guava nectar
  • 1/4 cup cornstarch
  • 1/2 cup granulated sugar
  • 1 tablespoon fresh lemon juice

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 325°F (165°C). Grab a 10-inch tube pan, but whatever you do, do not grease it. The cake needs to literally climb up the dry walls of the pan to rise. 2. In a large bowl, sift together the cake flour, 1 cup of the sugar, the baking powder, and the salt. 3. Make a deep well in the center of these dry ingredients. Pour in the oil, egg yolks, guava nectar, vanilla, and pink food coloring. Whisk until perfectly smooth. 4. In a spotlessly clean bowl, whip the egg whites and cream of tartar with an electric mixer until foamy. Gradually rain in the remaining 1/2 cup of sugar, beating continuously until you get stiff, glossy peaks. 5. Scoop about a third of the whipped whites into the guava batter and fold it in gently to lighten the mixture. Fold in the rest of the whites in two batches. Be gentle—you don't want to knock the air out of the batter. 6. Pour the batter into your tube pan. Bake for 50 to 55 minutes. A wooden skewer inserted into the center should come out clean. 7. The second you take the cake out of the oven, flip the pan upside down onto a wire rack or the neck of a glass bottle. Let it cool completely upside down for 2 hours before you try to slip it out of the pan. 8. Make the glaze: Whisk the guava nectar, cornstarch, sugar, and lemon juice in a small saucepan over medium heat. Bring it to a boil, stirring constantly. It will thicken up and turn beautifully clear. Let it cool to room temperature, then pour it over the cooled cake.

Chef's Tip

Seriously, do not grease the pan. If there is even a trace of grease on the walls, the batter won't be able to cling and rise, and you’ll end up with a dense, flat disk instead of a towering chiffon.

Plantation-Style Mango Bread

If you live in Hawaii, mango season is a contact sport. Suddenly, your backyard tree is dropping fruit faster than you can pick it, your kitchen smells like ripening skin, and you are desperately trying to give away grocery bags of fruit to anyone who will take them. This incredibly moist, spiced bread is the absolute best way to use up your sweet, juicy backyard haul.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 60 mins | Servings: 8 to 10

Ingredients

  • 2 cups all-purpose flour
  • 1 1/2 teaspoons baking soda
  • 2 teaspoons ground cinnamon
  • 1/2 teaspoon salt
  • 1/4 cup granulated sugar
  • 3 large eggs, room temperature
  • 3/4 cup vegetable oil
  • 2 cups ripe mango, diced small
  • 1/2 cup chopped macadamia nuts (optional)
  • 1/2 cup raisins (optional)

Instructions

1. Preheat your oven to 350°F (175°C). Grease and flour a 9x5-inch loaf pan, or line it with parchment paper. 2. In a medium bowl, whisk together the flour, baking soda, ground cinnamon, and salt. 3. In a large bowl, whisk the sugar, eggs, and vegetable oil together until the mixture turns a pale yellow and thickens slightly. 4. Gradually stir the dry ingredients into the wet mixture using a wooden spoon. Stop stirring the second the flour disappears—overmixing makes the bread tough. 5. Gently fold in the diced mango, nuts, and raisins. The batter will seem incredibly thick, but don't worry; the mangoes release a ton of moisture as they bake. 6. Spread the batter into your prepared loaf pan and smooth out the top. 7. Bake for 55 to 65 minutes. Poke a toothpick into the very center; it should come out clean. 8. Let the bread rest in the pan for 10 minutes, then tip it out onto a wire rack to cool completely before you slice into it.

Chef's Tip

This bread actually tastes significantly better the next day. Wrap the cooled loaf tightly in plastic wrap and let it sit on the counter overnight. The mango juices and cinnamon will meld together into something spectacular.

Banana Turon

Brought to Hawaii by Filipino immigrant families, these crispy, sweet spring rolls are a legendary street food. Here is a warning: do not rush into a hot plate of turon. The molten, caramelized sugar shell on the outside of these rolls will easily blister the roof of your mouth if you're impatient. But god, is that wait worth it.

Prep time: 20 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 10 pieces

Ingredients

  • 5 ripe saba bananas (or 3 regular bananas, firm but ripe)
  • 1/2 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 10 lumpia wrappers (or spring roll wrappers)
  • 1/2 cup canned jackfruit (langka), shredded into thin strips
  • Neutral oil, for shallow frying
  • 2 tablespoons water (to seal wrappers)

Instructions

1. Slice each saba banana in half lengthwise. If you're using regular bananas, slice them in half lengthwise, then cut those pieces in half crosswise. 2. Put the brown sugar in a shallow dish. Roll each banana slice in the sugar, pressing gently so a thick layer of sugar clings to the fruit. 3. Lay a lumpia wrapper on a clean cutting board so it looks like a diamond. Place a sugared banana slice horizontally across the lower third of the wrapper. 4. Lay 3 or 4 strips of the shredded jackfruit right on top of the banana. 5. Fold the bottom corner of the wrapper up over the filling, then fold in the left and right sides. Roll it up tightly toward the top corner. 6. Dip your finger in water, wet the top corner of the wrapper, and press it closed to seal it tight. Repeat with the rest of the ingredients. 7. Heat about a half-inch of oil in a skillet over medium heat. 8. Here's the trick: sprinkle 2 tablespoons of brown sugar directly into the hot oil. As the sugar melts and floats to the top, carefully lay your wrapped turon into the skillet. 9. Fry for 3 to 4 minutes per side. Use tongs to roll the turon through the melting, caramelizing sugar in the oil so the outside of the wrapper gets coated in a shiny, candy-like glaze. 10. Remove them from the pan and place them on a metal wire rack to cool. Whatever you do, do not drain them on paper towels—the hot caramel will glue itself to the paper instantly. Let them cool for a few minutes and eat warm.

Chef's Tip

Keep an eye on your heat. Sugar goes from beautifully caramelized to bitter and burnt in a matter of seconds. If the oil starts smoking or the sugar turns black, turn the heat down immediately.

Hawaiian-Style Macaroni Salad

Let's get one thing straight: if your mac salad is dry, you failed. It’s not a dainty side dish. It is a heavy, creamy, structural pillar of the plate lunch. It has to stand up to hot teriyaki beef and greasy gravy without breaking. The secret? Overcooking the pasta. Yes, you read that correctly. Forget al dente. We want the noodles plump, soft, and ready to drink up the vinegar and fat.

Prep time: 15 mins | Cook time: 10 mins (plus 2 hours chilling) | Servings: 8

Ingredients

  • 16 oz elbow macaroni
  • 2 tbsp apple cider vinegar
  • 2 cups Best Foods (Hellmann's) mayonnaise
  • 1/3 cup whole milk
  • 1 tbsp granulated sugar
  • 1 cup grated carrot
  • 1/4 cup grated sweet onion
  • 1 tsp kosher salt
  • 1/2 tsp freshly ground black pepper

Instructions

1. Cook the macaroni in boiling salted water. Ignore the package directions. Let it ride for an extra 2 minutes until the noodles are quite soft and slightly bloated. 2. Drain the macaroni thoroughly, then transfer it to a large bowl. While it is still steaming hot, splash the apple cider vinegar over the pasta and give it a gentle toss. Let it cool for about 15 minutes. This step is crucial—the warm starch will pull that vinegar right into the center of the noodle. 3. In a separate bowl, whisk together the mayonnaise, milk, sugar, salt, and black pepper until completely smooth. 4. Fold the grated carrot and sweet onion into the cooled macaroni. 5. Pour the heavy mayonnaise dressing over the pasta mixture. Fold gently. Make sure every single noodle is swimming in dressing. It might look a little too wet right now; don't panic. The pasta will absorb the liquid as it sits. 6. Cover the bowl and refrigerate for at least 2 hours. Do not skip this. The flavors need time to settle their differences.

Chef's Tip

Look, don't come at me with Duke's or Kewpie for this one. It has to be Best Foods (Hellmann's if you're east of the Rockies). It’s a non-negotiable cultural law of the islands.

Rich Brown Loco Moco Gravy

This is the liquid gold that turns a basic hamburger patty and a scoop of rice into the legendary loco moco. It needs to be glossy. It needs to coat the back of your spoon like liquid velvet. We build the flavor on a foundation of butter and flour, darkened until it smells like roasted hazelnuts.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 15 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 4 tbsp unsalted butter
  • 1/4 cup all-purpose flour
  • 2 cups low-sodium beef broth
  • 1 tbsp shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1 tsp Worcestershire sauce
  • 1/2 tsp onion powder
  • 1/2 tsp freshly ground black pepper
  • Salt to taste

Instructions

1. Melt the butter in a medium saucepan over medium heat. 2. Whisk in the flour to create a smooth paste. Keep whisking. Don’t walk away. Cook for 3 to 4 minutes until the mixture turns a deep, rich, golden brown. 3. Slowly pour in the beef broth in a steady stream, whisking like mad to prevent any lumps from forming. 4. Stir in the shoyu, Worcestershire sauce, onion powder, and black pepper. 5. Bring the gravy to a gentle boil, then drop the heat to low. Simmer for 5 to 7 minutes, stirring occasionally. You want it thick enough to drape over a fried egg without immediately sliding off. Taste it. Add a pinch of salt if it needs more punch.

Chef's Tip

For an even deeper color and nuttier flavor, let your butter and flour roux cook until it reaches the shade of milk chocolate before adding your liquids. Just watch the heat so it doesn't burn.

Classic Teriyaki Sauce

This isn't the watery, preservative-laden stuff you buy in a plastic bottle at the grocery store. This is the sweet, sticky, lacquer-like glaze that has been bubbling on back burners in local kitchens for generations. It clings beautifully to beef, chicken, or spam, transforming humble ingredients into instant classics.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1 cup shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1 cup granulated sugar
  • 1/4 cup mirin (Japanese sweet cooking rice wine)
  • 1 tbsp fresh ginger, finely grated
  • 2 cloves garlic, finely minced
  • 1 tbsp cornstarch
  • 1 tbsp cold water

Instructions

1. In a small saucepan, combine the shoyu, sugar, mirin, grated ginger, and minced garlic over medium heat. 2. Bring the mixture to a gentle boil, stirring occasionally to ensure the sugar is completely dissolved. 3. In a small cup, whisk the cornstarch and cold water together to create a smooth slurry. 4. Slowly pour the cornstarch slurry into the boiling sauce while whisking constantly. 5. Reduce the heat to low and simmer for 2 to 3 minutes, stirring continuously, until the sauce thickens into a glossy glaze. Remove from heat and let cool.

Chef's Tip

Grate your ginger directly over the saucepan so you don't lose any of the fresh, aromatic ginger juice that cuts through the sweetness.

Ginger-Scallion Dipping Sauce

Have you ever tasted something so simple it made you angry? That’s ginger-scallion sauce. It is raw, sharp, and intensely aromatic. The magic happens when the white-hot oil hits the bowl. It crackles, bubbles, and instantly scalds the raw edges off the ginger, leaving behind a fragrant, savory oil that makes even a plain poached chicken breast taste like a luxury.

Prep time: 10 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup fresh ginger, very finely minced
  • 1 cup scallions (green onions), finely chopped (greens and whites)
  • 1/2 cup peanut oil (or neutral canola oil)
  • 1 tsp kosher salt
  • 1/2 tsp sugar

Instructions

1. Combine your finely minced ginger, chopped scallions, kosher salt, and sugar in a heatproof glass or ceramic bowl. Give it a good stir to distribute the seasoning. 2. Heat the peanut oil in a small saucepan over medium-high heat until it begins to shimmer and show faint wisps of smoke. 3. Carefully—very carefully—pour the hot oil directly over the ginger and scallion mixture. The oil will sizzle violently and bubble up. That is exactly what you want. 4. Stir the mixture gently with a spoon to ensure all the aromatics are scalded by the oil. 5. Let the sauce sit at room temperature for at least 10 minutes to let the flavors infuse before serving.

Chef's Tip

Ensure your ginger and scallions are bone-dry after washing. Any excess water will cause the hot oil to splatter dangerously when it hits the bowl.

Toyomansi Dip

Bringing the sharp, citrusy heat of Filipino kitchens straight to the backyard table, this quick dipping sauce is a favorite for fried fish and crispy lumpia. It perfectly balances salty shoyu with the bright, floral punch of calamansi juice.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1/4 cup fresh calamansi juice
  • 3 cloves garlic, finely minced
  • 2 red bird's eye chilies, finely sliced
  • 1/2 tsp granulated sugar

Instructions

1. In a small bowl, whisk together the shoyu and fresh calamansi juice until combined. 2. Stir in the minced garlic, sliced chilies, and sugar. 3. Whisk the mixture gently until the sugar has completely dissolved. 4. Let the dipping sauce stand at room temperature for 15 minutes before serving so the garlic and chili heat can steep into the liquid.

Chef's Tip

If fresh calamansi is unavailable in your local Asian market, substitute it with a mixture of equal parts fresh lime juice and fresh lemon juice.

Sweet and Sour Sauce

Forget the neon-pink cornstarch goo from the cheap takeout joints. Real island sweet and sour has depth, thanks to a healthy splash of pineapple juice and apple cider vinegar. It is the ultimate companion for crispy wontons, sweet and sour spareribs, or golden fried shrimp.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 10 mins | Servings: 6

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup unsweetened pineapple juice
  • 1/3 cup apple cider vinegar
  • 1/3 cup brown sugar, packed
  • 1/4 cup ketchup
  • 1 tbsp shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1 tbsp cornstarch
  • 2 tbsp water

Instructions

1. Whisk together the pineapple juice, apple cider vinegar, brown sugar, ketchup, and shoyu in a small saucepan. 2. Place the saucepan over medium heat and bring the mixture to a boil, stirring to dissolve the brown sugar. 3. In a small bowl, mix the cornstarch and water to create a smooth slurry. 4. Pour the cornstarch slurry into the boiling saucepan in a slow stream, whisking constantly to prevent clumping. 5. Reduce the heat to low and simmer for 2 minutes, stirring continuously, until the sauce is thick, glossy, and translucent. Remove from heat and allow to cool slightly before serving.

Chef's Tip

Add a tablespoon of syrup from a can of pineapple chunks for an extra layer of tropical sweetness and fruit aroma.

Spicy Mayo Poke Dressing

Walk up to any seafood counter from Kahului to Kapolei and you'll see bowls of ahi tuna glistening with this pale pink, fiery dressing. It coats fresh ahi tuna or salmon beautifully, adding a rich heat that keeps you coming back for another bite.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 0 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup Japanese Kewpie mayonnaise
  • 2 tbsp sriracha sauce
  • 1 tsp toasted sesame oil
  • 1 tsp shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1/2 tsp honey
  • 1 tbsp orange masago (flying fish roe, optional)

Instructions

1. Add the Kewpie mayonnaise and sriracha to a medium mixing bowl. Whisk until the mixture is uniform and a pale pink color. 2. Drizzle in the toasted sesame oil, shoyu, and honey, whisking until fully incorporated. 3. Gently fold in the masago, if using, which adds a beautiful color and a delightful, popping texture. 4. Cover and chill in the refrigerator until ready to fold into your favorite cubed raw fish or use as a drizzle over rice bowls.

Chef's Tip

Standard mayonnaise can be substituted in a pinch, but Japanese Kewpie mayonnaise is essential for that ultra-creamy, savory, and slightly sweet flavor profile.

Tangy Tonkatsu Sauce

Thick, dark, and deeply savory, this Japanese-style barbecue sauce is the traditional accompaniment to crispy, panko-crusted pork cutlets. It cuts through the richness of fried foods with a complex blend of sweetness, spice, and umami.

Prep time: 5 mins | Cook time: 5 mins | Servings: 4

Ingredients

  • 1/2 cup ketchup
  • 1/4 cup Worcestershire sauce
  • 2 tbsp oyster sauce
  • 1 tbsp granulated sugar
  • 1 tsp shoyu (soy sauce)
  • 1/4 tsp garlic powder

Instructions

1. Combine the ketchup, Worcestershire sauce, oyster sauce, sugar, shoyu, and garlic powder in a small bowl. 2. Whisk the ingredients vigorously until the sugar is completely dissolved and the mixture is smooth. 3. Pour the mixture into a small saucepan and heat over low heat for 3 minutes, stirring constantly, to help the flavors integrate and deepen. 4. Remove the sauce from the heat and allow it to cool completely before transferring it to a jar or serving bowl.

Chef's Tip

Let this sauce sit in the refrigerator overnight before serving; this gives the bold ingredients time to mellow and harmonize.
