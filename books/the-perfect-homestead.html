The Perfect Homestead: A Domestic Gothic Mystery

Chapter 1: The Root of the Rot

The wild yeast lived in an oatmeal-glazed ceramic crock. On the internet, that jar was the holy grail of slow living. Two million people watched it bubble. They tuned in for the quiet, the sun-drenched corners of a reclaimed-barn kitchen, the illusion that a life could be stripped of its noise and made perfectly clean.

Kestrel Janz traced the bottom of the crock with her thumb. Her skin caught on the rough, unglazed ring where the potter’s stamp—a tight, hypnotic spiral—was pressed deep into the clay. It was heavy. Solid. In her videos, she moved with a rhythmic, almost liturgical slowness, linen sleeves rolled to the elbows, her hands dusted with organic stone-ground rye. The camera loved it. It loved the copper pots catching the amber Vermont light, the dried lavender hanging from hand-hewn rafters, the silence.

Then came the metal-on-metal screech from the orchard.

It was a sharp, ugly sound. It sliced straight through the quiet hum of the sub-zero refrigerator and the gentle, yeasty breathing of the starter.

Kestrel stopped. Her thumb stayed pressed against the clay spiral.

Outside, the mini-excavator’s engine whined, coughed, and died with a sputtering rattle. Then a voice rose—high, thin, and jagged with panic. It was Viggo Brand, the local contractor she’d hired to dig the deep-winter irrigation trench near the organic greenhouse.

"Mrs. Grote!" Viggo was screaming. "Leander! Get out here now!"

Kestrel set the crock down on the soapstone counter with a soft clack. She didn't wash her hands. The flour dried into a chalky, tight glove over her knuckles as she crossed the wide-plank floor, her boots clicking against the reclaimed pine.

The moment she hit the screen door, the Vermont air struck her like a wet towel. Late October. The sky was the color of a wet slate shingle, sagging low over the ridge of the Green Mountains. It smelled of bruised apples, rotting hemlock needles, and the cold, metallic tang of the first hard frost. But as she walked down the gravel path toward the greenhouse, a different scent began to assert itself.

It was faint. Sweet. A sickly, medicinal drift that didn't belong to the autumn damp.

Viggo was standing at the edge of the trench, his hands pressed to his thighs, his body bent double as if he were about to vomit. The yellow excavator sat idly behind him, its iron bucket tilted upward like a broken jaw.

"I thought it was an old hemlock root," Viggo whispered. He didn't look at her. His skin had gone the color of skim milk. "It didn't give. I put the teeth into it. And then... the fabric came up first."

Kestrel reached the lip of the trench. The earth here was dark, a heavy clay-loam that she had spent five years sweetening with organic compost. Now, a three-foot slice of it had been peeled back like skin.

In the belly of the gray clay lay a ribbed curve of white.

It wasn't a root.

The excavator’s teeth had torn through a layer of rotted canvas, exposing a ribcage. The bones were stained a dull, iron-ore brown from the soil, but they were unmistakably human. The skull was partially visible, tilted backward into the dirt as if looking up at the gray sky through socketed, mud-filled eyes.

Kestrel’s breath caught. Her lungs felt dry, coated in the flour she had just been kneading.

But it wasn't the bones that made her heart drop into a cold, dark well. It was what lay draped across the cervical vertebrae.

A necklace.

It was a crude thing, threaded on a strip of decayed leather that had somehow survived the damp earth. The pendant was a thick cylinder of deer bone, yellowed and scarred, hand-carved with three interlocking notches.

The Children of Dawn.

"Oh, God," Viggo muttered, taking a step back. "Oh, Jesus. Is that... is it from the old commune days?"

Kestrel didn't answer. Her mind was a wild, roaring television screen with the volume turned down.

Maceo.

She knew those notches. She had watched him carve them with his pocketknife by the light of a kerosene lamp in the drafty cabin they shared during the winter of her seventeenth year. He had smelled of pine sap, cheap tobacco, and the sour yeast of their communal bread. He had promised her they would leave the valley together, that they would run before the Elder, Eldora Cole, decided they were ready for the "cleansing."

Maceo had disappeared two nights later. Eldora told everyone he had stolen from the community chest and fled to Boston. Kestrel had believed it. She had hated him for it. She had spent fifteen years building this pristine, high-end life on the very ashes of that cult’s land, convincing herself that she was the survivor, the one who made it out clean.

And all this time, he had been six feet from her heirloom tomatoes.

"Kestrel."

The voice was cool, level, and entirely devoid of panic.

She turned. Leander Grote stood at the edge of the grass, his cashmere sweater immaculate, his leather boots barely marked by the damp gravel. His eyes were already scanning the treeline, checking the distance to the road, calculating the angles. He didn't look at the bones. He looked at Kestrel.

"Viggo," Leander said, stepping forward. His voice had the smooth, practiced resonance of a man who handled high-net-worth investors for breakfast. "Turn off the machine. Go sit in your truck."

"Shouldn't we call the sheriff?" Viggo’s voice cracked. "That's... that's a body, Leander. That's a fucking skeleton."

"We are going to call the sheriff," Leander said, his hand sliding into his pocket. "Immediately. But I need you to calm down. Take five minutes. Drink some water. We don't need the local deputies arriving to find you in hysterics."

Viggo hesitated, looked at the white ribs in the mud, then nodded quickly. He scrambled toward his Ford F-250, his boots slipping on the wet grass. The truck door slammed, a sharp exclamation mark in the silence.

Once the truck cabin was sealed, Leander closed the distance between himself and Kestrel. He didn't touch her. He stood close enough that she could smell his expensive cedarwood cologne, a sharp contrast to the damp rot rising from the trench.

"Who is it?" he asked. His voice was barely a breath.

"Maceo," she whispered.

Leander’s jaw tightened. A small muscle ticked beneath his left eye. "You're sure."

"The necklace. I... I know the necklace."

"The boy who ran away." Leander’s eyes drifted down to the trench, scanning the muddy bones with clinical detachment. "The one you told me about when we bought the property."

"He didn't run away," Kestrel said. Her voice felt fragile, like dry leaves. "He was buried here. Under our feet, Leander. All these years."

"Listen to me," Leander said, grabbing her upper arm. His grip was firm, just shy of painful. "We don't know that. The police will find a body on a historic commune site. It could be anyone. It could be a transient. It could be someone from the seventies."

"He has the necklace, Leander. The sheriff will ask questions. People in town still remember the Children of Dawn. They remember Eldora Cole. They remember me."

"They remember a victim," Leander hissed. "They remember a teenage girl who was rescued from a radical eco-cult. That's your brand, Kestrel. Survival. Resilience. The girl who went back to the land but made it beautiful. If this becomes a murder investigation on our property, the sponsors pull out. The book deal is gone. The lifestyle network? They won't touch a homestead built on a graveyard."

He pulled his phone from his pocket, his thumb hovering over the screen.

"I'm calling Kateri Choy," Leander said.

"The detective?"

"She owes me. I helped fund her husband's mayoral campaign. She’ll handle this quietly. No sirens. No press releases until we have a narrative." He looked at Kestrel, his eyes hard and bright as flint. "You don't know anything about this. You found some old bones while landscaping. That's the story. You have no idea who it is."

"I can't lie about Maceo."

"You can," Leander said, his voice dropping to a harsh whisper. "Because if you don't, everything we've built is gone by sunset. The house. The brand. The safety. Do you want to go back to being the broken girl from the woods, Kestrel? Because the internet loves a victim, but they hate a suspect."

He didn't wait for her answer. He walked toward the driveway, his phone already pressed to his ear, his voice transitioning seamlessly into a tone of grave, civic-minded concern.

Kestrel stood alone at the edge of the pit.

The wind shifted. The sweet, sharp odor rose again, stronger now that the clay had been disturbed.

She knelt. Her linen skirt dipped into the mud, soaking up the gray water, but she didn't care. She leaned over the edge, her face inches from the excavated earth.

The scent was unmistakable. It wasn't just the wet-dog smell of ancient canvas and old leather. Beneath the damp mold of the soil, there was a chemical sweetness that clung to the bones.

Bitter almond. And a faint, oily trace of aniseed.

Her mind flashed back to the winter of her seventeenth year. The dark kitchen of the Children of Dawn commune. Eldora Cole standing over a massive copper vat, stirring a thick, steaming mash of wild elderberries and wintergreen.

“Nature provides the cure, and Nature provides the boundary,” Eldora had said, her voice a low, hypnotic purr as she poured an amber liquid from a dark glass bottle into the vat. “We only weed the garden of those who wish to poison the soil.”

Maceo had sickened the next day. They said it was the winter grippe. They said he had packed his bags in the night and walked down the mountain to the highway.

Kestrel reached down into the cold mud. Her fingers hovered over the hand-carved bone necklace. Her hand shook.

A shadow fell over the trench.

"I wouldn't touch that, Mrs. Grote."

Kestrel flinched, pulling her hand back so fast she scraped her knuckles against the rough clay.

Standing on the opposite side of the trench was a woman in a heavy canvas coat. She was short, square-jawed, with her dark hair pulled back into a tight, no-nonsense braid. Her boots were muddy, but unlike Leander, she didn't seem to care.

Detective Kateri Choy.

"Detective," Kestrel stammered, scrambling to her feet. She tried to wipe her muddy hands on her skirt, only to make the stain worse. "I didn't hear you pull up."

"I left the car at the gate," Choy said, her dark eyes scanning the trench, then Kestrel's face, then the excavator parked fifty yards away. "Leander called me on my personal line. He sounded... organized."

"He wanted to make sure we did things properly," Kestrel said, her voice sounding thin and rehearsed.

Choy knelt by the pit, her joints popping with a dry sound. She didn't look like a savior. She looked like a woman who had spent twenty years looking at the worst things Vermont had to offer and had found them all deeply boring.

She pulled a pair of latex gloves from her pocket and snapped them on. The sound was loud in the damp air.

"Viggo Brand is sitting in his truck looking like he’s about to have a stroke," Choy observed, her voice flat. "Your husband is on the phone with a publicist in New York. And you're kneeling in the mud."

"It's... a shock," Kestrel said.

Choy leaned over the trench. She didn't touch the bones, but she tilted her head, her nose wrinkling slightly.

"Smell that?" Choy asked.

Kestrel's heart did a slow, heavy roll. "The clay?"

"Almonds," Choy said. "And anise. Strange. Usually, a body this old just smells like wet peat and iron. But the clay here is thick. It seals things in. Keeps the oxygen out." She looked up, her gaze locking onto Kestrel with the force of a physical blow. "You lived here when it was the commune, didn't you, Kestrel?"

"I was a child," Kestrel said quickly. "I was seventeen when it was disbanded."

"And you bought the land back five years ago." Choy stood up, peeling the gloves off with a slow, deliberate scrape of latex. "Must be nice. To own the place that broke you."

"We wanted to reclaim it," Kestrel said, her throat tight. "Make it something beautiful."

"Right," Choy said. "The perfect homestead."

She looked past Kestrel, toward the greenhouse. Through the glass panels, the rows of heirloom starter plants looked like tiny green soldiers waiting for a war.

"We'll need to cordon this off," Choy said. "The state forensic team will be here in an hour. I told Leander to keep his publicist on ice until we get a preliminary ID."

"How long will that take?"

"Depends," Choy said, stepping closer. She reached out and gently touched the linen of Kestrel's sleeve, her fingers lingering near the flour-crusted cuff. "If we have dental records, or if someone recognizes the effects. Like that necklace."

Kestrel felt the air leave her. "The necklace?"

"The bone pendant," Choy said. "Quite distinctive. Looks like the work of the Children of Dawn. They used to sell things like that at the farmers' market in town to pay for their grain. Eldora Cole's people."

She paused, her eyes dropping to Kestrel’s hands.

"You've got flour on your hands, Mrs. Grote. Were you baking?"

"Sourdough," Kestrel said, her voice barely audible.

"My grandmother used to keep a starter," Choy said, her voice softening, though her eyes remained cold. "Kept it in an old crock in the cellar. Said if you don't feed it, it turns sour. It rots from the bottom up. But if you keep feeding it, it can live forever."

She turned and began walking back toward the driveway, her heavy boots leaving deep, square prints in the wet lawn.

"Don't leave the property, Kestrel," Choy called back over her shoulder. "We're going to have a lot of questions about who used to sleep in the cabin that stood right where your greenhouse is."

Kestrel watched her go.

The wind blew a gust of cold rain across her face. The smell of the trench seemed to expand, filling her nose, her mouth, her lungs. Almonds. Bitter, crushing almonds.

She looked back down at the bones.

She hadn't just known Maceo.

She had been the one who handed him the mug of elderberry tea the night he disappeared. Eldora had told her it was for his cough. Eldora had smiled her wide, maternal smile, her hands sticky with the sweet, dark juice.

“Take it to him, Kestrel. Let him know we care for him.”

Kestrel had carried the mug across the snowy yard. She had watched him drink it. She had kissed his warm, sweet-scented lips afterward.

She turned and walked slowly back toward the house, her boots heavy with the clay of the grave.

Inside the kitchen, the warmth of the stove was a physical shock. The sourdough starter sat in its crock on the counter, the pale, wet dough bubbling slowly under the linen cloth.

Kestrel picked up the ceramic jar.

She turned it over. The spiral mark stamped on the bottom was clogged with the dried gray clay from her fingers.

She had built a beautiful house on top of a tomb. She had invited the world to watch her live in it. And now, the earth was spitting up the things she had helped bury.

From the driveway, the sound of a distant siren began to wind its way through the valley, thin and screaming, rising through the hemlocks like a ghost.

Chapter 2: The Soil and the Spoil

The fog didn't rise. It just sat there.

A thick, wet wool weight draped over the apple trees, smelling of fermented pomace and the raw, chemical stink of diesel from the cruiser's tailpipe. Red and blue light pulsed through the mist, turning the wet birch bark into something cheap and neon, like a bad tavern sign.

Kestrel Janz kept her hands shoved deep in her pockets. Waxed canvas. Stiff from the frost, smelling of paraffin and the dried pine needles she'd swept off the porch last week. She didn't want to look at the lights. Instead, she focused on her own fingernails—cracked, lined with gray soil that wouldn't scrub out, even with the stiff-bristled brush she kept by the sink.

The gravel screamed under the cruiser's tires. A sharp, dry crunch that went straight up her spine.

Choy got out.

She wore no uniform. Just a long, heavy wool coat the color of wet slate, its hem dragging through the dead weeds. Her boots were already gray with dust from some other county's roads. She didn't look like the local deputies who’d spent the last two hours trampling the grass, spitting tobacco, and looking for a place to piss. Her eyes were tiny, dark, and flat. Like two chips of coal pressed into old leather.

She didn't offer a hand. Why would she?

Then came Leander's hand.

His fingers clamped onto the meat of Kestrel's shoulder, squeezing hard enough to pinch through the heavy canvas. He smelled of cold tobacco and those cheap wintergreen mints he sucked on to hide it—a sweet, medicinal rot that always made her throat close up.

"Keep your mouth shut," he hissed, his breath a warm, wet puff against her ear. "Let me do the talking."

Choy didn't waste time. She walked right up, her eyes dropping to Leander’s hand on Kestrel's shoulder before they even reached Kestrel’s face.

"Mrs. Janz. Grote." A voice like dry grass blowing across asphalt. "Where is it?"

"Past the Spies," Leander blurted. His voice was too thin, pitched at a frantic, rattling frequency. "The Northern Spies, third row. Kestrel was checking the lower branches for late blight. She’s the one who spotted the heel. I told her right off—don't touch a damn thing. You know how people are, stomping around and messing up the ground. I made sure she stayed clear."

Choy didn't even look at him. She kept her eyes on Kestrel. "That how it went, Mrs. Janz?"

Kestrel’s tongue felt like a dry piece of leather. "The dirt was wrong," she said. "Too high. Piled up around the roots of the tree."

"Ground’s been frozen off and on for two weeks," Choy said, squinting into the gray rows of trees. "Hard digging. Let’s take a look."

The mud sucked at their boots as they walked. Leander didn't let go of her shoulder. He guided her like a horse, his thumb digging into her collarbone every few steps. A warning. Don't trip. Don't speak.

The yellow tape was already drooping, wet with dew. Inside the line, the hole looked small. A messy, dark gash in the silt loam.

A single boot stuck out.

Old leather, split and black with rot, pointing toward the sky like some terrible, unsprouted bulb.

Choy went down on her knees right in the muck. She didn't look like she cared about her trousers. The snap of her blue latex gloves was the loudest sound in the orchard.

"This dirt hasn't been turned since spring," Choy said, her gloved hand lightly brushing the edge of the pit. "Except here. It’s loose. Soggy, but loose. Dug recently. Forty-eight hours, maybe. Right before the hard freeze on Tuesday."

She looked up, squinting through the fog at Kestrel. "You keep your tools locked up, Mrs. Janz?"

"Always," Leander said, taking a step forward, his boot nearly catching the hem of Choy's coat. "Have to. Kids from the valley come up looking for scrap or stuff to sell. The barn, the glasshouse—they’re always deadbolted."

"I was asking your wife, Grote," Choy said. Very quiet. But it shut him up.

Kestrel pulled away from his grip, her shoulder instantly cold where his fingers had been. "Yes. The glasshouse is locked. I have the only key on my ring."

Choy nodded. She reached into her leather bag. When she pulled her hand back out, she was holding a clear plastic evidence bag.

Inside was the trowel.

Kestrel's lungs went flat.

Copper. Solid, hand-hammered, with that dull, orange-brown glow she knew by heart. The cherry handle was dark from the oils of her own palms, fastened with those two clumsy brass rivets the smith down in the next valley had beaten into place. Near the base, the wood was branded: K.J.

"Found it tucked under the root ball," Choy said, turning the plastic so the light hit the copper. "Recognize it?"

"That's..." Kestrel couldn't get the air out.

"It looks like yours, Kes," Leander said, his voice dropping into something sharp and panicky. He stepped closer, his boots churning the gray mud into froth. "But that's impossible. Tell her. Yours is in the glasshouse right now. Locked up. Someone must’ve lifted it."

"Is it yours, Mrs. Janz?" Choy repeated.

"Yes," Kestrel whispered. "It’s mine."

"Funny thing," Choy said, turning the bag over. She pointed to the cherry wood near the brass rivets. "The soil out here is silt loam. Heavy. Dark. But look at this stuff caught in the wood grain."

Kestrel leaned down, her heart thumping against her ribs like a bird in a chimney.

A white, chalky smear. Stark against the dark wood.

"Kaolin," Choy said. "White clay. Potters use it. No natural deposits of that around here for twenty miles. But Eldora Cole’s got that studio down the road, doesn't she? And Viggo Brand’s been clearing old materials out of her cellar all week."

"I..." Kestrel’s mind felt sluggish, cold. "I haven't been to Eldora’s since August. And I haven't used that trowel since I put the garlic in back in October."

"Well, somebody used it," Choy said, rising with a dry pop of her knees. "And they didn't wash their hands first. Let's go see that glasshouse."

The greenhouse was old cedar and gray glass. Leander had built it for her when they first bought the place, back when they still talked about things like fresh starts. In July, it smelled of sweet geraniums and wet earth; now it just looked like a ribcage of rotted wood and dead vines.

Her hands shook so hard the keys clinked against each other. The Yale lock was pitted with green rust, but when she turned the key, the bolt slid back with a smooth, heavy thunk.

No scratches on the brass. No splintered wood.

She pushed the door. The air inside was warm, thick, and smelled of decaying rosemary and sour peat.

They walked down the gravel path to the potting bench. Above the soil bin, the tool rack hung on the wall.

There was a clean, dust-free outline on the cedar where the copper trowel should have been. Right between the shears and the rusty steel dibber.

"Somebody stole it," Leander said. He was sweating now, a bead of it cutting a clean line through the gray dust on his temple. "Maceo Quint was up here Tuesday. Fixing the irrigation by the side glass. He could’ve slipped in. Or Oona Hallowell—she’s always sneaking around the woods looking for wild berries. Anyone could’ve grabbed it if the door was left off the latch."

"But your wife said she keeps it locked," Choy noted, her boots crunching slowly on the gravel as she drifted around the benches. She stopped by a shelf of empty red pots, her eyes scanning the floor.

Kestrel couldn't breathe. Her throat felt tight, dry.

She knew what they'd find on the body. She’d heard Choy talking to the deputy earlier.

The tattoo.

An eye inside a circle. Blue ink, faded and blurry, on the dead man's inner arm.

The Eye of the Dawn.

It made her sick to think about it. The commune in New Hampshire. The place she’d run from nine years ago, leaving everything behind, including her own name. She’d had the same mark once, on her left hip, before she took a paring knife to it and told Leander she’d caught herself on a barbed-wire fence. She’d spent nine years burying that girl in the Vermont dirt, hoping she’d rot down into fertilizer.

But the dirt was spitting her back up.

"Mr. Grote," Choy said, turning around. "Give us a minute, would you? I want to talk to your wife."

Leander’s jaw bunched. "No need for that. I'm her husband. You want to ask her something, ask me."

"I'm not asking you, Grote," Choy said. Her voice didn't rise, but it had the weight of an iron bar. "Go wait by the house."

Leander stared at Kestrel. His eyes were wide, white around the edges, screaming at her: Shut up. He waited until she looked down at the gravel, then spat on the floor and walked out, slamming the heavy door so hard the glass panes rattled in their lead frames.

When the sound of his boots died away, Choy leaned against the potting bench.

"Attentive guy," Choy said.

"He worries," Kestrel said.

"That's one word for it," Choy said, her dark eyes fixed on Kestrel's face. "You look like a woman who’s used to being kept, Mrs. Janz. Or maybe hidden?"

Kestrel kept her hands in her pockets, her nails biting into her palms. "I don't know what you mean. We wanted a quiet life."

"Everyone wants that," Choy said. She picked up a small terracotta pot, turned it over, then set it back down. "But you can't build on a swamp and expect the timber not to rot. We’re going to identify him, Mrs. Janz. Might take a day, might take three. But we'll find out who he was, and we’ll find out how he got into your orchard."

She held out her hand. "I’ll take those keys. Just for tonight. We’ll need to check the lock."

Kestrel pulled them out and dropped them into Choy's palm. The metal felt ice-cold.

By dark, the rain had started—a cold, stinging drizzle. The state boys had set up portable lights in the orchard, their white glare turning the apple trees into long, skeletal shadows that danced across the kitchen ceiling. The light cut through the window, throwing a stark grid across the table.

Leander was pacing, a glass of cheap whiskey in his hand. He’d already gone through half the bottle, his face red, his eyes bright and wet.

"It was Maceo," he muttered, stopping at the glass to stare out at the lights. "Has to be. He’s wanted that lower pasture for years for his trucks. He put the body there to break us. Or Eldora. She always looked down her nose at us. Her and her artsy friends..."

"Leander, stop," Kestrel said from the corner. She hadn't turned the lamps on. She preferred the dark.

"No, you don't get it!" He spun around, his boots loud on the pine floor. He was over her in three strides, grabbing her by the arms, his fingers digging into her sweater. He smelled of sour grain and sweat. "They’re going to try and pin this on you. It’s your tool, Kestrel. If they ask you again, you tell them we lost the key. You hear me? We lost the greenhouse key three weeks ago and forgot to change the lock."

"That's a lie," she said. "Choy watched me open it."

"Then you tell her we found the spare! We have to manage this, Kes. I built this place for us. I kept you safe. You think you’d be sitting here if I hadn't taken you in? You’d be in a cell in Concord."

"Don't," she whispered.

"I’m the only one who knows what you are," he hissed, his face inches from hers. "Don't forget that. We stand together, or we both go down."

He let go of her, leaving her arms cold, and went back to his bottle.

Kestrel couldn't stand the sight of him. She turned and walked down the dark hallway toward her study—the only room in the house where he didn't go without asking.

The floorboards were cold under her wool socks. The house was freezing; the wood stove in the parlor was nothing but gray ash.

She reached her door and stopped.

A small white rectangle lay on the dark oak floor, slipped halfway under the draft blocker.

Kestrel froze.

The light from the high hall window was dim, but she could see the paper. Thick, heavy, with those rough, deckled edges. Watercolor cardstock. The kind Eldora Cole used for her pottery tags.

Her fingers shook as she picked it up.

The typing was faint, done on an old manual machine with a dry ribbon that split the letters.

Eight words.

I know what you did at the commune, Vashti.

Kestrel’s breath died in her chest. The name—the name she had left behind in the ash and smoke of that New Hampshire winter—seemed to burn her fingers.

She looked up at the window at the end of the hall. The glass was black, showing nothing but her own hollow, white face, and outside, the cold gray fog pressing against the pane like a hand.

Chapter 3: The Drifter's Tool

The smell of cedar is different when it’s dying.

Up in the vaulted, ribs-out greenhouses Viggo Brand had built for Kestrel Janz, the wood smelled clean. Sharp, like newly shaved pencils and fresh rain. But down here, in the hollow behind Viggo’s listing silver-maple barn, the scent was choked out by rotting hemlock needles and the sour, chemical tang of old kerosene. Water dripped from the rusted tin roof. Tap... tap... tap. A slow, aggravating rhythm against the hood of an engineless John Deere.

Detective Kateri Choy pulled her oilskin collar tighter against her neck. The Vermont damp didn't just sit on you; it burrowed. It bypassed the wool, bypassed the skin, and settled directly into the marrow of your collarbones until you felt brittle. She stood at the threshold of the workshop, her boots sunk three inches into black, spongy mud that smelled of leaf rot and cold iron.

Behind her, out by the gravel turnaround where the cruiser’s blue lights threw long, rhythmic bars of cobalt across the wet birch trunks, Kestrel Janz stood waiting. Kestrel’s hands were shoved so deep into her pockets that her elbows flared out—she was wearing one of Leander’s oversized waxed canvas jackets, the shoulders slumping halfway down her arms. She looked tiny against the gray wall of the woods. Pale. Her eyes were locked on the barn’s low-slung door like she expected something to jump out.

"You don’t have to be out here," Choy said.

She didn't raise her voice. The damp air swallowed sound anyway.

"I want to see," Kestrel said. Her voice was thin, like dry parchment. "He was in my house, Kateri. He built the frames for my heirloom seedlings. He sat at my table, drank my coffee, and wrote his estimates on the back of my napkins. If he had something to do with Maceo... I need to see."

Choy didn't argue. She’d seen this look a dozen times before on the faces of people who’d had their lives cracked open. It was the desperate, ugly need to witness the machine of justice up close, as if watching a deputy bag a piece of evidence could somehow un-break a door lock or un-shatter a sense of safety. But looking too closely at a monster usually just showed you your own reflection.

She turned and shoved the door.

The hinges shrieked—a raw, dry-iron scrape that sent a pair of crows flapping out of the hemlocks with wet, heavy croaks. Inside, the workshop was a cavern of cold shadow. The air was thick with ancient pine dust, coal smoke, and the heavy, lard-like scent of mutton tallow used to grease rusted shears.

Viggo Brand didn’t look up. He was fifty-something, with shoulders like a bull and a beard the color of dirty slush. His hands looked like they’d been hacked out of hickory roots. Right then, he was scraping the orange scale off an old drawknife with a block of pumice. Scritch. Scritch. Scritch.

"You're trespassing, Choy," Viggo said, not looking up. "State road ends thirty yards back. This is family land. Has been since before they started selling off forty-acre parcels to flatlanders with trust funds."

"I have a warrant, Viggo," Choy said, stepping into the gloom. She clicked on her Streamlight, the bright white beam cutting through the floating dust to illuminate a pegboard of tools that looked more like medieval dentistry than carpentry. "We found your tread marks down by the creek on the Janz property. Not the road—the old logging trail that runs behind the sugar shack."

The scraping stopped. The drawknife hovered an inch above the wood. A single drop of sweat rolled out of his hairline and vanished into his gray beard.

"Looking for burls," he muttered. "Deadwood. Stuff the high-and-mighty Grotes call trash until they want it polished up for a four-thousand-dollar coffee table."

"You were pretty loud on the town forum last week," Choy said, her light drifting over a stack of dry-rotting wooden crates. "Called Leander Grote a 'parasite.' Said the homestead was a 'plague of fake farmers playing dress-up while the real people starve.'"

Viggo let out a short, wet bark of a laugh. He finally looked up, his eyes small, wet, and angry under bushy brows. "Am I wrong? They come up here from Boston, buy three hundred acres of prime timber, put up some glass houses, and call themselves pioneers. They don't know the first thing about winter. They don't know what it’s like when the well freezes solid and you have to melt snow on a kerosene stove just to flush the damn toilet. I built those greenhouses. I built them with mortise-and-tenon joints that’ll outlast their marriage. And what did Leander do? Complained about the price of the brass hinges. Said I was gouging him."

Choy walked deeper, her boots crunching through curls of dry pine shavings. Her mind slipped—just for a second—back to a freezing interrogation room in Burlington four years ago. A kid named Joey, barely twenty, sobbing while she pressed him because he had the right kind of anger and the right kind of grease under his fingernails. She’d been so sure. She’d missed the neat, tiny slice in the back window screen that pointed to the landlord’s son until it was too late, until Joey had used his own flannel shirt to end it in the holding cell.

That mistake was a cold stone in her gut she carried every day. Don’t look at the loud anger, she told herself. Look at the grease.

"We're looking for Maceo Quint's watch, Viggo," Choy said, her voice level. "And his notebook."

Viggo sneered, spitting a dark stream of tobacco juice into a bucket of sawdust near his stool. "Check the antique shops in Woodstock. That’s where those people sell their souls."

Choy didn't answer. She began her search.

The workshop was a labyrinth of hoarding. Old window sashes with wavy glass, bundles of dried lavender hanging from the rafters like shriveled bird carcasses, jars of mismatched screws, and rusted tin cans filled with congealed grease. She felt the weight of Viggo’s gaze on her back—hot, wet, and heavy.

She approached a large, green-painted steel tool chest that sat beneath a window thick with grime. The chest was ancient, its corners reinforced with brass brackets that had turned a dull, greenish-black.

"Don't touch that," Viggo growled, his stool scraping against the floorboards as he stood.

Choy’s hand hovered over the heavy iron latch. "Why? What's in here, Viggo?"

"My grandfather’s chisels," he said, his chest heaving beneath his stained flannel shirt. "They're English steel. Better than anything you can buy now. I don't want your greasy fingers rusting them."

Choy didn't hesitate. She flipped the latch.

The lid rose with a heavy, metallic groan. Inside, the chisels were laid out in a custom canvas roll, their wooden handles dark with the sweat of three generations of hands. Beneath the roll lay a layer of greaseproof paper, and beneath that, a thick, oily rag.

Choy reached in, her fingers sinking into the cold, sticky texture of the rag. She lifted it. It was heavy. Too heavy for a cloth.

She peeled back the greasy flannel.

There, nestled in the dark folds like a silver egg in a soot-stained nest, was a vintage pocket watch.

The silver was heavily tarnished, but the ornate engraving on the lid—a hand-carved depiction of a soaring osprey—was unmistakable. Choy pressed the release button on the crown. The lid popped open with a faint, metallic ping. The glass face was cracked, a hairline fracture running from the nine to the three, but the delicate, gold-plated hands were frozen at exactly 4:17.

"Maceo’s," Kestrel gasped from the doorway. She had crept inside, her face white as birch bark in the dim light of the workshop. "He... he always kept it in his vest pocket. He said his father gave it to him when he left the merchant marine."

Viggo stared at the watch. The anger seemed to drain from his face all at once, leaving his skin the color of skim milk. "That's... that's not mine."

"It's in your toolbox, Viggo," Choy said, her voice dropping into the quiet, professional register she used when the trap sprung.

"I didn't put that there!" Viggo’s voice rose, cracking into a desperate, high-pitched register that sounded entirely different from his earlier bravado. He lunged forward, but Choy’s hand went instantly to the holster at her hip, her thumb flicking the retention strap.

"Stay back," Choy ordered.

"You don't understand!" Viggo cried, his hands flailing in front of him, thick fingers clawing at the air. "They put it there! The Grotes! Leander... he came by here two days ago. Said he wanted to talk about a cedar pergola for the terrace. He was standing right by that chest while I went to the house to get the blueprint roll. He set me up! He’s been trying to get me off this mountain since they bought that place!"

Choy looked down at the watch in her gloved hand. She turned it over.

There was a smudge on the polished silver backing. Not grease—not the thick, black motor oil that coated Viggo’s fingers or the yellowed tallow he used for his tools. It was a pale, yellow smudge that smelled faintly of honey and pine.

Beeswax.

It was the same specialty wax Kestrel used to seal the seams of her hand-poured candles and her herbal salves. The same wax Leander used to finish the reclaimed oak timber in their main house. It was a detail so small, so insignificant, that most deputies would have wiped it away with a thumb without thinking.

Don't look at the anger, she reminded herself. Look at the grease.

"Turn around, Viggo," Choy said softly.

"No! You can't do this!" Viggo screamed, his eyes darting to Kestrel, who stood paralyzed near the door. "Kestrel! Tell her! You know me! I built your greenhouses! I worked twelve-hour days in the freezing rain to make sure your seedlings didn't rot! I wouldn't hurt Maceo! He was the only one in this town who didn't look at me like I was dirt!"

Kestrel took a step back, her shoulder hitting the doorframe. Her eyes were wide, fixed on Viggo’s face. "You... you were always watching me, Viggo. You knew my schedule. You knew when Leander was in the city."

"Because I cared about the work!" Viggo wept, his heavy shoulders shaking as Choy pulled his hands behind his back and clicked the steel cuffs into place. "I wanted it to be perfect for you! Not for him! For you!"

The admission hung in the damp, cold air of the barn, heavy and greasy with obsession. It was the perfect motive. The local hand, bitter and forgotten, watching the beautiful young homesteader from the shadows of her own woods, nursing a silent, twisted devotion until the old family friend—Maceo—stepped in and saw too much.

It was too clean. Too easy.

"Let's go, Viggo," Choy said, guiding him toward the door.

As they walked past Kestrel, Viggo stopped. He didn't struggle against Choy’s grip, but he leaned toward Kestrel, his breath smelling of stale coffee and wintergreen. "They're using you," he whispered, his voice suddenly dead and flat. "The high-and-mighty Grotes. They're going to burn this whole mountain down, Kestrel. And you're going to be the one holding the match."

Kestrel didn't flinch, but her hands tightened inside her pockets until the waxed cotton stretched taut.

Choy led Viggo out into the gray light of the afternoon, the rain-slicked hemlocks reflecting the blue glare of the cruiser’s lightbar like shards of broken glass. But as she watched the deputy settle Viggo into the back seat, Choy felt the cold weight of the silver watch in her pocket.

The beeswax was still there. A tiny, honey-scented seal on a dead man's time.

Chapter 4: A Bitter Harvest

Aniseed first. Sharp, like black licorice left in a medicine cabinet too long. Then the heavy, greasy drag of bitter almond.

Kestrel Janz worked the ointment deep into the splits on her knuckles. Her cuticles were stained a stubborn, soot-gray from a month of hauling salt-glazed crocks out in the unheated pantry. The stuff was Eldora Cole’s recipe—boiled pig fat and wintergreen harvested down by the creek road, sold in tiny stone jars that smelled of woodsmoke. It didn't soak in. It just sat on the skin like cold butter, leaving a yellow glaze that caught the dirt. But without it? Her hands would have cracked to the bone by November.

Outside, the sky was the color of a wet slate shingle. The wind didn't howl. It was worse than that. It hissed through the orchard, rattling the dead wood of the Northern Spies like a handful of loose teeth.

Then came the hum.

Not Leander’s truck. His Dodge had a hole in the muffler that sounded like a dry cough. This was smooth, quiet, a heavy state-owned engine coasting over the frozen ruts of the driveway until the gravel crunched right under the kitchen porch.

Kestrel didn't wipe her hands. Why bother? She stood by the iron stove, watching the frost on the windowpane turn blue in the twilight. Kateri Choy was already out of the cruiser. She wore her winter patrol coat, collar flipped up to her ears, walking with that slow, heavy-heeled roll of someone who knew exactly how much the state paid her to put up with this weather.

The door opened, and the cold came in first—stale woodsmoke and mud frozen hard as iron.

"Kestrel." Choy didn't ask. She just shut the door, leaned her shoulder against the frame, and let her eyes do the talking. They went to the cold kettle, the ashes in the hearth, and then settled on Kestrel’s greasy palms. "Leander around?"

"North ridge," Kestrel said. Her voice felt like dry paper. "Checking the culverts before the freeze locks them up. He took the crowbar."

"Good." Choy pulled her leather gloves off, one finger at a time, tucking them behind her utility belt. Her own hands were dry, dark, and tough as boot leather. "Better if it's just us for a minute."

Kestrel gripped the edge of the dry sink. "Did you find something in the woods?"

"We let Viggo Brand go," Choy said.

Silence. The kind that makes your ears ring. Kestrel’s thumb stopped rubbing her raw knuckle. "You let him go? He had Maceo’s watch in his pocket. Leander saw you find it."

"He’s a thief, Kestrel. We’re holding him on a larceny charge, sure, but he didn't kill Maceo. He couldn't have."

"He was lurking here," Kestrel said, her voice rising, losing its edge. "He’s been in the brush for weeks. We saw the tracks."

"Viggo was at the county milk depot in Middlebury from ten Wednesday night until six Thursday morning," Choy said. She didn't sound angry; she sounded bored, which was worse. "Three cameras on the loading dock. He’s on every roll of tape, hauling silver cans in the sleet. The night dispatcher bought a pack of Chesterfields off him at midnight. Unless Viggo figured out how to split himself in two, he wasn't down in your orchard putting a dead man under the snow."

The kitchen seemed to tighten. The pine walls, yellowed by fifty years of bacon grease and wood soot, felt like they were leaning inward.

"Then the watch," Kestrel muttered. "How did he get it?"

"Viggo spent three hours in a dry cell before he got cold enough to talk," Choy said. She took two steps toward the small oak desk in the corner. Leander's desk. It was piled with tax receipts, greasy tractor manuals, and dried corn husks. "He didn't take it off a corpse. He slipped in here Monday night. Leander was down at the taproom, and you were over at the historical society giving that talk on timber frames. Viggo knew the back door was only on a latch."

Choy touched the corner of the desk with one bare finger. "He wanted cash. Didn't find any. But Leander’s waxed coat was hanging on the peg by the stove. The silver Hamilton watch was in the breast pocket. Viggo took it and spent three days trying to find someone stupid enough to buy it."

Kestrel looked at the empty wooden peg. The coat was gone. Leander was wearing it right now.

"He’s a liar," Kestrel said. "Viggo would say anything."

"He's a coward," Choy corrected. "He thought we were going to hang a murder on him. He gave up every detail—the tear in the left sleeve lining, the grease on the cuff. He couldn't have known that unless he'd had his hands in the pockets."

A cold, heavy knot settled under Kestrel’s ribs. It made her chest feel tight, like her lungs were filling with wet sand. "That doesn't mean anything. Maceo was his friend. Maybe Leander lent it to him. Maybe Maceo left it on the table."

"The coroner finished his work this morning," Choy said. She wasn't looking at Kestrel now. She was looking at the dry window ledge where three stalks of brown hemlock sat in an old stoneware jar—leftovers from Kestrel’s summer foraging workshop. "Maceo didn't freeze. His heart didn't quit, either. His lungs did. His diaphragm just stopped moving."

Kestrel’s fingers twitched toward her apron pocket. The keys were there, heavy and cold.

"They found Conium maculatum in his stomach," Choy said. "Poison hemlock. A lot of it. The examiner said it had to be boiled down. A concentrate. Mixed with something sweet to cover the taste. Elderberry wine, maybe. Or hard cider."

"It’s a weed," Kestrel whispered. "It grows in every ditch from here to the lake."

"Not in December," Choy said. "Right now, those ditches are ice and dead stalks. But you’ve got that preservation garden behind the carriage house, don't you? The one with the five-foot iron fence and the padlock."

"The key is in the sideboard," Kestrel said. Her heart was hammering against her ribs. "Under the silver. Only I use it."

"Are you sure about that?" Choy asked. "Or does Leander know where the silver is?"

Kestrel couldn't breathe. We stand together, Leander had whispered last night, his face gray in the dark, his breath smelling of sour beer. If one of us goes down, we both do.

Choy walked past her, her heavy boots clicking as she crossed from the linoleum to the wide, rough-sawn pine of the pantry hall. In the middle of the floor was the square oak hatch, its iron ring flush with the wood. The cellar.

"There was something else," Choy said, staring down at the wood. "The mud on Maceo’s boots. The state lab in Burlington ran it. It wasn't the red clay from the orchard. No leaves. No pine needles."

Kestrel stood frozen by the stove, the scent of the aniseed salve rising off her skin like steam.

"It was lime," Choy said. "Old parging mortar. The dry stuff they used to seal stone foundations back in the eighteen-hundreds. Bone-dry. The kind of dust you only find under a house that hasn't seen rain in a century."

Choy knelt. She didn't touch the iron ring. She reached into her wool coat and pulled out a small steel spatula. She leaned over the seam of the hatch.

"How do you get a dead man out of a cellar, Kestrel, without carrying him through a clean kitchen?"

"The old coal chute," Kestrel managed to say. Her throat felt dry as ashes. "In the back. Behind the lilac bushes. It’s been nailed shut since my father died."

"The nails were pulled last week," Choy said. She slipped the thin blade of the spatula into the groove between the floorboards, scraping upward with a light, practiced flick of her wrist.

A small, yellow smudge of grease clung to the steel.

Choy brought it to her nose. She didn't look surprised.

"Aniseed," Choy said. "And bitter almond."

She held the blade out. The grease caught the gray light from the window—yellow, thick, and oily. Exactly like the smear on Kestrel’s raw knuckles.

"Eldora only sold three jars of this batch," Choy said, her voice dropping all the neighborly pretense. "One to you. One to Mrs. Hallowell. And one to the minister’s wife, who hasn't left her bed in Rutland since the first frost."

Kestrel felt her knees go hollow. She reached back, her fingers finding the cold rim of the dry sink. "I haven't been down there. Not since we put the squash in."

"Then who greased the hinges on the coal chute?" Choy asked. She scraped the grease into a small plastic tube, capping it with her thumb. "Who took your key, dug up the roots, boiled them down, and dragged Maceo Quint into the dark until they could slide him out into the snow?"

"Leander doesn't use it," Kestrel said, her mind spinning, trying to find a way out. "He hates the smell. He says it smells like a grave."

"Maybe he didn't want his own skin sticking to the iron," Choy said. "Or maybe he wanted us to find it."

"No," Kestrel said. "No. He wouldn't do that to me."

"He wouldn't?" Choy stood up, tucking the tube into her pocket. Her face was hard. "The watch was in his coat, Kestrel. The poison was in your garden. The body was under your kitchen floor. If it wasn't him, and it wasn't you, then we’ve got a ghost with very specific taste in hand cream."

The floorboards vibrated.

A low, rhythmic thrum shook the glass in the pantry door. The Dodge. It came up the lane too fast, the tires throwing frozen gravel against the clapboards of the barn. The engine shut off with a wet, heavy shudder—like a spade hitting clay.

Choy didn't look at the window. Her shoulders went stiff, her weight shifting back onto her heels. Her right hand dropped, her fingers resting light and loose near the black leather holster on her hip.

The porch steps groaned. A heavy, uneven tread—one foot dragging, the bad knee Leander had carried since his high school football days—came up the stairs.

"Don't say a word," Choy whispered. Her voice was thin and sharp as a razor blade. "Not a single word."

On the door, the brass latch began to turn.

Chapter 5: The Silent Vault

The vault smelled of cold earth and rot. Not the sweet, rich decay of a healthy compost pile, but a damp, mineral draft that tasted of iron filings and old turnip skins. It was burrowed deep into the northern slope of the hill, where the poured-concrete walls sweated a fine glaze of mineral-heavy water even in August. Here, forty-two degrees was the law. A tomb for things waiting to be born.

Kestrel Janz stood under the glare of the single forty-watt bulb. Her fingers were frozen around the handle of an empty zinc seed bucket. The bucket didn’t rattle, but only because she was holding it so tight her knuckles had gone white.

At her feet lay Oona Hallowell.

Oona’s head was tilted at an angle that made her neck look impossibly long. Like a heron struck down in the reeds. Her bright, dyed-orange hair—that frantic, artificial color she used specifically to spite the homestead’s strict natural-dye mandate—shone under the yellow light. A splash of rusted copper against the wet gray floor. Around her head lay a scattering of 'Scarlet Runner' beans. They looked like small, speckled kidneys spilled from a torn paper packet.

She was twenty-two. Yesterday, she’d spent forty minutes complaining about the quality of the organic jute twine. Now, she was completely still.

Kestrel dropped. The concrete bit straight through the wool of her trousers, cold and unforgiving. She didn’t scream. The silence of the homestead had a way of swallowing sound before it could even clear your teeth. She reached out, her fingertips hovering just above Oona’s cheek. The skin was already the temperature of the well water they used to scrub the dairy tiles.

Beside Oona’s left shoulder, resting on the corner of the cedar sorting table, was a sheet of thick, cream-colored paper.

It was heavy, cotton-rag stock—the expensive brand Kestrel imported from the mill in New Hampshire for the homestead’s hand-pressed labels. On it, typed in the clean, mechanical violet ink of the library’s old Remington, were three lines:

I cannot live with what we did to Maceo. He was mine. He was always mine. Forgive me.

Kestrel stared. Then her gaze drifted from the violet ink down to Oona’s cold, curled fingers.

The left hand.

Whenever Oona worked in the potting shed, she held her paring knife in her left hand, her thumb permanently scarred from the times she’d forgotten to adjust the safety catch designed for right-handed tools. When she cleaned the cedar flats, she worked from right to left, a backwards, awkward motion that Leander always criticized because it disrupted the assembly flow. When she typed the seed catalogs, she sat with her left shoulder hunched forward, her left hand flying over the keys while her right hovered, sluggish and auxiliary.

She wouldn’t have typed this. Not with her right. And she couldn’t have typed it with her left.

Kestrel reached out and gently turned Oona’s chin toward the light.

The bruising on her neck was not the messy, chaotic swelling of a self-inflicted cord. It was a neat, terrible map of force. A massive, purple crater was pressed deep into the soft hollow behind Oona's left collarbone—the unmistakable mark of a dominant right thumb. The fingernail marks—four clean crescent moons—were sliced into the right side of her neck, dragging upward toward the hinge of her jaw.

It was a right-handed grip. Someone had stood over her, pinned her against the heavy galvanized shelving, and squeezed until the small bones in her throat gave way.

The heavy iron latch of the vault door groaned.

"Kes? What’s taking so long? The distributor from Middlebury is—"

Leander’s voice cut through the damp chill. He stopped on the third step from the bottom, his work boots frozen on the damp hemlock riser. His flannel shirt was wet from the mist outside, the scent of pine bark and wet wood smoke clinging to his shoulders.

He didn't scream either. His eyes went from Oona’s bright hair to the spilled beans, and then, with a strange, defensive quickness, to the paper on the sorting table.

"Oh, God," Leander whispered. His voice was too thin, too high. "Oh, God, Kes. She did it."

"No," Kestrel said. Her voice sounded like dry leaves scraping across flagstones. "She didn't."

"She’s dead, Kes. Look at her." He took three rapid, heavy steps down, his hand reaching out to grab Kestrel's shoulder. His grip was wide, his fingers wrapping completely around her collarbone. He was a large man, built for lifting grain bags and turning heavy soil. "And that's her paper. The stuff from the office. She wrote it."

"She was left-handed, Leander."

He paused, his thumb tightening on Kestrel's coat. "What does that have to do with anything? People use whatever hand they want when they're... when they’re desperate."

"The bruising," Kestrel said, pointing a finger that felt like ice. "Look at her neck. The pressure was applied from the front, by a right hand. A very strong right hand."

Before Leander could answer, another shadow fell across the stairs. Detective Kateri Choy descended slowly, her dark wool coat unbuttoned, her boots silent on the wood. She smelled of stale car heater and peppermint gum. Her sharp, dark eyes took in the room in a single, sweeping glance that seemed to catalog every seed packet, every drop of moisture on the concrete, and the exact distance between Kestrel and her husband.

"Step back from the body, please, Mrs. Janz," Choy said. Her voice had the flat, unhurried cadence of someone who had spent twenty years watching people lie in small rooms.

Kestrel stood up, her knees cracking in the damp air. She didn't look at Leander, but she could feel the heat radiating from him—the sour, panicked warmth of a man who saw his perfect world beginning to fray at the edges.

Choy knelt. She didn't touch the typed note, but she leaned close enough to read it, her spectacles catching the yellow light of the bulb. "An Underwood?"

"A Remington," Kestrel corrected. "In the library. Anyone can use it."

"A confession," Leander said, his voice regaining some of its familiar, commanding resonance. He stepped between Kestrel and the detective, his shoulders squaring. "It makes sense, Detective. We knew she was close with Maceo. She was... she was obsessive. She assisted him with the orchard yields. If she thought he was going to leave, or if she found out about..." He trailed off, waving a hand vaguely toward the ceiling. "It’s a tragedy. A terrible, private tragedy. But it’s over."

Choy didn't look up from Oona’s neck. Her gloved fingers gently traced the margin of the purple welts, never quite touching the skin. "You have a lot of theories, Mr. Grote."

"I have a business to run," Leander said, his tone sharpening. "We have twenty-two apprentices living on this ridge. We have investors. If this gets out as another murder—if the press thinks there’s some kind of... of maniac out here—the homestead is finished. We lose the land. We lose everything."

"A girl is dead," Kestrel said.

"And she killed Maceo!" Leander turned on her, his face flushed, a thin bead of sweat running down the temple where his hair was starting to gray. "Can't you see that? She’s confessed. It’s right there. We don't need to dig up any more of this. For your sake, Kestrel. For the brand."

Choy stood up, brushing a speck of dried dirt from her trousers. Her face was an unreadable mask of weathered brown skin. "The coroner will be here in twenty minutes. Until then, this room is sealed. In fact, the whole estate is under restriction. No one leaves. No one calls out."

"Of course," Leander said. He reached into his pocket and pulled out his phone, then turned to Kestrel. "Give me your phone, Kes."

Kestrel stepped back, her heel hitting the edge of the iron shelving. "Why?"

"For your safety," Leander said, his voice dropping into that low, soothing register he used when the cameras were rolling for the homestead's promotional videos. He held out his hand, palm up. His skin was rough, calloused, the nails short and scrubbed clean, though a crescent of dark soil still remained under his thumbnail. "The comments on the main page are already vicious. People are guessing. They're accusing you, they're accusing me. If you start replying, or if you see what they’re saying about Maceo... it’ll break you. Let me manage the accounts. I'll put up a statement about Oona. A temporary pause for a family emergency."

"I don't need you to manage my phone, Leander."

"Kestrel," he said, his voice hardening just enough for her to hear the steel beneath the velvet. "We are in this together. If the brand falls, the bank takes the valley. You remember what it’s like to have nothing? To be back in that cabin in the woods with your mother, eating salt pork and waiting for the winter to kill you?"

The mention of her mother was a small, sharp blade. He knew exactly where to twist it.

Detective Choy watched them, her eyes darting between Leander’s outstretched hand and Kestrel’s pale face. She didn't intervene. She was waiting to see who would break first.

Slowly, Kestrel reached into her pocket and pulled out the small, silver device. She placed it in Leander’s hand. The metal felt cold, like she was handing him a key to her own cage.

"Thank you," Leander whispered. He slid it into his pocket beside his own. "Go up to the house. Get some tea. Eldora is in the kitchen. She’ll look after you."

The kitchen of the main house was too quiet.

Eldora Cole sat by the woodstove, her hands busy with a pair of shears, trimming the dried roots from a basket of garlic bulbs. The papery skins fell to the floor in soft, white whispers, smelling of sulfur and old dust. She didn't look up when Kestrel entered.

"The girl was always too light for this valley," Eldora said, her voice like grinding stones. "Too much color in her hair. Too many thoughts in her head. A girl like that, she’s like dry pine. One spark and she’s gone."

Kestrel didn't answer. She walked past the stove, the draft from the cellar door hitting her ankles like a cold hand. She felt the weight of her past pressing against her temples—the "Ghost" she had spent ten years trying to outrun.

The Fold.

That was what they had called the community in the northern woods of Maine. No phones. No paper that hadn't been approved by the Elder. Every evening, the girls had to line up outside the main lodge, their hands held out, palms up, while the Elder inspected them for dirt, for ink, for any sign of the world outside. When Kestrel’s mother had tried to write a letter to her sister in Boston, the Elder had found it in her mattress. They had burned it in the central fire pit, and then they had taken her shoes for three weeks so she couldn't walk beyond the perimeter of the clearing.

For your safety, the Elder had said, his voice identical to the tone Leander had used in the vault. To keep you clean.

Kestrel walked up the stairs to the second floor, her boots heavy on the pine treads. The house was empty. Viggo Brand was somewhere down by the milk depot, and Choy was still down in the cold concrete of the seed vault with the dead girl.

She reached the landing and turned toward her small private study at the end of the hall. It was the only room in the house Leander didn't use for filming. It had no bright windows, no white-painted shelves designed to look good on a screen. It was dark, lined with unpainted pine, smelling of old paper and the beeswax she used to polish her desk.

Behind the wainscoting next to the chimney breast was a loose panel.

Kestrel knelt, her fingers searching for the small knot in the wood. She pressed it, and the pine board slid back with a soft, dry click. Inside was a small, black iron document box—a fireproof safe she had bought with her own money from a hardware store in Burlington before she and Leander were married.

She set her fingers to the dial.

Left twenty-four. Right twelve. Left thirty-six.

The latch clicked. But when she pulled the handle, the door swung open too easily. There was no resistance, no heavy seal of rubber against steel.

The box was empty.

Kestrel’s breath caught in her throat, a dry, greasy taste like tallow.

Her journal was gone.

It was a small, thick book bound in black calfskin, the pages filled with her own tight, left-handed script. It didn't contain recipes or garden plans. It contained the names. The names of the seven people who had died of winter fever at the Fold in the winter of 2014. It contained the details of how the Elder had buried them in the orchard behind the lodge without notifying the county coroner, and how Kestrel, seventeen and terrified, had helped dig the shallow graves in the frozen earth because her mother was too weak to hold the spade.

It also contained the truth about Leander.

It detailed how he had come to the Fold as a young, charismatic photographer, looking for "authentic" subjects, and how he had helped her escape—not out of mercy, but because he saw the value in her story, the raw, marketable tragedy of a cult survivor. He had rebuilt her, branded her, and married her, but he had kept her story like a knife to her throat.

If Detective Choy found that journal, the "Perfect Homestead" would not just be ruined. Kestrel would be accessory to a dozen federal crimes.

She stood up, her back scraping against the pine wall.

Who had taken it?

Leander had the key to the room, but he had never known the combination to the safe. Or had he? He was a man who noticed details. He could have watched her from the doorway, his eyes catching the reflection of her fingers in the polished surface of the desk.

Or Viggo. Viggo, who spent half his time cleaning the chimneys and knew every loose board in the house. Viggo, who looked at her with that quiet, resentful pity every time Leander praised her "natural grace" in front of the cameras.

Kestrel walked to the window. Outside, the mist was thickening, swallowing the tops of the hemlocks. Down in the driveway, the red lights of the coroner's wagon were pulsing against the gray fog, casting long, bloody shadows across the gravel.

She was trapped on the ridge. Her phone was in Leander’s pocket. Her past was in someone else’s hands.

And on the floor of the seed vault, Oona’s left hand was still curled, waiting for a pen she would never hold again.

Chapter 6: A House Built on Ashes

The smell of his absence was the only clean thing left in the house. Without his sandalwood soap and the sour, hot steam of his morning espresso, the place reverted to its true nature: damp plaster, joists rotting behind the beadboard, and that cold, iron draft rising from the cellar stairs.

Kestrel stood in his office, her boots leaving gray, salty rings on the wide-pine floor. Outside, the Vermont sky was the color of a bruised turnip. Down in the valley, the corporate lawyers from Burlington were probably already opening their calfskin cases, uncapping their heavy pens to carve up the land. Leander had left before dawn. He’d kissed her cheek with a dry, hard mouth, his wool coat smelling of cedar shavings.

"Keep the hearth low," he’d whispered, his thumb pressing into her collarbone until his signet ring left a red crescent. "We don’t need to look like we’re burning through wood."

Now, she was alone.

Her heart wasn't hammering; it was a cold fist knocking against her ribs. She walked to the desk—a seven-foot slab of reclaimed white oak that Leander had oiled until it looked like dark honey. He called it his "station," but it was an altar to a life he’d built out of whole cloth. On it sat his brass inkwell, his linen-bound ledger, and a ceramic jar of graphite pencils, each sharpened to a uniform, lethal point.

The bottom drawer was locked.

She didn't panic. Panic was a luxury she’d spent a decade outrunning. Besides, she knew Leander’s neat, predictable little pieties. He believed in the poetry of hidden things, but his imagination was limited by his need for order. She crossed to the fieldstone fireplace, where the cast-iron kettle sat cold on the hearth. Behind it, tucked into a crevice where the mortar had crumbled to gray sand, was the small, rusted key. She found it by touch, her fingers scraping the rough granite.

The lock didn't click. It yielded with a dry, heavy clack that she felt in her teeth.

The drawer slid open on waxed wooden runners. Inside, the organization was surgical. File folders of heavy, cream-colored cardstock were aligned by date, each labeled in Leander’s elegant, blocky calligraphy. Taxes: Grote Homesteading LLC. Permits: Barn Restoration. Land Registry: Quint Boundary Dispute.

She reached past them, her knuckles brushing against a small, velvet-lined box at the very back.

Inside lay three keys on a brass ring: a square house key, a silver padlock key, and a black plastic fob with a worn Subaru logo.

Oona’s keys.

Kestrel’s thumb smeared the grit on the plastic. The blue thread of the braided lanyard was stiff with dried mud, smelling faintly of the hemlock swamp behind the orchard. Oona had been missing three days. The state troopers had found her wagon on the shoulder of Route 7, its hazard lights blinking their battery down to a dull orange glow. They assumed she’d wandered into the woods, old and confused by the early November squall.

But her keys were here. Tucked behind the property deeds.

Kestrel’s hand went deeper, hitting something stiff and bound in blue plastic drafting paper. She pulled it out. Sovereign Media Group. A New York address.

Then the title:

THE ASH AND THE VESSEL: The True Story of Vashti

The name didn't just hurt; it tasted like wood ash and raw iron.

Vashti.

Ten years of scrubbing her skin with yellow soap to get the smell of kerosene out of her hair, and here it was, typed out on sixty-pound paper. The girl who survived the fire at the Emmanuel Fellowship in the Maine woods. The girl who watched the sheep barn burn, who ran through the blackberry briars until her bare feet were black as charcoal.

She turned the page. Her eyes jumped across the clean type:

...an eight-part investigative docuseries focusing on Kestrel Janz (born Vashti Miller), the sole adult survivor of the Emmanuel sect...

>

...exclusive access to archival photographs, childhood journals, and personal artifacts...

>

...first-person testimonial from Leander Grote regarding the daily psychological collapse of the subject...

The room seemed to tilt, the white-washed pine walls closing in like the sides of a fresh coffin. She turned to the back of the agreement.

There were two signature lines. The first was signed in blue ink by a woman named Eldora Cole, listed as Producer. The second, labeled Spouse/Representative of the Subject’s Estate, bore Leander’s signature. The ink was long dry. He had signed it three weeks ago.

The payout was listed in a neat table on page fourteen: Advance payment upon execution: $250,000. Production milestone payment: $500,000. Back-end royalties: 15% of net streaming revenue.

A total valuation of nearly three million dollars.

But it was the clause on page three that made her breath stop: In the event of the subject’s non-cooperation, representation of historical accuracy shall be deferred entirely to the Representative of the Estate, provided that such representation is accompanied by physical evidence of the subject’s identity and past activities.

The journals she thought she’d burned. The silver communion cup she’d dropped down the well at the old farm. He had them.

She understood the whole grand, terrible architecture of the last five years now. Leander hadn't fallen in love with a quiet girl selling squash at a Middlebury farm stand. He’d found a commodity. He’d built this "perfect" homestead as a set. The copper pots, the unglazed crocks, the sheep-fleece blankets—they weren't a return to a simpler life. They were set dressing for her exposure.

“The internet loves a victim,” he’d said once over a glass of Syrah, his face half-shadowed by the hearth. “But they love a resurrected ghost even more.”

She’d thought it was just his marketing talk. The dry hum of a man who lived in metrics. She wasn't laughing now.

Memory is a sharp thing. Three months ago, Maceo Quint had come up to clean the chimney flue. He’d been quiet, his boots smelling of coal oil, his eyes always dropped to the floorboards. She’d found him in the barn later, holding an old leather trunk Leander kept in the loft.

"This yours, Mrs. Grote?" Maceo had asked, his finger tracing the brass initials—V.M.—under the dust.

"No," she’d said, her voice tight. "It's Leander's."

Maceo had spat a dark stream of tobacco into the dirt. "Funny. Swear I saw a picture of you in one of them old newsprint rags from down east. A girl with the same eyes, standing in front of a burning silo."

Two weeks later, they found Maceo at the bottom of the gravel quarry, his skull split by a timber. The sheriff called it an accidental slip—a local drunk taking the short cut home. But Viggo Brand had told her different at the milk depot. "Maceo didn't drink on Tuesdays, Kestrel. And he didn't walk the quarry road unless he was meeting someone who didn't want to be seen in the tavern."

Did Leander kill him? Or did Maceo try to squeeze him, and Leander simply took care of the one thread that could ruin his three-million-dollar ending before the cameras arrived?

And then Oona. Oona, who helped her jar the damson plums. Oona, who had seen Leander moving the old leather trunks down from the loft. Oona must have found the blue binder.

Kestrel dropped the document back into the drawer, her hands slick with sweat. The brass keys of Oona’s Subaru seemed to catch the gray light from the window, gleaming like cold teeth.

Leander wasn't just a controlling husband who wanted her to wear linen dresses and bake sourdough for his feed. He was a monster who was staging her ruin. And if she refused to play her part?

The Representative of the Estate.

That clause only worked if she was unable to speak for herself. Or if she was dead.

A sudden sound shattered the silence of the house.

It was a low, heavy thud from below—the wet rattle of the side door mudroom latch.

Kestrel froze. Her breath caught in her throat, her lungs burning as she listened.

Step.

Step.

The floorboards in the kitchen groaned under a familiar, heavy tread. It was too early for Leander. The Burlington meeting wasn't supposed to end until three, and the road was a mess of gray slush.

"Kestrel?"

The voice wasn't Leander's. It was lower, gravelly, wet with the cold.

Viggo.

She locked the drawer, her fingers shaking so hard the key slipped twice. She shoved it into her apron pocket and walked down the stairs.

Viggo stood on the rag rug, his yellow oilskins dripping gray water onto the spruce boards. He was holding a grease-stained paper sack. He didn't look up immediately; he was staring at the small, antique sideboard where Leander kept the mail.

"Kestrel?" he called again, his voice dropping to a whisper. "You up there?"

"I'm here, Viggo," she said, descending the stairs slowly, her hand sliding along the rough banister.

Viggo flinched, his head snapping up. "You shouldn't be in his office," he said. It wasn't a threat. It was the flat advice of a neighbor who knew which dogs on the ridge bit.

"He's in Burlington," Kestrel said. "He won't be back for hours."

"He isn't in Burlington," Viggo said, stepping closer. The smell of woodsmoke and damp wool rose from him like a fog. "I saw his truck down at the old creamery. He was talking to that woman from the land office. Eldora Cole."

The name felt like a physical blow. Eldora Cole. The producer.

"She isn't with the land office, Viggo," Kestrel whispered.

Viggo worked a piece of cedar bark between his teeth. "She had a clipboard. And a camera. They were looking at the old foundation where Maceo's uncle had his barn before the fire."

He reached into the paper sack and pulled out a rusted spade-foot latch. "Found this in the quarry creek," he said, holding it out. His hand was thick, the skin split around the knuckles from the winter wind. "It’s from Oona's root cellar. The screws didn't rust out, Kestrel. They were backed out with a driver. Clean."

Kestrel looked at the latch. The metal was cold, covered in a thin skin of yellow clay.

"He’s selling the valley, Kestrel," Viggo said, his voice dropping so low she had to lean forward. "He’s selling the whole damn ridge to a sand-and-gravel outfit out of New Jersey. But they can't break ground while the state’s still looking for Oona. A missing person investigation keeps the permits frozen."

The red herring was beautiful. Leander the greedy developer, clearing out the locals. It fit his vanity. But she knew better. The ridge was nothing.

She was the prize.

"Viggo," she said, her voice shaking as she stepped off the last stair. "You need to leave. If he finds you here—"

"I'm not scared of Leander," Viggo said, though his eyes darted to the window as a crow screeched from the hemlocks. "But you should be. He's got Oona's dog down at his barn. The black terrier. It’s been whining since yesterday. Why would he have her dog if she hitched a ride to the highway?"

Tires crunched on the frozen gravel outside.

Viggo stiffened. "That’s him."

"The back door," she whispered, grabbing his sleeve. "Through the pantry. Go down the orchard lane—stay in the apple rows."

Viggo didn't argue. He shoved the latch back into his sack and slipped through the kitchen.

Kestrel stood in the hall. The headlights of Leander's truck swept across the frosted glass of the front door. The diesel engine died with a heavy, rattling sigh.

She took a deep breath, her fingers curling around the cold key in her pocket. She had to be the wife he wanted: quiet, dull, broken by the mountain. She went to the stove, took the copper kettle, and filled it from the pump. Her hands were steady now. The same cold survival that got her out of the Maine fire was taking over.

The door opened. The cold came in first—a sharp, pine-scented knife—then Leander. His coat was dark with melting sleet. He scanned the hall, then looked at her.

"Kestrel. You're up late. I thought you'd be in the greenhouse."

"The heater went out," she said, setting the kettle on the iron ring. "I was coming down to make tea."

He pulled his leather gloves off, finger by finger. He laid them on the table—right next to the faint, wet circle left by Viggo's boot.

Leander stopped. He stared at the spot. "Did Viggo come by?"

"He brought some eggs," she said, her voice dry as salt. "I told him we didn't need them."

Leander nodded slowly. He walked to the window, his hand touching the glass, his thumb leaving a small, greasy smear as he traced a frost pattern.

"The wind's coming from the north tonight," he said. "We'll have a hard freeze. We need to make sure everything is locked down."

He turned, his smile showing just the tips of his teeth.

"Everything. We can't have anything valuable getting lost in the cold."

Chapter 7: The Cold Divide

The wind didn’t just blow; it threw itself against the homestead’s western wall like a shoulder trying to splinter wood. By five, the April sky was the color of a wet slate shingle. By six, the snow wasn't falling—it was dumping. Heavy, sodden clumps that loaded down the hemlocks until the branches gave up with a wet, splintering crack.

Inside, the lights died. Just a couple of weak yellow flickers and then a dry, metallic ping from the cellar fuse box. The dark came down hard. The only sound left was the rhythmic, frantic rattle of the iron sash weights inside the parlor windows.

Kestrel Janz didn't move from the kitchen hearth. Her fingers stayed curled around the cold, unglazed rim of a stoneware crock, soaking in the room's sudden chill. She listened. Without the refrigerator’s low rattle or the steady thump of the water pump, the house felt gutted. A hollow ribcage of pine and hemlock settling into the frozen valley mud.

The county road was gone; she knew that without looking. Forty minutes ago, the distant, muffled roar of Black Branch creek had changed key—the sound of three hundred tons of mountain runoff and gray slush blowing right through the culvert by the old lime kiln. Detective Kateri Choy wasn't coming back from the town clerk's office tonight. Nobody was coming up the ridge now. The mountain had closed its teeth.

A match flared in the hall. It threw a greasy, yellow smudge of light against the pine paneling.

Leander stepped into the kitchen, his boots dragging. He held a hurricane lamp from the pantry, its glass chimney already fouled with soot from a raggedly trimmed wick. The sharp, chemical stink of old kerosene hit her first, cutting right through the smell of green woodsmoke and damp plaster.

"Choy’s stuck," he said. His voice was too loud, vibrating with a dry, nervous rattle. He kept his eyes off her, focusing instead on setting the lamp onto the zinc-topped table. His fingernails were black with grease from his useless tinkering with the generator. "The branch took out the bridge. State road is buried under three feet of gravel and hemlock timber. Phone's dead, too. That old pine by Eldora’s lane must have finally gone over the wires."

Kestrel let her hand drop from the stoneware. Her fingers were stiffening. "Viggo?"

"Viggo’s got his own stove and the sense to sit by it," Leander muttered. He fished a silver flask from his mackinaw pocket and twisted the cap off with one hand. Cheap rye joined the kerosene smell. "We're on our own. Just us and the woods."

"We shouldn't have sent her," Kestrel said. The wind was rising, clawing at the clapboards. "She was looking for the old land registry deeds, Leander. You know what's in those boxes."

He took a long, hard pull from the flask. His throat clicked. When he finally looked up, his eyes were wet, dark points buried in the hollows of his skull. He had the exact look his father used to get after the bank took the lower pasture—shamed but mean, his shoulders hunched as if the ceiling might collapse on him any second.

"Let her look," he said, wiping his mouth with a knuckle, leaving a smear of soot on his lip. "There’s nothing in those ledgers that wasn't bought with clean paper. I didn't steal this ridge, Kestrel. I paid for it. Every goddamn stone."

"With my books," she said.

The corner of his mouth twitched. A sour, dead smile. "Your books. That’s the story we’re sticking to, then?"

A draft slid across Kestrel’s shins, rising through the gaps in the floorboards near the cellar door. It brought the smell of the vault with it—that flat, lime-and-mortar dust of recently sealed stone. It pooled around her boots like rising well water.

"They are mine," she said. But the words felt thin, like dry birch bark scraping across the floorboards.

Leander took a step closer. Not with his usual heavy-footed confidence, but slow, almost stalking, his boots catching on the uneven edges of the hemlock planks. He stopped on the far side of the zinc table. The yellow flame of the lamp sat between them like a small, dirty fence.

"You’ve got a rare memory, Kestrel. Or should I call you Vashti?"

The name hit her like a wet hand to the face. It didn't even sound like a name anymore; it sounded like the key to some rusted trunk buried five feet deep in river silt. The silence between them grew so thick she could hear the tiny, wet rattle in Leander’s lungs.

"I wondered when you'd finally say it out loud," she whispered. She buried her hands deep in her cardigan pockets, her thumbs digging into the seams to hide the shaking. "How long have you known?"

"Since the second month we were in Burlington," he said. He leaned down and turned the wick key. The flame sputtered, spitting a thin ribbon of black grease toward the ceiling joists. "You were careless, Vashti. That library card in your sewing box. The one from the state asylum in Waterbury. I didn't even have to look hard. A girl who walked out of the ward during the boiler fire eight years ago and just... went into the brush. Grew over like an old logging trail."

He leaned his weight on the zinc table. His skin had the yellow, greasy look of tallow in the lamp-glow. "I kept quiet because I didn't care. I liked the quiet of you. But then I found that first notebook under the floorboards in the cabin. I read it while you were down at the creek beating the dirt out of the linens. I saw what you had. The way you put words together. The way you wrote about the dark like it was some old uncle sitting in the corner of the room."

"You stole it," she said. Her voice stayed flat, dead.

"I made you," he corrected. It was the low, stubborn drone of a local man who had finally put one over on the world. "Who the hell was going to buy a book by a runaway crazy girl from Waterbury? A girl who couldn't buy a loaf of bread without her knees knocking? I bought this name. I bought the tax records. I fed the reporters exactly what they wanted—the mysterious, brilliant weaver who lives in the high country and writes about the valley ghosts. I gave you Kestrel Janz. And you lived in her like a warm coat. Don't tell me you didn't like the safety of it."

"Safety," she said. She looked past him. The window was a solid wall of white now, the snow packing against the glass, erasing the barn, the apple trees, the whole mountain. "You kept me in a cellar and called it a marriage."

"It was a living," he said, his voice dropping into a rougher register. "But then Choy started poking around. And that Oona woman. They’ve been talking to the excavators down in Weybridge. Asking who poured the floor for the old creamery. And then... that thing we found."

He shook his head—a short, ugly jerk. His hand went back to the flask, gripping the silver until his knuckles turned white as lard.

"I didn't put her in that wall, Vashti. I swear to Almighty God I didn't."

"Then who did?" Her heart was hammering a dry, frantic rhythm against her ribs. "You had the keys. You poured the footings. You’re the one who wanted the old creamery foundation sealed. You paid Viggo to bring his mixer up here on a Sunday."

"I wanted it buried because the drainage was a disaster!" Leander hissed. He leaned in, the lamp light showing the red, broken veins in his eyeballs. "If the district inspector saw how the run-off pooled twenty feet from the well-head, they’d have shut the whole build down. We wouldn't have even framed the kitchen. It was a local fix, Kestrel. You pour six inches of concrete over the mess and you don't ask the town clerk for a permit. That’s all it was. I didn't know there was something under the gravel. Why the hell would I kill anyone? Why would I wreck all of this?"

He waved his hand at the kitchen—the copper pots hanging from the clean hemlock beams, the dried sage, the custom soapstone sink. The perfect country life. The one that got six pages in the winter issue of Metropolitan Home. A beautiful shell built over a rotten ditch.

"You're the writer," he said, and now the swagger was completely gone, leaving only the thin, sharp panic of a contractor who had gotten too deep in the mud. "You're the one who knows how people disappear. You're the one who came out of the weeds. Not me. My grandfather built the grist mill down in Weybridge. Grotes don't... we don't do things like this."

"Then let Choy do her job," she said, backing up until her heel hit the trim of the pantry door. "Let her look at the files."

"No," he said. He moved around the zinc table with a sudden, bulky quickness that made the kerosene slosh against the glass. He blocked the hallway. "You don't get how the world works, Vashti. Once the state police start digging into the money—once they look at the deeds and see they’re deeded to Kestrel Janz, a person who doesn't have a social security number—they’ll tear the walls down. They'll find Waterbury. They'll see the tax fraud. They’ll take the house, the publisher accounts, the land. Everything. We'll end up with nothing but two state orange jumpsuits and a public defender who doesn't give a damn."

"I don't care about the money."

"Well, I do!" Leander roared. His voice cracked, the sound rattling the uninsulated ceiling beams. He sucked in a breath, trying to steady his shoulders, but his chest was heaving under his wool coat. "I’m not going back to some single-wide in Salisbury, Kestrel. I’m not going to be the joke of Addison County because my genius wife turned out to be a runaway lunatic with a skull in her foundation."

He took another step. His frame cut off the weak light from the hurricane lamp, throwing her into total shadow.

Kestrel’s hand slid behind her, searching the dark trim of the pantry until her fingers found the cold iron latch. "What now, Leander? You going to mix another batch of concrete for me?"

He stopped dead. For a second his face went completely slack, the rage replaced by a flicker of real horror. "No. Jesus, Kestrel, no. I want you alive. I need you. Without you, there aren aren't any more manuscripts. There’s nothing. I just need you to be still. Just until this blizzard lets up and I can find out who put that... who put her in the wall."

"Who do you think did it?" she whispered, her fingers locking around the latch.

"Viggo," Leander said, his eyes cutting toward the dark window pane as if he expected a face to be pressed against it. "He’s been sour since we bought the south lot. His people grazed sheep there before the town took it for back taxes. And Eldora... Eldora’s had her nose in our ditch since the day we broke ground. She’s the one who called the clerk about the well-head. She wants us off the ridge. She wants the whole valley back the way it was before the flatlanders started building."

He turned his face back to her, his expression tightening into something small and ugly. "I’m going down to Viggo’s. I’m going to find out what he told Choy before the road washed."

"You can't," Kestrel said. "The road is gone."

"I've got the bear-paws," Leander said. "I can cut through the birch stand by the old logging trail. But I can't leave you here to talk to Choy if she gets a county rig through the wash. Or if the phone line comes back. I can't trust you right now, Kestrel. You’ve got that look. The same one you had when you tried to burn the notebooks in the yard last spring."

"Leander, don't—" She lunged sideways, trying to clear the doorframe.

He was too fast. He didn't hit her—he just threw his weight forward, pinning her chest against the pine frame. The smell of rye and sweat was hot against her neck. She clawed at the thick wool of his mackinaw, but he didn't even blink. He used his forearm like a bar across her throat, crowding her back, his other hand locking around both of her wrists.

"It’s just for now," he muttered. His breath was hot, smelling of cheap whiskey and sour teeth. "Just until I get back with some answers."

He didn't shove her into the pantry. He dragged her backward toward the stairs, his heavy boots finding the treads one by one. Kestrel kicked out, her heel catching his shin with a dull crack, but he barely registered it. He was built from twenty years of moving granite and green hemlock; she was thin, her body soft from years spent behind a typewriter.

He hauled her up the dark stairwell, their shadows twisting across the cold plaster like giant, broken spiders.

"Leander!" Her throat was dry, full of plaster dust. "You can't do this!"

"I can," he said. The panic had gone cold now, hardening into that stubborn, stupid Grote pride. "I’ve been doing it for eight years, haven't I? Keeping you out of the ditch."

They hit the top landing. He kicked the bedroom door open. The air in the room was freezing, the iron bedstead standing like a black ribcage in the center of the dark.

He threw her in. Kestrel stumbled, her knees hitting the braided rug, the rough wool burning through her stockings. She scrambled back up, her hands reaching out, but the heavy oak door was already moving.

The latch clicked. Then the sound she’d been waiting for—the dry, heavy grate of the iron key turning in the mortise. It was the original 1840 lock. Leander had restored it himself with a brass file and turbine oil. He’d been so proud of those old fittings. Real iron, he’d told the photographer from New York. You don't get security like this in modern houses.

"Leander!" She slammed her palms against the wood. The oak didn't even shudder. It was three inches of solid heartwood, held together with hand-struck iron nails.

"There's water in the washbasin pitcher," his voice came through the thick wood, flat and muffled. "And the wool blankets are in the cedar chest. I’ll be back before dawn, Vashti."

His boots went down the stairs, slow and heavy. Then the thump of the back door opening. A sudden draft rushed through the floorboards, making the house hum like an old cello, and then the latch clicked. He was gone.

The quiet came back, heavier this time, settling into the corners like coal dust.

Kestrel stayed on the floor, listening. Gradually, her own breathing slowed enough for the wind to take over again—that long, dry hiss of the snow through the spruce needles.

She got up. Her knees were stiff, her shins throbbing where she’d hit the floor. The room was dark, but her eyes had adjusted to the pale gray light reflecting off the drifts outside. She walked to the window.

The glass was ice against her forehead. Outside was nothing but a white slate. No yellow light from Eldora’s kitchen, no smoke from Viggo’s chimney down by the creek. Even the barn had been swallowed by the gray drift.

She reached for the sash, her hand searching for the brass catch. As she did, her thumb dragged across something sticky on the muntin.

She leaned closer, her nose almost touching the glass, squinting through the gray gloom.

It was a smear of grease. Gray-black. The kind of soot you get from a dirty kerosene wick or a poorly oiled lock. But it wasn't on the inside of the pane.

It was on the outside of the glass.

Her breath fogged the window. She wiped it away with her sleeve. The wood near the brass sash lock was freshly splintered. The white latex paint was curled back in tiny, clean shavings that hadn’t even been yellowed by the weather yet.

Someone had tried the window. Not last week. Today.

She touched the brass latch. It was turned to the locked position, but when she wiggled it, the whole mechanism gave. The screws had been backed out of the wood until they were barely holding.

Through the glass, the snow kept coming, filling the valley, burying the county road, the creek, and whatever lay under the concrete of the old creamery. Kestrel stood in the freezing dark, her hand frozen on the latch, wondering if the tracks Leander was about to look for in the woods had already ended right outside her door.

Chapter 8: The Spiral in the Clay

The cold didn't drift into the room. It dropped. Heavy, wet, and smelling of damp cellar dirt, it settled over everything like a soaked wool blanket.

Inside the locked bedroom, the air had gone brittle. Every breath Kestrel drew felt like swallowing shaved pine needles. She stood by the window, her forehead pressed flat against the glass—cold enough to stick—watching the frost feather outward across the pane. The silence in the house was too thick, the kind of heavy, suffocating quiet that only comes when the heating dies. Below her, the floorboards of the old homestead remained dead. No creaks. No groans. None of the familiar, rhythmic pacing that usually tracked Leander’s restless nights.

The bastard had taken her phone. He’d turned the key in the heavy pine door, the brass tongue clicking into the strike plate with a dry, heavy snap like a finger bone breaking in a fist.

Kestrel squeezed her ribs, her fingers digging through the coarse wool of her sweater to find the heat of her own skin. Her hands were already losing the fight, the tips white and stiff. She had spent twenty years of her life sweat-equitying The Perfect Homestead, turning the rocky, stubborn soil of this valley into a business that actually paid a mortgage. Now, she was locked in the very frame she’d spent her life savings to restore.

How long does it take a body to freeze in an unheated upstate bedroom? Six hours? Eight?

She turned away from the window. The darkness wasn't total. The moon had cleared the ridge of hemlocks, casting a cold, milky light across the wide pine floorboards. It caught the washstand, the high wardrobe, and the row of hand-thrown stoneware crocks lined up on the lower shelf near the brick chimney.

Her sourdough crocks.

She crossed the room, her boots clicking softly. When she knelt, her knees hit the floor with a dull, aching thud that rattled up her shins. Down here, near the baseboards, the air was even colder. It smelled of dry rot, old lime mortar, and the faint, vinegary tang of the dormant starters she kept in the dark.

She reached out. Her fingers brushed the side of the nearest crock. The glaze was thick, salt-fired, and cold as a headstone.

The garden.

Kestrel’s hand drifted to her pocket, her fingers brushing the cold metal of the sideboard key she still carried. The root cellar. The smell of wet earth and rotting beets. The way the dirt floor had been freshly turned near the back wall, beneath the old potato bins.

"The trowel," Kestrel said, her voice shaking. "The one with the white slip on the handle."

"I had to dig," Eldora said simply. "The clay down there is heavy. It sticks to iron. But the white slip keeps the soil from clinging to the blade. It’s a trick I learned forty years ago. But Oona..." Eldora’s voice cracked then, a genuine note of regret slipping through her flinty exterior. "Oona was a sweet girl. But she had those big, curious eyes. She was supposed to be at the studio glazing the ramekins, but she came down to the cellar house to get some garlic. She saw me carrying the trowel out. She saw the mud on my boots. She saw the state I was in."

Eldora shook her head, her hand reaching back toward the iron stoking rod. "She didn't understand. She asked me if I’d been burying the old turnips. But then she saw Maceo’s car parked behind the tractor shed. She wasn't stupid, Kestrel. She would have talked to Choy. She would have told her about the mud."

"So you killed her too," Kestrel said. Her heart was hammering against her ribs like a trapped bird. "An accident. A fall on the ice."

"It was quick," Eldora said, her fingers tightening around the cold iron grip of the rod. "She didn't suffer. Not like Maceo."

She took a step forward, the iron rod swinging slightly, its red tip glowing in the dark space between them.

"And now?" Kestrel asked, backing up until her shoulder blades hit the rough pine upright of the kiln shed. "What about me?"

"The kiln is at eighteen hundred degrees, Kestrel," Eldora said, her eyes vacant, fixed on some point just behind Kestrel's head. "Anagama fires take days to cool. By the time anyone looks inside, there will be nothing left but bone ash and glass. A terrible accident. The propane line... the one you noticed earlier. Everyone knows Leander's work is sloppy. They’ll think he did it. They’ll think you were caught in the flare-up."

The metal tip of the rod rose, pointing directly at Kestrel's chest. The heat from the iron scorched the nylon of her jacket, the smell of melting synthetic thread sharp and biting.

"Eldora, don't," Kestrel said, her hands coming up.

"It’s the only way to keep the peace," Eldora whispered. "The only way to keep the homestead perfect."

A sharp, metallic snap echoed from the edge of the clearing.

"Drop the rod, Eldora."

The voice was cold, flat, and entirely devoid of the local Vermont drawl.

Detective Kateri Choy stood at the entrance of the shed, her regulatory navy-blue wool coat open, her service weapon held in a steady, two-handed grip. Her boots were caked with grey clay, and her face was pale under the flashing blue lights that had just begun to filter through the hemlocks behind her.

Behind Choy, Leander stood in the shadows, his face hollow, his mouth slightly open as he looked from Eldora to the burning journals. He looked smaller than he ever had, his broad shoulders hunched against the cold, his expensive flannel shirt torn at the elbow.

Eldora didn't move for a long three seconds. She looked at Choy, then down at the iron rod, then at Kestrel. For a moment, the madness in her eyes seemed to flicker, replaced by a profound, heavy exhaustion.

"It was such a good system," Eldora muttered.

She let the iron rod go. It hit the gravel with a heavy, dead clang, sparks flying from the glowing tip as it rolled toward the puddle of water near the woodpile.

Choy moved in quickly, her movements efficient and practiced. She pushed Eldora against the brick buttress of the kiln, her hands running over the canvas apron, finding nothing but a small pocket knife and a handful of dry lavender.

Leander stepped forward, his eyes fixed on Kestrel. "Kestrel... I didn't know. I swear to God, I didn't know about Maceo. I thought... I thought Eldora was just helping us with the taxes."

Kestrel didn't look at him. She couldn't.

Instead, she watched the open firebox of the kiln. Inside, the blue-bound journal had completely disintegrated. The pages had turned to white ash, floating upward through the flue, carried by the immense draft into the cold, dark sky. The names of the people she had fled, the recipes she had stolen from her grandmother, the lists of ingredients for the bread that had built her life—all of it was being converted into pure, unyielding heat.

"It's over," Choy said, her voice loud over the roar of the chimney as she clicked the handcuffs onto Eldora's wrists. "We found the trowel in the back of your Volvo, Eldora. The state lab already ran the quick-test on the clay trace. It matched the soil from the root cellar."

Eldora didn't reply. She allowed herself to be led away, her head bowed, her small boots dragging in the mud.

Leander reached out to touch Kestrel’s arm. "Kestrel, we can rebuild. We can clear the name. The business—"

"The business is gone, Leander," Kestrel said. Her voice was surprisingly steady, her chattering teeth finally stilled by the warmth of the fire.

She walked over to the woodpile, grabbed a long split of dry ash, and used it to push the remaining journals from the wire crate directly into the firebox. She watched them catch, the flames turning green and gold as the chemical inks burned.

The brand was dead. The Perfect Homestead had always been a lie, anyway. Just a pretty screen to hide the bones of two dead kids and the rotting corpse of an Oregon cult's cash. The followers online would find another rustic icon to admire, another woman in a linen apron to tell them how to live.

But as Kestrel stood there, her face burning in the heat of the dying kiln while the frost settled on her hair, she didn't feel the cold.

She looked up at the white ash drifting into the black pine boughs, dissolving into the night air until it was nothing but grey dust on the snow. She felt the heavy, suffocating weight of the perfect life slip from her shoulders, leaving her cold, broke, and entirely, frustratingly free.
