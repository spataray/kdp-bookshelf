    The Charybdis Gravity: A Hard Sci-Fi Locked-Room Mystery

    Chapter 1: The Weight of Silence

    The silence aboard the Astraea Prime wasn’t empty. It was thick. Like cold grease coating the back of your throat.

    Deep in the ship’s primary spine, the air carried the stale, metallic tang of recycled oxygen, heavy with the sweet, hot-iron smell of leaking hydraulic fluid. Three seconds. That was the rhythm. Every three seconds, a low-frequency hum vibrated through the steel deck plates—not a sound, but a physical pulse that rattled your molars. The Charybdis Singularity was pulling at the ship's keel from three thousand kilometers out. A massive, silent mouth yawning in the dark.

    Vasilis Karras tugged at the collar of his stiff gray inspector’s tunic. It was cheap wool, scratchy and damp with sweat. His left knee clicked with every step. A little souvenir from the Ceres docks. Six years ago, a pressure seal blew, three men evaporated, and Karras lost half his patella. Now, whenever the atmospheric pressure dropped or the local gravity shifted, the joint throbbed like a rusted hinge. Today, it was screaming.

    He paused in the corridor outside Sector Four. The overhead strip lights had reverted to a low-power amber, painting the bulkheads in the dirty color of a bruised peach. The ship-wide blackout was four hours old now. The official line from the bridge was high-density space dust scraping the external arrays. Sure. And he was the Emperor of Ganymede. In his earpiece, the static was a dry, endless hiss. Like sand sliding down a glass pane.

    "Karras."

    He spun—or tried to, his knee protesting. Zola Ndiaye was leaning against the bulkhead arch, her dark skin washed purple by the emergency lights. She wasn't carrying a sidearm—weapons were a luxury and a hazard in a high-gravity sector—but she was white-knuckling a heavy, brass-tipped pneumatic pry bar anyway. She looked like she wanted to hit something.

    "Tell me the deck isn't actually settling," Ndiaye said. Her voice was too quiet. The usual dry, sarcastic edge was gone.

    "It’s not the deck," Karras replied. His tongue felt like cardboard. "It's the Charybdis tide. The structural dampeners are drawing power from the secondary grid to fight the drift. That’s why the lights are down."

    "And the comms?"

    "The dust is thick out there, Zola. Or so the bridge says."

    "Do you believe them?"

    Karras didn't answer. What was the point?

    He looked past her toward the heavy, round hatch of the Gravity Well Vault. The door was made of reinforced titanium-carbide, built to handle an internal explosion—or keep ten atmospheres of pressure from blowing out into the corridor. In the center of the wheel was a viewing port. Six inches of armored glass.

    The other two were huddled near the diagnostic terminal, their faces lit by the amber screen. Genevieve DuPont, the mission director, had her arms crossed tight against her ribs. Her hair—usually pinned into a silver, drill-sergeant bun—was coming undone, damp strands sticking to her temples. Beside her, Ravi Chandra was sweating. Not a light sheen, but a frantic, dripping panic. His fingers slid uselessly across his tablet's stylus.

    "How long has he been in there?" Karras asked, stepping forward.

    "The log says he entered at zero-three-hundred," DuPont said. Her voice was brittle. Try to touch it, and it would shatter. "Vitals dropped off the telemetry fifteen minutes later. Right when the comms died. We assumed it was the interference. The sensor suite dropping the feed."

    "And the manual lock?"

    "Locked from the inside," Chandra whispered. He didn't look up from his screen. "The electronic interface is completely dead. It's not taking bridge codes. The vault’s local generator is looped on its own circuit. It’s... it’s a black box, Vasilis. We're totally cut off."

    Karras stepped up to the glass. He pressed his forehead to the frame. The steel was freezing, but inside, the vault was pitch. No main lights. Just the slow, rhythmic blue pulse of the central gravity core, washing the room in ghostly, underwater sweeps.

    He pressed his palm to the glass. A faint, rhythmic vibration. "The core is still running."

    "Running?" Chandra’s voice cracked. He looked up, his eyes bloodshot. "It’s not just running. The hardwired sensors are reading ninety-eight meters per second squared. Ten Gs, Vasilis. Ten."

    A cold weight dropped into Karras’s gut. Ten Gs. A human body doesn't survive that. It becomes an anchor. The blood pools in your legs instantly, your heart can't push against the crushing weight of its own fluid, and your ribs splinter under the sudden, impossible mass of your own chest cavity.

    "Where is Wolfe?" Karras asked.

    "Near the console," DuPont said, her jaw tightening. "We can’t see him clearly. The distortion from the core is scrambling the camera feeds. But he’s in there."

    Karras used his thumb to wipe a smear of moisture from the glass. His skin caught on something sticky. Tacky. He pulled his hand back, frowning. In the orange light, it was a tiny smudge of dark grease at the lower edge of the bezel. He rubbed his fingers together. Slippery. Smelled like graphite.

    "Ravi," Karras said, keeping his voice level. "When was the last time you performed maintenance on this door lock?"

    Chandra shook his head quickly. "Not since Ganymede. The mechanical seals are rated for five hundred cycles. We haven't even run fifty."

    "Then why is there fresh lubricant on the glass?"

    He didn't wait for an answer. He let his hand drop, rubbing the residue off on his trousers. A tiny detail. But details were what kept you alive in the black. Or, in Wolfe's case, what killed you.

    "We need to get the door open," DuPont insisted. She stepped closer, the smell of her expensive lavender soap momentarily cutting through the stink of oil and sweat. "Wolfe is the only one with the navigation keys for the Charybdis transit. If we don't correct our course in the next twelve hours, Charybdis takes our stern. We’ll be pulled past the event horizon before the system comms even come back online to hear us scream."

    "We can't just blow the seal," Ndiaye said. She tapped her pry bar against the deck plate. Clang. Clang. "At ten Gs, the pressure differential will buckle the frame. The corridor air will rush in, and the door will turn into a kinetic slug. It’ll punch right through the hull."

    "The field has to be cut from the local panel," Karras said. He turned to Chandra. "Ravi. Show me."

    Chandra led him to a small recessed alcove in the bulkhead. He yanked the cover off, revealing a tangled mess of fiber optics and copper-shielded lines.

    "Three capacitors under the deck," Chandra said, pointing a shaking finger at three fat, cylindrical fuses. "Pull them, and the field should drop. But it’s a gamble. A sudden collapse can trigger a gravimetric whip-lash."

    "How bad?"

    "A few seconds of zero-G, then a sudden spike. It could tear the core right off its mounts."

    Karras touched the primary line. Warm. Way too warm. "This cable is pulling serious current. If the core was just locked in a loop, it wouldn't draw this much power. Not unless..."

    "Unless what?" DuPont asked.

    "Unless someone is forcing it," Karras said. He looked at the diagnostic screen. The power curves weren't flat. They were jagged. Sharp peaks and deep valleys. "This isn't a glitch. A glitch is flat or wild. This is a pattern. It’s modulating."

    "Modulating?" Chandra leaned in, his brow furrowed. "That’s impossible. The safety governors would have tripped if the variance exceeded five percent."

    "They would," Karras agreed. "Unless the governors were bypassed."

    He looked back at the graphite grease on the glass. He knew that grease. It was used on the gears of the manual override levers—the ones inside the vault, meant for emergency escapes if the electronic systems failed.

    If Wolfe had been trying to get out, he would have grabbed that lever. Why didn't the door open?

    "We pull the fuses," Karras said. "Zola, get the pry bar in the seal. When the field drops, the pins will pull back, but the frame might be warped. We’ll have about four seconds before the backup safety magnets kick in."

    Ndiaye nodded, her knuckles turning white on the iron bar. "Ready."

    Karras grabbed the first capacitor. The metal was cold, but the current hummed right through his palm, sending a sharp spark of pain up his bad arm. "On three. One."

    The ship groaned. A heavy, sickening lurch. Charybdis was pulling.

    "Two."

    He braced his feet.

    "Three."

    He yanked.

    The fuse blew free in a shower of blue sparks, casting a split-second glare over their faces. The deck vibration dropped an octave.

    "Again!" Chandra screamed.

    Karras grabbed the other two, threw his weight back, and ripped them out. His bad knee gave out, throwing him to the deck, but he kept his grip on the fuses.

    The hum died. The corridor went dead silent. Inside the vault, the blue pulse flickered once and went dark.

    "Now!" Karras yelled, scrambling up.

    Ndiaye slammed the pry bar into the seam. She threw her entire weight against it. The steel shrieked.

    "It’s stuck!" she gasped. "The damn lock is melted!"

    Karras threw himself beside her, grabbing the bar. They pulled together. He could smell her sweat, the ozone of the blown fuses, and then—something else. A sickening, sweet smell slipped through the crack. Like a butcher's shop on a hot afternoon. Mixed with burnt hair.

    With a violent crack, the locking bolt sheared. The door swung open and slammed against the wall.

    The air that hit them was hot, thick, and smelled of iron.

    Karras went in first.

    The vault was twelve meters wide. In the center, the graviton core sat suspended in its magnetic rings, dark and dead.

    But Karras wasn't looking at the core.

    Dr. Tiberius Wolfe lay near the central control console. Or, rather, what remained of him.

    At sixty, Wolfe had been a tall, gangly man who smelled of tobacco and old paper. Now, he was flat. The ten-G field had crushed him against the steel plates like an insect under a boot. His limbs were splayed at impossible, broken angles, the joints driven straight into the muscle. His blue uniform was soaked black with fluid forced through his skin. His face was a bloated, purple mask, his eyes dark, jelly-filled craters.

    DuPont made a small, choking sound and turned away, hand over her mouth.

    Chandra took one look, gagged, stumbled back into the hall, and threw up.

    Karras knelt. The deck plates were hot. The sheer friction of the gravity had heated the steel. He reached out, his hand hovering over Wolfe's chest. The sternum had been driven straight back to meet the spine.

    "It was fast," Ndiaye said from the doorway, her voice dead. "At ten Gs, he would’ve blacked out in seconds."

    "No," Karras said. He pointed to the floor. "He didn't."

    Near Wolfe’s left hand—twisted into a rigid, bloody claw—were three deep scratches in the deck plating. His fingernails were gone, ripped back to the raw beds, leaving dark, greasy smears of blood on the metal.

    "He was crawling," Karras said. His voice was empty. "He was alive when it started to ramp up. He was trying to reach the release."

    "But the manual lever is right there," Ndiaye said, pointing to the wall beside the frame.

    Karras stood, his knee clicking. He walked to the mechanical lever, painted in yellow and black stripes. He reached out and touched it. It flopped down with a loose, hollow click.

    He bent down. The lever was supposed to turn a steel rod that mechanically retracted the locking pins. The rod was bare. The steel cotter pin that held the linkage together was missing.

    "The pin is gone," Karras said.

    Ndiaye peered over his shoulder. "Did it snap under the pressure?"

    "A hardened steel pin doesn't snap without leaving shards in the sleeve," Karras said. He clicked on his pocket flashlight, shining the narrow beam into the mechanism. "It's clean. No shavings. No dust. Someone pulled it out. By hand."

    "That's crazy," DuPont said, her voice shaking but hard. "Only engineering and navigation have access here. Why would anyone pull a safety pin?"

    "To make sure he couldn't get out," Karras said.

    He swept the light over the console. The screen was shattered. He leaned over, breathing in the stench of burnt plastic. The primary screens were dead, but the analog backups—the mechanical dials—were still there.

    He shone the light on the pressure recorder. "It wasn't a sudden spike."

    Chandra wiped his mouth and looked in. "What?"

    "Look at the ink line," Karras said, pointing the flashlight at the paper drum. "It didn't jump. If it had, the automatic breakers would have tripped. This was a slow ramp. It took four minutes to reach ten Gs."

    "Four minutes," Ndiaye whispered. She rubbed her neck.

    "He felt it," Karras said. "He stood here while his own body weight doubled, tripled, quadrupled. He tried to walk. He fell. He crawled. The door was locked, so he tried to claw his way under the seal."

    "But who?" DuPont demanded. "We're in a blackout. We're blind. If the crew thinks there's a murderer on board, we'll have a mutiny before the gravity well pulls us in."

    "An accident doesn't pull a cotter pin," Karras said. He shone his light under the lip of the console.

    Something metallic glinted. He reached under the terminal's base and pulled out a small, rectangular plastic block.

    A data-slate. Scorched, but intact. A tiny yellow light was blinking on the side: Low Battery.

    He turned it over. Scratched into the casing were two initials: R.C.

    Karras looked up. Directly at Ravi Chandra.

    Chandra backed away, his boots squeaking on the wet floor. "That's... that's my old slate. I lost it three weeks ago! I logged it with security. Zola, tell him!"

    "You said you lost it in the mess hall, Ravi," Ndiaye said, her eyes cold.

    "I did! Someone must have taken it!"

    "And left it under a console in a locked vault?" DuPont asked.

    "I don't know!" Chandra's voice cracked into a shriek. "I haven't been in here since Tuesday! The logs will show that!"

    "The logs are on the mainframe," Karras said. "The one that's currently dark because of the 'space dust.'"

    He walked toward Chandra, his bad knee dragging. With every step, he felt the heavy, thrumming pull of Charybdis. Just outside the door, his boot hit something small.

    He stopped. Shone the light down.

    In the gutter of the door frame lay a tiny, silver cotter pin.

    He picked it up. Clean, except for a smear of graphite grease. And a hair.

    A single, long, straight, jet-black hair caught in the loop.

    Karras looked at Chandra's short, curly hair. Then Ndiaye's tight braids. Then DuPont's silver curls.

    Wolfe was bald.

    "Who else has access to the override keys?" Karras asked.

    DuPont swallowed. "The bridge officers. And the science team."

    "And who has long, black hair?"

    They looked at each other as the realization hit.

    "Genevieve," Ndiaye said slowly. "Where's Hana?"

    "She was on the upper deck," DuPont said, her voice dropping. "Helping with the sensor calibration."

    "The calibration that caused the blackout," Karras said.

    He stared at the tiny pin. The hair caught in it was a cold, quiet thread leading straight into the dark.

    The hull groaned. A deep, metal-tearing sound that rattled their teeth.

    The amber lights flared bright white, hissed, and died.

    Total darkness.

    The hum of the singularity outside grew louder, a heavy vibration that felt like it was pulling the marrow right out of Karras's bones.

    "Vasilis?" Ndiaye's voice was tight with panic. "What's happening?"

    "The secondary grid is dead," Chandra whimpered. "We lost power."

    "No," Karras said, his voice quiet in the dark. He could feel a cold draft moving down the hall. "The power didn't fail. Someone cut it."

    He reached for his flashlight. Before his fingers touched the switch, a heavy, sliding sound echoed from the end of the corridor.

    The airlock door. Cycling open.

    Chapter 2: The Frozen Lever

    The gravity near the core of the Charybdis didn’t pull. It dragged. It was a greasy, uneven weight that settled behind Vasilis Karras’s kneecaps and made the air in his lungs feel like wet flannel. Down here, in the throat of the station, the micro-singularity powering their propulsion drive was only three bulkheads away. Every weld in the deck plates groaned a low, sub-audible B-flat. You didn't hear it so much as feel it vibrating through the thick soles of your magnetic boots, rattling your teeth, and leaving a sour metallic taste under your tongue.

    Karras knelt before the outer hatch of the Gravity Well Vault.

    The air was frigid. It smelled of ozone, machine grease, and that distinct, sweetish rot of scorched wiring insulation. Inside that vault, Dr. Tiberius Wolfe’s body was already stiffening under the terrible, fluctuating pressure of a localized gravitational field. But Karras wasn't looking at the reinforced viewport. He was staring at the manual safety override lever.

    "It shouldn't be in this position," Ravi Chandra said. The Chief Engineer's voice was a thin wire stretched past its limit. He stood three feet back, hands buried deep in the pockets of his grease-stained utility jumpsuit, fingers twitching against his thighs. "The system is automated. If the field destabilizes, the software triggers the release. It doesn't need a manual throw."

    "And yet," Karras said. The word was a flat rasp. He didn't look up. He extended a gloved hand and touched the metal.

    The safety override was a solid bar of tungsten-carbide alloy, painted a high-visibility yellow that had begun to flake near the pivot. It was a fail-safe for absolute emergencies. If the digital grid fried, a human operator was supposed to dump the vacuum seals and vent the chamber to save the station.

    It was thrown downward, into the lock position.

    But it wasn't just thrown. It was deformed.

    The thick bar of alloy was bent to the left at a nineteen-degree angle, the metal puckered and white at the point of flexion. Karras ran his thumb over the distortion. The cold of the metal seeped through the thin, insulated synthetic skin of his glove.

    "To pull this," Karras murmured, "takes four hundred kilograms of torque. The pneumatic assists are disabled during active gravity runs to prevent some idiot from bumping into it. This was pulled by hand."

    "That’s impossible," Genevieve DuPont said from the gantry above. Her voice fell like ice water. She descended the metal ladder with a precise, clinical grace, her silver-threaded corporate tunic uncreased despite the twenty-hour shift they had all just endured. "No one on this station has that kind of physical capacity. Not without an exoskeleton. And the cargo loaders are locked in the bay."

    "Not all of them," Karras said. He stood up slowly, his joints popping in the heavy gravity. He turned to face her.

    Genevieve didn't look at Karras. Her eyes were fixed on the door, reflecting the amber strobe of the vault's warning lights. "If you are implying sabotage, Karras, you should be very careful. This project is funded by the Ministry of Maritime Space. A delay of even a week costs more than your five-year contract is worth."

    "Wolfe is dead, Genevieve," Karras said, his tone dry as dust. "The cost of his life has already been deducted from the balance sheet."

    He felt the familiar, cold knot tightening in his stomach—the Ghost that never quite left him. It was the same dry taste he’d had in his mouth five years ago on the Astraea, when the seal on Sector Four had failed. He had believed the telemetry then. He had trusted the automated logs that said the hatch was clear, right up until the metal buckled and three hundred crew members were swept into the black. How do you trust a digital display after that? You don't. Metal doesn't lie. Only the people who bend it do.

    He turned his attention back to the deck.

    At the base of the override housing, the floor plates were dark, almost black. Karras knelt again, ignoring the protest of his lower back. He drew a small, silver-cased diagnostic stylus from his belt and clicked the blue spectrum light onto the floor.

    Under the ultraviolet beam, a faint, crystalline residue shivered. It looked like spilled salt, but it wasn't.

    "Ravi," Karras said. "Bring the sniffer."

    The engineer hesitated, then scrambled forward, pulling a yellow sensor unit from his tool belt. He knelt beside Karras, his breath coming in short, ragged puffs. He held the nozzle of the sensor over the white crystals.

    The device beeped twice—a sharp, nervous sound.

    ``` [SYSTEM LOG ANALYSIS: CORE SENSOR GRID 04] -------------------------------------------------- 02:14:05 UTC - Thermal Sensor 09 (Vault Ext): Offline [Signal Intercepted] 02:14:11 UTC - Thermal Sensor 10 (Vault Ext): Offline [Signal Intercepted] 02:15:00 UTC - Gravity Well Manual Override: Engaged 02:20:12 UTC - Core Diagnostics: Nominal (No anomalies detected) -------------------------------------------------- WARNING: Local temperature drop detected at Sensor Grid 04 prior to disconnect. ```

    "Helium-3," Ravi whispered, reading the tiny screen. "Liquid state. But that's... that's impossible. The cooling lines for the gravity coils are double-jacketed. There are no leaks."

    "It's not a leak," Karras said. He pointed the blue light toward the environmental sensor housing mounted on the bulkhead three inches above the deck. The sensor was a small, slotted metal dome designed to monitor air quality and infrared signatures around the vault entrance.

    The slotted dome was choked with a fine, glittering frost.

    "Someone sprayed it," Karras said. "Direct application. Liquid helium-3 has a boiling point of three Kelvin. You spray that onto a thermal sensor, and what happens?"

    Ravi swallowed hard, his Adam's apple bobbing. "It... it thermal-shocks the unit. The sensor registers an absolute zero reading instantly. The system thinks the sensor has failed or gone offline, so it ignores the telemetry for sixty seconds while it attempts a cold reboot."

    "A sixty-second blind spot," Karras said. "Exactly enough time to step out of the shadows, grab that lever, and throw it with enough force to bend the lock-pin."

    "But the force," Genevieve insisted, her arms crossed tight over her chest. Her fingers tapped a steady, impatient rhythm against her elbow. "Even if someone blinded the cameras, how did they bend tungsten? Zola Ndiaye is the only one who wears a hydraulic frame during her security sweeps. Are you accusing her?"

    As if summoned by her name, a heavy tread sounded on the gantry above.

    Zola Ndiaye descended the steps. She was a tall, broad-shouldered woman with dark, weathered skin and eyes that seemed permanently narrowed against the harsh glare of the station's fluorescent strips. She didn't wear her full tactical gear, but the black nylon harness of her security frame sat snug over her shoulders, the small, titanium-cased servo-motors on her joints glinting in the amber light.

    "Let's leave my harness out of this," Zola said, her voice a deep, gravelly rumble. She stepped onto the deck, her weight shifting easily despite the gravity. "I was in the command module when the alarm went off. The logs show my biometric keycard was used to access the comms deck at 02:10."

    "Keys can be cloned, Zola," Karras said, his voice quiet. He stood up, wiping his hands on a rag from his pocket. He smelled the faint, bitter tang of the cryo-coolant on his fingertips. "And harnesses can be borrowed."

    "The frame is biometrically locked to my neural signature," Zola said, stepping closer. She stood a full head taller than Ravi, her presence occupying the narrow corridor like a bulkhead. "Nobody wears this but me. And I didn't lock Wolfe in."

    "Then who did?" Genevieve asked. Her voice had lost some of its cold composure; a small, sharp edge of panic was beginning to show through. "If it wasn't one of us, then we have an intruder. Which is statistically impossible given our distance from the jump-gate."

    "There are no intruders on the Charybdis," Karras said. "Just five people who wanted Wolfe to stop his work."

    "Four," Ravi corrected softly, his eyes darting between them. "With Wolfe gone, there are only four of us down here."

    Karras didn't answer. He turned his back on them, walking slowly toward the viewport of the Gravity Well Vault. The glass was triple-reinforced quartz, six inches thick, designed to withstand the massive gravitational shear of the core experiments.

    He leaned his forehead against the cold glass.

    Inside, the vault was dark, lit only by the violet glow of the static containment field. In the center of the chamber, suspended three feet above the floor, was the gravity generator—a spherical brass cage that spun slowly, its brass arms rotating with a silent, hypnotic precision.

    And below it, crumpled like a discarded flight suit, lay Dr. Tiberius Wolfe.

    His face was pressed against the deck plates. Even from here, Karras could see the unnatural elongation of the corpse's neck. The localized gravity field had been dialed up to twelve Gs before the system was locked down. Wolfe's bones had simply collapsed under his own weight. His chest was flattened, his rib cage crushed against his lungs. High gravity didn't just kill you; it turned you into a puddle of meat.

    But it was his hand that caught Karras's attention.

    Wolfe’s right hand was outstretched, his fingers stiffened into claws. He had been dragging himself toward the emergency release panel on the inside of the door. But the panel had been deactivated when the outer lever was thrown.

    There was a smudge on the inner glass.

    It was faint, barely visible against the violet light of the containment field. A grease mark, left by a frantic, dying hand. But it wasn't a palm print. It was a single, long streak that ended in a sharp, circular smear.

    Karras squinted. He tapped his diagnostic stylus against the glass, changing the light spectrum to a high-intensity green.

    The smudge on the inside of the glass was a perfect circle, about two centimeters in diameter. It looked like the imprint of a rubber seal or a specialized tool.

    "Ravi," Karras said, his voice dropping an octave. "What kind of maintenance suits do we use for the core repairs?"

    Ravi took a step forward, looking over Karras’s shoulder. "The Mark-7s. They... they have magnetic boots and pressurized gloves with vulcanized silicone pads on the fingertips."

    "And the diameter of those pads?"

    Ravi went pale. "Twenty millimeters. Exactly."

    "So Wolfe was wearing his maintenance suit when he went in," Genevieve said quickly. "He must have tried to clean the window before he lost consciousness."

    "No," Karras said. "Wolfe's suit is still hanging in the locker in Section Two. I checked it on my way down. The one he’s wearing in there is a light-duty pressure suit. It doesn't have vulcanized pads. It has leather-reinforced palms."

    A heavy silence settled over the corridor. The only sound was the low, rhythmic thrum of the singularity drive, a heartbeat for a ghost ship.

    "If Wolfe didn't make that mark," Karras said, his eyes fixed on the circular smudge, "then someone else was inside that chamber before the gravity was turned up. Someone who was wearing a Mark-7 maintenance suit. Someone who wanted to make sure Wolfe couldn't reach the manual release."

    "That’s crazy," Ravi said, his voice cracking. "The vault was pressurized. The lock-out sequence takes three minutes. If anyone was in there with him, they would have been trapped when the field activated."

    "Unless they had a remote bypass," Karras said. He turned around, his back to the glass. "A software override that allowed them to cycle the inner hatch from a handheld terminal. Ravi, who has the root access codes for the environmental control systems?"

    Ravi looked at Genevieve. Genevieve looked at Zola.

    "All of us," Zola said flatly. "But only the Chief Engineer has the diagnostic terminal capable of executing a live bypass during a gravity run."

    "That's a lie!" Ravi shouted, his face turning a deep, blotchy red. "You have the security overrides, Zola! You could have done it from the main console!"

    "The main console logs every command," Zola said, her hands dropping to her sides, near the heavy metallic hinges of her harness. "If I had bypassed the lock, my name would be on the telemetry. It’s not."

    "Because you deleted it!"

    "You can't delete core telemetry, Ravi," Genevieve said, her voice dripping with venom. "You designed the system. You know that. Unless, of course, you left a backdoor in the code when the station was built."

    Karras watched them. He let the accusations fly, listening to the cadence of their panic.

    Show me the fear, he thought. The guilty don't just lie; they redirect.

    Genevieve’s fingers were still tapping. It wasn't a nervous tic; it was a rhythmic, calculated movement. She was an executive, a politician, used to managing crises by shifting the weight. But her eyes were too bright. They darted to the safety lever again and again, as if she were waiting for it to move.

    And Ravi. The young engineer was sweating through his collar, his skin gray in the sodium light. He looked like a man who knew his own machines had betrayed him. Or perhaps he was just terrified that Karras would find out how they had.

    "Enough," Karras said.

    His voice wasn't loud, but it had the weight of twenty years of iron-bound authority behind it. They stopped talking.

    "We are going to open the outer hatch," Karras said.

    "We can't," Ravi whispered. "The gravity well is still holding a residual charge. If we open it now, the pressure differential will blow the seals."

    "Then we bleed it," Karras said. "Slowly. Zola, get the manual wrench from the tool bay. Ravi, prepare the decompression protocols. We’re going inside."

    "And what about me?" Genevieve asked, her chin lifting.

    "You," Karras said, "are going to stand right there. And you are going to tell me why Dr. Wolfe's private log files were accessed from your terminal forty minutes before he died."

    Genevieve did not flinch. She simply stared at him, her eyes two chips of polished gray stone. "I was his supervisor, Karras. I have every right to review his work."

    "Even when that work is marked as classified by the Ministry?" Karras asked. He stepped closer to her, his magnetic boots clanking softly against the deck. "Even when he was planning to shut down the project because the gravity core was structurally unstable?"

    He saw the tiny muscle in her jaw twitch. A fraction of a millimeter. But it was there.

    "The core is perfectly stable," she said.

    "We'll see," Karras said. "We'll see what the metal tells us."

    Zola returned a moment later, carrying a heavy, hydraulic torque-wrench. The tool was two feet long, its carbon-fiber chassis scarred from years of heavy industrial use. She stepped up to the bent lever, her harness whining as she lifted her arms.

    "Stand back," she said.

    Karras retreated a few paces, his eyes never leaving the lever.
